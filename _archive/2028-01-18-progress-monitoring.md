**Your First 2028 Progress Monitoring Check-In — Turning the Data Baseline Into Governance Decisions**

January 18, 2028

---

Your board is finalizing its 2028 goals. The data packet is in your hands. The baseline is set.

Now the question that determines whether those goals produce actual improvement for students, or whether they become one more document that sits in a binder until June.

Does your board have a real Progress Monitoring system — a process for checking goal progress at regular intervals and making decisions when the data says something is off track?

I am asking this question specifically about the first check-in of the 2028 cycle. The January data packet is not just the starting point for goal adoption. As I said last week, it is the baseline for Progress Monitoring. The first check-in is the one that establishes the rhythm for the entire year. Get this right, and the rest of the year is a matter of follow-through. Get this wrong, and the cadence never establishes itself.

Here is exactly what the first Progress Monitoring check-in of 2028 should look like.

---

**The baseline conversation — where January starts.**

The January data packet is your board's most recent picture of where students are. That is the starting point for every goal discussion your board will have in 2028.

Here is what the first check-in requires. Before the meeting, every board member reviews the goal data dashboard — one page per goal or goal area, showing baseline, current trajectory, and student group breakdown. That dashboard exists because the superintendent built it as part of the January data packet the board received for the goal adoption process.

Your board president frames the check-in with one question: "Are we on track to achieve the 2028 goals, based on the January baseline?"

That is the only question that matters at the first check-in. Not "is the data perfect?" Not "do we need more information?" Not "should we revise the goals before we have looked at data against them?" The question is: relative to the baseline the board accepted as the starting point, where are students today?

The answer is either on track, at risk, or off track. Those three categories are the output of every Progress Monitoring check-in. They are not opinions. They are the result of comparing the current data point to the goal's expected trajectory.

If the current data point is at or above the trajectory line, the goal is on track. If it is within one reporting period of the trajectory line, the goal is at risk. If it is below that, the goal is off track.

The board that names the trajectory for every goal at the first check-in is building an honest picture of where the district stands. The board that avoids naming trajectory — that talks about "positive trends" or "areas of concern" without being specific — is avoiding accountability for what the data says.

---

**The three decisions — the only three a board needs to make.**

Once the board knows each goal's trajectory, the next step is decision-making. Progress Monitoring without a decision is an observation. Observations do not produce improvement.

Here are the three decisions a board can make at any check-in.

Decision one: accept the trajectory. The goal is on track. The board does not direct a change. The board documents acceptance in the meeting record and moves to the next goal area. This decision takes thirty seconds. It matters because it tells the superintendent: keep doing what you are doing.

Decision two: direct a change. The goal is at risk or off track. The board directs the superintendent to take action. The board's job is not to design the action. The board's job is to name the gap and ask the superintendent to return at the next check-in with a specific plan for closing it. "The third-grade reading trajectory shows a two-point decline from the January baseline. Superintendent, please return at the February check-in with your three highest-leverage interventions and a timeline for each."

Decision three: request more information. The board does not have enough data to make one of the first two decisions. The board names exactly what data is missing and directs the superintendent to provide it. "The graduation rate data is incomplete — the fourth-quarter credits are not yet posted. Please provide updated graduation data at the February check-in."

The board that makes one of these three decisions for every goal at every check-in is governing. The board that reviews data, discusses it, and adjourns without a documented decision is holding a data information session — not a Progress Monitoring check-in.

Here is the rule I use when I coach boards through this: if the meeting ends and your board has not recorded a decision for each goal on the dashboard, the meeting is not finished. Extend it. Schedule a follow-up. Do not let ambiguity replace accountability.

---

**The first check-in is the hardest one — here is why.**

The first Progress Monitoring check-in of a new cycle is different from every subsequent check-in. Here is why.

First, there is no prior trajectory to compare against. The baseline is the comparison point. That means the board and the superintendent are establishing the pattern for the first time. The superintendent may present data differently than the board expected. The board may ask questions the superintendent did not anticipate. That friction is normal. Do not let it discourage you. The second check-in will be smoother because the pattern is set.

Second, the first check-in covers goals that were just adopted. The board is still learning the language of each goal. The superintendent is still building the monitoring infrastructure. Neither party should expect perfection. What both parties should expect is honesty — the superintendent presents the data as it is, the board makes the best decision it can with the data available, and both commit to improving the process at the next check-in.

Third, the first check-in sets the community's expectations for transparency. The board that publishes the Progress Monitoring dashboard — or at least a summary of the decisions made — is building community trust. The community that sees the board making decisions at regular intervals based on data knows that the board is serious about its goals. The community that never sees evidence of Progress Monitoring assumes the goals are aspirational statements, not operational commitments.

Here is my recommendation for the first check-in. Treat it as a learning session as much as a decision session. Schedule ninety minutes instead of sixty. The board president names up front that this is the first time the board is applying this process to the 2028 goals, and the board's priority is getting the process right — establishing the baseline trajectory, making the three decisions, and documenting the outcomes. The next check-in will be faster because the board will be working from its own precedent.

---

**Connecting the first check-in to the governance calendar.**

The first Progress Monitoring check-in does not happen in isolation. It is locked into the governance calendar the board adopted at the January organizational meeting.

Here is what the Q1 governance calendar should show.

**January 18:** First Progress Monitoring check-in. Board documents baseline trajectory for each goal. Board makes the three decisions for each goal. Board publishes dashboard summary.

**January 25:** Budget direction meeting. Board uses the Progress Monitoring baseline to inform budget priorities. A goal that is already at risk in January should influence budget allocation differently than a goal that is on track.

**February regular meeting:** Second Progress Monitoring check-in. Board reviews decisions from January and receives superintendent's action plans for any goals directed for change. Board makes new decisions.

**March regular meeting:** Third Progress Monitoring check-in. Board reviews ninety-day trend data. Board decides whether any goals need to be revised based on cumulative evidence.

The board that connects Progress Monitoring to the budget direction meeting in January is the board that governs with integrated decision-making. The board that treats Progress Monitoring and budget as separate conversations — happening in different rooms, with different agendas, disconnected from each other — is the board that adopts goals in March and discovers in September that the budget does not match the priorities.

---

**What comes next.**

This first Progress Monitoring check-in establishes the baseline trajectory for every 2028 goal. Next week's piece — the January budget direction conversation — shows exactly how those trajectories should inform your board's budget priorities.

Here is the connection. A goal that is on track in January does not require the same budget allocation as a goal that is already at risk. The budget direction conversation is where your board makes that distinction visible. If every goal receives the same proportional allocation regardless of trajectory, the budget is not driven by the Progress Monitoring data. It is driven by the historical spending pattern — which is the opposite of what your board committed to when it adopted data-driven goals.

The January organizational meeting set the structure. The data packet and public hearing established the evidence. The first Progress Monitoring check-in turns that evidence into a decision-making rhythm. The January budget direction conversation turns those decisions into resource commitments.

That is a connected governance launch. Every piece feeds into the next. Every meeting produces a decision that the next meeting acts on.

And by February, your board will be working from momentum instead of trying to build it from scratch.
