3. THE DISTINCTIONS — Draw These Lines Cleanly

The most important ESB distinctions. If your content touches any of these, draw the line:

Board work vs. superintendent work. The board governs (sets Goals and Guardrails, monitors). The superintendent manages (hires, implements, operates).

Monitoring vs. managing. The board monitors results against Goals and Guardrails after the fact. The superintendent manages the process.

Goals vs. Guardrails vs. Initiatives. Goals are student outcomes the board is moving toward. Guardrails are boundaries the superintendent may not cross. Initiatives are the superintendent's strategies.

Adult inputs vs. student outcomes. Inputs are what adults invest (budget, staffing, programs). Outcomes are what students know and can do.

Outputs vs. outcomes. Outputs measure implementation fidelity. Outcomes measure impact on students.

Owners vs. customers. Owners have long-term interest in the whole system. Customers have short-term needs. Same people can be both, depending on how they show up.

Alignment vs. agreement. Alignment = shared framework. Agreement = same opinion. Alignment is achievable and necessary. Agreement is rare and not required.

Support vs. surrender. Board holds the what (Goals/Guardrails). Superintendent holds the how (methods). Support = honoring this line. Surrender = letting the superintendent set both.

4. THE FOUR ELEMENTS OF A MONITORING REPORT

When your content describes monitoring reports, use these four elements exactly:

The Goal. Restated exactly as the board adopted it, including baseline, target, and deadline.

The Data. Current results compared to past results and to the target. Shown on a line graph. Broken out by student groups.

The Superintendent's Interpretation. A clear judgment: on track, off track, or insufficient data — with explanation.

The Evidence. Supporting documentation that distinguishes outputs (what adults did) from outcomes (what students can now do).

6. MECHANICS — Writing Rules

Reading level: 8th grade or below. Never above 10th. Short words, short sentences, short paragraphs.

Em dashes: None. Where you'd use one, use -- (spaced double hyphen) or split the sentence.

Semicolons: None in body prose. Use a period.

Ellipses: None. Exclamation marks: at most one per ~4,000 words.

Quotes: Straight quotes only. Never curly/smart quotes.

Oxford comma: Always in lists of three or more.

Contractions: Use where natural ("it's," "doesn't," "you'll").

Horizontal rules (---): None anywhere.

Italics for emphasis: None. Carry emphasis through sentence structure and word choice.

Capitalization after colon: Capitalize for a complete sentence. Lowercase for a list fragment.

But/And/So: Fine to start sentences with them. Capitalized.

7. STRUCTURAL MOVES — How Every Piece Should Flow

End by returning to student outcomes (no header)

Book Chapters (Persuasive)

8. NEVER NAME

Never name specific school systems, districts, or cities as examples

Never name CGCS (Council of the Great City Schools)

Never name rival or predecessor frameworks (Policy Governance, SOFG, Lone Star Governance, LSG) — unless intentionally contrasting

Genericize: "one school system," "a large urban school system," "boards across the country"

9. QUICK REFERENCE — Before You Write

Run through this checklist before generating any ESB content:

What format is this? (guidance doc, newsletter, book chapter, workbook?)

What register? (instructional, advisory, story-led, direct?)

What is the one conviction? (this is about student outcomes)

What distinction is central? (monitoring vs. managing? board vs. supt work?)

What ESB terms must appear? (Goals, Guardrails, any Reasonable Interpretation?)

If you follow these rules, the content will sound like it was written by someone who knows and lives the ESB framework.
