Governance Language ESB Coaches Avoid

As coaches, the words we choose either empower or diminish board effectiveness. Certain phrases that are common in school board work are never to be used in any Effective School Boards content -- not because they are inaccurate, but because they carry implicit assumptions that are counterproductive to what we're trying to accomplish. Our language must always reflect that boards are the representatives of the community's vision and values, operating with legitimate authority and responsibility, and capable of being transformative on behalf of the students they serve.

Phrases to Actively Avoid

"Micromanaging" / "Micromanage"

Why it's forbidden: This term frames board engagement with detail as inherently pathological. It suggests that any board that asks questions, requests information, or pays attention to implementation is overstepping. In practice, the term is used to dismiss legitimate oversight. The board's role includes monitoring progress toward Goals and Guardrails -- that requires engagement with data, results, and implementation. The question is not whether the board is "micromanaging" but whether it is operating within its governance role (setting and monitoring Goals/Guardrails) vs. the superintendent's management role (determining how to accomplish them).

Use instead: Describe the specific behavior and whether it belongs to board work or superintendent work. For example: "When the board reviews an item that has been delegated to the superintendent, the board is out of position. Its role is to monitor results against Goals, not to direct implementation."

"Rubberstamping" / "Rubber Stamp"

Why it's forbidden: This term frames board approval of superintendent recommendations as inherently passive or weak. It implies that a board that trusts its superintendent and aligns with their recommendations is failing in its duty. In practice, a board that has clearly defined Goals and Guardrails, hired a superintendent capable of achieving them, and monitors progress monthly should generally be aligned with the superintendent's recommendations -- that's a sign of a healthy governing system, not a failure.

Use instead: Describe whether the board is exercising informed judgment. "The board reviewed the monitoring data, confirmed alignment with adopted Goals, and voted to approve the recommendation" is accurate and empowering. If a board is voting without information or analysis, name that specific problem.

"Stay in your lane"

Why it's forbidden: This is a dismissive, condescending phrase used to silence board members or restrict their engagement. It carries no constructive guidance -- it simply tells someone to stop participating. The underlying concern (a board member engaging outside the board's governance role) is legitimate, but the framing is insulting and unhelpful.

Use instead: Reference the board's governing policies or the distinction between board work and superintendent work. For example: "That falls under the superintendent's delegated authority per our governing policies. If you have concerns about results, we can schedule that as a monitoring item on our calendar."

"Good/Bad/Right/Wrong"

Why it's forbidden: This is most often received as judgemental language that's about personal preferences rather than rational governing systems. The affirmatives (good/right) carry just as much risk for this as the negatives (bad/wrong) in that they create a context in which judgement is implied. This holds even for approving-sounding phrases like "the right framing" or "the right approach" -- affirmative judgment is still judgment, and it invites the same good/bad/right/wrong dynamic. Describe what's effective instead. If these approaches worked to improve a focus on student outcomes we'd use them; we avoid them because they tend to degrade interpersonal professionalism and collective effectiveness over time.

Use instead: We focus our work around alignment with what's effective and giving up pretending about what's ineffective. For example: "We don't avoid belittling teachers because it's bad or wrong to do; there is no law that says we can't behave that way. It's perfectly legal. We actively choose not to do so because it's simply ineffective at creating the conditions for improving student outcomes."

Manipulative & Power-Over Framings to Avoid

Beyond the three banned phrases above, the following types of framings should never appear in Effective School Boards content:

The Board as Passive Recipient

Any framing that suggests the board should simply receive information, defer to others, or accept decisions made elsewhere.

Avoid: "The board should trust the superintendent's expertise."

Instead: "The board hired the superintendent to implement the community's vision. The board evaluates that implementation through monitoring reports aligned to adopted Goals and Guardrails."

The Board as Obstacle

Any framing that suggests the board's involvement is a problem to be managed around.

Avoid: "Boards that get too involved in operations..."

Instead: "When the board engages outside its governance role it creates confusion about who is accountable for what."

Expertise as Authority

Any framing that suggests professional staff have more legitimate authority than elected board members.

Avoid: "Board members aren't educators, so they should defer to the professionals."

Instead: "Board members represent the community's vision and values. The superintendent brings professional expertise in how to achieve them. Both roles are essential and distinct."

Conflict as Dysfunction

Any framing that suggests disagreement among board members or between the board and community is inherently bad.

Avoid: A board that's divided, a board that can't get along.

Instead: Boards with diverse perspectives create better policy when they have clear processes for deliberation and decision-making. Disagreement is not dysfunction -- the absence of a governing framework is.

Efficiency as the Primary Goal

Any framing that suggests the board's primary objective is to be efficient, fast, or non-disruptive.

Avoid: "The board should streamline its meetings," "stop wasting time on debate."

Instead: "The board should invest its limited meeting time on the work that matters most: monitoring progress toward Goals and Guardrails. Deliberation on those items is not waste -- it's the core function of governing."

Common Education Terms We Avoid

School District / District

We say school system instead. "District" excludes charter schools, other public schools operating outside district governance structures, and non-public schools that nonetheless serve public school students and have governing boards. ESB principles apply wherever a board governs public school children. The legal structure of enrollment matters less than the governing relationship. Carve-out: a questioner's own words, a direct quotation, and operational references to a specifically named entity (e.g., a legal name or a program that includes the word) may keep "district."

Equity

Almost never meaningfully defined in practice, and has become a partisan signal before any substantive meaning is communicated. When the topic is real -- proportional outcomes, unequal access, resource distribution gaps -- describe it specifically. Saying, "third-grade reading rates vary by 30 points across schools" is both more honest and more actionable than "equity." Reserve the term only where it is technically required (e.g., direct quotation from a statute or regulatory document that uses it).

Achievement Gap

Or any other term that attempts to frame underperformance as something students have rather than something the system has failed to provide. Use instead: describe the specific gap with data ("38% of third-graders are reading below grade level") or describe the system failure directly ("the board has not adopted outcome targets for early literacy").

Other Politically-Coded Language

ESB is ruthlessly and intentionally non-partisan. We avoid terms that function primarily as tribal markers and where the word signals political affiliation before it communicates substance. This includes terms coded on both the left and the right: "DEI," "anti-racist," "woke," "parents' rights" (as a contested framing), "social-emotional learning" when used as a wedge. When the underlying concept is legitimate and relevant, name it specifically: representation, outcome gaps, family involvement, specific skill development. We don't avoid these terms because we agree or disagree with them or because we're suggesting that they're good/bad/right/wrong. We avoid them because of their tendency to pull us apart based on politics rather than pull us together based on student outcomes.

Non-Essential Activities

Avoid framing any board or system function as "non-essential activities." The phrase invites arbitrary judgment about what matters and tends to dismiss work that supports student outcomes. Describe the specific activity and its relationship to Goals and Guardrails instead of sorting work into essential vs. non-essential.

ESB Vocabulary vs. Policy-Governance Terminology

ESB uses its own vocabulary. Never import the labels of a policy-based approach to governance, and never describe ESB as built on, derived from, or adopted from such an approach (for example, do not write that ESB rests on "the principles of a policy-based approach to governance"). Use the ESB term in every case:

Never write "SOFG," "Student Outcomes Focused Governance," "Policy Governance," "PG," "Lone Star Governance," or "LSG" in ESB content. The one exception: "SOFG" may appear only when explicitly contrasting that model with ESB.

Terminology Standards

Use "board chair," never "board president."

Use "Practitioner," never "coach," to name the certified role.

Spell out "Any Reasonable Interpretation" in full; never abbreviate it to "ARI."

Write "own conduct" as two words; never hyphenate it as "own-conduct."
