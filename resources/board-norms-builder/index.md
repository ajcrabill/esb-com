<h1>Governance Team Norms Builder</h1>

<p class="template-intro">
  <strong>Every board has a culture. The question is whether you designed it — or whether it designed you.</strong>
  <br/><br/>
  This one-page framework helps your board name the norms that matter most and commit to them together.
  Use it as a work session exercise with your full governance team. No consultants. No retreat facilitators.
  Just your board, honest conversation, and fifteen minutes per norm.
</p>

<div class="fillable-note">
  <strong>How to use:</strong> Go through each norm as a full board. Read the description, discuss the guiding question, and check where you stand. Add your board's own norms in the blank spaces. Then complete the commitment page together.
</div>

<hr/>

<h2>The Three Core Norms</h2>

<p>These three norms separate high-functioning boards from the rest. Start here.</p>

<div class="norm-card">
  <h4><span class="norm-num">1</span> Candor Without Cruelty</h4>
  <div class="norm-tagline">"We can say anything to each other, as long as we say it with respect."</div>
  <p>High-functioning boards tell each other hard truths. They don't avoid the conversation about a board member who isn't prepared, a superintendent whose performance is slipping, or a goal the district is clearly missing. But they do it without making it personal.</p>
  <p>The key word is <strong>respect</strong>, not "niceness." Respect means you say it to their face, not behind their back. Respect means you focus on the issue, not the person. Respect means you assume good intent even when you disagree.</p>

  <p><strong>Guiding question for your board:</strong></p>
  <div class="guide-question">
    <em>When was the last time a board member told another board member something hard to hear — and the conversation made things better, not worse?</em>
  </div>

  <h5>Where does your board stand?</h5>
  <div class="checklist-item">We avoid hard conversations — it's easier to stay quiet</div>
  <div class="checklist-item">Hard conversations happen, but sometimes they turn personal</div>
  <div class="checklist-item">Hard conversations happen regularly and stay focused on the issue</div>
  <div class="checklist-item">Hard conversations are the norm — we seek them out because we know they make us better</div>
  <h5>What would this norm look like in practice for your board?</h5>
  <textarea class="fill-area" placeholder="Describe one concrete behavior your board would commit to..." style="min-height:50px;"></textarea>
</div>

<div class="norm-card">
  <h4><span class="norm-num">2</span> Student Outcomes as the Centering Force</h4>
  <div class="norm-tagline">"Every decision passes through a student outcomes filter."</div>
  <p>This sounds obvious. Every board says they're focused on students. But here's the test: when a board meeting runs late and you have to cut something, what gets cut?</p>
  <p>If it's the student outcome data review — the twenty-minute segment where you look at whether third-grade reading is on track — then your culture has a different center of gravity than the one you'd claim.</p>
  <p>The boards that do this well don't just talk about students in their opening statement. They structure their meetings so that student outcomes are the <strong>first item</strong>, not the last. They organize their goals around outcomes, not activities. They measure their own success not by whether meetings ran smoothly, but by whether the data moved.</p>

  <p><strong>Guiding question for your board:</strong></p>
  <div class="guide-question">
    <em>Is student outcome data the first thing on your meeting agenda, or the thing you skip when you run out of time?</em>
  </div>

  <h5>Where does your board stand?</h5>
  <div class="checklist-item">Student outcome data is rarely discussed in board meetings</div>
  <div class="checklist-item">Student outcome data is discussed, but it's often the last agenda item</div>
  <div class="checklist-item">Student outcome data is a standing agenda item with dedicated time</div>
  <div class="checklist-item">Student outcome data drives every major decision — budget, policy, personnel</div>
  <h5>What would this norm look like in practice for your board?</h5>
  <textarea class="fill-area" placeholder="Describe one concrete change your board would make..." style="min-height:50px;"></textarea>
</div>

<div class="norm-card">
  <h4><span class="norm-num">3</span> Collective Voice</h4>
  <div class="norm-tagline">"We debate in private. We stand together in public."</div>
  <p>This is the norm that protects you when things go wrong. Collective voice means the board speaks as one body, not seven individuals. It doesn't mean you agree on everything in closed session. It means that once a decision is made, every board member supports it publicly.</p>
  <p>If you lost the vote, you lost it. You don't go to the community and say "I voted against that" or "I tried to stop it."</p>
  <p>This is the single most visible test of board culture. When a board member breaks collective voice, the public sees it. The press sees it. The staff sees it. And the trust that holds the district together starts to fray.</p>

  <p><strong>Guiding question for your board:</strong></p>
  <div class="guide-question">
    <em>Has every board member honored collective voice in the last six months — including after a vote they lost?</em>
  </div>

  <h5>Where does your board stand?</h5>
  <div class="checklist-item">Individual board members regularly express disagreement publicly after a vote</div>
  <div class="checklist-item">Most board members honor collective voice, but there are exceptions</div>
  <div class="checklist-item">Collective voice is the established norm and followed consistently</div>
  <div class="checklist-item">Collective voice is so ingrained that the board has a process for handling disagreements before they go public</div>

</div>
