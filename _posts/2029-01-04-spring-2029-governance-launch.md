**Spring 2029 Governance Launch — Year Three Starts Now**

January 4, 2029

---

Let me name what your board has built over the last twelve months.

Spring 2028 — your first full cycle. Goal adoption. Budget crosswalk. Data review. Superintendent evaluation. Year-end closeout. You did it for the first time.

Summer 2028 — your renewal arc. You protected what worked, applied what was still new, added what was missing.

Fall 2028 — your second full cycle. You did not learn governance this time. You executed it. October data review. November goal trajectory. December closeout and organizational meeting.

Twelve months. Two complete cycles. One board that does not govern the way it did in 2027.

Now Spring 2029 is here. And this cycle is different. Not because the work is harder — your board has already done this work. Twice. The work is the same cycle. What changes is the bar.

The bar is no longer completion. The bar is precision.

Let me tell you what that means.

**What Year Three Requires That Year Two Did Not**

Year One asked: did we adopt goals? Year Two asked: did we adopt them on time? Year Three asks: did the goals we adopted in January produce better student outcomes in June?

That shift — from compliance to results, from calendar management to outcome verification — is the difference between a board that runs the cycle and a board that runs the cycle well.

In Year One, everything was new. You learned the protocol. You figured out what a budget crosswalk actually looked like. You sat through your first data review and realized you had never really looked at student outcome data before.

In Year Two, you got faster. The protocol was familiar. You could run the crosswalk study session without looking at the instructions. You knew what questions to ask in a data review.

In Year Three, you need to get better.

The question is not whether your board will run the governance cycle. Your board has run it twice. You will run it again. The question is whether you will run it better this time than last time.

That is continuous improvement. And it requires you to operate differently from Day One.

**The January Milestones**

January has one governance action: goal adoption. One item on the agenda. No reports. No presentations. No public comment. Just goal adoption.

Here is what needs to happen in the first two weeks of January:

The superintendent delivers draft goal language early in the month. That language is not aspirational. It is grounded in three years of trend data — your board's actual outcomes from 2027, 2028, and fall 2028.

The board president holds one-on-one conversations with each board member to confirm alignment before the meeting. This is not a straw poll. It is a pre-meeting check: do all seven of you understand the three-year trend for each proposed goal? Are there questions that need to be answered before the board votes?

The goal adoption meeting is scheduled for mid-January. The board president opens by naming the three-year trend for each goal: "In Year One, reading proficiency was at 42 percent. In Year Two, it moved to 44 percent. Our proposed target for Year Three is 48 percent. Here is why we believe that target is achievable."

Your board does not adopt goals from a blank page. Your board adopts goals from a data-informed draft.

That is Year Three precision.

Every month has a specific governance action. Every action has a protocol your board has used before. Year Three is not about learning new protocols. It is about applying the ones you already know with greater precision.

**What Precision Looks Like on Day One**

Let me be specific about what January launch week should look like.

The board president opens the goal adoption meeting by naming the three-year trend. Not reading from a script — speaking from command of the data.

The board adopts goals with written rationale tied to data, not impressions. Not "we think literacy is important" — but "here is the three-year trend in reading proficiency, here is what we invested last year, here is what the data says the investment produced, and here is why the proposed target is both ambitious and achievable."

The board names the monitoring schedule for the spring. Out loud. On the record. "We adopted Goal 1 with a 3 percent improvement target. We will review literacy data in May. We will check budget alignment in April. We will report to the community in June."

The board closes the meeting by naming what the community should expect next. Not a vague "we will keep you updated" — but "on February 1, we will publish a post explaining how the budget connects to these goals. On March 15, we will hold a public hearing on the budget. You will receive a progress report in May."

That is transparency. That is accountability. That is Year Three precision.

**Common Pitfall in Year Three**

The most common mistake I see in boards entering their third cycle is treating it like Year Two.

The calendar says January, so the board schedules the goal adoption meeting. The superintendent drafts goal language. The board adopts the goals. Done.

But the board does not use the three-year trend data. The board does not name the monitoring schedule. The board does not connect the adoption to the budget crosswalk in February.

The board runs the protocol but loses the precision.

Do not let your board be that board. You have two full cycles of data. Use it.

**Your Board Is Ready**

Your board is not the same board that started this work twenty-four months ago. You have two complete cycles behind you, a governance calendar that works, a president who knows how to facilitate a goal adoption meeting, and a superintendent who knows what it means to work with a board that governs on purpose.

The question January asks is not whether your board is ready to run the cycle. You are. You have run it twice.

The question is whether your board will run it better this time than last time.

Go find out.
