**Mid-Spring Governance Check — Superintendent Evaluation Preview and Year-End Projections**

2028-05-17

---

Two weeks ago, your board reviewed the spring data.

One week ago, your board reviewed whether the budget allocations produced measurable outcomes.

Now your board has two pieces of evidence sitting in front of it: the trajectory for every adopted goal and the effectiveness of every resource allocation.

This week, the board combines those two pieces of evidence into a single question: Based on everything we know in mid-May, what should our June superintendent evaluation emphasize?

This is the Mid-Spring Governance Check. It is not the superintendent evaluation itself — that happens in June. It is the board's preparation conversation, designed to ensure the board arrives at the June evaluation with a clear, evidence-based focus rather than a general impression.

Let me show you what this conversation looks like.

---

**What the Mid-Spring Governance Check is — and what it is not**

Let me be precise about this.

The Mid-Spring Governance Check is a board-only conversation. The superintendent does not participate. The board reviews the evidence the superintendent has provided — the spring data, the allocation-outcome analysis, the Progress Monitoring findings — and uses that evidence to preview the evaluation conversation.

The Check is not a decision. The board does not finalize the superintendent's evaluation rating in mid-May. The board does not communicate concerns or commendations to the superintendent during this check. The purpose is internal preparation: the board aligns on what the evidence says so the June evaluation conversation is grounded, focused, and productive.

Here is the framework I recommend.

---

**Three inputs, one output**

The Mid-Spring Governance Check takes three inputs and produces one output.

**Input one: The spring Progress Monitoring findings.**

The board reviews the written finding from the May 3 data check-in — the goal-by-goal spring data points, year-end projections, and maintain-adjust-escalate decisions. This input tells the board where the district stands on every adopted goal with three data points and a projection.

**Input two: The Budget Outcome Review findings.**

The board reviews the written finding from the May 10 outcome review — the allocation-outcome analysis for each adopted goal, the three-category classification, and any Q3 adjustment directives. This input tells the board whether the resource allocations produced the expected results.

**Input three: The district's year-end trajectory narrative.**

The superintendent prepares a single-page narrative that answers three questions: What do we know about where we will land by June 30? What are the most significant factors driving our trajectory? What would the superintendent recommend the board emphasize in the evaluation conversation?

The board reads this narrative as context — not as a directive, but as the superintendent's own assessment of the year's progress. It gives the board insight into whether the superintendent's self-assessment aligns with the data.

**The output: The evaluation focus memo.**

The board produces a single-page internal memo — not shared with the superintendent — that names: the goals where the district is on track (and what the board attributes that success to), the goals where the district is off track (and what the board understands about the factors driving the gap), the specific areas the board wants the evaluation rubric to emphasize in the June conversation, and any questions the board wants answered before the June evaluation.

This memo is the board's preparation document. It ensures every board member arrives at the June evaluation with the same evidence foundation and the same focus.

---

**What to look for in the three inputs**

I have watched boards across the country conduct this preparation conversation. The ones that do it well share three practices.

**Practice one: They separate the person from the data.**

A board member who sees an off-track goal on the Progress Monitoring finding should read that as information about a goal, not as an indictment of the superintendent. The spring data reflects a complex system of strategy, implementation, resource allocation, and external factors — some within the superintendent's control, some not.

The board's job in the Mid-Spring Check is to ask: For each off-track goal, what was within the superintendent's control? Did the superintendent propose an evidence-based strategy? Did the administration implement it with fidelity? Did the board allocate sufficient resources to support it?

I have watched a board in **Kansas City (KS)** miss this distinction. Their board president opened the Mid-Spring Check by saying, "We have a literacy problem." That framing assumed the superintendent had caused the problem. A board member corrected the framing: "We have a literacy goal that is off track. Our question is why — and the why may have nothing to do with the superintendent's performance."

That correction shifted the entire conversation from blame to analysis.

**Practice two: They use the Budget Outcome findings as a lens.**

The Budget Outcome Review tells the board whether the allocations produced the expected outcomes. This is a more precise lens than the spring data alone. A goal that is off track but received an inadequate allocation tells a different story than a goal that is off track after receiving the full requested allocation. The first story is about resource sufficiency. The second story is about strategy or implementation effectiveness.

The board that reads both stories together has a more accurate picture of the superintendent's performance than the board that reads the spring data alone.

**Practice three: They prepare the evaluation focus memo in the meeting, not after it.**

The board should write the evaluation focus memo during the Mid-Spring Check meeting, not delegate it to the board president to draft later. Writing in the room ensures every board member's input shapes the focus. It also prevents the memo from becoming one board member's opinion about what the evaluation should emphasize.

---

**The connection to the June evaluation**

The Mid-Spring Governance Check is the second-to-last step before the June superintendent evaluation.

Here is the sequence your board should follow:

- **Late May:** The board conducts the Mid-Spring Governance Check and produces the evaluation focus memo.
- **Early June:** The board president shares the evaluation focus memo with the superintendent, not as a final assessment but as a preview of what the board will emphasize in the evaluation conversation. This is the professional courtesy that prevents surprises in the evaluation meeting.
- **Mid-June:** The board conducts the superintendent evaluation using the adopted evaluation rubric, the spring Progress Monitoring data, the Budget Outcome Review findings, and the evaluation focus memo as the evidence base.

A board that follows this sequence walks into the June evaluation with clarity. A board that skips the Mid-Spring Check walks into the June evaluation with whatever each board member remembers from the spring data presentations — which is never the same picture from member to member.
