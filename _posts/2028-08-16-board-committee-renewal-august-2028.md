**Board Committee Renewal — Fresh Assignments for the New Governance Cycle**

August 16, 2028

---

Last week, we built the fall governance calendar. The calendar tells the board what governance actions it will take, in what order, and with what level of protection. It names the mandatory actions, the proactive actions, and the discretionary actions. It sets the non-deferrable milestones, maps the dependency chain, and builds in buffer for the operational demands that will inevitably arise.

That calendar is your board's roadmap for the fall cycle. Without it, you govern by inbox.

But a roadmap does not drive itself. The calendar tells the board *what* to do. Committees tell the board *who* does the work between board meetings. If the governance calendar is the engine, committees are the pistons — they are the structures that convert the board's governance commitments into actual deliverables.

The August 9 post built the calendar. This post completes the calendar by renewing the committee structure that will execute it.

---

**Why committees matter more in the second cycle**

Most boards treat committee assignments as procedural housekeeping. They happen at the August organizational meeting, they take fifteen minutes, and they produce the same list as last year. Bob stays on Finance. Susan stays on Policy. The committee names stay the same, the chair stays the same, and the committee's actual purpose — if it ever had a clear one — is assumed rather than articulated.

A first-cycle board can get away with that. In the first cycle, the board is learning the governance pattern. The committee structure from the previous year, even if imperfect, provides continuity. The board does not know enough about what its committees should produce to redesign them productively.

A second-cycle board has no excuse.

By now, your board has data on every committee from the spring cycle. You know which committees produced deliverables that influenced board decisions. You know which committees met regularly and produced nothing. You know which committees had members who carried the workload and which committees had members who attended meetings without contribution.

That data is your starting point. Committee renewal is not about shuffling names. It is about examining each committee's purpose, performance, and fit for the fall cycle and making intentional decisions about what to keep, what to change, and what to stop.

---

**The four dimensions of committee renewal**

I recommend that every board that has completed a full governance cycle approach committee renewal through four dimensions. Each dimension asks a specific question. Each question has a specific output.

**Dimension one: Charge statement review**

This is the most important dimension and the one most boards skip.

A charge statement is a written document that names what the committee will produce, by when, and how that deliverable connects to the board's governance calendar. It is not a domain description — "the finance committee oversees the budget." It is a deliverable — "the finance committee will review the first quarter budget variance report and present findings to the full board at the October progress monitoring session."

Most boards do not have charge statements for their committees. They have committee names and membership lists. The committee's purpose is whatever the chair decides it is, and that purpose can shift from meeting to meeting depending on what landed on the superintendent's desk that week.

A second-cycle board writes a charge statement for every standing committee before the fall cycle begins. The charge statement answers four questions:

1. What specific deliverable will this committee produce this cycle?
2. What is the deadline for that deliverable?
3. How does that deliverable connect to the governance calendar?
4. What information, resources, or administrative support does the committee need to produce it?

Here is what a charge statement looks like in practice.

*The Finance Committee will review the Q1 budget variance report against the adopted priorities from the April 2028 budget adoption, identify any variance greater than five percent, and present findings to the full board at the October work session. The committee will also produce a preliminary timeline for the November budget alignment discussion, identifying which allocations need review and which are tracking within expectations. The committee chair will coordinate with the CFO to receive the Q1 variance report by September 30.*

That charge statement gives the finance committee a specific, measurable deliverable with a deadline. The committee knows what it is accountable for. The full board knows what to expect from the committee. The committee chair has a clear framework for running meetings.

Ask every committee chair to draft a charge statement for their committee based on the fall governance calendar. Review the charge statements as a full board. Approve them at the August work session.

**Dimension two: Member-role fit assessment**

Once the committee has a clear purpose, the question is whether the members assigned to that committee are the right people for that purpose.

The standard approach to committee assignments is seniority-based or preference-based. Members choose the committee they are already on because they know it, or members choose the committee that sounds most interesting. The board president assigns any remaining slots based on who raised a hand.

A second-cycle board replaces preference-based assignment with capacity-based assignment. The question shifts from "What committee do you want to be on?" to "Where does your governance contribution have the most impact based on what we learned this spring?"

Here is what that looks like.

If the finance committee's charge statement requires analyzing a fifteen-page variance report and presenting findings, the committee needs at least one member with financial literacy. Not a CPA, necessarily — the C-suite is already a committee chair elsewhere — but someone who can read a budget document and identify a variance.

If the goals and data committee's charge statement requires reviewing Fall 2028 baseline data and assessing trajectory against the adopted goals, the committee needs at least one member who understands how to read a trend line and distinguish a data signal from data noise.

If the governance committee's charge statement requires designing the board self-assessment protocol and coordinating the fall committee calendar, the committee needs members with meeting facilitation skills and attention to administrative detail.

Assess each board member's demonstrated strengths from the spring cycle. Assign them to committees where those strengths will be used. Do not assign members to committees where the work requires skills they have not yet developed, unless the committee includes a stronger member who can develop them.

This is how a board builds committee depth. Not by rotating members through every committee equally, but by matching members to charges where their contribution matters.

**Dimension three: Committee-to-goal alignment check**

This is the dimension that connects committee renewal directly to the governance calendar.

Every standing committee should be able to name which adopted goal its work supports. If a committee cannot name a goal, the committee has no direct connection to the board's governance priorities.

The September 20 post covers goal renewal in depth, but here is the principle for the August committee structure: the board's adopted goals for the fall cycle determine which committees are needed, what their charges should be, and how their work is evaluated.

If the board adopted three goals in the spring — let us say third-grade reading proficiency, eighth-grade math proficiency, and chronic absenteeism reduction — every committee's charge should map to at least one of those goals.

The finance committee maps to all three, because the budget decisions it reviews determine whether resources are allocated to interventions that move those metrics. The goals and data committee maps to all three, because its data reviews provide the evidence for trajectory assessment. The policy committee maps to any goal with a policy barrier — a policy that constrains intervention design, a policy that limits data sharing, a policy that needs to be adopted to enable a new program.

The governance committee maps to the board's meta-goal: an effective governance framework that produces better decisions than the board would make without it.

When a committee cannot map to a goal, you have three options. Redesign the committee's charge so it maps to a goal. Absorb the committee's work into another committee. Or sunset the committee.

A committee whose work does not connect to an adopted goal is a committee that should not exist. It consumes board member time, administrative staff time, and governance capacity that should be directed at goal outcomes.

**Dimension four: The sunset provision**

This is the dimension most boards avoid entirely.

I have worked with boards that have twelve standing committees. Twelve. Some of those committees have not met in six months. Some have not produced a deliverable in two years. They exist on paper because no one has the nerve to dissolve them.

A board that has completed a full governance cycle has enough data to know which committees are producing and which are not. The sunset provision is the mechanism for closing the committees that have outlived their governance purpose.

Here is the test.

If a committee's most recent deliverable was more than one cycle ago, sunset the committee. If the committee's charge overlaps with another committee's charge to the point where the work is duplicated, sunset one committee and reassign its charge to the other. If the committee's original purpose has been accomplished — the policy was adopted, the audit was completed, the facility study was presented — sunset the committee unless there is an active, upcoming deliverable in the fall governance calendar.

Sunsetting a committee is not a failure. It is a signal that the board is intentional about where it invests governance capacity. Every committee that remains on the books consumes meeting time, member energy, and administrative support. A committee that is not producing is not harmless. It is consuming resources that could go to committees that are producing.

If the spring cycle data shows that your governance committee's calendar management function was absorbed by the board president's office, and the governance committee has not produced a committee-level deliverable since the spring organizational meeting, sunset the governance committee and redistribute its remaining functions — board self-assessment and policy review — to the board's standing work session or to an ad hoc committee with a specific charge and a sunset date.

Ad hoc committees are a governance tool that most boards underuse. An ad hoc committee has a single charge, a defined deliverable, and an automatic sunset when the deliverable is produced. An ad hoc committee to review the facilities master plan, produce a recommendation by the November work session, and dissolve in December. An ad hoc committee to develop a community engagement protocol, produce a recommendation by the August work session, and dissolve in September. These committees are focused, time-bound, and less likely to drift into permanent existence.

**The committee renewal protocol**

Pulling these four dimensions together, here is the committee renewal protocol for the August cycle.

**Step one.** List every committee that existed in the spring cycle — standing committees and ad hoc committees.

**Step two.** For each committee, collect three data points: whether the committee produced a deliverable that was presented to the full board, whether the deliverable influenced a board decision, and whether the committee's work connected to an adopted goal.

**Step three.** Apply the sunset test. Any committee that produced no deliverable, influenced no decision, or connected to no goal should be sunset unless there is a specific, anticipated need in the fall cycle.

**Step four.** Draft charge statements for every committee that remains — and for any new committee needed based on the fall governance calendar. Each charge statement names the deliverable, the deadline, the connection to the governance calendar, and the support needed.

**Step five.** Assess member-role fit. Assign members to committees based on demonstrated strengths from the spring cycle. Ensure every committee has at least one member with the skills required by the charge statement.

**Step six.** Review the committee structure as a full board. Approve the charge statements. Publish the committee roster, the charge statements, and the committee meeting calendar.

The goal is not to produce a perfect committee structure on August 16. The goal is to produce a committee structure that is better than the one your board had in the spring — one that is aligned to the fall governance calendar, connected to the adopted goals, and staffed with members who are positioned to contribute.

---

**The committee structure that the fall calendar requires**

Let me put this in the specific context of the fall governance calendar we built last week.

The August 9 calendar names the following dependency chain:

- August: Committee charges adopted — each committee knows its deliverable and its deadline
- September: Data system confirmed, budget baseline established — October data review is possible
- October: First data review conducted, Q1 budget variance reviewed — November budget alignment discussion has evidence
- November: Budget alignment discussion completed — December goal trajectory assessment has full picture
- December: Goal trajectory assessment completed — January goal renewal and evaluation kickoff are informed

Each link in that chain requires a committee deliverable.

The September step requires a finance committee deliverable — the Q1 budget baseline, prepared for the board's review. The October step requires a goals and data committee deliverable — the fall baseline data, prepared for the board's progress monitoring session. The November step requires a finance committee deliverable — the budget variance analysis, prepared for the board's alignment discussion. The December step requires a goals and data committee deliverable — the goal trajectory assessment, prepared for the board's year-end review.

If the committees have not been renewed, the chain breaks. The finance committee does not produce the Q1 baseline because its members are the same people who did not produce it last time. The goals and data committee does not prepare the trajectory assessment because its charge statement was never written.

The committee renewal is not a separate activity from the governance calendar. It is the calendar's execution mechanism. The calendar tells the board when the work happens. The committees do the work.

---

**What this looks like on the ground**

I worked with a board last spring that had five standing committees. When we applied the committee renewal protocol at the August organizational meeting, three committees survived the sunset test. One was reorganized into an ad hoc committee with a specific deliverable and a dissolution date. One was absorbed into another committee.

The board worried that reducing from five committees to three would look like a reduction in governance capacity. The opposite happened. The three remaining committees all produced deliverables by the end of the fall cycle. The ad hoc committee completed its work and dissolved. The consolidated committee produced better analysis than the two separate committees had produced in the spring, because the members who were now working together had complementary skills.

The board president told me something I repeat to every board I work with: "We were afraid to reduce committees because we thought it meant we were doing less. It turned out we were doing more, because every committee we kept had a reason to exist."

That is what committee renewal produces. Fewer committees. Clearer charges. Better deliverables. Less governance overhead. More governance impact.
