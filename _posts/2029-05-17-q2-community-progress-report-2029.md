**Q2 Community Progress Report — Year-Over-Year Transparency**

May 17, 2029

---

Your community has been receiving governance progress reports for two and a half years.

They received the Spring 2028 community report after the board's first full governance cycle. They received the Fall 2028 community progress report after the board's second cycle. And now they will receive the Spring 2029 Q2 report.

Your community is not comparing your board to other boards. They have no basis for that comparison. They are comparing your board to your board. They are asking: is the board getting better? Are the goals producing results? Is the money being spent on what matters?

In Year Three, the community expects to see year-over-year trends — not just this year's data, but where the district was in Year One and Year Two. They want to know whether the trend is up, flat, or down. And they want to know what the board is doing about it.

The Q2 Community Progress Report is the board's most public accountability document of the spring cycle. Here is what the Year Three report contains and how to produce it.

**What the Year Three Report Contains**

The one-page report shows for each goal:

The goal — what the board committed to in January. The community needs to be reminded. They are not in the board room.

The trend — three-year data points: spring 2027, spring 2028, spring 2029. Not just this year's number. All three years on one line so the community can see the trajectory.

The year-over-year change — is the direction up, flat, or down? A simple arrow or percentage change tells the community everything they need to know about progress.

The board's decision — continue, adjust, or redesign. The community needs to know not just what the data shows, but what the board is doing about it. Data without a decision is information. Data with a decision is accountability.

The budget connection — what percent of the budget funds this goal. The community needs to see how their tax dollars connect to student outcomes. "Goal 1 represents 32 percent of the budget. The trend is up. The board decided to continue the current strategy and monitor progress in August."

What to expect next — the next data review or community report date. The community needs to know when they will hear from the board again. Not a vague "we will keep you updated" — but "the next community progress report will publish in August."

**The Year Three Accountability Frame**

Here is the accountability frame the Year Three report uses: "In the spring of Year One, reading scores were at 42 percent. In Year Two, they were at 44 percent. In Year Three, they are at 46 percent. The board is investing 32 percent of the budget in literacy. The trend is up. The board has decided to continue the current strategy."

That is accountability. That is Year Three precision.

The community can see: the board set a goal, invested money, tracked the outcome, and made a decision based on the data. The entire governance system is visible in six sentences.

And here is the most important part: the report does not hide flat or negative trends. It names them and explains the board's response.

"In the spring of Year One, math scores were at 38 percent. In Year Two, they were at 38 percent. In Year Three, they are at 37 percent. The board is investing 28 percent of the budget in math. The trend is flat to declining. The board has decided to redesign the math strategy and will present the new approach at the June board meeting."

That is accountability. The board is not hiding the problem. The board is naming it and committing to a response.

**The Publication Protocol**

Step One: The board reviews the draft report in closed session. Not the data — the board already reviewed the data in the spring data review. This is a review of the report's accuracy, clarity, and completeness.

Step Two: The board president approves the final version. The president ensures that the report reflects the board's decisions, not the administration's preferences.

Step Three: The report is published on the district website within five business days of the data review. Not two weeks later — five business days. Speed matters. The community should hear from the board while the data is fresh.

Step Four: The board president issues a brief video or written message to accompany the report. A one-minute video from the president naming the key findings adds a human element to the data.

Step Five: The community is invited to submit questions, which the board addresses at the next regular meeting. The report is not the end of the conversation — it is the beginning.

**What Year Three Adds to the Report**

Year-over-year trend lines, not just current data. This is the most significant addition. The community can see the trajectory, not just the snapshot.

Budget connection for each goal. The community can see how their tax dollars connect to student outcomes. "Goal 1 represents 32 percent of the budget. Goal 2 represents 28 percent. Here is what those investments produced."

Board decision summary. The community can see not just the data, but what the board decided based on the data. That is the difference between transparency and accountability. Transparency shows the data. Accountability shows what you did with it.

Forward-looking statement. The community can see what the board expects to see in the next cycle. "We will review reading data again in August. We expect to see continued improvement consistent with the current trajectory."

**Common Pitfall in Year Three**

The most common mistake is publishing a report that looks identical to the Year One report, just with updated numbers.

The Year One report was about establishing the practice. The board was learning how to communicate with the community about goals and data. The report was a first attempt.

The Year Three report should have a different structure — one that emphasizes trends over snapshots, decisions over data, and accountability over information. If the community cannot tell the difference between Year One and Year Three, the board is not governing with transparency.

**A Governance Document**

The Q2 Community Progress Report is not an annual update. It is a governance document.

It tells the community what the board committed to, what the data shows, what the board decided, and what to expect next. In Year Three, that document should show three years of progress — or three years of honest work toward progress.

Accountability is not about having good news to report. Accountability is about reporting what happened, whether the news is good or bad, and naming what the board is doing in response.

Your community has been watching for two and a half years. Give them the report that reflects three years of work.
