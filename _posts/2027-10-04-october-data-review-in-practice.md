**The October Data Review in Practice — from presentation to governance decision**

October 4, 2027

---

Your September launch sequence is complete. Your board confirmed its fall calendar. It set its data review expectations. It completed its self-evaluation.

Now the October data review arrives.

This is your board's first substantive governance event of the fall cycle. It is not a presentation. It is a decision point. Here is the exact practice for making it one.

---

**The board president's preparation — three decisions before the data review**

The board president sets the tone for every data review. That work starts before the meeting.

Three decisions to make before the agenda is released.

First, the framing decision. How will the data review be introduced? The president's opening sets the board's posture. A strong opening says: "We are here to make a decision about trajectory, not to receive information." A weak opening says: "Let's hear what the administration has prepared." The first invites governance. The second invites passivity.

Second, the question decision. What question will the board answer by the end of the data review? The president needs one clear question written before the meeting. Examples: "Do we accept the current trajectory on third-grade reading?" or "Is a course correction needed in middle-school math?" Without a pre-written question, the board wanders.

Third, the time decision. How much time will the board spend on each data set? Presidents who let the administration control the pacing lose the governance window. The president should allocate time by governance priority, not by presentation length.

Your board president's preparation determines whether the data review produces a decision or just takes up time.

---

**The three-decision data review protocol: accept trajectory, direct change, or request more information**

Every data review ends with one of three decisions.

Decision one: accept the trajectory. The data shows acceptable progress toward the goal. No course correction needed. The board documents this decision and moves on.

Decision two: direct change. The data shows the current strategy is not producing the expected results. The board directs the superintendent to develop an adjustment. The adjustment has a timeline and a return-to-board date.

Decision three: request more information. The data raises questions the board cannot answer with what is in front of them. The board makes a specific request for additional data, analysis, or context. The request includes a return date.

These three decisions replace the vague "thank you for the presentation" that ends most data reviews. They force clarity. They create accountability. They make the data review a governance event.

Boards that use this protocol report higher confidence in their oversight role. Boards that do not report frustration, confusion, and wasted meeting time.

---

**The five-business-day data packet rule and superintendent exceptions**

Data reviews fail when boards receive materials the night before the meeting.

The five-business-day rule is simple: the board receives the data packet no later than five business days before the data review meeting. That means Wednesday of the prior week for a Tuesday meeting. That gives board members the weekend to study, reflect, and formulate questions.

Superintendents sometimes push back. Data takes time to compile. State reporting deadlines conflict. Here is the standard for exceptions.

There are exactly two acceptable exceptions. One: the data is from a state or federal agency that released it after the five-day deadline. Two: the data is from an emergent situation that could not have been anticipated. Both exceptions require the superintendent to notify the board president immediately and to provide whatever partial data is available on the original timeline.

Late data packets without an exception produce reactive boards. Reactive boards produce bad governance.

---

**How September self-evaluation findings inform October data review facilitation**

Your board completed its self-evaluation in September. Those findings are not a report to file. They are a facilitation guide for October.

Here is how to connect them.

If the self-evaluation revealed that your board struggles with data analysis, the October data review should include a brief data-literacy warm-up. The board president or a trained facilitator walks through the data display, defines the metrics, and clarifies what trajectory looks like before the board makes a decision.

If the self-evaluation revealed that your board tends to overscope — asking for more data instead of making a decision — the president should tighten the decision window. Use shorter time blocks. Require a decision at the end of each segment.

If the self-evaluation revealed that your board has strong data skills but weak follow-through, the president should assign a board member to document every decision and track return-to-board dates.

Your September self-evaluation is not a document. It is a diagnostic. Use it to facilitate October.
