**The April Budget Adoption Timeline: You Have One Extra Day This Year. Don't Waste It.**

February 29, 2028

---

Today is Leap Day.

You have one extra day this year. Twenty-four hours that don't exist in most calendar years. And I want you to think about what that means for your budget.

Not in a sentimental way. In a practical way.

Most boards run out of time between the preliminary budget and the adoption vote. They mean to review the crosswalk more carefully. They mean to fold community input into the final document. They mean to give the superintendent clear direction on adjustments. But the calendar runs short — there is always another meeting, another agenda item, another crisis — and the budget gets adopted without the governance work the board intended to do.

This year, you have a day you did not have last year.

You could spend it the way you spend every other day — on the urgent things that crowd out the important ones. Or you could spend it on the budget timeline that closes this arc the right way.

This piece is the roadmap for what the next six weeks look like. If you have followed the Crosswalk arc from the beginning — the preliminary budget review on February 8, the goal-to-budget crosswalk on February 15, the community engagement process on February 22 — this is where it all comes together. And if you have not followed the arc, this is where you catch up.

Let me walk you through the remaining milestones, the decisions your board must make, and how to fold everything you have done this month into a final budget you can defend — and a community that trusts it.

---

**The four weeks that matter most**

Between today and the April adoption vote, your board has three distinct phases. Each one builds on the last. Each one requires specific governance work. And each one has a deadline that, if missed, makes the next phase harder.

**Phase 1: The adjustment window (now through mid-March).** This is when the board reviews community input from the public hearing and engagement sessions, finalizes any budget adjustments with the superintendent, and gives direction for the final budget document. This phase closes when the superintendent needs a hard stop to prepare the final budget for publication — usually ten to fourteen days before the adoption vote, depending on your state's required notice period.

**Phase 2: The alignment check (mid-March).** This is when the board runs a final crosswalk review on the adjusted budget. Not a line-by-line audit. A goal-by-goal verification: does the final budget still fund each adopted goal at the level the board intends? Did the adjustments from community input change the allocation balance? Are there any new gaps that need attention before the vote? This phase is the bridge between the February preliminary budget review and the March arc — a governance health check on the budget itself.

**Phase 3: The adoption preparation (late March through the vote).** This is when the board presents the final budget to the community, conducts any legally required second hearing or notice period, and prepares for the adoption vote itself. The governance work here is not about the numbers — the numbers are locked by now. The governance work is about the narrative: can the board explain, clearly and credibly, how the final budget reflects the goals the community helped set, the input the community provided, and the trade-offs the board made?

Let me unpack each phase.

---

**Phase 1: The adjustment window**

This is where community input becomes actionable.

If your board ran a genuine public engagement process over the past week — a hearing, a town hall, an online Q&A, targeted sessions — you now have documentation of what the community said. Not impressions. Actual, attributable input organized by goal area.

The work of this phase is simple but specific.

**First, review the input as a board.** Set aside agenda time in your first March meeting specifically for this. The superintendent should present a summary of all community input received, organized by the goal or budget category each comment addressed. The board should discuss three questions:

- Which issues appeared most frequently across all input channels?
- Which issues were raised by families most directly affected by the budget decisions?
- Which issues can the board address before the final budget, and which require longer-term planning?

**Second, give direction on adjustments.** Based on the input review, the board tells the superintendent what changes to make before the final budget. Some adjustments will be immediate — reallocating funds to address a concern the community raised that the board agrees with. Others will be deferred — the board acknowledges the concern but explains why the current allocation is necessary. Both responses are valid. What matters is that the board makes the call while there is still time to implement it.

**Third, communicate the decisions back to the community.** Before the budget is finalized, publish a board response to the public input. Here is what we heard. Here is what we adjusted. Here is what we could not adjust and why. This document is not a press release. It is a governance document that shows the community its voice was heard and processed. I have watched a board publish this document and receive more goodwill from a skeptical community than any sixty-minute hearing could generate.

The adjustment window closes when the superintendent needs a final budget document for publication. For most districts, that deadline falls between March 15 and March 20. Your board should complete the input review and adjustment direction no later than the first board meeting in March. That gives the superintendent two weeks to produce the final document.

---

**Phase 2: The alignment check**

Once the adjusted budget is ready, your board runs one more crosswalk review.

This is not a redo of the February review. It is a narrower, more focused check. The February review asked: does the preliminary budget fund our goals? This review asks: did the adjustments preserve the alignment, shift it, or create new gaps?

Here is what your board should verify in a single meeting.

**Did every goal survive the adjustments?** If community input caused the board to shift resources — say, increasing the ELL staffing allocation and reducing the professional development budget — does the reading proficiency goal still have adequate resources? The adjustment was probably right for the community concern. But the board should verify that the adjustment did not create an unintended gap in another goal area.

**Is the crosswalk still accurate?** The goal-to-budget crosswalk your board reviewed in February needs a revision. The adjusted final budget has different numbers. The crosswalk should reflect the final allocation, not the preliminary one. Your board should receive and approve the updated crosswalk as part of the final budget adoption process. This is the document that will be referenced for the rest of the fiscal year — get it right.

**Are there any last-minute additions that lack goal alignment?** The period between the preliminary and final budget sometimes produces eleventh-hour additions — a grant opportunity that opened up, a last-minute community request that found funding, a program that received an unexpected donation. These additions are not bad. But they should be mapped to a goal before the board votes, not after.

This phase connects directly to the March arc. Starting next month, the board should conduct a budget alignment review as part of the regular governance calendar — not just during adoption season, but throughout the year. The March arc will include a quarterly governance health check using the updated crosswalk as the reference document. If your board runs a clean alignment check now, the March review is a confirmation. If the alignment check reveals remaining gaps, the March review becomes a correction cycle.

---

**Phase 3: The adoption preparation**

The final budget is published. The notice period has begun. The adoption vote is on the next agenda.

This is the phase where governance discipline matters most, because there is almost nothing left to adjust.

**The board should prepare for the adoption vote the way it prepared for the goal adoption vote in January.** That means a board conversation, not a finance director presentation. The board president or committee chair should lead the adoption discussion, structured around four questions:

- What goals did this board adopt in January?
- How does this final budget fund each goal?
- What community input did the board receive, and how did it inform the final document?
- What trade-offs did the board make, and why?

These four questions turn the adoption vote from a procedural rubber stamp into a governance moment. The community watching the meeting — in person, online, or through the recording — should be able to answer those four questions after watching the board's discussion. If they can, the board has governed transparently. If they cannot, the board has adopted a budget without explaining its rationale.

**The vote itself should reference the crosswalk.** Not the line-item total. The crosswalk. The board should vote to adopt the budget and the updated goal-to-budget crosswalk as a package. This makes the crosswalk an official governance document, not a one-time analysis tool. It means that every subsequent budget review — including the March alignment check and the quarterly progress monitoring — uses the crosswalk as the baseline.

**After the vote, set up accountability.** The board should schedule a mid-year budget review in the fall that revisits the crosswalk. Not to change the adopted budget — that train has left the station — but to verify that spending is tracking against the crosswalk. If the board funded Goal 1 at a specific level, is the money actually being spent on Goal 1 activities? A mid-year crosswalk check answers that question before the next budget cycle begins.

---

**What the Leap Day frame means for this work**

You have an extra day this year. Twenty-four hours between February 28 and March 1 that do not exist in most calendar years.

I am not going to tell you to work that day. You probably have enough in your calendar already.

But I am going to tell you to use it.

Use that extra day to read the community input summary your superintendent prepared. Use it to review the updated crosswalk before the March board meeting. Use it to prepare the questions you want to ask during the alignment check. Use it to draft the board's response to the community input, so the communication goes out before the final budget is published.

The difference between a board that governs its budget and a board that approves its budget is not usually about knowledge. It is about time. Boards that govern take the time to review, discuss, and direct. Boards that approve run out of time and vote.

This year, you have one extra day. The question is whether you will spend it governing, or whether you will let it slip into the gap between meetings, agendas, and emergencies.
