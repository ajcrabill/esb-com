**Your First Progress Monitoring Check-In of 2028 — What to Measure, How to Discuss It, and What to Do Next**

January 18, 2028

---

Your board adopted 2028 goals at the January organizational meeting or in the weeks that followed. The data packet was complete. The community input process was planned. The superintendent had clear direction on targets and timelines.

That was the easy part.

The hard part — the part that separates boards that govern from boards that hold meetings — is what comes next. And what comes next is your first Progress Monitoring check-in of the 2028 cycle.

Progress Monitoring is not a report the superintendent delivers. It is not a slide deck the board reviews in silence. It is a governance conversation — structured, disciplined, and designed to produce a decision. The board that runs its first check-in well sets the standard for every check-in that follows.

Here is exactly what that check-in should include.

---

**What to measure: The data that matters in January.**

Your first Progress Monitoring check-in serves a specific purpose. It is not a comprehensive review of every data point the district tracks. It is a baseline confirmation. The board needs to confirm that the data infrastructure is in place to track progress against the adopted goals for the rest of the year.

Here is what that means in practice.

The board should see data for every adopted goal, broken down by the student groups identified in the goal language. If the board adopted a third-grade reading goal that names Black students, Hispanic students, students with disabilities, and English learners as target groups, the data must be disaggregated for each of those groups at every check-in. Aggregate numbers that hide group-level gaps are not Progress Monitoring.

The board should also see the validated baseline for each goal. Sometimes the baseline changes when new assessment data arrives or when the district refines data collection. The board needs to know about those changes now — not discover them at the April check-in when progress suddenly looks different.

The January check-in should include three data categories for each goal.

First, current status. Where is the district right now against the adopted baseline? If the goal is measured by a spring assessment, the current status may be limited to formative or interim data. That is fine. The board needs to see the available signal, not wait for the perfect data set.

Second, trajectory. Is the district on pace to meet the targets? The superintendent should present a trajectory projection based on current data and the historical rate of improvement. The projection is a tool for the board to evaluate whether the current pace is adequate, not a prediction. If the current trajectory shows the district on track to hit 68 percent but the board adopted a 72 percent target, the board needs to ask what it will take to close that gap.

Third, the resource connection. Does anything need to change for the district to stay on trajectory? The superintendent should identify resource implications — staffing, scheduling, instructional materials, professional development — that the board needs to address. This conversation feeds directly into the budget process. The board that identifies a resource gap in the January check-in can direct the superintendent to address it in the preliminary budget.

I have watched the **Aldine (TX)** board handle this check-in well. Their board packet includes a two-page "Goal Status Report" for each adopted goal — current status on page one, trajectory and resource implications on page two. Every board member receives the packet five days before the meeting.

---

**Data sources to use: Where the data comes from and how the board validates it.**

The board needs to be clear about which data sources count for Progress Monitoring — and the January check-in is the time to establish that standard.

The board should agree with the superintendent on the approved data sources for each goal at the first check-in. For a reading goal, that might be the district's universal screener and the state interim assessment. For a graduation rate goal, the cohort tracking system and the state's official calculation.

Here is the core principle: the data source should be consistent across every check-in. If the board uses one assessment for the January check-in, it should use the same assessment — or a comparable administration window — for the March, May, and June check-ins. Boards that switch sources never know whether progress is real or just a measurement artifact.

The board should also validate the data, not just receive it. Validation does not mean the board third-guesses the superintendent's numbers. It means the board asks the questions that confirm the data is reliable. Did the administration window cover all student groups? Were accommodations provided as documented? Has the data been reviewed for anomalies?

A fifteen-minute validation conversation at the January check-in saves three hours of debate in March when a board member discovers that a key data point was collected differently for different student groups. I have seen that meeting. It is not productive, and it erodes trust between the board and the superintendent.

The superintendent should have a one-page data source document at the meeting: what data source is used for each goal, when it was collected, who collected it, and what quality assurance steps were applied. A board that reviews that document in January trusts the data in March.

---

**Discussion protocols: How the board runs the Progress Monitoring conversation.**

This is where most boards lose the value of Progress Monitoring. An agenda item that opens with "Superintendent, please report on the goals" produces a forty-minute monologue followed by a few questions and a motion to accept the report. The board leaves with no decisions and no follow-up assignments.

An agenda item that opens with a specific protocol produces a thirty-minute governance conversation with a clear outcome.

Here is the protocol I recommend for the January check-in.

The board president opens the item with a one-sentence frame: "This is our first Progress Monitoring check-in of the 2028 cycle. Our purpose is to confirm the data infrastructure, validate the baseline, and identify any resource issues the board needs to address in the budget process. We have thirty minutes for this item."

The superintendent delivers a five-minute data summary covering the current status and trajectory for each goal, using the board packet distributed five days before the meeting. The board should not hear new information in this summary. The summary exists to orient the conversation, not to present surprises.

The board then conducts a twenty-minute structured discussion focused on three questions.

First, is the data reliable? Any board member who has a concern about the data speaks now. The superintendent responds. If the board agrees the data is reliable, the board moves on.

Second, is the trajectory adequate? If the district is on pace to meet the targets, the board acknowledges that. If the district is behind pace, the board does not panic. The board asks the superintendent what is needed to get back on track and whether the budget recommendation will include those resources.

Third, what does the board need to communicate? If there is a significant concern — a goal behind pace, a resource gap, a baseline change — the board should decide what to communicate to the community. The board that decides its message during the meeting controls it. The board that decides after the meeting reacts.

The board president closes the item with a clear decision: confirm the data infrastructure, request additional data, direct the superintendent to address resource questions in the budget proposal, or approve a community communication.

The board member who leaves that meeting wondering what the Progress Monitoring conversation produced participated in a discussion, not a decision.

---

**Follow-up actions: What the board does after the check-in.**

The Progress Monitoring conversation does not end when the agenda item closes. The board has four follow-up actions that determine whether the check-in produced real governance value.

First, the board secretary records the specific decisions from the check-in as distinct action items in the meeting minutes. Not buried in the verbatim transcript. Published as separate entries: "The board confirmed the data infrastructure. The board directed the superintendent to include a resource timeline for the early literacy goal in the February board packet."

Second, the board president communicates those decisions to the superintendent in writing within forty-eight hours. A brief email — three to five bullet points confirming the decisions and deadlines — is sufficient. The superintendent should not have to wonder whether the board intended what it said.

Third, the board publishes a one-paragraph summary of the check-in for the community within one week. The community should know what the board reviewed, what it concluded, and what it directed. A board that publishes Progress Monitoring summaries treats accountability as a practice, not a slogan.

Fourth, the board confirms the date and data requirements for the next check-in. The governance calendar should already name the February and March dates. Confirm the specific data required and direct the superintendent to begin preparing.

---

**The connection to January's full launch arc.**

This check-in connects directly to everything your board has done since the organizational meeting.

The governance calendar adopted in January names the Progress Monitoring dates. The officer elections and committee assignments determine who facilitates the conversation and which committee tracks follow-up actions. The goals define what is being measured. The budget direction conversation on January 25 will use the resource implications surfaced here to guide the board's budget priorities.

Each piece of the January launch arc builds on the ones before it. A board that completes all four — organizational meeting, goal adoption, Progress Monitoring check-in, budget direction — enters February with a complete governance foundation.

Your first Progress Monitoring check-in is where you discover whether your governance calendar has substance or just dates. Run it with a protocol. Measure the right data. Validate the sources. Assign follow-up actions. Publish the outcome.

That is how you make January the month that defines your governance year.
