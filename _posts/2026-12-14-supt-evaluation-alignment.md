Here's a question that usually makes school board members shift in their seats: what is your superintendent evaluation actually measuring?

I've read hundreds of superintendent evaluations. Not the forms — the actual completed evaluations. And I can tell you with confidence that most of them are measuring the wrong things.

The typical superintendent evaluation scores the superintendent on things like: attendance at board meetings, responsiveness to board member emails, quality of reports to the board, community relations, staff morale. These are not nothing. But they're also not the core of what a superintendent should be evaluated against.

A superintendent should be evaluated on one primary question: *is the district making progress toward the student outcome goals the board has adopted?*

Everything else is secondary.

**Why most evaluations miss the mark**

Let me show you what I mean. Imagine your board adopted a goal in January: increase third-grade reading proficiency from 62% to 70% within three years. Your superintendent has been leading the district toward that goal all year. In December, you sit down to evaluate them.

If your evaluation form asks about "meeting attendance" and "communication skills" but never mentions "progress on third-grade reading," you're sending a message — to the superintendent, to your staff, and to the community. The message is: we said reading proficiency was important, but when it came time to evaluate our leader, we evaluated something else entirely.

I see this pattern constantly. Boards adopt ambitious goals at the beginning of the year. They spend meeting time discussing those goals. They reference them in community communications. And then, at evaluation time, they fill out a form that has more to do with management style than student outcomes.

The ESB framework has a clear sequence for this work:

**Goals first → Budget second → Evaluation third**

Here's why that sequence matters. The goals define what the board wants to accomplish. The budget allocates resources toward those goals. And the evaluation measures whether the superintendent is leading the district to achieve them. If you break that chain — if you evaluate against criteria that have nothing to do with your goals — you've disconnected accountability from purpose.

**The three-question evaluation framework**

Earlier this year, in September's Three Gaps series, I introduced a simple evaluation framework built on three questions. Let me revisit it here because December is when you need it most:

**Question 1: Did the district make progress on the board-adopted student outcome goals?** This is the primary question. The answer should be data-based. Third-grade reading went from 62% to 65% — that's progress toward the 70% target. Algebra proficiency stayed flat — that's a concern. Chronic absenteeism is down two points — that's a win. What does the data say?

**Question 2: Did the superintendent provide the board with the information it needed to govern?** This is the accountability question. Did you receive regular progress monitoring data? Were the data reports clear, timely, and actionable? Did the superintendent flag problems before they became crises? If you can't answer this question honestly, you can't evaluate whether your governance structure is working.

**Question 3: Did the board-superintendent relationship support effective governance?** This is the relational question. Was there trust? Were there disagreements that went unresolved? Did the board respect the superintendent's operational authority while maintaining accountability for results? This is not about whether everyone likes each other. It's about whether the governance relationship is functional enough to support the work.

Three questions. That's the core. Everything else — the management criteria, the communication scores, the rating scales — is supplemental to these three.

**The sequence that matters**

Here's the most important thing about superintendent evaluation timing: board self-assessment must come first.

The NSBA recommends this sequence. The research supports it. And I've seen the difference it makes in practice. When a board completes its own self-assessment before evaluating the superintendent, two things happen:

1. The board evaluates itself against the same goals it's using to evaluate the superintendent. This creates alignment and shared accountability.
2. The board enters the superintendent evaluation conversation with humility — having already acknowledged its own governance gaps — rather than assuming the superintendent is solely responsible for every outcome.

The Iowa Association of School Boards study I mentioned on Wednesday found that boards that self-assess before superintendent evaluation improve their governance calibration by 23%. That means their evaluation of the superintendent is more accurate, more fair, and more useful.

**The December calendar**

For boards operating on a calendar-year evaluation cycle, December is peak season. If your board is in this window:

- **This week:** Complete data collection. Gather the evidence on each goal. Review the superintendent's self-evaluation if your process includes one.
- **Before Christmas break:** Conduct the evaluation discussion. A closed session is appropriate, but the output should include specific, documented feedback connected to board goals.
- **January organizational meeting:** Deliver the written evaluation, discuss areas of alignment and concern, and agree on priority areas for the coming year.

If your board is on a fiscal-year evaluation cycle (July-June), December is the midpoint check-in. The same framework applies — but the emphasis is on course correction rather than final assessment.
