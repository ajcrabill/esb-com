Let me ask you a direct question: when was the last time your board did an honest self-assessment?

Not a compliance exercise where everyone fills out a form and the results get filed away. Not a casual conversation in the parking lot after a meeting. An honest, structured evaluation of how your board functions as a governing body — how you use your time, how you make decisions, whether you're actually making progress on the goals you set.

If your answer is "never" or "I can't remember," you're not alone. Most school boards don't do self-assessment. They don't have time. They don't have a framework. And honestly, they don't want to — because self-assessment means confronting uncomfortable truths about how you operate.

But here's the research you need to know: the Iowa Association of School Boards studied the impact of board self-assessment and found that boards who did a structured self-assessment before their superintendent evaluation improved their governance calibration by 23%. That's not a small number. That's the difference between a board that thinks it's doing well and a board that actually *knows* it needs to improve.

The NSBA recommends annual board self-assessment as standard practice. And the ESB framework takes it a step further: board self-evaluation should happen within 45 days *before* the superintendent evaluation. Why? Because you can't evaluate someone else's performance against a set of goals if you haven't first evaluated whether your own governance is supporting those goals.

**Why boards avoid self-assessment**

I've heard every reason. Let me name the most common ones and address them directly:

**"We don't have a tool."** Yes you do. The ESB framework gives you four domains to assess: Goal Clarity, Progress Monitoring, Governance Team Function, and Community Trust. Each domain has specific indicators you can rate yourself against. This isn't abstract. It's a scored assessment with concrete questions.

**"It's uncomfortable evaluating each other."** Good. Discomfort is the price of honesty. If your board can't have an honest conversation about how it operates, you don't have a governance team — you have a collection of individuals who happen to sit in the same room once a month. A self-assessment isn't about criticizing individual members. It's about evaluating the board as a whole. *Did we, as a board, govern in a way that advanced student outcomes this year?*

**"We don't have time."** You don't have time *not* to do it. A board that doesn't assess itself spends the year operating on assumptions — assuming the superintendent's evaluation is about the right things, assuming the budget reflects board priorities, assuming the community trusts the board's decisions. Every one of those assumptions can be wrong. A 90-minute self-assessment session is cheap insurance against governing blind.

**"Nothing will change anyway."** This is the most honest objection and the most dangerous. If your board believes that self-assessment won't lead to change, you're saying your board is incapable of improvement. I don't believe that. I've seen too many boards transform their governance through the simple act of looking in the mirror and deciding to be different.

**The four-domain self-assessment framework**

Here's what a board self-assessment looks like in practice, using the ESB framework:

**Domain 1: Goal Clarity.** Did the board adopt specific, measurable student outcome goals? Can every board member name those goals without looking at a document? Did the board's decisions throughout the year consistently connect back to those goals?

**Domain 2: Progress Monitoring.** Did the board receive regular data updates on goal progress? Did board members engage with that data — asking questions, identifying gaps, holding the superintendent accountable? Or did the data packets arrive and nobody read them?

**Domain 3: Governance Team Function.** Did the board operate as a team? Was there a clear division between governance and management? Did board members respect the superintendent's operational authority while maintaining accountability for results? Did the board use its meeting time effectively, or was it consumed by operational trivia?

**Domain 4: Community Trust.** Did the board communicate transparently with the community about its decisions? Did it create meaningful opportunities for public input? Did it follow through on commitments to engagement? Did the community see the board as credible?

Rate yourselves on each domain. 1 to 5. Then ask the hardest question: *what would need to change for us to be a 5 next year?*

**The sequence that works**

The research is clear on timing. The NSBA recommends annual board self-assessment. But the specific sequence — this is critical — is:

Board self-assessment → Superintendent evaluation → Goal-setting for next year

The self-assessment comes first because it gives you the context for the superintendent evaluation. If the board evaluated itself honestly and discovered gaps in progress monitoring, that context changes how you evaluate the superintendent's data reporting, right? The superintendent evaluation comes second because it flows from the goals and the board's own assessment of its governance. And goal-setting comes third because it benefits from everything learned in steps one and two.

Most boards do it backward. They set goals without evaluating the superintendent. They evaluate the superintendent without assessing their own governance. And then they wonder why the cycle never produces improvement.

**The December deadline**

Here's the practical timeline: if your board hasn't completed a self-assessment yet, early December is your last usable window. Distribute the assessment in late November or the first week of December. Give members a week to complete it individually. Collate the results. Discuss them at your December board meeting or a separate work session. Identify one or two areas where the board commits to improving in 2027.

If you wait until January, you've already lost the sequencing advantage. The superintendent evaluation will be rushed, the goal-setting will be reactive, and you'll spend February trying to reconstruct the reflection work December was supposed to provide.
