**Your First Community Listening Session of 2027 — What Are You Actually Listening For?**

February 17, 2027

---

Your board held a public hearing on January 11 as part of the goal adoption process. Community members came to the microphone. They asked questions. They offered input. The board listened and adopted its goals.

That was a good start. But let me name something that most boards don't want to admit: the people who show up for a public hearing at a Tuesday night board meeting are not representative of your community. They're the people who have transportation, childcare, flexible schedules, and the confidence to speak at a microphone in a formal setting. They're your most engaged citizens — and they're also the ones you hear from most often.

The community you don't hear from — the family working two jobs, the parent who doesn't speak English fluently, the community member whose experience with the school system has been negative for so long they've stopped engaging — those voices are invisible in a public hearing format. And they're the voices your board most needs to hear.

Your first community listening session of 2027 should be designed to reach them.

**What a listening session is — and isn't**

A listening session is not a town hall. It's not a public hearing. It's not a board meeting held at a different location. A town hall is designed for information sharing — the board presents, the community reacts. A public hearing is designed for formal input — the community speaks, the board records. A board meeting at a different location is a board meeting — the agenda is the same, the format is the same, the audience is just different.

A listening session is designed for one thing: the board hearing from people it doesn't normally hear from. The board doesn't present. The board doesn't react. The board doesn't defend. The board listens.

I've watched a board hold a listening session at a community center in a neighborhood where 40% of the district's English learner families live. They provided interpretation. They held it at 6 PM instead of 7 PM. They served dinner. They asked three questions and then spent ninety minutes listening to the answers.

The input they received changed how the board thought about their ELL programming. Not because the board had bad information before. Because the board had incomplete information. The listening session filled in the gaps.

**Three questions your listening session should ask**

A listening session with no structure produces stories without direction. A listening session with too much structure produces answers the board already expects. The sweet spot is three questions — specific enough to produce useful input, open enough to surface what the board doesn't know.

**Question 1: What's working well for your student?**

This question is designed to surface what the district does well from the perspective of families. It gives the board a community-level picture of strengths. And it tells the board what the community values — because people talk about what matters to them.

I've watched a board learn from this question that families loved the after-school tutoring program at one elementary school but didn't know it was available at others. The board didn't know that because the program data looked fine — the district-wide averages showed participation. The listening session revealed that participation was concentrated in a few schools. The board asked the superintendent to explore equitable access to the program across all schools.

**Question 2: What's the biggest challenge your student faces at school?**

This question surfaces the barriers families see but the board doesn't. The answer is often something the board hasn't thought about. Transportation. Communication from the school in a language the family understands. A specific intervention that's not available at the student's school.

The board doesn't need to solve these challenges in the listening session. It needs to hear them. The challenges families name will inform the board's monitoring questions, budget conversations, and policy reviews for the rest of the year.

**Question 3: If you could tell the school board one thing, what would it be?**

This is the open-ended question that surfaces what the board doesn't know to ask. It's the question that produces the unexpected answer — the one that changes the board's understanding of a problem.

I've watched a parent answer this question with: "My son was held back in third grade because he couldn't read. Nobody told us he was struggling until it was too late. By the time we knew, he was two grade levels behind." The board heard that answer and asked themselves: "When do families in our district first learn that their student is below grade level? Is it early enough to intervene? Or is it, as this parent described, too late?"

That question — the board's question, prompted by a family's answer — became a governance priority for the next six months.

**How to design the session**

Here's what a well-designed listening session looks like in practice.

**Location.** Hold it at a school or community center in the neighborhood you're trying to reach. Not at the district office. Not at a location that requires transportation families don't have.

**Time.** Hold it at a time that works for working families. 6 PM or 6:30 PM. Not 7 PM, when families with young children have already started the bedtime routine. Not a Tuesday, when many parents have standing commitments.

**Duration.** Ninety minutes. Long enough for meaningful conversation. Short enough that families with childcare constraints can attend.

**Structure.** Fifteen minutes for welcome and context. Sixty minutes for small-group conversation around the three questions. Fifteen minutes for closing and next steps.

**Interpretation.** If your district serves families who speak languages other than English, provide interpretation in every language that represents at least 5% of your student population. The families you most need to hear from are often the families who don't speak English at home. If you don't provide interpretation, you're designing the session to exclude them.

**Food.** Serve dinner. A listening session at 6 PM is during dinner time for most families. A light meal tells families you understand that their time is valuable and that you're grateful for their presence.

**What you do with what you hear**

The most important rule of a listening session: the board must do something with the input. A listening session that produces no follow-up damages trust more than not holding the session at all. The community invests time and vulnerability. The board needs to invest governance action in return.

After the listening session, the board should produce a one-page summary of what it heard. The summary should identify three themes from the session and name one action the board will take in response.

The action doesn't need to be comprehensive. It needs to be visible. "In response to the March 3 listening session at Jackson Elementary, the board will ask the superintendent to report on the current timeline for notifying families when a student is below grade level in reading." That's one action. It's specific. It's visible. The community that showed up for the session will see that the board heard them and responded.

The district I mentioned earlier — the one that learned about the ELL families' concerns — produced a one-page summary from their listening session, filed it in the board packet, and directed the superintendent to include ELL family engagement data in the March progress report. Six months later, the board reviewed updated ELL programming that had been shaped by what the community said in that session.

That's what governance looks like. Not a board that listens and moves on. A board that listens, records, directs, and follows through.

**How many listening sessions should you hold?**

I recommend one listening session per board member per year. A five-member board holds five sessions. A seven-member board holds seven. Each board member is responsible for attending at least one session per year — ideally the one in the neighborhood they represent or the one serving a community they're most connected to.

February or March is the right time for the first session. The goals are adopted. The budget is in process. The community engagement calendar has room before the budget adoption cycle begins in April. A February or March listening session gives the board time to use what it learns in the April mid-year check-in, the May evaluation preparation, and the June budget adoption.

Schedule your first session now. Ask the three questions. Listen for what you don't know. And follow up with visible action. Your community will notice the difference between a board that hears them and a board that listens.
