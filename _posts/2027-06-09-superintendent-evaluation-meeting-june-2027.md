**The Superintendent Evaluation Meeting — Protocol, Discussion, and Documentation**

June 9, 2027

---

The board has completed its individual written evaluations. The board has discussed those evaluations together and reached consensus on the consolidated assessment. Now comes the meeting that determines whether all that preparation produces results or gets lost in translation.

The joint evaluation meeting — the board and the superintendent together — is the most important professional conversation the board has with the superintendent all year. A well-structured meeting produces clarity, shared direction, and a stronger working relationship. A poorly structured meeting produces confusion, defensiveness, and erosion of the trust the board has spent six months building.

Here's how to structure the meeting so it produces the first outcome.

**Meeting structure — the three segments**

The evaluation meeting should have three distinct segments. Each segment has a specific purpose and a specific format. The board president or evaluation committee chair facilitates throughout.

**Segment 1 — Evaluation presentation (30 minutes).** The board president presents the consolidated board evaluation. The presentation should be organized by the evaluation framework the board and superintendent agreed to in February: goal-progress assessment first, then growth area progress, then board support assessment, then overall assessment.

The presentation should be specific. The board president reads the board's assessment for each area, referencing the evidence that supports the assessment. Not: "The board is generally satisfied with progress on literacy." Instead: "The board's assessment on goal one — early literacy — is that the superintendent has effectively executed the board's direction. The trajectory the board set was 85% by June. May data showed 76%, which is two points ahead of trajectory. The superintendent delivered complete data packets on time for each review, and the instructional coaching adjustments the board directed in February were implemented within six weeks."

The superintendent does not respond during the presentation. The superintendent listens and takes notes. The presentation is the board communicating its assessment. The superintendent's response comes in segment two.

**Segment 2 — Superintendent response and discussion (30 minutes).** The superintendent responds to the evaluation. The superintendent should be encouraged to address each area — areas of agreement, areas where the superintendent has a different perspective, and areas where the superintendent needs clarification or additional support from the board.

This segment is not a negotiation. The board's assessment is the board's assessment. But the superintendent's perspective is essential information. If the board's assessment of a growth area is that the superintendent needs to improve in data communication, and the superintendent responds that the board's data format expectations changed three times during the year, that information tells the board something about its own performance. The board should hear it, document it, and factor it into the support commitments for the next cycle.

The board president keeps the discussion focused on the evaluation framework. If the superintendent raises an issue that is outside the framework, the board president notes it for the next cycle planning conversation and returns the discussion to the evaluation.

**Segment 3 — Next cycle commitments (20 minutes).** The board and superintendent agree on:

The growth areas for the next evaluation cycle. The board identifies two to three specific areas where the superintendent should focus. The areas should be directly connected to the evaluation findings — not a new set of priorities unrelated to what the evaluation revealed.

The board's support commitments. The board commits to specific actions that will support the superintendent's growth in each area. The commitments should be specific and time-bound. Not: "The board will provide clearer guidance." Instead: "The board will provide the data review format expectations no later than August 15, before the fall data review cycle begins."

The first check-in date. The board and superintendent agree on the date for the 90-day check-in of the next governance year — typically October, after the board has completed its summer preparation and the fall goal-setting process.

**Documentation — what gets recorded**

The evaluation meeting should produce a written record. The record includes:

The consolidated board evaluation document — the final version, including any adjustments the board made after the discussion.

The superintendent's written response — the superintendent's perspective on the evaluation, documented as an addendum to the board's evaluation.

The agreed-upon commitments — growth areas, board support commitments, and timeline, captured in a single document that both the board and superintendent receive.

The first check-in date — confirmed in writing and added to the board's governance calendar.

The documentation matters because it creates accountability for both sides. The board and superintendent have a written record of what was agreed to, what each side committed to, and when the next check-in happens. Without it, the evaluation meeting becomes a good conversation that fades into memory. With it, the evaluation meeting becomes a governance event that produces direction for the next cycle.

**Common pitfalls and how to avoid them**

**Pitfall one: The evaluation becomes a negotiation.** If the board presents its assessment and the superintendent disagrees, the board should hear the superintendent's perspective and document it. The board should not adjust its assessment simply because the superintendent disagrees. The board's assessment is based on five months of governance work. Adjusting it in a single meeting undermines the entire evaluation process.

**Pitfall two: The discussion goes off-framework.** The superintendent may want to discuss operational challenges, budget constraints, or personnel issues that are not part of the evaluation framework. The board president should acknowledge those concerns and schedule a separate conversation. The evaluation meeting stays focused on the evaluation framework.

**Pitfall three: The commitments are vague.** "The board will support the superintendent" is not a commitment. "The board will provide the data review schedule for the fall by August 1" is a commitment. The commitments need to be specific enough that both sides can evaluate whether they were fulfilled at the next check-in.

**Pitfall four: The documentation doesn't happen.** The board president or board clerk should prepare the written record within 48 hours of the meeting and distribute it to the board and superintendent for confirmation. If the documentation takes two weeks, the details blur, and the commitments lose their specificity.
