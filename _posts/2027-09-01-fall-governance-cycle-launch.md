**The Fall Governance Cycle Launches This Month — Here Is What Every Board Should Do in September**

September 1, 2027

---

The August work session closed the summer governance cycle. The board completed the July follow-up, published the community progress report, launched the data review system, finalized the budget guidance document, and left the August board work session with a September launch agenda.

Now September is here. And the fall governance cycle begins.

September is the most consequential month for board governance, and not because of any single decision the board makes this month. It is consequential because the patterns the board establishes in September become the patterns the board follows through December.

The board that enters September with an intentional agenda — built around the goals adopted in January, the data review calendar confirmed in August, and the board's governance commitments from the summer — will run an intentional fall cycle.

The board that enters September reacting to whatever lands on the agenda will run a reactive fall cycle. And by December, that board will wonder why the fall felt like a series of emergencies rather than a governance plan.

Here is what every board should have on the September agenda.

**One: The September board meeting agenda should be built around goals, not around reports.**

The most common mistake boards make in September is treating the first meeting back as a general update session. The superintendent presents a back-to-school report. The board receives enrollment numbers. The board approves routine items. The meeting ends.

That is an operational meeting, not a governance meeting.

A governance September agenda has the goals at the center. The superintendent presents progress against each goal — even if the data available in September is limited to summer program outcomes, enrollment projections, and early-year assessment placement data. The board reviews the first data point on the fall metric set. The board makes its first written decision on each goal.

The board that reviews goal progress in September establishes that the fall governance cycle is about monitoring student outcomes, not about receiving staff reports.

**Two: The October data review format should be confirmed in September.**

The August work session launched the data review system. The board confirmed the metric set, the data format, and the decision-recording protocol. Now the September board meeting is the deadline for confirming the October data review logistics.

What needs to be confirmed:

- The date and time of the October data review — is it on the regular meeting agenda as a dedicated agenda item, or is it a separate work session?
- The data packet deadline — when does the superintendent need to deliver the data packet to the board? I recommend at least seven days before the review.
- The board president's facilitation role — who facilitates the data review discussion? The board president should be prepared to lead the conversation, not defer to the superintendent to present and interpret the data.
- The community-facing summary format — how will the board communicate the data review outcomes to the community? A written summary published within 72 hours of the data review is the standard I recommend.

**Three: The superintendent partnership rhythm should be set for the fall.**

The summer check-in follow-up covered the growth areas and support commitments from the June evaluation. The September board meeting is when the board confirms the fall partnership rhythm: how often the board president and superintendent meet, what the check-in agenda covers, and how the board communicates its governance priorities to the superintendent between meetings.

I recommend a standing biweekly check-in between the board president and superintendent through December. The check-in is not a governance meeting — it is a communication meeting. The board president confirms the board's governance direction. The superintendent updates the president on emerging issues. The check-in prevents the October surprise that disrupts the governance calendar.

**Four: The year-end governance calendar should be distributed to the board.**

August's fall governance calendar adoption gave the board the dates. September is when the board should see the calendar in context — the data review dates, the committee meeting dates, the work session dates, the regular meeting dates, and the December organizational meeting date, all on one page with the governance commitments and goal review milestones.

Every board member should leave the September board meeting with the full fall calendar. Not in the packet. In hand. Because the board member who knows what October, November, and December require is the board member who shows up prepared.

---

September is the month that sets the tone for the governance year. The board that launches the fall cycle with goal-centered agendas, confirmed data review logistics, a superintendent partnership rhythm, and a distributed calendar enters October with a running start.

The board that treats September as a back-to-school administrative meeting enters October reactive — and spends the rest of the fall catching up.
