**The Year-End Progress Report — How to Tell Your Spring Governance Story**

April 19, 2027

---

March's community progress report was your first public communication on goal progress. It introduced the community to your board's adopted goals, showed the baseline data, and reported the first quarter of progress. It was a good first step.

The year-end progress report is a different document. It covers the full January-through-June governance arc. It reports on every adopted goal — the ones that improved, the ones that stayed flat, and the ones that declined. It names what the board did in response to the data and whether those actions produced results.

And it closes the loop on the governance year. The community sees the full story — what the board set out to accomplish, what progress was made, and what the board learned. The board has a record of its governance work that it can point to for the rest of the year. The year-end report is the document that answers the question every board should expect: "What did you accomplish?"

**How the year-end report is different from the March report**

The March community progress report was designed for readers who were encountering the board's goals for the first time. It introduced the framework. It reported first quarter data. It set expectations for the spring.

The year-end report is designed for readers who have been following the board's work. It assumes familiarity with the goals. It focuses on trajectory — not just where the data landed in June, but whether the rate of improvement matched the trajectory the board adopted in January.

The March report answered: what are our goals and how are we doing so far? The year-end report answers: did our governance produce the progress we committed to, and what did we learn that will shape next year's goals?

That difference determines how you structure the report, what data you include, and how you frame the results.

**The year-end report structure**

The year-end report has four sections, each serving a specific purpose.

**Section 1: The governance year in review.** One page. The board names what it set out to accomplish in January and summarizes the governance work it completed — goal adoption, data reviews, budget alignment, community engagement. This section establishes that the board had a plan and executed it. It's the narrative that frames the data that follows.

The board should include a simple calendar visualization or timeline showing the key governance events of the spring — goal adoption in January, the first data review in February, the budget alignment review in March, the public hearing and mid-year check-in in April, the second data review in May, and the superintendent evaluation in June.

**Section 2: Goal-by-goal progress.** One page per goal. For each adopted goal, the report shows the baseline, the June data point, and the trajectory. It answers three questions for each goal:

1. Did the June data point meet the trajectory? (Yes/No/Partially)
2. What governance actions did the board take that affected this goal?
3. What does the board conclude from the full six-month arc of data?

This section is where transparency matters most. A goal that declined should be reported with the same prominence as a goal that improved. The community can tell when the board is hiding bad news. And the data is the data — the board's credibility depends on reporting it accurately.

I've seen a board publish a year-end report that showed improvement on two of three adopted goals and decline on the third. The board reported the decline with context — what the data showed, what the board did in response, and what the board learned. The community trusted the report because it was honest about what didn't work. The same board could have buried the decline in a footnote. Instead, they led with it — and the community response was: "We appreciate the honesty."

**Section 3: Board self-evaluation.** One page. The board reports its mid-year governance check-in findings and the adjustments it made. This section answers: did our governance practice improve over the course of the spring? The board reports on the five indicators — meeting structure, board member contribution, calendar integrity, communication discipline, and governance model alignment — and names what changed between February and April.

This section is the one most boards omit. They report student data but don't report on their own governance performance. The community deserves to know whether the board is evaluating itself with the same rigor it applies to the superintendent and the district.

**Section 4: What's next.** One page. The board names what it plans to do with the year-end findings. Three categories:

1. **Summer governance work** — What the board will read, train on, and plan between June and August.
2. **Next-year goal implications** — How the year-end data will inform the next goal-setting cycle. Which goals should continue, which need adjustment, and which metrics the board will use.
3. **Community engagement** — How the community can participate in shaping next year's priorities.

This section is the bridge from the current governance year to the next. It tells the community that the board isn't just reporting results — it's using those results to get better.

**When to publish the year-end report**

The year-end report should be published in late June or early July — after the superintendent evaluation, after the final data review, and before the board disperses for summer break.

The June board meeting is the natural publication moment. The board reviews the report, discusses the findings, and votes to publish it. The publication coincides with the close of the governance year and sets up the summer work.

The board should schedule the year-end report as an agenda item at the June board meeting — not as a consent item. The board discusses each section, names the key findings, and takes questions from the community. The discussion is the board's opportunity to demonstrate that it owns the results — the good ones and the difficult ones.

**The year-end report as an accountability document**

The year-end progress report is the final spring accountability event. It closes the loop on every goal, every data review, every board decision. It tells the community what the board accomplished with the year it had.

And it sets up the next governance year. The data the report contains — what worked, what didn't, what the board learned — becomes the foundation for the next goal-setting cycle. The board that publishes a thorough year-end report enters the summer with a clear picture of where its governance practice is strong and where it needs improvement.

The report is the document that answers the question the community will ask when the board presents its next budget: "What did you accomplish with the last one?" A board that can point to a published year-end report with data, analysis, and honest conclusions is a board that has earned the community's trust to ask for more.
