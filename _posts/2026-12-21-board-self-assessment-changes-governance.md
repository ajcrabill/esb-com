**The Board Self-Assessment That Actually Changes How You Govern**

December 21, 2026

---

Here's a question I want you to answer honestly.

When was the last time your board assessed itself?

Not the time your state association sent a generic survey that everyone filled out in five minutes and filed away. Not the time the board president asked "how are we doing?" and everyone nodded. A real assessment. Completed individually. Discussed as a team. With results that drove a change in how your board operates.

I'll tell you what most boards tell me: they've never done it. Or they did it once, years ago, and never looked at the results.

The board self-assessment is consistently the most underutilized tool in school governance. Most boards complete one because their state association provides a template. They fill it out, file it, and never discuss the results. It's a compliance exercise, not a governance practice.

Here's what I've learned from boards that use self-assessments well: the assessment itself doesn't matter. What matters is what you do with the results.

## Why Most Self-Assessments Fail

Let me tell you why the typical board self-assessment doesn't change anything. It's not because the questions are wrong. It's because the process is wrong.

**Problem 1: Only the board president sees the results.** The assessment is collected and summarized by the board president, who shares a high-level report that avoids anything uncomfortable. Individual board members never see the full picture of how their colleagues responded.

**Problem 2: The results are discussed in closed session.** The board reviews the findings behind closed doors, agrees on a few platitudes about "working better together," and never returns to the topic.

**Problem 3: There's no follow-through.** The assessment produces a score but no action plan. The board doesn't identify what it will do differently. By the next meeting, the assessment is forgotten.

**Problem 4: It's individual only.** Board members assess themselves but never assess the board as a collective body. The most important data — the pattern of responses across the full board — is never analyzed.

A self-assessment that follows this pattern isn't a tool for improvement. It's a box that gets checked.

## The Self-Assessment That Works

Here's the alternative. Four principles that turn a self-assessment from a compliance exercise into a governance practice.

### Principle 1: Individual + Collective

The assessment has two parts. First, each board member assesses themselves — their own performance, their own governance habits, their own contributions to the team. Second, each board member assesses the board as a whole — how it functions, where it's strong, where it's weak.

Both sets of data matter. The individual assessment gives each board member a mirror. The collective assessment gives the board a map.

The pattern of individual responses IS the data. If six out of seven board members rate the board's goal clarity as "weak," that's not one person's opinion. That's a consensus that demands attention.

### Principle 2: Anonymous + Discussed

The assessment is completed confidentially. Names are not attached to responses. Board members need to be able to answer honestly without worrying about how their colleagues will react.

But the aggregated results are discussed openly. As a full board. In a structured conversation facilitated by someone who can keep the discussion productive.

This is the part most boards skip. They collect the data and never discuss it. The discussion is where the learning happens. It's where board members discover that they all see the same problem — but nobody was willing to name it.

I've sat in these discussions and watched board members realize for the first time that their colleagues share their frustrations. "I thought I was the only one who thought we were spending too much time on operational details." No, you weren't the only one. The data was there all along. You just never talked about it.

### Principle 3: Scored + Prioritized

The assessment produces a score. That's useful — it gives the board a baseline to measure against in future years.

But more importantly, the assessment surfaces the one or two areas where the board collectively agrees it needs to improve.

Not ten areas. Not five. One or two. The areas with the lowest scores AND the highest agreement. The places where the board can make a change that everyone will feel.

I worked with a board that identified its top priority as "meeting time use." Every board member rated the board low on how it used its meeting time. They committed to restructuring their agenda. Six months later, they reassessed. The score had improved dramatically. Because they didn't try to fix everything at once. They fixed the one thing that mattered most.

### Principle 4: Annual + Actionable

The assessment doesn't end with the discussion. The board commits to specific improvement actions, tracks them, and revisits them at mid-year.

Here's the accountability structure I recommend:

- **December:** Complete the self-assessment. Discuss results as a board. Identify one or two priority areas for improvement.
- **January:** Adopt a board improvement plan. Specific actions, owners, and timelines.
- **June:** Mid-year check-in. How are we doing on our improvement priorities?
- **December:** Full reassessment. Has the score improved? What's our next priority?

This turns the self-assessment from a once-a-year event into a continuous improvement cycle. The board doesn't just assess — it acts, tracks, and reassesses.

## The Questions That Matter

I'm not going to give you a forty-question survey. The most effective self-assessments have a focused set of questions across the four ESB domains.

Here are the questions your board should be asking:

**Goal Clarity:**
- Does our board have specific, measurable student outcome goals?
- Does every board member know what they are?
- Are the goals referenced in our meetings, or are they filed away?

**Progress Monitoring:**
- Do we review goal progress data at every board meeting?
- Does that data drive our decisions, or is it a presentation we sit through?
- Do we adjust our approach when we see that goals are off track?

**Governance Team:**
- Do we function as a team, or as seven individuals with separate agendas?
- Do we trust each other enough to have honest conversations?
- Do we hold ourselves accountable for preparation, attendance, and conduct?

**Superintendent Partnership:**
- Is our relationship with the superintendent clear and productive?
- Do we have a shared understanding of roles and boundaries?
- Does our superintendent evaluation measure progress toward our adopted goals?

Answer those questions honestly. Individually and collectively. Anonymously and then together.

## The December Opportunity

I know this is a busy time of year. December is the shortest governance month. Board members have holiday obligations. The temptation is to skip the self-assessment and promise to do it in January.

Don't do that.

January is when you're setting goals, adopting the evaluation framework, and holding the organizational meeting. If you don't do the self-assessment in December, it won't happen until next year. And that means your board will walk into 2027 without knowing how it needs to improve.

The best boards I work with do their self-assessment in December. They schedule a two-hour work session — just the board, no public, no staff except the superintendent. They complete the assessment individually beforehand. They discuss the results together. They identify their one or two priorities for improvement.

Then they walk into January knowing not just what they want the district to achieve — but what they need to improve as a governing body.

That's the self-assessment that actually changes how you govern. Not a compliance exercise. A governance practice.
