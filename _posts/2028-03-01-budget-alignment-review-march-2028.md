**The Budget Alignment Review — Where the Crosswalk Meets Progress Monitoring**

March 1, 2028

---

Your board covered more ground in February than most boards cover in an entire budget cycle.

You reviewed the preliminary budget against the three-bucket framework — goal alignment, fiscal guardrails, and community priorities — and produced written findings on all three. You built the goal-to-budget crosswalk, the one-page matrix that maps every major expenditure to the adopted goal it serves. You engaged the community in the public hearing conversation and published the input summary.

That work positioned your board to do something most boards never do in March: answer the question of alignment with evidence rather than intuition.

Let me show you exactly what that looks like.

---

**What the March alignment review is — precisely**

The March budget alignment review is not a re-review of the full preliminary budget. Your board already did that work in February.

The March review is a targeted check on two specific questions. Answer them, and your board has confirmed the budget is ready for the April adoption vote. Miss either one, and your board will adopt a budget without knowing whether the dollars actually fund the goals.

**Question one: Did the superintendent address the corrections the board requested after the February review?**

This is the accountability question.

If your board found a goal alignment gap in the February review — a priority goal that received a disproportionately small allocation — the board directed the superintendent to adjust the allocation in the revised budget. The March alignment review answers one question: Did the adjustment happen?

I am not talking about a general conversation. I am talking about a specific finding. The board's February written findings named the gap, named the adjustment requested, and named the timeline. The March review confirms the adjustment was made and the revised allocation is consistent with the goal priority the board adopted in January.

If your board found a fiscal guardrail that had been exceeded — the administrative cost ratio was above the board's stated ceiling, for example — the March review confirms whether the revised budget falls within the guardrail. If the guardrail still reads as exceeded, the board has a decision to make: direct a further adjustment before the April adoption, or acknowledge that the guardrail needs revision because of changed circumstances and adopt a revised guardrail with an explanation.

I have watched boards in **Lawrence (KS)** use a two-page "Budget Alignment Check" for this exact purpose. The document has two columns. The left column lists every February finding — the alignment gap, the guardrail concern, the community priority adjustment. The right column shows the superintendent's response for each item: the budget line adjustment, the dollar amount change, and the date of the adjustment. The board can see in five minutes whether every February finding was addressed.

A board that confirms every February finding was addressed enters the April adoption with confidence. A board that finds unanswered corrections has four weeks — through March — to resolve them before the final vote.

**Question two: Does the March Progress Monitoring data validate or challenge the budget allocations?**

This is the strategic question.

The February crosswalk showed the board where the money is going. The March Progress Monitoring data shows the board whether the money is producing results. Together, they tell the board whether the resource allocation is working.

Here is how this works in practice.

Your board adopted three measurable goals in January — early literacy proficiency, math growth for targeted subgroups, and ninth-grade on-track rate. You built the crosswalk in February and saw the budget allocations for each goal: $2.1 million for early literacy intervention, $1.4 million for math acceleration, and $600,000 for ninth-grade support.

The March Progress Monitoring data arrives. The early literacy data shows year-over-year growth is on trajectory — the allocation is producing results. The math growth data shows the targeted subgroups are not making expected progress — the allocation may not be sufficient. The ninth-grade on-track data shows the intervention is working but the scale is too small to reach the full cohort.

Now the board has a specific question for each goal. For the early literacy goal: confirm alignment and continue monitoring. For the math growth goal: is the allocation sufficient, or does the board need to direct a reallocation before the April adoption? For the ninth-grade goal: can the board expand the intervention within the current allocation, or does it need to adjust the budget to scale the program?

The board does not need to answer these questions from scratch. The crosswalk shows the current allocation. The Progress Monitoring data shows the current trajectory. The board's question is whether the two are consistent — and if they are not, whether a correction is needed before the final vote.

---

**The two-page Alignment Check format**

Let me give you the exact format I recommend for the board packet.

Page one: the crosswalk from February, updated with any adjustments the board requested, with the current dollar amounts and percentages. This is the same one-page, three-column format your board saw in February — goals in priority order, budget allocations, percentage of general fund.

Page two: the March Progress Monitoring data for each adopted goal, presented alongside the crosswalk allocation for that goal. Two columns per goal: the allocation on the left, the trajectory data on the right.

The board can see in one document whether the resource allocation and the performance data tell the same story.

If they do, the board confirms alignment and moves to the April adoption timeline.

If they do not, the board has one month to direct corrections before the final vote. The crosswalk tells the board where the dollars are. The Progress Monitoring data tells the board whether the dollars are working. The Alignment Check tells the board what to do about it.

---

**What the board does with the Alignment Check**

The March alignment review produces one of two outcomes.

**Outcome one: Alignment confirmed.** The superintendent addressed every February correction. The Progress Monitoring data shows every goal is on trajectory or making expected progress. The board adopts a written finding confirming alignment and moves the focus to the April public hearing presentation and the adoption vote.

**Outcome two: Alignment gaps identified.** One or more February corrections were not fully addressed. One or more goals are off trajectory and the current allocation may not be sufficient. The board adopts a written finding naming each gap, directs the superintendent to propose adjustments before the next meeting or the April meeting, and sets a deadline for the adjustments to be presented and reviewed.

I have watched boards in **Adams 14 (CO)** handle Outcome Two well. Their board president schedules a fifteen-minute special board call or adds a board discussion item to the March committee meeting. The superintendent presents the proposed adjustments. The board reviews them against the crosswalk. The board confirms alignment before the public hearing. The process takes thirty minutes total and prevents the board from arriving at the April adoption with unresolved questions.

A board that reaches Outcome One in March will have a smooth adoption. A board that reaches Outcome Two in March will have a productive month of correction. A board that does not conduct an alignment review at all will adopt a budget in April without knowing whether the February findings were ever addressed.
