**The Budget Alignment Review — Where the Crosswalk Meets Progress Monitoring**

March 1, 2028

---

February was a crosswalk month.

Your board reviewed the preliminary budget through a goal-alignment lens. You built the goal-to-budget crosswalk — one page, three columns, goals mapped to dollars. You held the public hearing, gathered community input, and produced written findings with specific directions to the superintendent.

The directions were clear. *Increase the early literacy allocation. Reallocate the non-goal program. Stay within the fiscal guardrails the board named in January.*

The superintendent had approximately four weeks to implement those directions and produce a revised budget draft.

March is where you check whether the revision reflects the directions.

This piece walks you through exactly what the March budget alignment review looks like — the agenda, the documents, the questions the board must answer, and the output the review should produce. It also connects the alignment review to the Progress Monitoring cycle that runs alongside it, because the crosswalk you built in February is not a static document. It is the baseline against which every future data check-in will be measured.

---

**Why the March alignment review matters more than the February review**

Let me be direct about something most boards get wrong.

Most boards think the February preliminary budget review is the main event. They spend the February meeting studying line items, asking the finance director detailed questions about account codes, and debating whether a specific line should be five percent higher or three percent lower. They leave the February meeting feeling like they have done their budget oversight work.

Then March comes. The superintendent produces the revised budget. And the board approves it without checking whether the February corrections were made — because the board has no formal mechanism for verifying alignment.

The March alignment review is that mechanism.

It matters more than the February review for one reason: the February review produced a direction, but the March review produces a decision. The February review asked *Does this budget align to our goals?* The March review asks *Did the superintendent respond to our direction, and is the revised budget ready for adoption?*

A board that conducts the February review without the March review is a board that gave directions but never checked whether they were followed. That is not governance. That is commenting.

A board that conducts both the February review and the March alignment review is a board that governs the budget cycle from beginning to end.

---

**What the March alignment review looks like**

Schedule a dedicated work session. Not a full board meeting with a dozen other agenda items. A focused ninety-minute session where every board member is present and the only topic is the revised budget.

Put three documents on the table.

**Document one: The board's February written findings.**

These are the specific directions the board gave the superintendent after the February public hearing. Not general observations — specific, actionable directions. *The early literacy allocation must increase by at least three percent. The administrative line must not exceed last year's total. The transportation allocation must be held flat pending the program review.*

If your board produced written findings in February, you have this document. If your board did not produce written findings, you are already behind — but the March review is the time to catch up.

**Document two: The revised budget draft.**

The superintendent's response to the February findings. This document should come with a cover memo that names each February direction and states whether the revised budget addresses it. The superintendent should not make the board hunt through line items to find the corrections.

**Document three: The updated goal-to-budget crosswalk.**

The crosswalk your board reviewed in February reflected the preliminary budget. Since the budget has changed, the crosswalk needs to change with it. The board should receive an updated crosswalk that maps the revised allocations to the adopted goals.

Here is the agenda for the ninety-minute work session.

**First thirty minutes: Walk the February findings one by one.**

The board president or finance committee chair reads each February finding aloud. For each finding, the board asks one question: *Is this correction reflected in the revised budget?*

If the answer is yes, the board confirms it in writing. The crosswalk should show the correction. The superintendent should name the line and the amount.

If the answer is no, the board asks why. Some corrections cannot be made within the available revenue. Some require contract renegotiations that cannot be completed before April. Some were based on assumptions about community input that the public hearing changed.

The board needs to hear the explanation, decide whether the constraint is legitimate, and either adjust the direction or give the superintendent a hard deadline for compliance.

I have watched this thirty-minute exercise reveal that a board's three most important February directions were never addressed — not because the superintendent ignored them, but because the board communicated them as suggestions in the meeting and never put them in writing. The March alignment review is often the moment a board discovers that its own communication was the problem.

**Second thirty minutes: Review the updated crosswalk.**

Once the February findings are reconciled, the board reviews the updated crosswalk. This is not a line-by-line audit. It is a goal-by-goal verification.

For each adopted goal, the board asks: *Does the revised budget still fund this goal at the level we intended?*

The February crosswalk showed the preliminary allocation for each goal. The March crosswalk should show the revised allocation. If the allocation increased — good, the board's direction was followed. If the allocation decreased, the board needs to know why and whether the decrease threatens the goal.

This is also the time to check for unintended consequences. Community input often causes the board to shift resources between programs. A shift that benefits one goal area can create a gap in another. The crosswalk review surfaces those gaps before the adoption vote, not after.

**Third thirty minutes: Confirm the timeline for the final budget draft.**

The work session should end with a specific date for the final budget draft delivery.

The superintendent needs to know, by the end of this session, when the final draft is due. I recommend four weeks before the adoption vote as the minimum. Three weeks is tight but workable. Two weeks is dangerous — it leaves the board insufficient time to review and the community insufficient time to see the final document before the vote.

Set the date. Document it in the meeting minutes. Hold the superintendent accountable to it.

---

**What the March alignment review should produce**

The output of the March work session should be three items.

**First, a completed updated crosswalk.** If the February crosswalk showed a misalignment and the superintendent corrected it, the March crosswalk should show alignment. If the superintendent could not correct it, the crosswalk should note the constraint and the board's decision on how to proceed.

**Second, a written confirmation that the board has reviewed the revised budget and finds it sufficiently aligned to the January budget direction and the February findings.** This confirmation can be a board resolution or a formal action item at the March board meeting. It signals to the superintendent and the community that the board has done its due diligence.

If the board finds the revised budget is not sufficiently aligned, the confirmation becomes a written list of remaining items the board expects to see resolved before the final budget draft. The superintendent gets a second deadline — shorter than the first — to address the remaining items.

**Third, a specific adoption vote date and pre-vote preparation schedule.** The board should leave the March work session knowing exactly when the adoption vote will occur, when the final budget draft will be delivered, and when the pre-vote Q&A session will be held.

---

**The connection to Progress Monitoring**

I want to name one more layer before I close.

The March budget alignment review does not exist in isolation. It runs alongside the second Progress Monitoring check-in of the 2028 cycle.

Your board held the first Progress Monitoring check-in in January. You reviewed baseline data, applied the three-decision protocol, and issued directed changes. February's data review tracked whether those directed changes produced movement.

March's Progress Monitoring check-in asks a different question: *Are the goals on track, and does the budget support the trajectory?*

This is where the crosswalk and the data converge.

If a goal is on track and the crosswalk shows adequate funding, the board confirms the strategy and moves on.

If a goal is on track but the crosswalk shows the funding was cut during the revision process, the board should investigate. The goal may be on track despite the funding reduction — in which case the board learns something about efficiency. Or the goal may appear on track only because the data has not yet reflected the funding change — in which case the board should prepare for a dip in the next data cycle.

If a goal is behind trajectory and the crosswalk shows adequate funding, the board asks about strategy — not funding.

If a goal is behind trajectory and the crosswalk shows the goal was underfunded relative to its priority, the board asks about itself — did the board set a priority it was not willing to fund?

The crosswalk gives the board the language to have these conversations. Without it, the board reviews data in one meeting and reviews the budget in another, never connecting the two. With it, every Progress Monitoring check-in is also a budget alignment check-in, and every budget alignment review is also a Progress Monitoring opportunity.

---

**Closing the bridge**

March is the bridge month in the governance cycle.

January was launch. February was the crosswalk. April is adoption. Between them sits March — the month where the board confirms that the January framework and the February work produced a budget that is ready for adoption.

The board that treats March as a quiet month — no major meetings, no heavy governance lifts — will discover in April that the February corrections were never made, the crosswalk was never updated, and the budget the board is being asked to approve does not align with the goals the board adopted in January.

The board that treats March as the alignment month — focused work sessions, targeted document reviews, disciplined follow-up on February directions — enters April knowing exactly where the budget stands and ready to adopt it with confidence.

Your board built the crosswalk. You gave the directions. Now verify the response.

That is what the March alignment review is for.
