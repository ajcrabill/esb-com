**From Printed Scorecard to Digital Dashboard — The 5-Stage Maturity Model for Board Data Review**

February 19, 2027

---

The ESB Framework doesn't prescribe a format for how boards should review goal progress data. It prescribes a cadence: regular, public, recorded intervals where the board examines progress toward its adopted goals. The Framework states: "The Board tracks its use of time and reports at least quarterly the percentage of Board-authorized public meetings where there is time focused on student outcomes."

The format is up to you. Printed page. Trifold packet. Hybrid brief plus online drill-down. Full public digital dashboard. Each format has trade-offs. And the right format for your board depends on where you are in your governance journey.

I've organized what I've seen across districts into a five-stage model. Not a prescription for every board to reach Stage 5. A framework for understanding where you are, what the next stage offers, and whether it's worth the investment.

**Stage 1: Printed One-Page Scorecard**

This is the starting point for most boards that adopt structured progress monitoring. One page. One goal per row. Baseline, current data, target, and a Red/Amber/Green status indicator. That's it.

**Council Bluffs CSD (IA)** runs this model. Their printed one-page scorecard has RAG status for each adopted goal, baseline versus current data, trend lines, and off-track flags. The board reviews it at every regular meeting — fifteen to twenty minutes. The format is simple enough that any board member can read it in under a minute. The agenda item is consistent enough that the superintendent knows exactly what data to prepare.

The advantage of Stage 1 is accessibility. No software. No login. No training. The board walks in, the scorecard is on the table, and the review happens. The limitation is depth. A single page can't show subgroup breakdowns, trend data over multiple years, or drill-downs into specific schools or programs. For the board just starting structured data review, that limitation is acceptable. You don't need sophisticated data tools to produce governance decisions. You need a consistent cadence and a clear format.

**Stage 2: Trifold with Decision Protocols**

Stage 2 keeps the printed format but expands the content and adds structure. Instead of a single page, the board receives a trifold packet that includes the scorecard plus decision protocols — what to do when a goal is off-track, escalation triggers, intervention flags.

**KCPS (MO)** uses a three-panel trifold with Check & Connect protocols. The first panel shows goal status. The second panel shows the data story — what's driving the trend. The third panel shows the protocol: what decision the board should make based on the data presented.

**Aldine ISD (TX)** uses a turnaround dashboard with off-track triggers and escalation flags. The board doesn't just see that a goal is Red. They see the threshold at which Red becomes a mandatory escalation conversation. The dashboard tells the board what to do, not just what to see.

The advantage of Stage 2 is decision readiness. The board doesn't have to figure out what the data means for governance — the format shows them. The limitation is still depth. Trifolds can hold more information than a single page, but they're still a fixed format. The board can't drill down into the data behind the dashboard.

**Stage 3: Hybrid (Printed Brief + Digital Source)**

Stage 3 recognizes that boards need both: a printed format for the meeting itself and a digital source for deeper analysis between meetings.

**Fairfax County (VA)** uses a two-page board data brief plus a Tableau Board View for drill-down. The printed brief covers key metrics and trend summaries. The Tableau view lets board members explore the data behind the brief — by school, by student group, by assessment.

The advantage of Stage 3 is flexibility. The board gets the summarised view during the meeting and can dig deeper when needed. The limitation is that it requires board members to actually use the digital source. If board members never open the Tableau view between meetings, the digital investment doesn't produce a governance return.

**Stage 4: Full Digital Strategic Plan Dashboard**

Stage 4 moves the primary data review experience online. The board (and the public) accesses goal progress data through a digital dashboard that updates regularly.

**Wichita USD 259 (KS)** has a public-facing strategic plan dashboard at usd259.org showing goal-level progress with visual indicators. The dashboard is updated regularly and accessible to the community. The board reviews the digital dashboard during meetings instead of a printed scorecard.

**Houston ISD** maintains a public performance dashboard showing district-level metrics across student achievement, climate, and finance — all accessible in real time.

The advantage of Stage 4 is transparency and depth. The community can see what the board sees. Data is current and drillable. The limitation is implementation cost. A good digital dashboard requires investment in data systems, maintenance, and training. Some boards invest in the dashboard but never train board members to use it effectively.

**Stage 5: Integrated Data Portals**

Stage 5 is where districts build comprehensive data portals that go beyond strategic plan metrics to include school-by-school data, demographic breakdowns, longitudinal trends, and operational metrics alongside academic ones.

**Dallas ISD** has a public-facing data portal with school-by-school metrics across multiple domains. **Denver Public Schools** has a strategic plan dashboard with real-time goal tracking.

At this stage, the data infrastructure is mature enough that the board's governance role shifts from "are we collecting the right data?" to "are we acting on the data we have?" The data is comprehensive. The question is what the board does with it.

**The insight that matters most**

Here's what I want every board to take away from this maturity model.

ESB coaching data across districts shows roughly 40% higher goal attainment in boards that run every-meeting data reviews compared to quarterly-only reviews. And here's the critical detail: the format doesn't predict the outcome. Council Bluffs uses a printed one-page scorecard — Stage 1 — and sees higher goal attainment than many boards with sophisticated digital dashboards. The format matters less than the consistency.

I've watched a board with a multi-million-dollar digital dashboard spend the first fifteen minutes of every data review trying to get the projection screen to work. I've watched a board with a printed one-page scorecard spend those same fifteen minutes making governance decisions about goal progress.

The ESB Framework insight is clear: the requirement is cadence, not format. Boards that review goal progress data at every regular meeting produce better outcomes than boards that review data quarterly, regardless of whether the data comes on paper or on a screen.

**How to choose your board's next stage**

If your board doesn't have a structured data review at all, start at Stage 1. Council Bluffs' model — one page, RAG status, fifteen minutes every meeting — is proven. You don't need to pilot a dashboard. You need to pilot a practice.

If your board has a consistent data review cadence but wants to go deeper, consider Stage 3 or 4. The printed brief gives you the meeting format you already trust. The digital layer gives you depth when you need it. The key is making sure the digital layer actually gets used — assign a board member to explore and report back, or dedicate ten minutes of a work session to letting board members practice with the tool.

If your board has the data infrastructure and the training budget, Stage 4 or 5 can serve both governance and community transparency. Wichita's model — a public dashboard that the board and community both access — builds trust alongside governance capacity.

But here's the rule I offer every board: never invest in the next format until the current cadence is consistent. A board that can't run a fifteen-minute data review on a one-page scorecard won't run a better data review on a $50,000 dashboard. The format amplifies the practice. It doesn't replace it.

The ESB Framework at effectiveschoolboards.com/framework/ provides the governance structure. Your progress monitoring cadence — every meeting, every goal, every data point — provides the consistency. The format is the vessel. Fill the vessel first.

Then decide whether the vessel needs to change.
