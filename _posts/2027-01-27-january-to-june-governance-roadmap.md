**January to June: Your Board's 2027 Governance Roadmap**

January 27, 2027

---

If you've been with me through this January arc, stop and look at what your board has accomplished in twenty-three days.

You held a strategic organizational meeting that aligned officers, committees, and direction. You locked in a month-by-month governance calendar that names what every meeting is designed to produce.

You adopted your 2027 goals — not as drafts, but as a public commitment with a hearing and a vote. You launched the superintendent evaluation cycle on a five-month timeline. You reflected on equity as a governance practice.

You conducted your first Progress Monitoring check-in — a structured thirty-minute data review that established your board's cadence for the year.

And Monday, you gave budget direction that connects your adopted goals to the February budget conversation.

That's more governance work in one month than most boards accomplish in a full year.

But January is the launch, not the finish. The governance year runs through June, and every month has a specific action that builds on the foundation you laid this month.

Here's your board's 2027 governance roadmap, month by month.

---

**February: First Data Review + Preliminary Budget**

February is where January's Goal Clarity meets February's budget reality.

Your January budget direction meets the superintendent's preliminary budget. The superintendent returns with a proposed budget shaped by the direction you gave in January — and your first full Progress Monitoring review establishes the cadence for the rest of the year.

The question for February: does the preliminary budget reflect the priorities you set in January? If a goal you adopted is not reflected in the budget's resource allocation, that's a February conversation, not a March crisis.

I've seen boards like **Council Bluffs (IA)** do this well. They publish a "Goal Alignment Summary" alongside the proposed budget — a one-page document that maps every major spending category to a board-adopted goal. The document makes the connection visible to the board, the superintendent, and the community. Your board can do the same thing with the Goal-to-Budget Alignment Worksheet from Monday's piece.

---

**March: Budget Alignment + Community Progress Report**

March is the budget alignment check. Your board reviews the proposed budget for alignment with adopted goals. Not every line item — the major expenditure categories and how they map to each goal.

This is also the month for your first community progress report. The goals you adopted in January, and the data you've reviewed in February, should be shared with the community. Not a press release — a formal report at a board meeting, with time for community questions.

The message to the community: *"Here are the goals we adopted. Here's the data we've reviewed. Here's how the proposed budget reflects those priorities."*

A board that communicates its Progress Monitoring results publicly is a board that builds trust with the community it serves. A board that only communicates internally is a board that treats governance as a closed process.

---

**April: Mid-Year Governance Check-In**

This is the month most boards skip — and it's the one that separates a Governance Team from a governance committee.

Your board's mid-year Governance check-in is your opportunity to review your own performance. Are you keeping the commitments you made during December's governance reset? Are your meetings structured the way you planned in January? Is your board functioning as a team?

I recommend a structured two-hour work session with three agenda items:

1. **Board self-assessment review** — Compare your current practices against the commitments you made in December. Which commitments are you keeping? Which need attention?
2. **Meeting effectiveness audit** — Review your last six meetings. Did each meeting produce the governance outcome it was designed to produce? If not, what needs to change?
3. **Governance calendar check** — Are you on track with your five governance milestones? If any milestone is slipping, what needs to happen to get back on track?

Districts like **Ladue (MO)** publish a "Governance Work Plan" that specifies what each board meeting is designed to produce. The April check-in uses that plan as its scorecard.

---

**May: Superintendent Evaluation Preparation**

The evaluation cycle you launched in January reaches its evidence-gathering phase. But there's a step here that most boards rush through — and rushing it is what damages the board-superintendent partnership.

By April, the board's evaluation committee should have reviewed the evidence collected across the year: goal progress data from every meeting, monitoring report quality, board-superintendent communication patterns. May is for the factual accuracy check.

The preliminary findings go to the superintendent for review before the full board sees them. Not for negotiation — for accuracy. *"The minutes show we adopted the math goal with a 52% target, but the data shows progress at 48%. Can you confirm the data source?"*

This single step eliminates the most common evaluation complaint: *"The board's evaluation doesn't reflect what actually happened."*

When the board treats the evaluation as a partnership tool rather than a judgment exercise, the Superintendent Partnership is strengthened, not strained. The evaluation becomes a forward-looking conversation about what the board and superintendent need to accomplish together next year — not a backward-looking report card.

---

**June: Superintendent Evaluation + 2027-28 Goal Preview**

June closes the cycle. The full board reviews and adopts the superintendent evaluation. The evaluation includes a bridge to next year's goal-setting — what did this year's performance tell you about next year's priorities?

At the same June meeting, preview the 2027-28 goal-setting process. What data will the board need this summer to prepare for fall goal-setting? What timeline makes sense for community input? What should the superintendent start preparing now?

The board that finishes June knowing what August and September require is a board that governs year-round. The board that finishes June and goes dark until August is a board that will spend September catching up.

**The January arc in perspective**

Here's what twenty-three days of focused governance work produces:

| Month | Governance Action | Connection to January |
|-------|------------------|-----------------------|
| **February** | First data review + preliminary budget | January's adopted goals and budget direction produce measurable checkpoints |
| **March** | Budget alignment + community progress report | The community sees January's goals translated into resource decisions |
| **April** | Mid-year governance check-in | The board reviews whether it's keeping the commitments it made in January |
| **May** | Superintendent evaluation preparation | The evaluation cycle that started in January reaches its evidence phase |
| **June** | Superintendent evaluation + goal preview | The year closes with a full governance cycle — Goal Clarity, Progress Monitoring, Governance Team, and Superintendent Partnership |

The difference between a board that governs and a board that meets is the difference between executing this roadmap and letting the calendar dictate your agenda.

You've executed January. The roadmap is set. The rest of 2027 is execution.
