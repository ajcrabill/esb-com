# How to Run a Board Self-Evaluation Work Session — the Actual Facilitation Playbook

Most boards that say they want to do a self-evaluation never actually do one.

Not because they lack the will. Because they lack a process.

Here's what I hear constantly: "AJ, we know we should evaluate how we function as a board. But what does that even look like? Do we hand out a survey? Talk about it for twenty minutes? Hire someone?"

The answer is: none of those, and all of those — in the right order.

Let me walk you through how to actually run a board self-evaluation work session. Not theory. The playbook I've used with dozens of boards across the country.

## Step One: The Pre-Work (3 Weeks Out)

The single biggest mistake boards make is trying to do a self-evaluation in one meeting with no preparation.

A good self-evaluation starts three weeks before the work session. Here's what needs to happen:

**Select your instrument.** I recommend a framework-aligned instrument that evaluates the board as a *team*, not as individuals. You're not rating whether Member Smith is a nice person. You're rating whether the board collectively:

- Sets clear goals that drive student outcomes
- Maintains consistent guardrails
- Holds itself accountable for both
- Communicates effectively with the superintendent
- Manages its own meeting time and behavior

**Distribute and collect responses individually.** Each member fills out the instrument on their own time. Anonymously. The facilitator — ideally an external one — collects and aggregates the results before the session.

**Set the frame.** Send a short memo with the session: "We are not here to critique individuals. We are here to improve how we function as a governance team. The goal is not comfort. The goal is clarity."

## Step Two: The Work Session (Half Day, Not One Hour)

If you're scheduling a self-evaluation for 45 minutes before lunch, cancel it. You're setting yourself up for failure.

A meaningful board self-evaluation work session needs three hours minimum. Half a day is ideal. Here's the agenda:

**0:00–0:30 — Norms and Framing**
Reiterate the purpose. Review the instrument. Remind everyone: this is about the board's performance, not personal grievances. Establish a norm that every member speaks, and every member listens without defensiveness.

**0:30–1:30 — Data Review**
Present the aggregated results. Don't read every question. Group them into themes:

- *Where do we score highest as a team?* (Celebrate these — brief but intentional)
- *Where's the biggest spread?* (These are the conversations you need to have — why do three members rate us a 4 and three rate us a 7?)
- *Where are we lowest?* (These are your improvement priorities)

**1:30–2:30 — Facilitated Conversation**
Here's the secret: the instrument is just the starter pistol. The real work is the conversation.

A good facilitator keeps the conversation focused on patterns, not personalities. "I notice we consistently score low on goal monitoring. What's getting in the way?" Not: "Susan, you never check the data dashboard."

**2:30–3:00 — Action Plan**
What are the top two or three governance priorities coming out of this session? Write them down. Assign owners. Set deadlines. Schedule a follow-up.

This is where most sessions fall apart. You do the hard work of honest reflection and then — nothing. No follow-through. No accountability.

Don't let that be you.

## Three Common Pitfalls (And How to Avoid Them)

**Pitfall #1: The Gripe Session**
Some boards turn self-evaluation into a complaint-fest about the superintendent, the district office, the state, the community.

*Fix:* The facilitator needs to redirect every time. "That's a valid concern. Let's write it down for another conversation. Right now, our focus is on what *we* can do better as a board."

**Pitfall #2: The Individual Score Debate**
"I gave myself a 3 because I think I could communicate better, but I know John gave himself a 6..."

*Fix:* Don't share individual scores. Ever. The instrument is anonymous for a reason. Discuss the *aggregate*.

**Pitfall #3: The "We're Fine" Trap**
Boards that score themselves universally high — with no variation — either genuinely are high-performing (rare) or are avoiding honest conversation (common).

*Fix:* A good instrument includes behavioral anchors that make it hard to inflate scores. "Our board has explicitly discussed and agreed upon decision-making norms" is harder to fudge than "our board communicates well."

## What Comes Out of a Good Session

A good self-evaluation work session produces three things:

1. **Clarity.** You know, with data, where your board's strengths and growth areas actually are — not where you *think* they are.

2. **Priorities.** Two or three specific governance improvements that the board commits to working on in the next 90 days.

3. **Accountability.** A follow-up date, a measurement method, and ownership for each priority.

This is not a feel-good exercise. This is how high-functioning boards stay high-functioning.
