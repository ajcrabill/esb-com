**Goal Adoption with Renewal — Three Years of Trend Data**

January 18, 2029

---

There is a difference between adopting goals because the calendar says so and adopting goals because the data tells you what to adopt. Most boards operate on the calendar. January arrives, so the board schedules a goal adoption meeting. The superintendent drafts goal language. The board adopts the goals. Done.

Your board has not been that board for two years. And in Year Three, the difference becomes even sharper.

**Year One was an act of creation.** Your board built a goal set from scratch — identified the student outcomes that mattered most, established baseline data, set annual targets for the first time.

**Year Two was an act of refinement.** You had one year of data. You knew which targets were achievable and which needed adjustment. The goal set got tighter.

**Year Three is an act of precision.** You have three years of trend data. And three years of trend data changes the conversation entirely.

---

### From Adoption to Renewal

The biggest shift between Year Two and Year Three is the question your board asks when it reviews the goal set.

In Year One and Year Two, the question was: "Which goals should we adopt?" The frame was forward-looking — almost speculative. You had limited historical data, so your board made its best judgment about appropriate targets.

In Year Three, the frame changes. Your board is not adopting a goal set. It is renewing a goal set — and renewal starts with a different question: "Here is what we have been doing. Here is the evidence. Based on that evidence, what should we do next?"

That shift changes how your board evaluates every proposed target. In Year One, your board set a reading proficiency target of 42 percent because 42 was the baseline and anything higher felt like progress. In Year Three, your board looks at a three-year trajectory — 42, 44, 46 — and asks: Is that trajectory fast enough? What would it take to accelerate? Do we need to change the strategy entirely?

The adoption frame says: "Here is what we will do." The renewal frame says: "Here is where we have been, here is the trajectory, and here is the adjustment that trajectory demands."

---

### The Goal Renewal Process — Three Steps in Year Three

The renewal process has three steps, and Year Three makes each more powerful because the data exists.

**Step one: Review the previous year's outcomes.** What did the 2028 goals produce? Were the annual targets met? Which goals need acceleration and which need to be sustained? In Year One, this step was aspirational — you had no prior data. In Year Three, you have actual outcome data from 2027, 2028, and fall 2028. You can see trend lines forming and distinguish between strategy problems that need redesign and implementation problems that need patience.

**Step two: Examine fall 2028 trend data.** Where are the trend lines moving? A single data point is a snapshot. Two data points show a direction. Three data points reveal a pattern. Your board can see: reading proficiency went from 42 to 44 to 46 percent. That is a pattern — the current strategy is working at a pace that may or may not hit the 48 percent spring target. Or your board can see: math proficiency went from 38 to 38 to 37 percent. That is also a pattern — and a target adjustment alone will not solve that problem.

**Step three: Assess whether annual targets need adjustment.** Based on two full years of data and a fall trajectory, do the 2029 targets need to be tighter, broader, or redirected? Here is the question: "In Year One, reading proficiency was at 42 percent. In Year Two, it moved to 44. Fall 2028 shows 46. The proposed spring target is 48. Should we hold at 48 or accelerate to 50 — and if accelerate, what additional resources does that require?" That is a data-informed conversation. It is only possible because your board has been collecting data for three years.

---

### The Goal Adoption Meeting in Year Three

The meeting itself has a specific protocol, and Year Three includes elements that were not possible in prior years.

The board president opens by naming the three-year trend for each proposed goal — not reading from a slide, but presenting the data with command: "Goal One is literacy proficiency. 2027 baseline was 42 percent. 2028 reached 44 percent. Fall 2028 data shows 46 percent. Proposed target for 2029 is 48 percent. Here is why that target is achievable given our current trajectory and resource allocation."

Each board member publicly affirms the adopted goals with a statement of commitment that references the data: "I support this goal because the three-year trend shows consistent improvement, and the 48 percent target reflects an ambitious but achievable acceleration. I commit to monitoring progress against this target at every data review this year."

The board produces a written goal adoption resolution naming the adopted goals, annual targets, monitoring schedule, and data review dates — published in the board packet and on the district website so the community sees the full commitment.

### A Note on Public Comment in Year Three

I want to address something directly. The roadmap previewed in December says January's goal adoption meeting has one agenda item: "No reports. No presentations. No public comment. Just goal adoption." Let me be explicit about what that means and what it does not mean.

It does not mean your board is adopting goals without community input. Year One's goal adoption included a public hearing with a 28-day comment window. The December organizational meeting established a community feedback window that precedes January's goal adoption meeting — the board set the timeline at the December meeting, the superintendent launched the community notification process, and the feedback window runs through early January.

What "no public comment" means is that the goal adoption meeting itself — the evening when the board reviews each goal, deliberates the targets, and votes — is not a public hearing. It is a board execution meeting. The community input phase has already happened. The board has collected feedback, reviewed community priorities, and adjusted goal language accordingly. The meeting where the board votes is the board's meeting — not a community hearing held at the same time as the board meeting.

By Year Three, the community has been part of this process three times. Your community knows what the goals are, knows how the board monitors progress, and knows when the spring budget hearing happens — which is the next public participation point. The goal adoption meeting is the board's opportunity to demonstrate that it has listened and is acting on what it heard.

If your board is in Year One and has never held a public hearing on goal adoption, go back and run that process. But if your board has been running this system for two full cycles, the community already knows what the goals are. January is execution.

---

### Common Pitfall in Year Three

The most common mistake boards make in Year Three is adding more goals. Three years of data reveals more problems — more student groups needing support, more indicators trending the wrong direction. The natural instinct is to expand the goal set to cover everything.

Do not do that.

More goals does not equal better governance. Better goals equals better governance. The discipline in Year Three is to keep the goal count manageable and tighten the targets. If reading proficiency shows steady improvement but math is flat, your board does not need a separate math goal. Your board needs to ask the superintendent: "The math trajectory is flat. What is the specific intervention, what does it cost, and how will we know by May whether it is working?" That is a monitoring question, not a goal-structure question.

Your board has had the same core goal set for three years. The goals work. The community knows them. The superintendent knows how to report on them. The system knows how to allocate resources to them. Hold the structure. Tighten the targets.

---

### What Three Years of Trend Data Makes Possible

Here is a concrete example. Your board has three years of reading proficiency data: 42, 44, and 46 percent. The superintendent presents draft language targeting 48 percent by spring 2029. The board president asks: "What would it take to reach 50 percent instead?"

That question — acceleration rather than acceptance — is only possible because your board has a trend line. Without three years of data, the question is speculative. With the trend, it is a strategic conversation about resource allocation, strategy effectiveness, and student potential.

The superintendent can respond: "Fifty percent would require an additional $200,000 in targeted intervention funding and a shift in instructional strategy in the three lowest-performing schools. Here is what that would look like and the projected timeline."

The board can then decide: invest the additional resources or hold the 48 percent target and evaluate the current strategy through the spring data review. Either answer is defensible — but the board is making the decision with evidence, not hope.

---

### Your Board Has a Gift Most Boards Never Receive

Your board has three years of trend data. That is a gift most school boards never acquire. Most boards start fresh every year — adopt goals in January, forget about them by March, and start over next January with no institutional memory of what the data actually showed.

Your board does not do that. You have the data, the trend lines, and a superintendent who knows how to report against established targets. You have a board president who knows how to facilitate a data-driven conversation. You have a community that knows what the board is governing toward.

Do not waste that gift on goals that look like last year's goals. Use it to adopt goals that reflect what the data actually tells you about what students need. Use it to set targets that stretch the district meaningfully — not because they feel right, but because the data says they are right.

January is one meeting. The preparation you do before it — trend data review, target assessment, board president one-on-one conversations, superintendent alignment — is what makes that meeting produce goals that guide your board through the rest of the year.

Your board is past the point of hoping goals work. You have three cycles of evidence. Use it.
