**Spring Community Engagement — What the Public Hearing Doesn't Cover**

April 21, 2027

---

The budget public hearing is the most visible community engagement event of the spring. The community arrives to learn about the budget and ask questions. The board listens, responds, and adopts.

But the public hearing serves a specific purpose: it gathers input on a specific decision — the budget. It's structured around a single document and a single timeline. It's not designed to uncover broad community priorities, surface concerns that aren't budget-related, or build relationships with community members who don't attend board meetings.

That's the gap that spring community engagement fills.

Spring is the season for broader engagement — listening sessions, stakeholder surveys, community conversations that are designed to uncover what the community wants from its schools and its board. The input gathered in spring becomes the foundation for the next goal-setting cycle, which starts in the fall.

**Why spring engagement matters**

The board that only engages the community at the public hearing is getting input on a single point in time — the budget. The board that engages the community in spring is getting input on a full arc of priorities, concerns, and aspirations.

Here's what spring engagement produces that the public hearing doesn't.

It produces input that isn't shaped by a specific decision. The community member who attends the public hearing comes with budget questions. The same community member, in a spring listening session six weeks later, might talk about the reading program, the transportation challenges, or the after-school opportunities their child doesn't have. The budget hearing frame narrows the conversation. Spring engagement broadens it.

It produces relationships with community members who don't attend board meetings. The board that schedules a listening session at a neighborhood school, a community center, or a faith institution reaches community members who won't drive to the district office at 7:00 PM on a Tuesday. Those community members have input that is just as valuable as the input from regular board meeting attendees.

It produces trust that sustains through tough decisions. The board that only appears at the public hearing is a board that only appears when it needs something — budget approval. The board that appears at neighborhood listening sessions, stakeholder meetings, and community conversations is a board that engages the community as a partner, not as a customer. When a tough decision comes — a school closure, a program reduction, a boundary change — the board that has built relationships through spring engagement has trust to draw on.

**Three spring engagement formats**

Not every board can run a full engagement campaign in spring. Budget season is demanding. But every board can choose one or two formats that fit their capacity.

**Format 1: The spring listening session.** This is the most direct format. The board schedules one or two evening sessions in different parts of the district — one in a neighborhood school, one in a community center. The session is structured around three questions:

1. What is working well in our schools that you want to see continue?
2. What concerns do you have about student progress that the board should be tracking?
3. What should the board consider as it sets goals for next year?

The board listens. Board members do not defend, explain, or respond to criticism during the session. The purpose is to hear — not to answer. The board president closes by naming what will happen with the input: the board will review it at the next board meeting, publish a summary, and incorporate it into the summer goal-setting work.

**Format 2: The stakeholder survey.** This is the lowest-effort, broadest-reach format. The board publishes a brief survey — five to seven questions, ten minutes to complete — that covers the same territory as the listening session. The survey is distributed through the district communication channels, posted on the board's website, and shared through parent and community organizations.

The survey should ask for priorities, not just satisfaction. A question like "how satisfied are you with the district's reading program" produces data that's hard to act on. A question like "which of these three areas should the board prioritize in next year's goal-setting — reading proficiency, math achievement, or post-secondary readiness" produces input that directly shapes decisions.

**Format 3: The stakeholder meeting.** This is the highest-engagement format. The board convenes a small group of stakeholder representatives — teachers, principals, parents, business leaders, community organization directors — for a ninety-minute conversation about district priorities. The group is small enough for meaningful discussion — ten to fifteen people — and diverse enough to represent multiple perspectives.

The board shares the year-end progress report highlights, names the key findings, and asks the stakeholder group: "Given this data, what should the board prioritize for next year?" The stakeholder group discusses, debates, and makes recommendations. The board leaves with input that has been tested by people who see the district from different angles.

**How to close the engagement loop**

Spring engagement produces input. What the community watches for is whether that input produces action.

The board should publish a summary of engagement findings within two weeks of the last engagement event. The summary names what the board heard, what themes emerged, and how the board plans to use the input in its summer and fall work.

The board should reference the engagement findings during the summer goal-setting work. When the board adopts next year's goals in the fall, the board president should name the connection: "This goal reflects input from our spring listening sessions. The community told us that reading proficiency by third grade is the priority, and this goal responds to that input."

The community that sees its input reflected in the board's decisions is a community that will participate in the next engagement opportunity. The community that gives input and never sees it acknowledged is a community that has learned that board engagement is performative.

**The connection to next year's goals**

Spring engagement feeds directly into the next goal-setting cycle. The input the board gathers in April and May becomes the data — alongside the year-end progress report — that informs the fall's goal adoption.

The board that gathers community input in spring enters the fall with a head start on goal setting. The board that doesn't starts from zero — and often ends up adopting goals that reflect the board's priorities without reflecting the community's.

The public hearing is one form of community engagement. Spring is the season for the rest of it. The community input that shapes next year's goals is gathered now — not in September, when the board is already in motion.
