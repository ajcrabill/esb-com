**Your January Board Agenda — Draft It Before the New Year Starts**

December 28, 2026

**Series:** Year-End Governance Reset & 2027 Foundation (Dec 2026)

---

Here's a question for the quiet days between Christmas and New Year's.

What's on your January board meeting agenda?

If your answer is "I haven't thought about it yet," you're not alone. Most boards don't think about their January agenda until the week of the meeting. They walk into January with a consent agenda, a few routine items, and an organizational meeting that eats up most of the time.

But here's what I want you to consider: your January board meeting isn't just another meeting. It's the meeting that sets the tone for your entire year.

If the January agenda is entirely routine — committee assignments, officer elections, calendar approval — that's a signal. It tells the community that this board is focused on process, not progress. It tells the superintendent that this board is here to manage, not to lead. It tells the board itself that January is for logistics, not for governance.

If the January agenda is strategic — goals, data, calendar, evaluation — that's a different signal. It tells everyone that this board is ready to govern from day one.

The difference between those two meetings is drafting the agenda in December instead of January.

## Why the January Agenda Matters More Than You Think

Three reasons.

**Reason 1: January is the organizational meeting.**

It's when you elect officers, appoint committee chairs, and adopt the meeting calendar. Those are necessary functions. But they're not governance. They're the administrative infrastructure that makes governance possible.

If your entire January meeting is organizational business, you've spent the first meeting of the year on logistics. The first signal you send to the community is: we're here to manage ourselves. Not to serve students.

A strategic agenda front-loads the governance work and handles the organizational business as a secondary item. Let the community see first what this board intends to accomplish in 2027. Handle committee assignments after.

**Reason 2: January sets the tone.**

The first meeting of the year tells everyone — the community, the staff, the superintendent — what this board values.

If the superintendent's report is the main event and the board's goals are an afterthought, the message is clear: the board follows the administration. If the board's goal presentation is the main event and the superintendent's report supports it, the message is different: the administration supports the board's direction.

I've seen boards that understand this. One board I worked with starts every January meeting with a fifteen-minute segment called "Our Commitment to the Community." The board president reads the board's goals for the year. The superintendent presents the baseline data. The board takes questions from the public. That fifteen minutes communicates more about what the board values than the entire rest of the meeting.

**Reason 3: January is when goals should launch.**

If you've been following this December arc, you've done the work. You've collected the three data points. You've drafted your 2027 goals. You've reviewed your superintendent evaluation framework. You've completed your board self-assessment. You've reflected on your between-meeting governance habits.

January is where all that work becomes visible.

Your January board meeting should be the moment when the board presents its 2027 goals to the community. Not as a draft. As a commitment. Here's what we're setting out to achieve. Here's how we'll measure it. Here's when we'll report back.

If you wait until February to present the goals, you've lost January. And January is when the community is paying attention.

## The January Agenda Template

Here's what a strategic January board agenda looks like. Three sections. One meeting. A clear signal that this board governs.

### Section 1: Required Organizational Business

Yes, you need to elect officers. Yes, you need to appoint committee chairs. Yes, you need to approve the meeting calendar. Handle this at the top or bottom of the meeting — but contain it. Fifteen minutes, maximum.

Here's my recommendation: schedule a fifteen-minute pre-meeting for reorganization. Elect officers, approve the calendar, appoint committees. Then start the main meeting with the governance work. The organizational business is necessary. It's not the main event.

### Section 2: Goal-Setting Launch

This is the main event. Thirty to forty-five minutes. Three parts:

**Part 1: Goal presentation.** The board presents its 2027 goals to the community. One page. Clear language. Specific targets. The superintendent provides the baseline data for each goal. The community sees both: where the board is going and where the district is starting.

**Part 2: Data review.** Present the data that informed the goals. Show the trends. Show the gaps. Show the governance health indicators. The community should understand not just what the board decided, but why.

**Part 3: Public input.** Open the floor for questions and comments on the goals. This isn't a performative exercise. Listen to what the community says. If there's a concern that the board hadn't considered, take it seriously. The goals can be refined in February after the board incorporates feedback.

### Section 3: Year Preview

End the meeting with a look ahead. The board's 2027 governance calendar:

- **January:** Goals adopted. Evaluation framework confirmed. Organizational business.
- **February:** First data check-in. Budget preview.
- **March:** Community progress report. Superintendent evaluation preparation begins.
- **April/May:** Superintendent evaluation.
- **June:** Mid-year goal review.
- **September:** New year data review.
- **December:** Year-end governance reset.

Publishing this calendar at the January meeting tells the community what to expect. It tells the staff what deadlines to prepare for. It tells the board what commitments it's made.

## The December Advantage

Here's the key insight: if you draft your January agenda in December, you have time to get it right. To think about what the meeting should communicate. To prepare the materials. To brief the board members. To coordinate with the superintendent.

If you draft it in January — the week before the meeting — you react. You fill the agenda with whatever is available. You default to routine because there's no time for strategic thinking.

The difference between a January agenda drafted in December and one drafted in January is the difference between a board that governs and a board that meets.
