**The Community Year-End Governance Report — How the Board Closes the Fall Governance Cycle for the Public**

December 8, 2027

---

The year-end governance review is complete. The board assessed what the fall governance cycle produced — the goals that were monitored, the data reviews that were held, the decisions that were made, and the adjustments that were directed.

Now the board owes the community the same information.

Most boards do not publish a year-end governance report. The board met. The board governed. The community was in the room for the public portions of the meeting. If the community wanted to know what the board accomplished, the community could read the meeting minutes.

That assumption is wrong. And it costs boards the community trust they need for the budget hearing, the bond election, and the next goal adoption cycle.

**What the community needs to know**

The community does not need to know every decision the board made in the fall. The community needs to know three things:

**One: What the board committed to.** The goals the board adopted in January. The targets the board set. The timeline the board established. The community needs to be reminded of what the board said it would do — because most community members do not follow board meetings closely enough to remember the January goal adoption from eleven months ago.

**Two: What the data showed.** For each goal, the year-end report should show the baseline, the October and November data points, the trend direction, and the board's decision. Did the board accept the trajectory, direct a change, or request more information? The community needs to see that the board was monitoring progress and making decisions — not just receiving reports.

**Three: What the board will do next.** The 2028 goal adoption timeline. The budget development calendar. The superintendent evaluation timeline. The January through June governance calendar. The community needs to know what the board is working on next and when the community can provide input.

**What the year-end report should look like**

A governance year-end report does not need to be long. I recommend a three-page document:

Page one: The cover page with the board's purpose statement, the governance year summary (what the board committed to, what the board reviewed, what the board decided), and the board president's signature or a brief introduction.

Page two: The goal-by-goal summary. One row per goal. The goal statement, the baseline, the October data point, the November data point, the trend direction (up, flat, down), and the board's decision (accepted, directed change, or requested more information). The board's year-end governance review findings are summarized in the final column.

Page three: The forward-looking page. The 2028 timeline — goal adoption (January), budget development (January through April), superintendent evaluation (February through May), data reviews (quarterly), and community engagement touchpoints. The community should see when the next opportunity for input is and how to provide it.

**How to publish and distribute**

The year-end governance report should be published in December, before the holiday break. The community that receives the report in December enters January informed about what the board accomplished and what the board is working on next.

I recommend publishing the report through three channels:

One. Published on the district website on the board's page. A permanent link that the board can reference throughout the spring.

Two. Distributed through the district's email newsletter or communication platform. A brief summary with a link to the full report.

Three. Presented at the final board meeting of the calendar year. The board president reads a brief summary and invites community questions. The presentation takes ten minutes and signals that the board values community awareness of its governance work.

**What the board should do this week**

Three actions:

One. Draft the three-page year-end governance report using the format above. The year-end governance review findings provide the content for page two. The fall governance calendar provides the content for page three.

Two. Publish the report on the district website and distribute through the district communication channels before the holiday break.

Three. Present the report summary at the December board meeting. The board president delivers a five-minute summary and invites community questions.

The community year-end governance report is not a compliance document. It is a trust-building document. The board that publishes it enters January with a community that understands what the board accomplished, what the board is working on next, and why the board's governance work matters.
