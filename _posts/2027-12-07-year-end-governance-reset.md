**The Year-End Governance Reset — Five Things Your December Board Meeting Must Accomplish**

December 7, 2027

---

Let me be direct about this. Do not let your board go dark in December.

I have seen the pattern a hundred times. The board holds its December meeting, publishes the minutes, and does not meet again until the third week of January. By then, the organizational meeting is rushed. The superintendent evaluation process is already behind. The 2028 goal conversation starts at a disadvantage.

The board that governs through December enters January with momentum. The board that goes dark in December enters January catching up.

The solution is a year-end governance reset — a structured December board meeting or work session that closes the 2027 governance cycle and opens the 2028 cycle on purpose, not by default.

Here are the five things your December board meeting must accomplish.

---

**One: Close the 2027 governance cycle.**

This is not a review of district operations. That is the superintendent's job. This is a review of board performance against the commitments the board made in January.

Did the board achieve what it set out to achieve in its January strategic organizational meeting? Did the board govern at the level it committed to? Did the board hold itself accountable for the self-evaluation priorities it identified in September?

The September self-evaluation was a diagnostic. The December close is the final grade.

Here is what that looks like in practice. Use the self-evaluation results from the full governance year — five domains across twelve months — to produce a one-page year-end governance summary. The document names what improved, what did not, and what carries into 2028. The board approves the summary at the December meeting. The summary becomes part of the public record.

I have watched boards in **Council Bluffs (IA)** do this. They publish a one-page year-end governance summary alongside the district's annual report. The document names the goals, the progress data, the board decisions, and the 2028 priorities. The community sees what the board is accountable for — which is the entire point.

Your board can do the same thing. The format is simple: a single page with four sections — goals adopted, progress data summary, board decisions made, and governance priorities for the new year. That is it.

---

**Two: Preview the 2028 goal-setting process.**

The board that answers the right questions in December does not enter February wondering what to do next.

Here are the questions the board should answer at the December meeting.

What data will the board need this winter to prepare for spring goal-setting? If your board adopted 2027 goals, the question is what the 2027 outcomes tell you about the 2028 targets. If your board is working through a baseline-gathering quarter, the question is whether the baseline data is complete enough for January adoption.

What timeline makes sense for community input? February through April is the traditional window, but the best boards start in January by publishing a draft goal set for community review before the February meeting. That gives the community sixty days to respond before the final adoption in March or April.

What should the superintendent start preparing now? The superintendent cannot build a responsive 2028 goal set in January if the board has not communicated the timeline and data requirements in December. The board that waits until January to tell the superintendent what it needs has added three weeks of unnecessary delay.

Document the answers to these three questions in a board action item at the December meeting. The superintendent leaves December knowing exactly what the board expects and when.

---

**Three: Set the January strategic organizational meeting agenda.**

Here is a test of your board's governance maturity. Ask every board member what the January organizational meeting is designed to produce. If the answers include "electing officers" and "setting the meeting calendar," your board is treating the organizational meeting as a ceremony.

A strategic organizational meeting produces more than officers and a calendar. It produces:

- Officer elections that are tied to a governance skillset, not a rotating obligation
- Committee assignments that reflect the board's strategic priorities for 2028, not last year's roster
- A governance calendar that names what every meeting is designed to produce through June
- Committed meeting dates through the first quarter — not "we will figure that out later"
- A board governance commitment that names how this board will operate differently in 2028 than it did in 2027

December is the month to decide what the January meeting will produce. The board president should bring a draft agenda to the December meeting for discussion. The board should leave December knowing what January 2028 will launch, not wondering what will happen in the hallway before the meeting.

A board that plans its January organizational meeting in December walks into January ready to govern. A board that plans its organizational meeting in the hallway before the meeting walks into January having already wasted its first governance opportunity of the year.

---

**Four: Begin the superintendent evaluation cycle.**

Every board I have coached that produces a meaningful superintendent evaluation starts the process early. Not in April. Not in May. In December.

The evaluation cycle that closes in June starts in December. Here is the sequence.

First, the board's evaluation committee — a small group of board members designated for this purpose — determines the evidence-gathering process. What data sources will the board use? What artifacts will the board review? What stakeholder input, if any, will be gathered?

Second, the committee establishes the timeline. The evidence-gathering period runs December through March. The factual accuracy check — where the superintendent reviews the draft evaluation for factual errors — falls forty-five days before the final evaluation date. The committee presents the draft evaluation to the full board for review. The board conducts the formal evaluation conversation and votes on the final evaluation in June.

Third, the committee communicates the evaluation framework to the superintendent before the new year. No surprises. The superintendent knows what the board will evaluate, what evidence the board will use, and what the timeline looks like.

The board that starts the evaluation process in December is prepared. The board that starts in April is rushed. The board that starts in May is reactive.

Fifteen years of coaching boards has shown me the same pattern every time. The boards that produce meaningful superintendent evaluations are the boards that start the evaluation conversation before the calendar demands it.

---

**Five: Publish a year-end governance report to the community.**

This is the capstone of the year-end reset.

A year-end governance report is not a press release. It is not a list of achievements written by the communications department. It is a governance document that tells the community what the board did with its authority and responsibility for the year.

The report should answer three questions. What goals did the board adopt? What progress did the district make toward those goals? What are the board's priorities for the coming year?

The format can be a single page. The content should be written for the community, not for the board. Use plain language. Include the data. Cite the board's decisions.

Publish the report at the December board meeting. Put it on the district website. Include it in the meeting packet. The community should not have to guess whether its board is governing. The report makes the answer visible.

A board that publishes a year-end governance report is a board that accepts accountability publicly. A board that does not is a board that treats governance as an internal process that the community does not need to see.

---

Here is the bottom line.

December is not the end of the governance year. It is the pivot point between the year that was and the year that will be. The board that executes these five items at its December meeting closes 2027 with integrity and opens 2028 with momentum.

The board that skips December's governance reset will spend January wondering why the year already feels behind.
