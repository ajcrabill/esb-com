**The Year-End Governance Reset — Five Things Your December Board Meeting Must Accomplish**

December 7, 2027

---

Let me be direct about this. Do not let your board go dark in December.

I have seen the pattern a hundred times. The board meets in December, publishes the minutes, and does not meet again until late January. By then the organizational meeting is rushed, the superintendent evaluation is already behind, and the goal conversation starts at a disadvantage.

The board that governs through December enters January with momentum. Here are the five things your December meeting must accomplish.

---

**One: Close the 2027 governance cycle.**

This is not a review of district operations. That is the superintendent's job. This is a review of board performance against the commitments the board made in January.

The September self-evaluation was a diagnostic. December is the final grade. Use the full governance year — five domains across twelve months — to produce a one-page year-end governance summary. The document names what improved, what did not, and what carries into 2028. The board approves it at the December meeting. It becomes part of the public record.

Publish that summary alongside the district's annual report. The community sees what the board is accountable for — which is the entire point.

---

**Two: Preview the 2028 goal-setting process.**

The board that answers the right questions in December does not enter February wondering what to do next. Answer these three questions at the December meeting.

What data will the board need this winter for spring goal-setting? If your board adopted 2027 goals, the question is what those outcomes tell you about 2028 targets. If your board is still in a baseline-gathering quarter, the question is whether the data is complete enough for January adoption.

What timeline makes sense for community input? February through April is traditional. The best boards start in January by publishing a draft goal set for community review — sixty days of feedback before final adoption.

What should the superintendent start preparing now? The superintendent cannot build a responsive 2028 goal set in January if the board has not communicated the timeline and data requirements in December.

Document the answers in a board action item. The superintendent leaves December knowing exactly what the board expects and when.

---

**Three: Set the January strategic organizational meeting agenda.**

A strategic organizational meeting produces more than officers and a calendar. It produces:

- Officer elections tied to governance skillset, not rotation
- Committee assignments reflecting 2028 priorities, not last year's roster
- A governance calendar that names what every meeting produces through June
- Committed dates through the first quarter
- A board governance commitment — how this board will operate differently in 2028

December is the month to decide what the January meeting will produce. The board president should bring a draft agenda to the December meeting for discussion. The board that plans its January organizational meeting in December walks into January ready to govern.

---

**Four: Begin the superintendent evaluation cycle.**

Every board I have coached that produces a meaningful superintendent evaluation starts the process in December, not April.

The evaluation cycle that closes in June starts in December. The board's evaluation committee determines the evidence-gathering process. What data sources will the board use? What stakeholder input will be gathered? The committee establishes the timeline: evidence gathering runs through March, factual accuracy check forty-five days before the final date, formal evaluation conversation in June.

The committee communicates the framework to the superintendent before the new year. No surprises. The superintendent knows what will be evaluated, what evidence will be used, and what the timeline looks like.

The board that starts the evaluation process in December is prepared. The board that starts in April is rushed.

---

**Five: Publish a year-end governance report to the community.**

This is the capstone of the year-end reset.

A year-end governance report is not a press release. It is a governance document that tells the community what the board did with its authority for the year. Answer three questions: What goals did the board adopt? What progress did the district make? What are the board's priorities for the coming year?

The format is a single page. The content is written for the community — plain language, the data, the board's decisions. Publish it at the December meeting, put it on the district website, include it in the meeting packet.

A board that publishes a year-end governance report accepts accountability publicly. A board that does not treats governance as an internal process the community does not need to see.

---

Here is the bottom line.

December is not the end of the governance year. It is the pivot point. The board that executes these five items closes 2027 with integrity and opens 2028 with momentum.

The board that skips December's governance reset will spend January wondering why the year already feels behind.
