**The Mid-Year Governance Check-In — Did Your February Adjustments Work?**

April 12, 2027

---

February's governance team check-in asked a question: are we governing the way we committed to? It was an early-warning system. The board identified issues — meeting structure needed tightening, board member participation was uneven, calendar milestones were slipping, communication discipline needed reinforcement, governance model alignment had gaps — and made adjustments.

April's mid-year governance check-in asks a different question: did those adjustments work?

That shift — from early warning to performance review — changes everything about how the check-in is conducted. February's check-in was diagnostic. It identified problems and set direction. April's check-in is evaluative. It takes the February findings, the adjustments the board committed to, and the five months of governance practice that followed, and asks whether the adjustments produced improvement.

**Why April's check-in is different**

February's check-in had two months of practice and three months of resolutions to evaluate. The board had been in session since January, but the governance year was still taking shape. February's adjustments were hypotheses: if we tighten the meeting structure, if we distribute participation more evenly, if we reinforce the calendar discipline, the board will govern more effectively.

April's check-in has five months of practice, five months of data, and a full budget cycle behind it. February's hypotheses have been tested. The board knows which adjustments worked and which didn't. The board knows which governance indicators improved and which stayed flat or declined.

I've watched a board conduct its April check-in and discover that three of the five February adjustments produced measurable improvement, one produced no change, and one made things worse. The board didn't treat that as failure. It treated it as information — and used it to make structural changes for the final two months of the governance year.

I've watched another board conduct its April check-in and discover the same problems it identified in February. The meeting structure was still too packed. The same two board members were still carrying the governance work. The calendar milestones were still slipping. The board identified these issues in February, committed to adjustments, and didn't follow through. By April, the problem wasn't the governance practice — it was the board's failure to implement its own decisions.

That second board needed a different kind of check-in. Not a diagnostic. An intervention.

**The five indicators — April edition**

The same five indicators from February apply. But April's questions are sharper because the board has more to evaluate.

**Indicator 1: Meeting structure.** February's question: are meetings producing what the calendar promised? April's question: have meetings improved since February's adjustments? A board that identified a meeting structure problem in February and the problem persists in April needs a structural change, not another adjustment. The board should ask: did we try to fix a schedule problem when we actually had a process problem? Did we adjust the agenda format, or did we just change the times?

**Indicator 2: Board member contribution.** February's question: is every member participating? April's question: has the load balanced or concentrated further? By April, the participation imbalance is visible in the record — who asked questions at each meeting, who requested data, who followed up on superintendent commitments. The board that identifies a concentration problem in February and names the same problem in April has a board culture issue, not a scheduling issue.

**Indicator 3: Calendar integrity.** February's question: are milestones on track? April's question: did we hit every milestone we set? The January-through-April calendar had specific deadlines — goal adoption by January 15, budget review by February 15, alignment review by March 15, public hearing by April 15. The April check-in evaluates whether each milestone was met and, if not, whether the delay was caused by a governance practice problem or an external factor.

**Indicator 4: Communication discipline.** February's question: is the board speaking with one voice? April's question: has the protocol held under budget pressure? The spring budget cycle — with its public hearing, community questions, and media coverage — is the most intense test of communication discipline. A board that maintained one voice through the budget cycle has proven its protocol works. A board that fractured under pressure needs to rebuild before the next test — which is the superintendent evaluation in June.

**Indicator 5: Governance model alignment.** February's question: does every member define governance the same way? April's question: has five months of execution deepened or eroded alignment? The board that entered the year with a shared understanding of governance — that the board governs policy and outcomes while the superintendent manages operations — should emerge from the spring budget cycle with that understanding reinforced. If the spring cycle eroded the board's alignment — if members started drifting into operational decisions or personal agendas — the April check-in is the moment to name the drift and correct it.

**How to run the April check-in**

The April check-in is a ninety-minute board work session, not a twenty-minute agenda item. Here's the structure.

| Time | Activity | Purpose |
|------|----------|---------|
| 10 min | Review February findings | Read the February check-in outcomes and the adjustments the board committed to |
| 20 min | Five-indicator evaluation | Board evaluates each indicator individually using April's sharper questions |
| 15 min | February-to-April comparison | For each indicator: did the adjustment work? Improved, unchanged, or declined |
| 20 min | Deep-dive on unchanged and declined indicators | What about the adjustment didn't work, and what structural change is required |
| 15 min | Action plan for May–June | Specific commitments with owners and deadlines for the final two months |
| 10 min | Documentation and close | Board records the April outcomes and schedules the May check-in |

The most important part of the April check-in is the February-to-April comparison. The board that doesn't compare its April findings to its February baseline is evaluating its current state without knowing whether it's improving.

**What the April check-in should produce**

The April check-in should produce three things.

First, a clear assessment of which February adjustments worked. The board should be able to name: these three adjustments improved governance practice, these two adjustments were insufficient and need structural changes.

Second, a specific action plan for May and June. The board commits to specific adjustments for the final two months of the governance year, with named owners and deadlines.

Third, a baseline for the year-end check-in. The board will conduct a year-end governance evaluation in June or July. The April findings — and the board's response to them — become the baseline for that evaluation. The board that identifies a problem in April and fixes it by June can report year-over-year improvement. The board that identifies a problem in April and doesn't fix it reports a problem that persisted through the full governance year.

The February check-in was the early warning. The April check-in is the performance review. The board that treats it that way enters May with a governance practice that has been tested, measured, and adjusted.
