**Your December Plan Means Nothing Without a January Follow-Through**

January 6, 2027

---

Here's the uncomfortable truth about the December 30 piece you just read: the January meeting agenda template I gave you is worthless if you don't follow through on it.

I don't mean worthless as in "less valuable." I mean literally zero impact. A template printed and filed is no different from a blank sheet of paper. The value isn't in the document. It's in what happens when your board uses it.

And this is where I see the biggest gap between boards that tell me "we have a plan" and boards that actually execute one.

## The Problem with Templates

Templates are seductive. They make you feel like you've made progress when you haven't. You download the January meeting agenda template, you look at it, you think "yes, this is exactly what we need," and you feel a small satisfaction — the satisfaction of having done something productive.

But the template didn't change anything. Your January meeting is still going to be the same default agenda unless someone — a specific person — takes the template and uses it to build the actual agenda, send it to the superintendent, and ensure it gets followed.

I've seen boards collect more templates than they use. Budget alignment worksheets. Goal-setting frameworks. Data dashboard templates. One-page community communication guides. They sit in a shared drive folder called "2027 Governance Resources" that no one has opened since January 3.

The difference between boards that improve and boards that collect resources is follow-through.

## The Follow-Through Fail

Let me describe a specific scenario I've watched play out more times than I can count.

December. A board sets three clear goals for 2027. They define the metrics. They establish a data cadence. They create a timeline for the first quarter. They feel great leaving the work session.

January. The board holds its first meeting. The agenda is full — always full in January. Reorganization. Committee assignments. Public comment about a bus route change. Superintendent report covering seventeen items.

At the end of the meeting, the board chair says: "We're going to start reviewing our goal progress at the next meeting."

That board will not review goal progress at the next meeting. They'll have a different reason — a personnel issue, a budget adjustment, a state mandate. And they'll push it again. And again. By March, they'll realize they've had three meetings without a single data review, and they'll say: "Let's pick this up in the summer when things calm down."

Things never calm down.

The follow-through failure isn't a planning problem. It's a structural problem. The board didn't build follow-through into its operating system.

## Three Follow-Through Structures That Work

I've tested dozens of follow-through mechanisms with boards across the country. Here are the three that actually work.

### Structure 1: The Standing Strategic Agenda Item

Most boards have a "committee reports" item and a "superintendent report" item and a "board member comments" item. They don't have a "our goals" item.

Add it. Permanently. Every board meeting agenda should have a standing item called "Progress Against 2027 Goals." Position it early in the meeting — after call to order and before public comment.

The format is simple: the superintendent presents the current data for each of your three to five metrics. The board asks questions. The board determines whether any course correction is needed. The discussion is recorded in the minutes.

This isn't complicated. It's just consistent. And consistency is what separates execution from intention.

### Structure 2: The Pre-Meeting Accountability Brief

Here's a tool I give every board chair I coach.

Twenty-four hours before each board meeting, the chair sends a brief email to the superintendent and the board. Three bullet points:

- What are we tracking this meeting that moves us toward our 2027 goals?
- What data will we review?
- What decision do we need to make?

That's it. Three bullets. The chair spends five minutes writing it. The superintendent spends five minutes reading it. The board spends one minute scanning it before the meeting.

The effect is transformative. Because now everyone walks into the meeting knowing what the strategic work is. The agenda doesn't bury it under process. The chair protects it.

### Structure 3: The Meeting-End Commitment

I learned this one from a board chair in Ohio, and it's the simplest structure of all.

At the end of every board meeting, before adjournment, the chair asks one question: "What commitments did we make tonight that we need to follow up on?"

Someone reads the list aloud. The chair assigns owners and deadlines on the spot. The commitments are recorded in the minutes and tracked in a simple spreadsheet.

The chair in Ohio told me this single practice eliminated the "we discussed this last month but never followed up" problem entirely. Because now, following up on commitments is part of the regular order. It's not extra work. It's just how the board operates.

## Why These Three Structures Work — It's the Framework

Here's what I haven't told you yet. These three structures aren't just clever ideas I collected from coaching. They're the operating components of a proven system — and there's real data behind it.

The Effective School Boards governance calendar prescribes four milestones that create an unbroken accountability cycle:

1. **Quarterly board self-evaluations** — Your board scores itself every three months, in public, using a standard instrument. This is the board version of "the test is on Friday." It forces you to stay focused.
2. **Time-use tracking and reporting** — You measure how much of your meeting time actually focuses on student outcomes and you report that number publicly at least quarterly. What gets measured gets protected.
3. **Annual goal adoption** — You set specific, measurable student outcome goals tied to community vision. This happens in January, right after the organizational meeting.
4. **Superintendent evaluation tied to goal progress** — The superintendent is evaluated 100% on whether students are meeting the adopted goals. No more subjective ratings. No more personality assessments.

Notice what this system does that individual structures can't. It makes follow-through automatic. You don't need willpower to remember to check goals in February — it's on the calendar. You don't need a special motion to conduct a self-evaluation — it's already scheduled. The calendar creates the discipline that individual resolutions can't sustain.

Multiple studies — including Ford's 2013 dissertation on board governance and student achievement and the Lighthouse Inquiry into board-superintendent team behaviors — confirm that boards with structured annual cycles consistently outperform those that operate meeting-to-meeting.

## Where This Is Working Right Now

Let me give you real districts.

### Council Bluffs, Iowa

Council Bluffs Community School District starts every calendar year the same way — with a January goal adoption session right after the organizational meeting. Then they check in quarterly. March: board self-evaluation. April: first data review against goals. June: second self-evaluation and budget alignment. The cycle repeats all year.

The difference isn't that Council Bluffs has better board members. It's that they have a better system. The calendar does the work that willpower alone never can.

### Kansas City Public Schools

Under this same governance calendar discipline — quarterly self-evaluations, time-use tracking, goal-aligned evaluations — KCPS doubled the percentage of students who are literate and numerate and achieved full accreditation for the first time in decades.

That's not a coincidence. That's what happens when a board follows through for more than one meeting, more than one quarter, more than one year. The calendar creates the structure. The board provides the discipline. Students get the results.

### DeSoto ISD, Texas

When I served as state-appointed Conservator in DeSoto, the district had F ratings in academics, finance, and governance. Under the governance calendar discipline — structured quarterly reviews, public time-use reporting, goal-aligned evaluations — DeSoto went to all A and B ratings with double-digit literacy gains.

Education Week documented the turnaround in March of 2023. The full study is available on the ESB resources page.

These districts didn't need better templates. They needed a calendar that forced follow-through, and the discipline to stick to it.

## What Follow-Through Does for Your January Meeting

Let me show you what these three structures — grounded in a real governance calendar — do to your January board meeting.

You walk in with the January meeting agenda template from December 30. You've added the "Progress Against 2027 Goals" standing item — positioned second on the agenda, right after call to order.

The chair sent the accountability brief the day before. The superintendent knows what data to bring. The board knows what decisions need to be made.

The meeting runs its course. As it winds down, the chair asks: "What commitments did we make tonight?" The answer is clear: we adopted three 2027 goals, we reviewed baseline data for each one, we directed the superintendent on budget priorities, and we scheduled the February data check-in. The assignments are clear. The follow-up is recorded.

That's a board meeting that moved the district forward. And it happened because follow-through was built into the structure, not left to individual willpower.

## The Cost of Not Following Through

There's a hidden cost to failing to follow through that most boards don't consider.

When your board sets goals in December and doesn't review them in January, you're not just losing time. You're losing credibility. Your community noticed. Your superintendent noticed. And most importantly, your board members noticed — they learned that the goals aren't real.

The next time you set goals, they'll be less ambitious. Because everyone in the room knows the goals won't be tracked. The cycle of low expectations perpetuates itself.

Research backs this up. Studies published through ERIC and the Journal of the Effective Schools Project consistently show that low-performing districts are more likely to operate with unstructured, reactive meeting patterns. The boards that break the cycle don't do it with a single dramatic action. They do it with discipline. A standing agenda item. A pre-meeting brief. An end-of-meeting commitment. Replicated every single meeting, until that's just how the board operates.

You planned in December. You built the template on December 30. Now follow through.

That's what January is for.
