**November Governance Reset — Closing the Calendar Before Year-End**

November 28, 2028

---

Thanksgiving week is behind you. The holiday break is ahead. In between, there is a week that most boards let slip by unnoticed.

Here is why that week matters more than most board members realize.

December is the year-end closeout. The organizational meeting. The goal adoption for the spring cycle. It is the most concentrated governance period on the fall calendar — and it does not leave room for open items from October and November.

If your board enters December with unresolved issues — a data review finding that was tabled, a budget question that was deferred, a partnership conversation that was scheduled but never held — those issues will either get crammed into December's already full calendar or they will carry into January, where they will compete with the spring cycle launch.

Neither outcome serves your board.

The November governance reset is the board's structured opportunity to close every open item before December arrives.

Here is what to check.

---

**Check one: The calendar — are the December milestones on track?**

Your board's December calendar has three critical milestones. Let me name them so you can check whether each one is set.

**Year-end governance review. Week of December 5.** The board produces a written summary of the fall cycle — goal outcomes, budget monitoring findings, governance practice assessment, partnership health. This is the document that closes Fall 2028 the same way the June closeout closed Spring 2028.

The superintendent needs to know what data to prepare. The board president needs the review framework in hand before the meeting. The agenda needs to be allocated for a governance review, not consumed by operational items.

If none of those three things are set, this week is the time to set them.

**Organizational meeting. Week of December 12.** The board establishes committee assignments for Year Three of governance. Unlike the Fall 2027 organizational meeting — when committees were being built from scratch — these assignments are made from experience. Board members know which committees match their strengths. The superintendent knows which committees need the most engagement. The board president knows which committee chairs produce results and which ones need development support.

The question is whether the board president has a committee assignment framework ready before the organizational meeting. Not on the day of — before.

**January goal adoption preparation. Before winter break.** The board adopted goals in January 2028 and renewed them through the September goal refresh. Spring 2029 goals should be a continuation, not a restart. But continuation requires a written recommendation from the governance committee — with any proposed adjustments documented and rationale attached — before the January meeting.

If that recommendation is not being drafted, this week is the start.

| Milestone | Week | Risk If Not Prepared |
|-----------|------|---------------------|
| Year-end governance review | Dec 5 | No written closeout — fall cycle outcomes are lost |
| Organizational meeting | Dec 12 | Committees are built from memory instead of evidence |
| Goal adoption prep | Pre-break | Spring cycle launches behind schedule |

---

**Check two: Governance health — is the board ready for the transition?**

The calendar check covers what is scheduled. The governance health check covers what is unspoken.

December is a transition month. The board closes one governance cycle and opens another. The superintendent shifts from fall execution to spring planning. The board president passes committees through the organizational meeting. Board members who carried fall leadership responsibilities hand off those roles to the next rotation.

Transitions create friction when expectations are not shared.

Here are the questions the board president should ask the superintendent this week — directly, not in an email chain, not through a committee chair:

*Are there any data review findings from the fall cycle that the board needs to resolve before the year-end closeout?* If the October data review produced findings that need board action before the cycle closes, the November reset is the last window to act.

*Is the superintendent's team on track to produce the year-end data summary by the first week of December?* If the answer is not a clear yes, the board needs to know now — not on December 4.

*Are there any partnership concerns that have not been raised in formal channels but should be addressed before the board and superintendent enter the spring cycle together?* This is the question most board presidents never ask. The ones who do ask it prevent the December-January surprises.

Here are the questions each board member should ask themselves:

*Have I completed every governance commitment I made for the fall cycle?* Committee assignments, data review preparation, community engagement, board self-assessment. Not halfway. Completed.

*Do I have questions about the fall cycle data that I have not raised?* Every board member enters the year-end closeout with one or two unanswered questions. The November reset is where those questions get named — and resolved — instead of deferred to the closeout meeting, where there will not be time to pursue them.

*Am I ready for the organizational meeting decisions?* Committee assignments, leadership roles, Year Three governance framework. Does every board member have enough information to vote on these? If not, what information is missing, and who is responsible for providing it?

The governance health check is not a formal evaluation. It is a temperature reading. Boards that take the temperature in November resolve issues before December resolves them for them.

---

**Check three: Year-end closeout readiness — what the board still owes itself**

The year-end closeout was introduced in Spring 2028. It was the board's first attempt at a written governance summary — goal outcomes, budget findings, partnership assessment, practice review.

Fall 2028 is the second closeout. It should be better than the first.

Here is what a strong Fall 2028 year-end closeout includes.

**1. Fall goal outcomes.** For each goal adopted in the September goal renewal, state the outcome: trajectory maintained, accelerated, or adjusted. Include the year-over-year comparison against Spring 2028 data at the same point in the cycle. The board that can show year-over-year improvement on its own goals has evidence that governance is working.

**2. Budget monitoring findings.** From the July budget implementation check-in, the September budget track launch, the October data-to-dollars connection, and the November mid-year budget direction work — what did the board learn about resource allocation? Were the summer budget adjustments producing the intended effect? What questions does the board carry into the January budget crosswalk?

**3. Governance practice assessment.** Did the board execute the fall calendar as planned? Which governance practices improved from the spring cycle? Which ones slipped? What was the board's protect-apply-add follow-through rate from the summer renewal?

This is the data point that connects the Summer 2028 arc to the Fall 2028 closeout. The board committed to specific practices in July, August, and September. The year-end closeout shows which ones were executed and which ones need renewed attention in the spring.

**4. Partnership health.** Did the superintendent partnership framework function as designed? Were the post-evaluation support commitments from the June evaluation producing measurable change? What is the partnership priority for the spring cycle?

The November 21 partnership piece asked whether the framework was working. The year-end closeout documents the answer.

**5. Board self-assessment summary.** What does the board self-assessment say about the fall cycle specifically? What does it say about the board's development from Spring 2028 through Fall 2028? Two cycles of self-assessment data provide the board's first trend line on its own governance improvement.

If your board cannot produce all five items, it is not ready for the year-end closeout.

The November reset is where the board fills those gaps.

---

**The question your board should answer this week**

Here is the single question that determines whether the November reset is real or rhetorical:

*What is the one governance item from the fall cycle that your board committed to but did not complete?*

Be specific. Name the item. Name the reason it is still open. Name the specific action that will close it before December.

That item is your board's work for this week.

Boards that answer this question honestly will enter December with no open items — a clean calendar, a shared understanding, and a governance framework that is ready for the year-end closeout.

Boards that skip the question will enter December carrying three months of deferred decisions into a month that was already full. Those decisions will not disappear. They will surface in January, when the board is supposed to be focused on spring goal adoption and the new cycle launch.

A governance reset is not an extra meeting. It is a structured review of what is open, what needs to close, and what the board needs to do before the calendar turns to December.

Your board has this week. Use it.
