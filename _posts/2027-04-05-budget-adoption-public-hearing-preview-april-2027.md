**Your Board Has a Budget — Now the Public Hearing Tests Whether You Own It**

April 5, 2027

---

March closed with a budget alignment review. Your board verified that the adjusted budget funds the goals you adopted in January. You checked the adjustments list. You validated the trajectory funding level. You examined the trade-offs. You recorded an outcome — aligned, aligned with conditions, or not aligned — and the superintendent knew where things stood.

That was the governance work of March. The public hearing is the governance work of April, and it asks a different question. Not "does the board understand the budget?" but "does the board own the budget?"

Here's the difference I've seen matter most. A board that understands the budget can explain line items. A board that owns the budget can explain why each line item exists — what goal it serves, what community priority it funds, what trade-off it represents. Ownership is the difference between knowing the answer and standing behind it.

**What the public hearing tests**

The public hearing tests three things about your board.

It tests preparation. The hearing happens on a set date. The community arrives with questions. You can't reschedule because you're not ready. The board that shows up prepared has published the progress report, briefed members on the hearing protocol, agreed on who responds to what types of questions, and confirmed that every board member can articulate the connection between the budget and the adopted goals. The board that shows up unprepared answers questions off the cuff, contradicts itself, and leaves the community wondering whether the board actually reviewed the budget.

It tests discipline. The hearing is the most watched board event of the spring. The community is present. The press is present. Every board member is visible. Communication discipline — the commitment to speaking with one voice about board decisions — gets its hardest test at the public hearing. A board member who disagrees with a line item can raise that concern in a work session. At the public hearing, the board speaks as the board. Individual members who introduce personal priorities or air internal disagreements signal that the board isn't a team.

It tests trust. The community has been receiving communications from the board since January — goal adoption updates, data reports, progress reports. The public hearing is the moment when the community evaluates whether those communications were genuine. If the board's hearing presentation matches the narrative the board has been building all spring, the community's trust deepens. If the board presents a different story at the hearing — if the progress report promised transparency but the hearing feels defensive — the community notices, and the trust built over three months erodes in one evening.

**How to prepare**

Preparation for the public hearing starts now, not the day before. Here's what I recommend your board do in the next seven days.

First, confirm that the community progress report from March has been published and distributed. The hearing should never be the first time the community sees the board's budget rationale. If the progress report isn't out yet, that's the first priority.

Second, schedule a board briefing on the hearing protocol. This is a thirty-minute session — can be a special meeting or the first thirty minutes of a regular meeting. The board president reviews: who responds to testimony, how to handle questions the board can't answer immediately, what to do if a community member raises a concern the board hasn't discussed. The board agrees on answers to the three most likely questions the community will ask.

Third, run a quick hearing simulation. This sounds formal, but it doesn't need to be. Fifteen minutes at the end of a meeting. Board members practice stating the board's budget rationale in one minute. The president practices opening the hearing and setting the tone. The superintendent practices presenting the budget in the context of board-adopted goals. The simulation reveals gaps in preparation that the actual hearing won't forgive.

**What not to do**

I've watched boards make three mistakes at the public hearing that I want to help you avoid.

Don't treat the hearing as a superintendent presentation with a board vote attached. The superintendent presents the operational budget. The board owns the policy decisions that shaped it. The board president should open the hearing by summarizing the board's work — goals adopted in January, budget reviewed in February, alignment verified in March — so the community understands that the budget reflects board direction, not just staff preparation.

Don't let individual board members answer community questions independently. The board president should designate one or two members as hearing spokespersons. Other board members support the spokespersons but don't compete with them. A public hearing where seven board members answer the same question seven different ways is a public hearing that produces confusion, not confidence.

Don't adopt the budget immediately after the hearing closes. I recommend a two-step process: the hearing, then a separate board meeting for adoption. The separation gives the board time to consider testimony that was raised, document any adjustments that testimony warrants, and vote with the full context. Boards that adopt immediately signal that the hearing was procedural. Boards that adopt after deliberation signal that community input mattered.

**Where this goes**

The public hearing is the third major event in the spring budget cycle. Goal adoption in January. Budget review in February. Budget alignment in March. Public hearing in April. Budget adoption — in a separate meeting — closes the cycle.

The work your board does this week determines whether the hearing produces community confidence or community concern. The preparation you do now — publishing the report, briefing the board, running the simulation — is what separates a hearing that builds trust from a hearing that just fulfills a requirement.

Your board has a budget. Wednesday's piece walks through the seven-day preparation checklist. For now, the question is simple: are you ready to own it when the community asks?
