**The Public Hearing Isn't a Procedural Requirement — It's a Trust-Building Event**

April 12, 2027

---

The hearing is tonight. Or it's this week. Your board has done the preparation work — the progress report is published, the president's statement is drafted, the spokespersons are designated, the simulation has been run. Now the question is execution.

I've walked into more public hearings than I can count as a board member, a coach, and an observer. Here's what I've learned: the hearing itself follows a predictable arc. The boards that build trust through the hearing understand that arc and use it. The boards that treat the hearing as a procedural step get through it faster but leave less confidence behind.

**The first five minutes — the opening**

The board president opens the hearing. This is the most important five minutes of the evening. The president's opening statement, drafted and approved during the preparation week, sets the frame for everything that follows.

A good opening does three things. It acknowledges the community's role in the budget process. It summarizes the board's governance work since January — goals, review, alignment, progress reporting — so the community sees the budget as the product of a process. And it establishes the hearing format and protocol, so the community knows what to expect and how to participate.

A bad opening skips the summary, reads the hearing notice, and yields to the superintendent. The difference between those two openings is the difference between a community that feels included and a community that feels processed.

**The superintendent presentation — governance context matters**

The superintendent presents the budget. This should take fifteen to twenty minutes, not forty. If the superintendent is still presenting at minute thirty-five, the presentation is too detailed for a public hearing.

Here's what matters most. The superintendent should present the budget in the context of the board's adopted goals — not as a standalone financial document. "This budget allocates X dollars to the early literacy goal the board adopted in January. The allocation is Y% above last year's baseline, reflecting the board's direction to increase reading intervention funding by Z%." That's a presentation that connects the budget to the story the community has been hearing all spring.

If the superintendent presents a line-item walkthrough, the community receives the budget as a spreadsheet. If the superintendent presents a goal-to-resource narrative, the community receives the budget as a governance document.

**Community testimony — the board's response protocol**

Community testimony is the core of the hearing. Here's the protocol I recommend.

Each speaker gets three to five minutes. The board president thanks every speaker for their testimony. The designated spokespersons respond to questions after each speaker or in clusters, depending on the volume of testimony. Responses should follow a simple rule: answer the question that was asked, connect it to a board decision or process, and note when the board needs to follow up.

This is where the preparation matters most. A community member asks about reading intervention funding. The designated spokesperson responds: "The board identified reading intervention as a priority during the February budget review. We directed the superintendent to increase reading intervention funding by 12%. The budget before you tonight reflects that direction. I can share the specific allocation amount after the hearing." That's a response that demonstrates ownership.

Compare that to a board member who responds: "That's a good question. I'm not sure of the exact number. The superintendent can follow up with you." That's a response that erodes confidence — even if the answer is technically accurate.

**What to do when the board doesn't know the answer**

A question will come that the board hasn't anticipated. It happens in every hearing. The right response is not to guess. The right response is: "Thank you for that question. The board doesn't have that information available tonight. We will document your question, request the information from the superintendent, and include the response in the record before the adoption meeting."

Then do that. Document it. Follow up. Include the response in the adoption meeting materials. The community is watching whether the board follows through. A board that promises follow-up and delivers it earns more trust than a board that answers every question on the spot — because the follow-up demonstrates that the board takes community input seriously enough to act on it.

**The closing — what happens after testimony closes**

The board closes testimony. The president thanks the community for participating. The president reviews what happens next: the board will consider all testimony, the superintendent will prepare any requested follow-up information, and the board will hold a separate adoption meeting on [date]. The community is invited to attend.

Then the board adjourns without adopting. This was recommended in Monday's piece and it's worth repeating. A separate adoption meeting signals that testimony matters. An immediate adoption signals that the hearing was a checkbox.

**The trust-building moment**

Here's what I've observed about the hearings that build the most trust. It's not about how smooth the presentation is. It's not about how few questions are asked. It's about whether the community walks out feeling heard.

That feeling comes from specific things: the board president making eye contact with speakers, the spokespersons taking notes while community members talk, the superintendent referencing community questions in responses, the board president closing with a summary of the key themes raised in testimony and a commitment to address them.

The hearing that builds trust looks less like a board meeting and more like a conversation. The board leads it, but the community drives it. When that happens, the budget isn't just adopted — it's adopted with community understanding and support.
