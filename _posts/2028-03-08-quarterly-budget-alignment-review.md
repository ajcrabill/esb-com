**The Quarterly Budget Alignment Review — Four Questions That Turn the Crosswalk Into a Year-Round Governance Tool**

March 8, 2028

---

Your board built the crosswalk in February.

You took the preliminary budget — organized by function and object, like every budget since you started serving on the board — and you rebuilt it organized by goal. Early literacy got this much. Math growth got that much. Ninth-grade on-track got this much. You saw, for the first time, whether the money was actually going where the goals said it should go.

That was a February exercise. It produced a February document.

March is when you find out whether the document is still accurate. Because between the crosswalk and the April adoption vote, things happen. The superintendent adjusts line items in response to board findings from the February review. The community raises priorities at the public hearing that shift some allocations. New Progress Monitoring data arrives that challenges the assumptions built into the budget.

A board that built the crosswalk in February and never looked at it again will adopt a budget in April based on an outdated map. The board that runs the quarterly budget alignment review — four questions, thirty minutes, one board agenda item — will adopt a budget based on a map it verified that week.

Let me show you the four questions.

---

**The problem the quarterly review solves**

Here is the pattern I see in districts across the country.

The board invests significant time building the crosswalk in February. The board president leads the work session. The finance committee reviews every allocation. The board produces written findings. Everyone leaves feeling the board did its job.

Then March arrives. The board has a public hearing, a committee meeting, an executive session, and a regular meeting on the calendar. The crosswalk sits in the board packet from last month. Nobody opens it.

By the time the April adoption vote arrives, the board has no way to know whether the allocations that received so much attention in February are still accurate. The superintendent made adjustments. The community input shifted some priorities. The March data showed something the February data did not.

The board votes to adopt a budget without knowing whether the crosswalk from February still matches the budget document on the table.

The quarterly budget alignment review fixes this. It is a thirty-minute board agenda item — not a work session, not a committee meeting — that answers four specific questions using evidence the board already has.

---

**Question one: Are the resources still flowing to the goals the board adopted in January?**

This is the baseline question and it is the one your board should answer first.

Pull the crosswalk from February. The one-page, three-column format: goals in priority order, budget allocation for each goal, percentage of the general fund for each goal. This is the document your board approved as the resource map for the 2028-29 school year.

Now pull the revised budget — the version the superintendent has prepared for the April public hearing and adoption vote. This version incorporates the adjustments the superintendent made in response to the board's February findings and any subsequent changes.

Compare them.

If the revised budget allocations for your priority goals are the same as or higher than the February crosswalk, the alignment is intact. The resources are still flowing where the board directed them.

If the revised budget allocations for your priority goals are lower than the February crosswalk — and the board did not authorize a reallocation — the alignment has shifted. The board needs to know why and by how much.

I have watched boards in **Lawrence (KS)** use a simple threshold for this question. If any priority goal's allocation changed by more than five percent between the February crosswalk and the revised budget, the superintendent provides a written explanation before the board signs off. The explanation names the specific line items that changed, the dollar amounts, and the reason for each change.

A board that answers question one affirmatively enters the remaining three questions with confidence. A board that discovers an unexplained shift has a problem to solve before the April vote.

---

**Question two: Did the public hearing adjustments preserve the goal-to-budget alignment?**

This is the community accountability question.

Your board held a public hearing on the budget between February and March. The community raised priorities. Some of those priorities the board already knew about from the January goal-setting engagement. Some of them were new. The superintendent made adjustments in response.

The question is whether those adjustments preserved the alignment the board established in the crosswalk.

Here is what this looks like in practice.

The community said at the hearing that the district needs more mental health support for middle school students. The board agreed. The superintendent added $200,000 for a new counselor position and a contract with a community mental health provider. That $200,000 came from somewhere in the budget — a reallocation from a non-priority area, a reduction in a planned expenditure, or a use of fund balance.

The question for the board is not whether the $200,000 was well spent. The board already decided that when it agreed with the community priority. The question is whether the $200,000 was taken from a goal-aligned allocation.

If the $200,000 came from a non-priority area or from a contingency line item, the alignment is preserved. The goal allocations in the crosswalk are unchanged.

If the $200,000 came from a priority goal allocation — the early literacy intervention budget, for example — the alignment has shifted. The board needs to know that the early literacy allocation is $200,000 lower than the February crosswalk projected, and the board needs to decide whether to restore it from another source before the adoption vote.

The crosswalk gives your board the tool to see this. Without the crosswalk, the $200,000 adjustment looks like a reasonable response to a community priority. With the crosswalk, the board sees the full picture: the community got what it asked for, and a priority goal lost a significant resource.

---

**Question three: Does the crosswalk need updating before the next review cycle?**

This is the maintenance question.

The crosswalk your board built in February reflected the budget as it existed at that moment. But the budget is not static. The superintendent made adjustments. The board made decisions. The community weighed in.

The question is whether the crosswalk document itself needs to be updated to reflect the current state of the budget before the next quarterly review.

Here is the standard I recommend.

If the adjustments since February changed any priority goal's allocation by more than five percent, the crosswalk should be updated and re-presented to the board at the next meeting. The updated crosswalk becomes the new baseline for the Q2 quarterly review.

If the adjustments changed no priority goal's allocation by more than five percent, the February crosswalk remains the baseline and the board notes for the record that the crosswalk is current.

A board that updates the crosswalk proactively enters the next quarterly review with a document it trusts. A board that does not update it enters Q2 wondering whether the February document still reflects reality. The difference between those two outcomes is one question, answered in five minutes, using data the board already receives.

---

**Question four: Does the March Progress Monitoring data validate or challenge the current allocations?**

This is the evidence question — and it is the one that connects the budget review to the data review your board will run again in May.

The February crosswalk showed the board where the money is going. The March Progress Monitoring data shows the board whether the money is producing results. Together, they tell the board whether the resource allocation is working.

Here is how this works in practice.

Your board adopted three measurable goals in January — early literacy proficiency, math growth for targeted subgroups, and ninth-grade on-track rate. The crosswalk shows the allocations: $2.1 million for early literacy intervention, $1.4 million for math acceleration, and $600,000 for ninth-grade support.

The March Progress Monitoring data arrives. The early literacy data shows year-over-year growth is on trajectory — the allocation is producing results. The math growth data shows the targeted subgroups are not making expected progress — the allocation may not be sufficient. The ninth-grade on-track data shows the intervention is working but the scale is too small to reach the full cohort.

Now the board has a specific question for each goal. For the early literacy goal: confirm alignment and continue monitoring. For the math growth goal: is the allocation sufficient, or does the board need to direct a reallocation before the April adoption? For the ninth-grade goal: can the board expand the intervention within the current allocation, or does it need to adjust the budget to scale the program?

The board does not need to answer these questions from scratch. The crosswalk shows the current allocation. The Progress Monitoring data shows the current trajectory. The board's question is whether the two are consistent — and if they are not, whether a correction is needed before the final vote.

---

**The two-page Alignment Check format**

Let me give you the exact format I recommend for the board packet.

Page one: the crosswalk from February, updated with any adjustments the board has identified through questions one, two, and three. Goals in priority order, current dollar amounts, percentage of general fund. One page.

Page two: the March Progress Monitoring data for each adopted goal, presented alongside the crosswalk allocation for that goal. Two columns per goal: the allocation on the left, the trajectory data on the right. One page.

The board can see in one document whether the resource allocation and the performance data tell the same story.

If they do, the board confirms alignment and moves to the April adoption timeline with the four-question check complete.

If they do not, the board has one month to direct corrections before the final vote. The crosswalk tells the board where the dollars are. The Progress Monitoring data tells the board whether the dollars are working. The Alignment Check tells the board what to do about it.

---

**Two outcomes from the review**

The quarterly budget alignment review produces one of two outcomes.

**Outcome one: Alignment confirmed.** The resources are flowing to the adopted goals. The public hearing adjustments preserved alignment. The crosswalk is current. The Progress Monitoring data validates the allocations. The board adopts a written finding confirming alignment and moves the focus to the April public hearing presentation and the adoption vote.

**Outcome two: Alignment gaps identified.** One or more priority goal allocations have shifted without board authorization. The public hearing adjustments pulled resources from a goal-aligned area. The crosswalk needs updating. The Progress Monitoring data challenges one or more allocations. The board adopts a written finding naming each gap, directs the superintendent to propose adjustments before the next meeting, and sets a deadline for the adjustments to be reviewed.

I have watched boards in **Adams 14 (CO)** handle Outcome Two well. Their board president schedules a fifteen-minute special board call or adds a board discussion item to the next committee meeting. The superintendent presents the proposed adjustments. The board reviews them against the crosswalk. The board confirms alignment before the public hearing. The process takes thirty minutes total and prevents the board from arriving at the April adoption with unresolved questions.

A board that reaches Outcome One enters the April adoption with confidence. A board that reaches Outcome Two has a productive month of correction. A board that does not conduct the quarterly review at all will adopt a budget in April without knowing whether the crosswalk from February still matches the budget on the table.
