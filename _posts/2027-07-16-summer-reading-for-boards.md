**Summer Reading for School Boards — What to Read, How to Discuss It, and Why It Matters**

July 16, 2027

---

Your board committed to summer reading. The self-assessment identified gaps. The summer work plan included a reading commitment. The books arrived or were downloaded. They're sitting on nightstands, in tablet folders, on the corner of desks.

The question that determines whether the summer reading produces governance improvement or a stack of books with bent spines is not: what should we read?

The question is: what should we read that will make us better at the specific areas where we need to improve?

Most boards approach summer reading the same way most people approach New Year's resolutions. The intention is good. The execution is vague. The result is that the reading gets done, but nothing changes. The board reads a book about goal-setting, nods along, and writes goals the same way it always wrote them. The board reads a book about data-driven decision-making, agrees that data matters, and holds the same unstructured data review it held the year before.

Summer reading for a school board is not a book club. It is targeted professional development designed to close specific governance gaps. The books do not need to be about education — some of the best governance books are about leadership, decision-making, organizational behavior, and strategic planning. What matters is not the subject. What matters is whether the reading produces a governance outcome: a better goal statement, a stronger data review protocol, a clearer board communication policy, a more productive relationship with the superintendent.

**A curated reading list by ESB domain**

The list below is organized by the four ESB domains. Each book exists because it addresses a specific governance challenge boards face — a challenge your board likely has, or you would not have identified it in the self-assessment. The governance question column tells you what the book should help your board answer.

Read the whole list, then select the books that match the gaps the self-assessment identified. Do not read every book on this list. Read the books that address what your board specifically needs to improve.

**Goal Clarity**

| Book | Author | Why it matters | Governance question it helps answer |
|------|--------|----------------|--------------------------------------|
| *Measure What Matters* | John Doerr | The definitive book on OKRs — outcome-driven goal-setting that connects organizational priorities to measurable results | How do we write goals that drive resource allocation and decision-making? |
| *The Outcome Mindset* | Stephen Shapiro | How to focus on outcomes instead of outputs — the distinction between activity and results | Are our goals about what students will achieve or about what adults will do? |
| *Levers of Control* | Robert Simons | How leaders use measurement systems to execute strategy — diagnostic versus interactive control systems | Are we measuring what matters, or are we measuring what's easy to measure? |
| *Stakeholder Strategy* | John Bryson | How public organizations set strategic priorities when multiple stakeholders have competing interests | How do we set goals that balance community priorities, board priorities, and superintendent recommendations? |

**Progress Monitoring**

| Book | Author | Why it matters | Governance question it helps answer |
|------|--------|----------------|--------------------------------------|
| *Leading Schools* | Tim Westerberg | The practitioner's guide to school leadership through data — specific protocols for data-driven decision-making | What does an effective data review actually look like in practice? |
| *The Data Coach's Guide to Improving Learning for All Students* | Nancy Love | A step-by-step framework for using data to improve instruction — the collaborative inquiry cycle | How do we move from data presentation to data-driven governance decisions? |
| *Performance Dashboards* | Wayne Eckerson | How to design performance dashboards that drive decisions instead of providing information | What should our board data dashboard include, and how should it be structured? |

**Governance Team**

| Book | Author | Why it matters | Governance question it helps answer |
|------|--------|----------------|--------------------------------------|
| *The Five Dysfunctions of a Team* | Patrick Lencioni | The framework for team dynamics — absence of trust, fear of conflict, lack of commitment, avoidance of accountability, inattention to results | What is our board's biggest team dysfunction, and how do we address it? |
| *Boards That Make a Difference* | John Carver | The original policy governance model — the framework for defining the board's role and the superintendent's role | Are we governing at the right level, or are we operating in the superintendent's lane? |
| *The Advantage* | Patrick Lencioni | Organizational health as a competitive advantage — clarity, communication, and accountability at the leadership level | Does our board have the organizational health to govern effectively through conflict? |

**Superintendent Partnership**

| Book | Author | Why it matters | Governance question it helps answer |
|------|--------|----------------|--------------------------------------|
| *Governance as Leadership* | Richard Chait, William Ryan, Barbara Taylor | The framework for boards as co-creators of organizational direction — beyond fiduciary oversight to strategic partnership | Are we governing as partners with the superintendent, or are we governing as overseers? |
| *Boards That Work* | Doug Eadie | The practical guide to board-CEO relationships — how to build trust while maintaining accountability | How do we build a relationship with our superintendent that is both trusting and accountable? |
| *The Art of the CEO* | Stephen Miles | Not a board book, but invaluable for understanding the perspective of the person being evaluated | What does our superintendent need from us to be successful? |

**How to structure the reading so it produces a governance outcome**

Do not ask every board member to read every book. That guarantees that every board member will read none of the books.

Assign books based on the self-assessment findings. If the self-assessment revealed goal vagueness as the top gap, the board president reads *Measure What Matters*, the vice president reads *The Outcome Mindset*, and whichever board member or committee is closest to the data steers the work reads the progress monitoring books. Each board member assigned a book produces one thing: a single-page reflection answering two questions — what this book taught me about our board's governance, and what I recommend we do differently as a result.

Then the board holds a ninety-minute summer work session. No board meeting business. No operational updates. Just the reading discussion. Each board member presents their reflection. The board discusses the implications for the fall governance cycle. The board president or a designated note-taker captures the recommendations that the board agrees to implement.

The discussion structure should follow a simple protocol. Each board member gets ten minutes: five minutes to present the book's core insight as it applies to the board's governance, and five minutes for board discussion of the recommendation. After all board members present, the board spends the remaining twenty minutes identifying which recommendations go on the fall governance calendar, who is responsible for executing each recommendation, and when the board will check progress.

**The deliverable that separates investment from activity**

The reading should produce a specific output for the fall: a revised goal-writing framework from the goal-setting books, a redesigned data review protocol from the progress monitoring books, a board communication policy revision from the governance team books, or an updated superintendent partnership framework from the partnership books.

The reading without the deliverable is an event the board attended. The reading with the deliverable is an investment that improves the board's governance practice starting in September.

A board that reads *Measure What Matters* and does not change how it writes goals has spent the same time and effort as a board that reads *Measure What Matters* and produces a goal-writing framework for the fall. The only difference is whether the board committed to the deliverable before the reading began.

Commit to the deliverable first. Then do the reading that produces it.
