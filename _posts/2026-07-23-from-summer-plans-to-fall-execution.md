You had a good summer.

Maybe you did a retreat. Set board goals. Aligned superintendent goals. Assigned committees for the year. Built an annual calendar. You did the work.

I'm going to tell you something that makes most boards uncomfortable: none of that matters if you don't have a plan for what happens in August.

There's a pattern I've seen in district after district. Boards do excellent summer planning work — thoughtful goal-setting, honest self-evaluation, well-designed committee structures. Then September hits and none of it shows up. The board goals don't appear on monitoring agendas. The superintendent goals get filed away until evaluation season. Committees meet without charters. The annual calendar gets swallowed by emergencies. The planning was real. The execution didn't materialize.

I call it the **August Trap**. It's the gap between summer planning and fall execution — and it's where board effectiveness goes to die.

The boards that escape the August Trap don't have better plans. They have better bridges from planning to execution. Here are the four bridges your board needs to build before your first August meeting.

---

## Bridge 1: Board Goals → Monitoring Schedule

You spent hours — maybe a full retreat day — getting your board goals right. Specific. Measurable. Time-bound. Student-outcome-focused. Good work.

Now answer this: when, exactly, is your board going to look at data on those goals?

A goal without a monitoring schedule is a wish. I've seen boards that set excellent goals in June and never return to them until the annual retreat the following year. They had the goals. They had the data. They just never scheduled a meeting to connect the two. That's not goal-setting. That's a PR exercise.

**Here's what I recommend.** Before your first August board meeting, map every board goal to a specific monitoring date on your meeting calendar. Not "quarterly." A date. October 15, Goal 1. December 3, Goal 2. March 12, Goal 3. On the agenda, in the packet, assigned to a committee for preliminary review.

The best boards I've worked with schedule their goal monitoring before they schedule anything else. The monitoring date is locked. The board retreat, the committee meetings, the community forums — everything else fits around the monitoring dates. That's how you signal that student outcomes drive your agenda, not the other way around.

One more thing: the data your board reviews must be leading indicators, not lagging ones. Don't wait for end-of-year assessment results to know whether you're on track. What data can you look at in October — attendance trends, early literacy screeners, course enrollment patterns — that tells you whether your goal trajectory is holding? If you don't know, your agenda is not yet ready for monitoring.

## Bridge 2: Superintendent Goals → Feedback Cycles

Most boards and superintendents spend significant time in the summer aligning on annual goals. They agree on priorities. They set benchmarks. They shake hands and head into the school year aligned on the destination.

Then nothing happens until the formal evaluation in the spring. Seven or eight months of silence, followed by a high-stakes conversation where both sides realize they've been operating from different assumptions since week two.

Here's the problem: a set of goals with no feedback cycle is not a management tool. It's a scorecard you pull out at the end and hope the numbers look good.

**Here's what I recommend.** Build three feedback checkpoints into the first 90 days. First checkpoint: mid-September, informal. The question: "What have we learned in the first three weeks that changes our assumptions?" Second checkpoint: late October, data-informed. The board brings whatever monitoring data it has reviewed. The superintendent brings progress indicators. The conversation is grounded in numbers, not impressions. Third checkpoint: December, ahead of the mid-year conversation. This one is more structured — a written update from the superintendent, a board discussion, a decision about whether to adjust targets for the second half.

These aren't formal evaluations. They're conversations. "Here's where we said we'd be. Here's where we are. What needs to change?"

The superintendents I've worked with who actually build trust with their board over the first year all have one thing in common: their board built a feedback rhythm into the fall, not a once-a-year surprise in the spring. The rhythm itself — the expectation that you'll talk about progress regularly — matters more than any single conversation.

## Bridge 3: Committee Assignments → Work Charters

I see this one every year. Boards announce committee assignments with great ceremony. Finance Committee, Policy Committee, Facilities Committee. Everyone knows their committee. Everyone nods.

Then committees meet for months — sometimes the entire year — without anyone being able to answer a simple question: *What are we supposed to produce?*

Vague committee assignments produce vague committee work. If your Finance Committee can't tell you what deliverable they're working toward, they're not governing. They're meeting. There's a difference.

**Here's what I recommend.** Every committee needs a work charter. Not a job description — a charter. One page. Three things: (1) the specific question the committee is answering this year, (2) the deliverable or recommendation they will produce, and (3) the date they report back to the full board for action.

Example: "The Policy Committee will review the district's current AI usage policy and deliver a recommended revised policy for full board consideration at the November regular meeting."

That's a charter. Specific. Time-bound. Accountable. Every board member can look at that in November and say: "Did the committee deliver? Yes or no?"

Committees with charters produce work. Committees without charters produce meetings. I've never seen an exception to this rule. If your committees don't have charters by the end of August, you have a governance structure problem that no amount of well-intentioned committee member effort can fix. Every month of unfocused committee work is a month of delayed progress on the student outcomes your board committed to in June.

## Bridge 4: Annual Calendar → Meeting Reality

Your board built a beautiful annual calendar in the summer. Well-structured meetings. Clear thematic focus for each month. A roadmap for the year.

The first August crisis is going to tear a hole in it.

A transportation shortage. A budget surprise. A parent group filing a formal complaint. A principal resignation in a high-needs school. I've seen all of these. Every board faces something. The question isn't whether your calendar will survive first contact with reality. The question is whether you know what to protect and what to flex.

**Here's what I recommend.** Go through your fall calendar and identify the three meetings that are non-negotiable — the ones that drive your most important board goals. Maybe it's the October data review for Goal 1. Maybe it's the November public hearing for the budget. Maybe it's the December midpoint conversation with the superintendent. Lock those dates. Everything else can flex if it has to. But those three stay.

And build a 15-minute **reality check** into the first 10 minutes of every board meeting agenda. Not for announcements. For one question: *What happened this month that changes our assumptions about what we planned in the summer?* This keeps your annual calendar alive instead of letting it become a document nobody looks at after September. The agenda item forces the board and superintendent to surface changes together rather than silently drifting.

Your board goals won't achieve themselves. They'll happen because you protected the meetings where you check progress, make decisions, and hold your system accountable.

## The First 90 Days Decide the Year

Your summer plans are only as good as your fall execution. The boards that make the August-to-October transition well don't do more work. They convert their summer planning into fall structures — monitoring schedules, feedback cycles, work charters, protected calendar dates.

That's the entire game. Planning is preparation. Execution is the game itself.
