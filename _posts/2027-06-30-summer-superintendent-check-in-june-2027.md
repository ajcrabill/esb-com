**The Summer Superintendent Check-In — Maintaining Leadership Alignment During the Pause**

June 30, 2027

---

The June superintendent evaluation is complete. The board has documented its assessment. The superintendent has received the feedback. Growth areas have been identified. Support commitments have been made. The board-superintendent relationship entered the summer with a shared understanding of where things stand.

That shared understanding is fragile.

Three weeks of summer break — no board meetings, no data reviews, no governance cadence — and the evaluation conversations start to fade. The growth areas get less crisp. The support commitments lose their specifics. The superintendent, operating without the board's regular presence, makes decisions based on what was discussed in June, not what was formally agreed. The board, operating without the superintendent's regular presence, starts to wonder whether the summer work plan is actually being executed.

The summer superintendent check-in prevents that drift.

**What the check-in is — and isn't**

The summer superintendent check-in is not another evaluation. The June evaluation is the board's formal assessment of the superintendent's performance for the full governance year. The summer check-in is a touchpoint — a structured conversation designed to maintain alignment, reinforce support commitments, and address any questions that have emerged since the evaluation.

The distinction matters. If the board treats the check-in as a mini-evaluation, the superintendent receives the message that the board doesn't trust the evaluation it just completed. If the board treats the check-in as an informal catch-up with no agenda, the superintendent receives the message that the board isn't taking its oversight responsibility seriously during the summer months.

The right framing: this is a board-superintendent alignment conversation. The board and superintendent are partners in governance. Partners check in.

**When to schedule the check-in**

The check-in should happen three to four weeks after the June evaluation meeting. That's enough time for the superintendent to begin acting on the growth area commitments but not so much time that the evaluation fades into background noise.

Late July is the sweet spot. Early August works if the board's summer calendar is full. Mid-August is too late — by then the board and superintendent are in back-to-school mode and the check-in becomes one more thing on a packed calendar rather than a deliberate alignment moment.

**The check-in agenda**

The check-in should be a sixty-minute virtual meeting — not a formal board meeting, not a public session. The board president, the board vice president (if applicable), and the superintendent participate. The full board receives a written summary.

The agenda has four items.

**Item one: Growth area progress.** Fifteen minutes. The superintendent reports on progress for each growth area identified in the June evaluation. The report is brief — what's been done, what's in progress, what's been deferred and why. The board president does not evaluate the progress. The board president asks clarifying questions and confirms support.

The most important question in this segment: what does the superintendent need from the board to make progress on the growth area between now and the August or September return? If the growth area is board-superintendent communication and the superintendent needs the board to commit to a specific communication protocol, the check-in is where that gets worked out.

**Item two: Summer work plan status.** Fifteen minutes. The board and superintendent review the summer work plan that was approved in June — board member reading, training attendance, policy development, data system preparation, fall calendar logistics. Each item gets a status: complete, on track, behind schedule, or needs adjustment.

The board president does not use this segment to check whether board members have done their reading. That's between each board member and their own commitment. The board president uses this segment to identify whether any summer work plan item needs the board's collective attention — a policy draft that needs board input before August, a data system change that requires a board decision before September, a fall calendar conflict that needs resolution.

**Item three: Emerging issues.** Fifteen minutes. The superintendent shares any developments — personnel changes, enrollment projections, facility issues, community concerns, legislative or regulatory changes — that have emerged since the June evaluation and that the board should be aware of before the fall.

The board president does not respond to every issue. The board president listens and, when appropriate, identifies issues that should be added to the fall governance calendar for formal board consideration.

**Item four: Check-in confirmation.** Fifteen minutes. The board president and superintendent confirm the next touchpoint — typically the August or September pre-meeting briefing — and confirm that the support commitments from the June evaluation remain appropriate. The board president commits to reporting the check-in summary to the full board.

**After the check-in**

The board president sends a written summary to the full board within forty-eight hours. The summary covers each agenda item, any decisions or adjustments made, and any issues that will require board attention in the fall. The summary is a governance document — it goes in the board's records, not because it's a formal action but because the board's governance documentation should include the check-in.

The full board does not need to discuss the check-in at the next regular meeting unless the check-in revealed an issue that requires board action. If the check-in revealed that the superintendent needs a formal board decision on a data system purchase before August, the board president schedules a special meeting or adds the item to the August regular meeting agenda. If the check-in revealed no issues requiring board action, the board president's written summary serves as the documentation, and the board moves forward.

**Why this matters**

The summer superintendent check-in is not required by statute in any state. It's not a board policy compliance item. It's a governance practice that distinguishes boards that maintain high-functioning superintendent partnerships from boards that let the relationship drift during every summer break.

The board and superintendent spent six months building a governance relationship — data reviews, budget conversations, community engagement, evaluation processes, self-assessment. The relationship is the board's single most important operational asset. Leaving that asset unattended for two or three months every summer is not a break. It's a risk.

The check-in takes sixty minutes. The relationship it protects is worth a full governance year.
