**The August Organizational Meeting — Setting Up the 2028-29 Governance Year**

August 2, 2028

---

Every board in the country has an organizational meeting. It is on the statutory checklist. Elect officers. Appoint committees. Adopt a meeting calendar. Vote, adjourn, check the box.

But if your board has been through one full governance cycle — one complete round of goal-setting, progress monitoring, and superintendent evaluation — you have something most boards never bring to their August meeting: data.

Actual data about how your governance decisions shaped the year your superintendent had.

That changes everything about how you approach the organizational meeting. You are not starting from scratch. You are starting from experience — your own board's specific experience of working as a governance team for the last twelve months.

Here is what a second-cycle board does differently.

**The Second-Cycle Officer Election**

The standard August question is: "Who wants to be president?" The better question is: "Who should be president based on what we learned about our governance team's strengths?"

Continuity has real value. A board president who has governed through a full cycle understands the rhythm — goal-setting season, progress monitoring cadence, evaluation prep, the board-president-superintendent triangle. Losing that institutional knowledge for the sake of rotation carries a cost.

But rotation also has value. A board that never rotates officers never develops more than one member's leadership capacity. The president's role is the most visible governance position in the district. If only one person ever holds it, the board is one election away from losing its governance leader entirely.

The second-cycle approach: evaluate fit, not term. Which board members demonstrated the skills required for each officer role during the spring cycle? The board president needs facilitation discipline — the ability to keep the board focused on governance actions, not drift into operational debate. The vice president needs goal fluency — the ability to step in and lead data discussions when the president recuses. Committee chairs need content depth — enough knowledge of their committee's domain to frame productive questions.

Assign officers based on demonstrated governance strengths. Not seniority. Not willingness alone.

**Committee Assignments — Fresh Charges, Fresh Purpose**

Most boards reconstitute their committees by reassigning the same members to the same committees. The education committee stays intact because someone has always been on the education committee.

A second-cycle board reexamines the committee's purpose before it reexamines the committee's membership.

For the fall 2028 cycle, I recommend four standing committees:

- **Finance** — budget monitoring, audit review, financial policy alignment
- **Policy** — policy review and development, policy-to-goal alignment
- **Governance** — board self-evaluation, calendar management, committee coordination
- **Goals and Data** — progress monitoring preparation, data quality review, goal framework maintenance

Each committee needs a charge statement — a written document that names what the committee will produce by December. Not "oversee finance." A specific: "Review the first quarter budget variance report and present findings to the full board at the October progress monitoring session."

A charge gives every committee member a deliverable, not just a title.

**Calendar Adoption — The Foundation for Next Week**

The organizational meeting formally adopts the board's meeting calendar. But the governance calendar — what the board will actually do in those meetings — is a separate document that gets built next week.

What the August 2 meeting should do: confirm meeting dates, set committee meeting cadence, establish the first governance action deadline, and publish the calendar to the community.

Transparency at this stage matters. When the community knows when the board will review data, approve budget amendments, and monitor progress, the board is accountable to a published schedule. That prevents the drift that replaces governance actions with operational items.

**The First Substantive Vote**

The first substantive vote of the governance year carries symbolic weight. For a second-cycle board, that vote could be approving the committee charge statements, affirming the fall goal framework, or ratifying the evaluation cycle adjustments identified in the June closeout.

The specific content matters less than the signal: the board is opening its governance year with a governance action, not a procedural one.

Set the tone on August 2. The rest of the year follows.

Next week, we build the governance calendar on top of the meeting calendar. The August organizational meeting sets the container. The governance calendar fills it with purpose.
