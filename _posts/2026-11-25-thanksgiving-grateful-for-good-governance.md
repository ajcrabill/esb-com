**Thanksgiving — Grateful for Good Governance: The Habits That Earned Trust This Year**

November 25, 2026

Tomorrow is Thanksgiving.

You're probably checking the turkey timeline, figuring out who's bringing the pie, and mentally preparing for that one relative's question about "so what exactly does the school board do?"

But before the chaos begins — before the mashed potatoes and the football and the annual debate about whether stuffing goes inside the bird — I want to offer something different.

A moment of gratitude.

Not the performative kind. Not the "we're so thankful for our amazing stakeholders" kind that shows up in board meeting opening statements and never gets said again.

I mean genuine gratitude for the governance habits that earned trust this year. The ones that made your board better. The ones that made your district stronger. The ones that, honestly, you might not have noticed until you stopped to think about them.

Here are four worth being thankful for.

**1. Structured agendas that protected your time.**

Remember meetings that ran three hours and ended with nothing decided? Remember the agenda that arrived in your inbox at 10 PM the night before?

If your board has moved past that — if you're walking into meetings with a consent agenda that actually works, action items that have clear owners, and a chair who enforces time limits like a border collie with a stopwatch — be grateful.

That structure didn't happen by accident. Someone decided that the board's time was a resource worth protecting. That decision ripples through every conversation your board has, every decision it makes, and every hour of staff time that doesn't get wasted in a room with no clear purpose.

**2. Transparent decision-making that rebuilt trust.**

School board trust is fragile. It takes years to build and one closed-session leak to crack.

If your board developed the habit of explaining the *why* behind the *what* this year — if you started publishing your rationale alongside your votes, recording clear minutes that a parent could actually follow, or holding a public work session before the high-stakes vote — you gave your community something rare.

You gave them a reason to trust the process.

Not every decision will be popular. But when people can see *how* a decision was made — the data you weighed, the tradeoffs you considered, the community input you incorporated — they're far more likely to accept the outcome, even if they disagree.

**3. Community partnerships that multiplied your impact.**

School boards can't do it alone.

If your district strengthened its partnerships this year — with the local Boys & Girls Club, the community college offering dual enrollment, the food bank running a weekend backpack program, the city coordinating crossing guards and facilities — you experienced a truth that too many boards miss.

The best governance doesn't try to control everything. It aligns everyone.

A board that invests in community partnerships multiplies its impact without multiplying its budget. It shares responsibility. It builds a web of support that catches students before they fall.

**4. Progress data that kept you honest.**

Here's the one I'm most grateful for.

Data is easy when it's good. When test scores go up and chronic absenteeism goes down, every board member wants to stand at the microphone.

Data is harder when it's not good. When the mid-year reading assessment shows third graders falling behind. When suspension rates for one student group haven't budged. When the graduation rate plateau is now entering year three.

But if your board looked at that hard data anyway — if you asked "what does this mean?" instead of "who's to blame?" — you practiced the hardest governance habit of all.

Honest progress monitoring.

It's the habit that keeps goals from becoming decorations. It's the discipline that turns "we care about literacy" from a slogan into a strategy.

**What are you grateful for?**

I could keep going — well-run board retreats, superintendent evaluations that actually mean something, policy reviews that catch problems before they become lawsuits. But I'd rather hear from you.

What governance habit earned your trust this year?

Hit reply and tell me. I read every response.

And tomorrow, when someone asks what the school board does, you'll have an honest answer: we build the habits that make our district worth trusting.

Happy Thanksgiving.
