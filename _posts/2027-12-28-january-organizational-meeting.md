**Your January Organizational Meeting Is About to Happen — Here Is What It Should Produce**

December 28, 2027

---

If your board has been with me through this entire fall arc, stop and look at what you have done since October.

You held your first full data review with written decisions for every goal. You published a community progress report. You conducted a fall governance health check. You closed the 2027 governance cycle with a structured year-end reset. You previewed the 2028 goal-setting process, started the superintendent evaluation cycle, and published a year-end governance report to the community.

That is not a board that holds meetings. That is a board that governs.

January is where that work pays off.

The January organizational meeting is the first governance event of 2028. Every board has one. But not every board uses it as a launch — most use it as a ceremony.

A ceremonial organizational meeting elects officers, approves a calendar, and adjourns. A strategic organizational meeting elects officers with purpose, builds a governance calendar that names what every meeting will produce, and commits the board to a specific way of operating in the year ahead.

Here is what your January organizational meeting should produce — and how it sets up the January 2028 launch arc that starts next week.

---

**One: Officer elections tied to governance skills, not rotation.**

The most common board practice I see is officer rotation. The vice president becomes president. The secretary becomes vice president. A new member becomes secretary. Everyone rotates through.

The problem with rotation is that the board's most important governance position — the board president — goes to whoever is next in line, not whoever is best suited for the job.

Your January organizational meeting should elect officers based on the governance skills the board needs for 2028, not based on who has not been president yet.

Here are the skills your board president needs for a strong 2028.

Facilitation skills. The board president facilitates every board meeting. If your board president cannot keep a data review on schedule, cannot redirect a board member who is wandering into operational territory, and cannot ensure every agenda item produces a decision rather than a discussion, the board will drift all year.

Relationship skills with the superintendent. The board president is the board's primary communication link to the superintendent. If the board president and the superintendent do not have a functional working relationship — defined by trust, transparency, and mutual respect — the governance year will be spent resolving relationship issues instead of governing.

Commitment to the governance calendar. The board president who cancels a Progress Monitoring data review because "we have a busy agenda this month" is a board president who does not understand Progress Monitoring. The board president must be the calendar's strongest defender.

Your board should evaluate candidates against these criteria before the organizational meeting. The nominating committee — if your district uses one — should present a slate with a rationale, not just a name.

I have watched boards in **Ladue (MO)** handle officer elections well. They publish the officer selection criteria in advance, nominate from the floor based on skills and commitment, and vote with full board discussion. The officer election is not a five-minute formality. It is a governance decision that sets the board up for the year.

---

**Two: Committee assignments that reflect strategic priorities.**

Most boards assign committees the same way they assign officers — by filling the same seats with new names.

The finance committee is the same committee that existed last year. The policy committee is the same. The facilities committee is the same. No one asks whether the committee structure still serves the board's strategic priorities for the coming year.

Your January organizational meeting should reconsider every committee.

What are the board's strategic priorities for 2028? If the board is focused on early literacy, is there a committee or task force that supports that focus? If the board is focused on budget alignment to adopted goals, does the finance committee have a specific charge related to goal-to-budget alignment?

Every committee should have a written charge that names the committee's purpose, deliverables, and timeline for the year. A finance committee charged with "review the budget" is a committee that will meet without knowing what it should produce. A finance committee charged with "present a goal-to-budget alignment report at the March board meeting" is a committee that knows exactly what it is expected to deliver.

Committees should also have a sunset date. A committee that exists because it has always existed is a committee that produces meetings, not results. Committees should be reviewed at the June governance check-in and renewed only if the strategic priority still requires a standing committee.

---

**Three: A governance calendar that names what every meeting will produce.**

Here is the single most important output of your January organizational meeting.

The board should adopt a governance calendar through June 2028 that names, for every regular board meeting and work session, what that meeting is designed to produce.

Not just the date and time. Not just "regular board meeting." The specific governance outcome.

A January governance calendar that names outcomes looks like this.

January organizational meeting: Elect officers, adopt governance calendar, appoint committees, adopt board governance commitment for 2028.

February meeting: First 2028 Progress Monitoring data review. Superintendent presents preliminary budget aligned to adopted goals.

March meeting: Budget alignment review. Board reviews community progress report. Board conducts mid-cycle superintendent evaluation update.

April meeting: Mid-year governance check-in. Board reviews its own governance effectiveness against the September self-evaluation baseline.

May meeting: Superintendent evaluation preparation. Board reviews the superintendent evaluation draft. 45-day factual accuracy check begins.

June meeting: Superintendent evaluation and goal preview. Board conducts formal evaluation conversation and previews the 2028-29 goal-setting process.

That is six meetings with six specific governance outcomes. Every board member knows what February is designed to produce. The superintendent knows what March requires. The community knows what to expect.

A board that adopts this calendar in January will execute every month on purpose. A board that adopts a calendar with bare dates will end every month wondering what the meeting was supposed to produce.

---

**Four: A board governance commitment for 2028.**

This is the piece most organizational meetings leave out.

The board should write and adopt a board governance commitment at the January organizational meeting — a one-page document that names how this board will operate differently in 2028 than it did in 2027.

The commitment answers three questions.

First, what are the board's governance improvement priorities for 2028? If the September 2027 self-evaluation identified that the board struggles with staying in its lane during the superintendent's reports, that becomes a specific commitment: "At every meeting in 2028, the board president will redirect operational questions to closed session or the superintendent's administrative report."

Second, how will the board hold itself accountable for these commitments? The board should designate a board member to track governance commitments and provide a brief status update at each board meeting. Not a formal evaluation. A two-minute check.

Third, what will the board do differently when it is not performing at its best? Every board has a rough meeting. The commitment names what the board will do after a rough meeting — debrief privately, adjust the agenda, communicate with the superintendent — so that the rough meeting does not become the board's operating norm for the next month.

A board governance commitment is not a legal document. It is a public promise the board makes to itself and the community. Publish it alongside the organizational meeting minutes. Read it at the start of every meeting in January and June. Let it be the document the board returns to when governance drift sets in.

---

**Five: The governance calendar and the community.**

One last thing. Publish the governance calendar.

The community should know what the board plans to produce in February, March, April, May, and June before those months arrive. The community should know when Progress Monitoring reviews will happen. The community should know when the budget alignment review is scheduled. The community should know when the superintendent evaluation will be conducted.

A board that publishes its governance calendar is a board that invites the community to hold it accountable. A board that keeps its calendar internal is a board that treats governance as a process the community does not need to track.

Publish the calendar on the district website. Include it in the January board packet. Send it to the local media. The community that knows what the board is planning is a community that can participate in governance.
