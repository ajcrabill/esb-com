**The Superintendent Evaluation Is in June. What Should You Be Doing in February?**

February 8, 2027

---

The Jan 13 piece launched the superintendent evaluation cycle on a five-month timeline. If you followed that timeline, your board has done two things so far: agreed on what the evaluation will measure (progress toward the goals you adopted on January 11), and established a shared understanding of the evidence that will support the evaluation.

That's good work. But here's the question I'm hearing from boards as February settles in: "We've started the process. Now what do we actually do for the next four months?"

The answer matters more than most boards realize. I've watched boards that start the evaluation process in January and go dark until May — and then spend May and June scrambling to reconstruct a year's worth of evidence from memory. I've watched boards that maintain a steady rhythm from January through June — and then walk into the June evaluation with a complete evidence file and a shared understanding of the superintendent's performance.

The difference between those two outcomes isn't the evaluation framework. It's what the board does between January and June.

**What the February check-in is designed to produce**

The February check-in is not an evaluation. It's not a progress report. It's a process check. Here are the three questions your board should be asking itself right now.

**1. Is the evidence being collected as planned?**

The evaluation framework your board adopted in January should have included an evidence collection plan. Board meeting minutes from every data review. The data dashboards from each progress monitoring check-in. Records of board-superintendent communication. Anecdotal evidence from board members regarding their observations.

February is the moment to verify that the evidence collection is happening. If the plan said minutes from every data review, are the minutes being filed in the evaluation folder? If the plan said data dashboards from each check-in, are the dashboards being saved in a shared location?

I've watched a board arrive at its May evidence review only to discover that half the data dashboards were never saved, that three board meeting minutes were never filed, and that the evidence file is incomplete. That board had to reconstruct four months of evidence in two weeks — and the reconstruction was inevitably less reliable than real-time collection would have been.

The February check-in prevents that. It takes fifteen minutes. The evaluation committee chair opens the evidence folder and verifies that what was supposed to be there actually is.

**2. Are the right questions already emerging?**

The evaluation timeline works best when the board and superintendent treat the evidence gathering as a year-long conversation rather than a single judgment. That means the February check-in should surface the questions that will shape the June evaluation — while there's still time to answer them.

If the first data review showed slower-than-expected progress on a literacy goal, the question is: "Is this a strategy issue, an implementation issue, or a timeline issue?" That question should be posed in February — not saved for June. The superintendent has four months to respond to it. The board has four months to observe the response.

If the budget review revealed a gap between the board's January direction and the superintendent's proposed allocation, the question is: "Was the gap a communication failure or a resource constraint?" Same logic. Pose it in February. Let the answer emerge over the next four months.

The February check-in doesn't answer these questions. It names them. It puts them on the record as topics the evaluation will address. And it gives both the board and the superintendent a shared understanding of what the evaluation conversation will cover.

**3. Are board members playing their roles in the process?**

The single biggest failure I see in superintendent evaluation processes is delegation to a committee. The board creates an evaluation committee. The committee does the work. The full board shows up in June, reads the evaluation summary, and either approves it or argues about it.

The problem isn't the committee. The problem is the assumption that four board members can represent the evaluation observations of seven board members. The full board needs to be involved in the evidence gathering — not in reading every document, but in contributing observations and questions throughout the five-month window.

The February check-in is the moment to ask: Is every board member engaging with the evidence? Are board members documenting their observations? Is there a mechanism for board members to submit questions or input to the evaluation committee?

I recommend a simple practice: every board member submits a one-page observation memo on the first of each month from February through May. Not an evaluation — an observation. "Here's what I'm seeing in the data reviews. Here's what I'm observing about board-superintendent communication. Here's a question I'd like the evaluation to address."

Those monthly memos are collected by the evaluation committee chair and filed in the evidence folder. By May, the evaluation committee has five to seven member memos per month — a rich, real-time record of board observations. The June evaluation doesn't rely on anyone's memory. It relies on documentation.

**What the timeline looks like from here**

Let me give you the February-through-June evaluation timeline that I've seen work in multiple districts.

| Month | Evaluation Action | What It Produces |
|-------|-------------------|------------------|
| **February** | Evidence collection check-in + emerging questions | Confirms the evidence file is building; names the questions the evaluation will address |
| **March** | Board member observation memos begin | Monthly one-page observations from each board member; filed in evidence folder |
| **April** | Mid-year governance check-in includes evaluation component | Board reviews whether the evaluation process is on track during the April governance self-assessment |
| **May** | Factual accuracy check | Evaluation committee sends preliminary findings to superintendent for factual review |
| **June** | Full board evaluation adoption | Board reviews, discusses, and adopts the evaluation |

Every month from February through May has a defined action. No month is skipped. No month requires more than the defined action.

**The difference between a process and a scramble**

I want to be direct about what's at stake here. The superintendent evaluation is the single most consequential governance action your board will take this year. It communicates to the superintendent, the staff, and the community what the board values. It determines whether the board-superintendent partnership continues on the same terms or needs to change. And it's the tool through which the board holds itself accountable — because the evaluation is about how well the superintendent executed the goals the board set.

A board that does the work between January and June produces an evaluation that reflects the full year. A board that waits until May to start collecting evidence produces an evaluation that reflects whatever everyone remembers from the past four months — and memory is not reliable.

The February check-in is the difference between those two outcomes. Fifteen minutes. Three questions. And your board stays on track for a June evaluation that produces clarity instead of controversy.
