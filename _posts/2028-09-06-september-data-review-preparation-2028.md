**September Data Review Preparation — Setting Up October's First Data Review**

September 6, 2028

---

Your last data review was probably in May or June. Your next one is October. That is a four-month gap — and in that gap, data systems drift, indicators stale, and new board members remain data-illiterate without anyone noticing until they are sitting in the October review unable to interpret the dashboard.

I have watched boards walk into October data reviews blind. The data is late. The format changed over the summer. The new board members have never seen a trend line. And no one realized any of this was a problem until the superintendent said, "Here are the numbers," and half the board did not know what to ask.

September is not the month to wait for October to arrive. September is the month to set October up.

Here is what the board should be doing right now.

**1. Run the data system readiness check**

Before October's data review can produce actionable information, the data must exist in a usable form. Do not assume it does. Districts change their student information systems, switch assessment vendors, and lose institutional knowledge over the summer. Ask the superintendent's team these four questions:

- Which data systems are we pulling from this fall — and are they all operational?
- Who is responsible for extracting and formatting the data for the board?
- Has the board dashboard been updated with current-year enrollment and staffing data?
- What is the verification process — who checks the numbers before the board sees them?

If the answers are vague, the October review will be vague. The data system readiness check is not an IT exercise. It is a governance discipline. If the board does not know where the data comes from, the board cannot trust the data when it arrives.

**2. Set up the year-over-year comparison structure**

The single most useful thing a board can do in September is align the fall indicators with the spring baselines.

Here is what I see boards get wrong: they carry every spring indicator into the fall without asking which ones still matter. Some indicators answered their question in the spring — you know the trend, you acted on it, you do not need to track it in October exactly the same way. Other indicators need to be replaced because the intervention changed over the summer.

Walk through each spring indicator from your May and June data reviews. For each one, ask:

- Does this indicator still measure progress toward our current goals?
- Does the baseline established in spring give us a fair comparison point for fall?
- Should this indicator be carried forward, replaced, or retired?

The answer does not have to be complicated. It just has to be intentional. A year-over-year comparison structure set up in September will tell you in October whether your summer and early-fall actions moved the needle.

**3. Run the indicator refresh protocol**

This is the companion to the year-over-year check. Some indicators carry over. Some do not. Some need recalibration because the data source changed, the population shifted, or the goal itself was refined over the summer.

The indicator refresh protocol has three steps:

- **Verify source data.** Confirm that the denominator, the time window, and the calculation method match the spring baseline. A changed calculation produces a false comparison. If the state changed its assessment between spring and fall, that matters — and you need to know about it now, not in October.
- **Update thresholds.** If your spring data review revealed that a threshold was too high or too low to be useful, adjust it before October. Do not waste an October meeting debating a threshold you already know is wrong.
- **Add fall-specific indicators.** There are indicators you only care about in the fall — enrollment stability, early attendance patterns, fall benchmark assessment results. Add them to the dashboard now so October is the first look, not a scramble to create them the night before.

**4. Prepare the board's data literacy**

This is the one most boards skip, and it is the one that matters most.

If you have new board members who joined after the spring data review cycle, they have never seen the board's data dashboard. They do not know how to read the trend lines. They do not know what is a meaningful change versus random variation in the context of your district. And they will not ask in the October meeting. They will sit quietly and hope no one notices.

September is the time for a board data literacy session. It does not have to be formal. Thirty minutes before a regular meeting, or a voluntary workshop. But the content must be specific to your board's actual dashboard. Do not train them on abstract data concepts. Train them on your district's literacy data, your attendance data, your discipline data — the actual indicators they will see in October.

The goal is not to make board members data analysts. The goal is to make them informed consumers of the district's data. Those are different things, and the distinction matters.

**The tie that binds**

These four steps share something important: none of them requires a board meeting to execute. The board president and data committee chair can run the readiness check with the superintendent's team. The indicator refresh can happen in a thirty-minute working session. The data literacy session can be scheduled as a board development hour.

What it does require is intention. October will arrive whether you prepare for it or not. The question is whether you will walk into that room knowing your data systems are live, your indicators are aligned, your comparisons are valid, and your board can read the numbers.

Do not wait until October to discover what you should have verified in September.
