**Stop Planning Your January Agenda. Start Planning Your January Goals.**

Every December, I see the same thing.

Boards across the country start drafting their January board meeting agenda. Committee assignments. Public comment rules. Consent agenda items. A resolution or two. Maybe a board reorganization for the new year.

And that's it. They spend hours on logistics and zero time on what matters most: what they actually want to accomplish in 2027.

I understand why it happens. January feels like a fresh start, and the natural instinct is to get organized — to establish the mechanics of how the board will operate before you figure out what it will do. It's the governance equivalent of buying a filing cabinet before you know what you're filing.

But here's the problem with that order of operations: your January agenda will write itself once you have clear 2027 goals. And without those goals, the agenda is just a list of procedures.

## The Right Starting Point

Most boards approach January backward. They decide when to meet, how long the meetings will be, who serves on which committee. Then they figure out what they want the board to focus on — and by then, the structure has already constrained the strategy.

I work with a board in Texas that used to spend its entire January meeting on reorganization — electing officers, appointing committee chairs, setting the meeting calendar for the year. They'd leave the meeting knowing who was on the facilities committee and which Tuesday of the month they'd meet. They would not have been able to tell you a single student outcome goal for the year.

That's not a board meeting. That's an administrative session with a quorum.

Here's what I want you to try instead. Before you finalize a single item on your January agenda, answer three questions:

1. What specific student outcome goals are we setting for 2027?
2. What data will tell us whether we're making progress?
3. What decisions do we need to make in January to start moving toward those goals?

Answer those three questions first. The agenda — committees, meeting schedule, public comment structure — fits around the answers.

## The Three-Step Goal-Setting Framework

I've watched boards get stuck on goal-setting because they make it too complicated. A strategic plan with twenty-three objectives and a five-year timeline. A survey of every stakeholder group. Consultants and committees and retreats that stretch into spring.

Here's a simpler approach. Three steps. One board work session. Done.

### Step 1: Identify Your Baseline

Before you can set a goal for where you want to go, you need to know where you are. Pull the most recent student outcome data your district has available. Not the narrative reports — the numbers.

- What percentage of third graders are reading at grade level?
- What's your current chronic absenteeism rate?
- What's the math proficiency trend over the last three years?

Your baseline isn't a judgment. It's a starting point. If the data is bad, you know where you need to focus. If the data is good, you know what to protect. Either way, you can't set a meaningful goal without it.

### Step 2: Set Your Target

Pick three to five student outcome measures that matter most for your community. Not everything — no board can track twenty metrics with discipline. The three to five that tell you whether students are better off than they were the year before.

For each measure, set a specific, measurable target for 2027.

Not "improve reading scores." That's a wish, not a goal.

Instead: "Increase third-grade reading proficiency from 62% to 70% by June 2027."

That's a goal. It's specific. It's measurable. It has a deadline. It creates accountability.

### Step 3: Define Your Data Cadence

This is the step most boards skip. You set the goals in January, and then you don't look at the data again until June when the state test scores come out. By then, it's too late to adjust anything.

Decide now: how often will the board review progress data?

Every board meeting is the standard I recommend. Put a data dashboard item on every agenda. Five minutes. The superintendent reports the three to five metrics. The board asks questions. The board identifies whether adjustments are needed.

Monthly data reviews are what separate boards that govern from boards that meet.

## The January Agenda That Builds Itself

Once you have your goals, here's what your January board meeting agenda looks like:

**Public comment.** The community gets to speak. Standard.

**Goal overview.** The board presents its 2027 goals to the community. Not a PowerPoint with fifty slides. A one-page summary. Here's what we're committing to, here's why it matters, here's how we'll measure it, and here's when we'll report back. This is the moment where governance becomes visible to the public.

**Data review.** Present the baseline data for each goal. The community sees where the district stands. The board demonstrates it understands the starting point.

**Budget priority direction.** The board gives the superintendent clear direction on what the 2027-28 budget priorities should be, based on the goals they just set. This is where goal-setting meets resource allocation.

**Action items for February.** What does the board need to do next month? Approve the meeting calendar? Schedule the next data review? Begin superintendent evaluation planning? Put it on the list.

That's it. Four substantive items plus public comment. No committees. No procedural filler. No meeting for the sake of meeting.

## What January Is For

Here's what I want you to remember as you close out December: January isn't the month to figure out what your board is going to do. January is the month to start doing it.

The boards that spend December on goal-setting walk into January ready to govern. The boards that spend December on agenda logistics walk into January ready to meet.

There's a difference. The community can feel it. And the students — the ones whose education hangs on your board's ability to focus on what matters — they're the ones who benefit when you get it right.

So put down the committee roster and the consent agenda template. Pick up the student outcome data and a blank sheet of paper.

Start with the goals. Everything else follows.
