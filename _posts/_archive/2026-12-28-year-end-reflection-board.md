Between Christmas and New Year's, when the email traffic slows and the board meetings pause, there's something every board owes its community.

Not a press release. Not a compliance document. An honest accounting of the year that was.

I call it an accountability snapshot — a public document that answers four questions:

1. **What did we promise?** (Our goals at the start of the year)
2. **What did we deliver?** (Progress data for each goal)
3. **Where did we fall short?** (Honest acknowledgment of gaps)
4. **What are we prioritizing in 2027?** (Forward-looking commitment)

Montgomery County Public Schools does this exceptionally well. They publish an annual governance report that includes self-assessment results, superintendent evaluation overview, and a goal progress dashboard. The report is public. It's accessible. It doesn't hide the hard parts.

Most districts don't do this. Most districts issue a year-end press release that reads like a highlight reel — accomplishments listed, challenges glossed over, future plans vague. And most communities read those press releases the way they read political campaign mailers: they don't trust them.

A genuine accountability snapshot takes a different approach.

**The four-quadrant framework**

Let me walk you through the structure of the year-end reflection. I call it the four-quadrant framework because it covers the four dimensions of governance accountability.

**Quadrant 1: Goal Progress**

Start with the goals. Not a summary — the actual goals, with numbers.

"In January 2026, this board adopted three student outcome goals:
- Increase third-grade reading proficiency from 62% to 70% by June 2028.
- Reduce chronic absenteeism from 9% to 5% districtwide by June 2027.
- Increase the four-year graduation rate from 84% to 88% by June 2028.

Here is our progress on each goal as of December 2026:
- Third-grade reading: 65% (up 3 points, on track)
- Chronic absenteeism: 7% (down 2 points, need to accelerate)
- Graduation rate: 85% (up 1 point, on track)"

This is the section where the board shows its work. No narrative spin. The numbers tell the story.

**Quadrant 2: Governance Accountability**

This is the harder quadrant. It asks: *how did the board itself perform this year?*

Not how the district performed. How the *board* performed.

- Did the board complete its self-assessment? Yes or no.
- Did the board conduct a goal-aligned superintendent evaluation? Yes or no.
- Did the board provide timely preliminary budget guidance? Yes or no.
- Did the board protect meeting time for strategic discussion? Yes or no.
- Did the board maintain goal continuity through the election cycle? Yes or no.

These are yes-or-no questions. They should be. The board either did these things or it didn't. And the community deserves to know.

**Quadrant 3: Honest Gaps**

This is where most boards stop. But honest reflection requires a section that names what didn't go well.

"Our chronic absenteeism goal is behind schedule. The schools we targeted showed improvement, but we didn't have the resources to scale the intervention districtwide. The board did not review attendance data as consistently as we should have during the spring semester. We are reworking the approach for 2027."

Here's what I've learned: the community doesn't expect perfection. They expect honesty. A board that publicly names where it fell short earns more trust than one that pretends everything went perfectly.

**Quadrant 4: 2027 Priorities**

Close with forward-looking commitments. What will the board prioritize in the coming year? What new goals will be set? What governance practices will the board improve?

"In 2027, this board will:
- Adopt updated student outcome goals by March 31.
- Complete a self-assessment by June 30.
- Conduct a goal-aligned superintendent evaluation by December 15.
- Hold four community forums across all attendance zones.
- Report publicly on progress at each quarterly board meeting."

Specific. Measurable. Public.

**Why this matters for community trust**

October's Community Engagement & Trust arc was about earning trust. This year-end reflection is about keeping it.

Trust isn't built by saying the right things at board meetings. It's built by demonstrating that you take accountability seriously enough to share the hard numbers — the wins and the losses — with the people you serve.

If your board publishes a year-end accountability snapshot, you send a signal to your community: *we know we're accountable to you. We're not hiding anything. We want you to hold us to what we've committed to.*

If your board doesn't publish one — or publishes a press release instead — you send a different signal.

**The deadline**

Post this before January 1. Use the district website, the board newsletter, a community email. It doesn't have to be long. It needs to be honest.

And if you missed doing this for 2026, commit to it for 2027. Before the new year starts, add "January 2028: publish annual accountability snapshot" to your governance calendar. Then you don't have to remember to do it next December. It's already scheduled.
