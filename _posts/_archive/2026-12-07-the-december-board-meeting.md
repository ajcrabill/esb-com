**The December Board Meeting: Your Last Chance to Finish the Year Strong**

I've been in December board meetings that lasted forty-five minutes. And I've been in December board meetings that lasted four hours and reshaped the entire following year.

The difference wasn't the agenda length or the district's budget. It was whether the board treated December as a genuine governance checkpoint — or as a holiday courtesy to get through before real work resumed in January.

Here's what I need you to understand: your December board meeting is your last chance to finish the year strong. If you don't use it well, you don't get another shot until the new year. And by then, January is gone.

## The Meeting Most Boards Get Wrong

November gave you a clear picture of where your board stands. You reviewed your goals. You assessed your performance. You checked your governance continuity plan. You have the seven-action checklist from the November 30 piece — goal verdicts, data cadence, self-assessment, evaluation prep.

That information is powerful. But without action, it's just expensive trivia.

Your December meeting is where you turn that information into decisions. If your December agenda doesn't reflect the work you did in November, then the accountability arc was an exercise, not a reset.

## What Your December Board Meeting Must Include

Not suggestions. Requirements — if you want to walk into January ready to govern.

### 1. A Year-End Progress Report with Verdicts

The superintendent presents a formal year-end report on every student outcome goal from 2026. Specific data for each goal, with a clear verdict: achieved, on track (continuing into 2027), or abandoned.

Pay attention to how your board handles the abandoned ones. Most boards avoid the direct language. They say "circumstances changed" instead of "we stopped paying attention." Acknowledge the gap honestly. The only goals that fail are the ones where the board stopped monitoring them. Document the verdicts — this becomes your January goal-setting foundation.

### 2. Board Self-Assessment Results — Discussed, Not Filed

If your board completed a self-assessment during November, the December meeting is where you review the results as a team. Not a PDF in the agenda packet that nobody reads. A real discussion: thirty minutes on the agenda.

Answer two questions: what did we do well this year that we need to protect, and what is one thing we need to improve that will make the biggest difference for 2027? Two things. Write them down. You've just turned a compliance exercise into a governance practice.

If you haven't completed the self-assessment, do it before the December meeting. Not in January. The self-assessment loses its power the moment you delay it.

### 3. Superintendent Evaluation Prep — Framework, Not Surprises

December's agenda should confirm the evaluation timeline, the criteria (goal progress, professional standards, board-superintendent agreement, or a combination), the evidence that will be used, and the meeting structure. Not a vote item. A process alignment conversation.

The superintendent should leave the December meeting knowing exactly what the evaluation looks like and when it happens. That's not weakness. That's good governance.

### 4. Lock In the January Goal-Setting Date

The December meeting should end with a board that knows exactly when it will set 2027 goals. Put it on the calendar before you adjourn.

The best format is a two-hour January work session — separate from the regular meeting — dedicated to goal setting. The agenda: year-end verdicts from the progress report, current data on student outcomes, gap analysis, and goal drafting.

If you don't leave December with a January goal-setting date, you'll spend the first two months of 2027 trying to find a time that works. By then, February is gone.

### 5. A Community-Facing Moment

A brief public reflection: what the board committed to this year, what it achieved, where it fell short, and what it's working on next year. Not self-congratulatory. Honest.

The community doesn't expect perfection. They expect honesty. A board that publicly acknowledges where it fell short and commits to doing better earns more trust than one that acts like everything went perfectly.

## The Agenda That Changes Everything

Here's what a strong December agenda looks like:

- Public comment (15 min) — community engagement
- Year-end progress report and goal verdicts (30 min) — where do we stand?
- Board self-assessment discussion (30 min) — governance team improvement
- Superintendent evaluation process alignment (15 min) — no surprises
- Goal-setting work session preview (10 min) — lock in the January date
- Year-end community acknowledgment (5 min) — honest reflection
- Consent agenda and routine items (10 min) — what must be done

Total: under two hours. Every item drives the board toward a stronger 2027.

## The Choice

Your November checklist gave you seven actions to close the year. The December board meeting is where you execute the most important one: Action 6 — Plan Your December Board Agenda. Not a thin agenda. A strategic agenda.

The boards that use December well don't just have a better January. They have a better entire year. The reset — honest goal review, self-assessment, evaluation prep, goal-setting planning — creates a foundation that January builds on.

The boards that don't? They spend Q1 figuring out what they could have figured out in December.

Your December board meeting is your last chance to finish the year strong. Don't waste it.
