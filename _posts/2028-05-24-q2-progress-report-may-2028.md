**Q2 Progress Report — Are You on Track for Year-End Goals?**

2028-05-24

---

Over the past three weeks, your board has done the hardest work of the spring cycle.

You reviewed the spring data. You asked whether your budget allocations produced measurable outcomes. You aligned on what the superintendent evaluation should emphasize.

Now it is time to consolidate everything into one document.

The Q2 Progress Report is the board's formal year-end projection. It takes every piece of evidence the board has gathered — three data points per goal, allocation-outcome findings, the evaluation focus memo — and consolidates them into a single goal-by-goal status report.

Here is why this document matters.

The Q2 Progress Report is the document the board will use to close out the governance year. It is the document the board will reference in the June superintendent evaluation. It is the document the board will hand to the incoming board members who join in July and need to understand where the district stands on every goal. And it is the document the board will use to set goals for the second half of the year — because governance does not stop at June 30.

Let me show you the exact format.

---

**The seven-element goal status format**

For each adopted goal, the Q2 Progress Report includes seven elements. Every board member reads the same seven elements for every goal. No variation. No skipped columns. Seven elements, every time.

**Element one: The goal statement.**

The goal as written in January — the exact language, the measurable target, the timeline. The board should not rewrite the goal in May. The goal is the goal. This element reminds every reader what the board committed to.

**Element two: The three data points.**

Fall baseline, winter growth, spring benchmark. Three numbers in sequence. No commentary. No interpretation. The data in its raw, sequential form so every reader can see the trajectory.

**Element three: The year-end projection.**

The projected year-end outcome based on the spring trajectory. This is the numerical projection — if the trend continues at the current rate, where will the goal land on June 30? This element answers the question: Are we on track?

**Element four: The allocation-outcome finding.**

The Budget Outcome Review category from the May 10 analysis — expected outcome achieved, partially achieved, or not achieved — and the dollar amount allocated. This element answers the question: Did the resources work?

**Element five: The board's maintain-adjust-escalate decision.**

The decision the board made during the May 3 data check-in, recorded as a discrete action. The board either maintained the current course, directed an adjustment, or escalated to a structural conversation. This element answers the question: What did the board decide?

**Element six: The board's written finding.**

A two- to three-sentence finding that states the board's assessment of the goal's status. Not a narrative paragraph. A concise finding that can be read in thirty seconds and tells the reader exactly where the board stands on this goal.

Here is an example from a board I worked with in **Topeka (KS)** :

> "Third-grade reading proficiency: Spring data shows 48 percent proficiency against a year-end target of 55 percent. The projected year-end outcome is 50 percent. The board confirms the current allocation and intervention strategy are producing steady progress. The board maintains the current course and directs the superintendent to report on whether the program can be sustained at the current funding level in 2029."

**Element seven: The next steps.**

The specific action the board or superintendent will take between now and the June closeout. Not a general intention. A specific action with an owner and a timeline.

Examples:
- "Superintendent will present the Q3 budget adjustment proposal at the June board meeting."
- "Board president will include the evaluation focus on this goal in the June superintendent evaluation conversation."
- "Superintendent will report on the expanded intervention timeline at the June closeout."

---

**How the board uses the Q2 Progress Report**

The Q2 Progress Report is not a document the board files away. It is a document the board uses in three specific ways.

**Use one: The June superintendent evaluation.**

The Progress Report serves as the evidence base for every evaluation conversation. When the board discusses a goal where the district is on track, the board references the Progress Report. When the board discusses a goal where the district is off track, the board references the Progress Report. The Report eliminates the gap between what board members remember and what the data says.

**Use two: The year-end closeout.**

The June closeout arc — which starts the week after this piece — takes the Q2 Progress Report and uses it to produce the board's year-end governance findings. The board will not need to re-review the spring data in June. The data is already consolidated, analyzed, and recorded in the Progress Report. The June closeout builds on it.

**Use three: The transition to the next governance cycle.**

The July and August arc — which your board will preview next week — starts with the goals from this year and asks whether they should be continued, revised, or replaced for the next cycle. The Q2 Progress Report tells the board which goals achieved their targets, which goals are worth continuing, and which goals need structural redesign.

A board that produces the Q2 Progress Report enters the summer with clarity. A board that does not produce it enters the summer with open questions that will linger through the August board retreat.

---

**A warning about the Q2 Progress Report**

I have watched boards produce versions of this report. Some handle it well. Some do not.

Here is the warning.

The Q2 Progress Report is a board document. The superintendent provides the data — the three data points, the year-end projection, and the allocation-outcome analysis. The board writes the findings and the next steps.

I have watched boards delegate the findings to the superintendent. The superintendent writes the findings and brings them back to the board for approval. The board reviews them, makes small edits, and approves them.

That approach defeats the purpose of the report.

The superintendent's job is to provide the evidence. The board's job is to write the interpretation. When the board writes the findings, the board owns the assessment. When the superintendent writes the findings, the board is approving the superintendent's self-assessment — which is a fundamentally different governance action.

I have watched a board in **Liberty (MO)** handle this distinction well. Their board president says to the board at the start of the Q2 Progress Report meeting: "We are not reviewing the superintendent's findings. We are writing our own. The superintendent's data is on the table. Our interpretation goes in the report."

That framing changes the entire meeting.

---

**What the board produces from the Q2 Progress Report**

The board produces one document: the Q2 Progress Report, with the seven-element status for each adopted goal.

This document goes into the board packet for the June board meeting. It is referenced in the June superintendent evaluation. It is shared with the board president and the superintendent as the foundation for the year-end closeout conversation.

One document. Seven elements per goal. Every member reading the same assessment.
