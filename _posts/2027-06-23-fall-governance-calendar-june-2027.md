**Building the Fall Governance Calendar — Summer Work That Produces a September Running Start**

June 23, 2027

---

The spring governance cycle is closed. The superintendent evaluation is complete. The board self-assessment is finished. The summer work plan — reading, training, policy development, and next-cycle planning — is approved. The board has everything it needs. Now comes the question that determines whether the summer produces results or drift.

The question is not: what will the board do this summer?
The question is: what will the board have ready by September?

The difference between those two questions is the difference between summer activity and summer preparation. A board that asks the first question spends the summer reading books, attending training, and writing policies. Those are all good things. But a board that asks the second question spends the summer building toward a specific outcome: a fall governance calendar that turns the board's preparation into a running start.

Here's what the fall governance calendar should include and how the board should build it during the summer.

**What the fall governance calendar covers**

The fall governance calendar runs from September through December. It covers the first half of the next governance year. It should include:

**Goal-setting or goal-review work session.** Most boards set goals in January. But the groundwork for January goal-setting happens in the fall. The board should schedule a fall work session — October or November — to review the previous year's goals, collect baseline data, and prepare for the January goal adoption. The work session should be scheduled early enough that the board has time to gather the data it needs.

**First data review.** The fall data review — typically October or November — establishes the data cadence for the new governance year. The board should decide on the metric set, the data format, and the review schedule before the first data review, not during it. That decision happens in the fall calendar planning.

**Budget cycle start.** The budget cycle for the next fiscal year starts in the fall. The board should schedule the budget guidance work session — where the board gives the superintendent direction on the priorities that should drive the proposed budget — no later than November. If the board waits until January to give budget direction, the superintendent has already been working on the budget for two months without board guidance.

**90-day superintendent check-in.** The board agreed to a check-in date during the June evaluation meeting. That date goes on the fall calendar. The check-in evaluates the superintendent's progress on the growth areas identified in the June evaluation and assesses whether the board has delivered on its support commitments.

**Organizational meeting preparation.** If the board has an organizational meeting in the fall — officer elections, committee assignments — the calendar should include the preparation work: the officer nominating process, the committee assignment discussions, and the committee charge reviews.

**How the board builds the fall calendar during the summer**

The fall calendar should not be built in August, when the superintendent and board members are re-entering the work rhythm. It should be built in June or July, when the spring governance cycle is fresh and the board has maximum planning time.

The board president or a designated calendar planning committee should draft the fall calendar based on:

**The summer work plan.** The reading board members do, the training they complete, and the policies they develop will shape what the board is ready for in the fall. If the board is reading a book on goal-setting, the fall calendar should include a discussion of the book's implications for the board's goal-setting process. If the board is developing a new data review policy, the fall calendar should include an adoption vote.

**The self-assessment findings.** The gaps the board identified in the June self-assessment inform what the fall calendar emphasizes. If the board identified data review format as a gap, the fall calendar should include a data review training session before the first fall data review. If the board identified board communication discipline as a gap, the fall calendar should include a board communication protocol review.

**The superintendent evaluation commitments.** The growth areas and support commitments from the June evaluation create specific calendar obligations. If the board committed to providing data format expectations by August 15, that becomes a calendar item. If the board committed to a 90-day check-in in October, that goes on the calendar.

**The governance calendar template from the previous year.** The spring cycle calendar — which was audited in the May governance closeout — is the board's best template. The calendar audit showed which events happened on schedule and which slipped. The fall calendar should be adjusted to reflect those lessons. If the data review was scheduled too close to the goal-setting session, the fall calendar should spread them out.

**What a complete fall calendar looks like**

Here's what a four-month fall governance calendar looks like, with the preparation work integrated:

| Month | Event | Preparation needed |
|---|---|---|
| Late August | Board president and superintendent review summer work | Confirm summer reading notes ready, training completed, policy drafts prepared |
| September | Board work session — book discussion and policy review | Board members come with reading takeaways; policy committee presents drafts |
| September | Organizational meeting (if applicable) | Officer and committee assignments prepared |
| October | 90-day superintendent check-in | Board support commitments reviewed; growth area progress assessed |
| October | Fall data review | Metric set and data format confirmed; data packet deadline set |
| November | Budget guidance work session | Goal baseline data collected; budget guidance document prepared |
| December | Goal-setting preparation work session | Previous year goal outcomes reviewed; baseline data assembled |
| December | Board self-assessment (strategic) | Full-year governance evaluation; next-year priorities identified |

**What the board should produce before the summer break**

Before board members leave for summer, the board should have:

A draft fall governance calendar — with dates and events filled in, even if some are tentative pending confirmation with the superintendent.

A clear assignment for who prepares each item on the calendar — typically the board president, the evaluation committee chair, and the board clerk or designee.

A confirmation that the superintendent has reviewed the fall calendar and it does not conflict with major district events.

The board that produces these three things before the summer break enters September knowing what it needs to do. The board that waits until August to think about the fall calendar enters September trying to figure out what comes next while the first board meeting of the new governance year is already on the calendar.
