**2028 Goal Adoption Preview — What Your Board Should Be Preparing Right Now**

December 14, 2027

---

Last week I walked you through the five things your December board meeting must accomplish. The first was closing the 2027 governance cycle. The second was previewing the 2028 goal-setting process.

This week I want to go deeper on that second item — because the difference between a board that adopts real, measurable goals by February and a board that still has "draft goals" on the April agenda is decided in the six weeks between mid-December and the end of January.

Here is what your board should be preparing right now.

---

**What your board should be preparing right now: The data that matters.**

January is the board's best month for goal-setting. The 2027 annual data is available. The fall semester data is fresh. The winter assessment windows have closed. The board has a full year of Progress Monitoring data from the October and November reviews.

But data does not organize itself. Someone has to decide what data matters for goal-setting, gather it in a usable format, and present it to the board in a way that supports a decision.

Here is what that looks like in practice.

Your superintendent should have on the January board agenda a 2028 Goal Data Packet that includes:

- The 2027 baseline data for each goal. The board needs to see where the district started in 2027 and where it ended. Improvement over the course of the year is the measure of progress, not the raw score against an aspirational target.
- The current trajectory for each goal area. If the district was improving in third-grade reading at a consistent rate in 2027, the board needs to see that trend line projected into 2028. If the district was flat in algebra readiness for three years, the board needs to see that, too.
- The student group disaggregation. A goal that looks good on the aggregate but reveals a fifteen-point gap between Black and white students is not a goal that has been achieved. The board needs to see the student group data alongside the aggregate data — not buried in an appendix that no one reads.
- The resource implications. What would it take to accelerate progress in a goal area that is improving but not fast enough? What would it take to turn around a goal area that is flat? The superintendent should not be expected to have a fully developed proposal in the data packet, but the superintendent should identify what resource questions the board will need to answer in the budget process that follows goal adoption.

I have watched districts like **Wichita (KS)** prepare this packet well. Their January board packet includes a one-page "Goal Data Dashboard" for each goal area — baseline, current, trend, and student group breakdown — that the board reviews before the January organizational meeting. The dashboard is not the full data set. It is the executive summary that every board member reads and understands. The full data is available for the board members who want to go deeper.

Your board can ask for the same format. Tell the superintendent now — in December — that the January packet needs a one-page dashboard for each goal area. The superintendent needs that direction before the holiday break to have the data ready for the January meeting.

---

**What your board should be preparing right now: The community input process.**

Here is where I see boards lose the most time.

A board decides in late January to adopt goals. The board drafts the goals in a February work session. The board adopts the goals in March. But the board never asked the community what it thinks the district should prioritize.

Then the community pushes back. Not because the goals are wrong. Because the community was not part of the conversation.

The board that builds community input into the goal-setting process from the beginning does not waste March explaining why it adopted goals the community did not help shape.

Here is the timeline that works.

December to January: The board publishes the 2027 year-end governance report and invites community input on 2028 priorities. This can be as simple as a survey link at the end of the report, a dedicated "community priorities" item on the December or January board meeting agenda, or a fifteen-minute public hearing at the January meeting.

February: The board publishes the draft 2028 goals for public review. The community sees what the board is considering. The community has a formal window — twenty-eight days is standard — to respond.

March or April: The board adopts final 2028 goals, incorporating community input, at a regular board meeting with the community present.

The total community input period: ninety to one hundred twenty days. Not one meeting where the public is invited to comment on a final draft that has already been written.

Your board should decide the community input timeline now — in December — and communicate it clearly. What month will the board seek input? What format? Public hearing? Survey? Board meeting agenda item? The community cannot respond to an invitation that has not been extended.

---

**What your board should be preparing right now: The superintendent's work plan.**

The superintendent cannot build a responsive 2028 goal set if the board has not communicated what it needs and when.

Here is the conversation the board president — or the board as a whole, at the December meeting — should have with the superintendent.

By what date does the board need the 2028 Goal Data Packet? If the board is adopting goals in February or March, the data packet should be in the board packet at least two weeks before the goal-setting work session.

What format does the board want the data in? If the board wants a one-page dashboard for each goal area, say that explicitly. Do not let the superintendent guess.

What level of specificity does the board expect in the draft goal language? "Improve student achievement" is not a goal. "Increase the percentage of third-grade students reading at or above grade level from 62 percent to 70 percent by May 2028" is a goal. The board should communicate the standard before the superintendent drafts.

Does the board want the superintendent to propose targets, or does the board want to set the targets as a board? I recommend the superintendent proposes the targets based on the data. Then the board evaluates whether the targets are ambitious enough, realistic enough, and aligned with the board's strategic direction. The board that sets targets in isolation from the data produces aspirational goals that the superintendent cannot operationalize.

Get the superintendent clear direction in December. The superintendent who knows what the board expects by December 15 is preparing the data packet on December 16. The superintendent who is still guessing what the board wants in January is already behind.

---

**The connection back to the baseline-gathering work.**

If your board has been working through the fall arc, this December preparation connects directly to the baseline-gathering work from October and November.

October's first full data review established the current trajectory for each goal area. November's goal-setting groundwork — for boards without adopted goals — produced draft goal language based on real baseline data, not assumptions.

December's goal-setting preview closes the loop. The baseline data from October becomes the starting point for the 2028 data packet. The draft language from November becomes the superintendent's starting point for the formal proposal.

If your board has not done the baseline gathering, start now. The data is still available. The community input window can still open. But do not skip the baseline work because December feels late. A January goal with real baseline data behind it is worth more than an August goal with good intentions.

---

**The bottom line.**

Goal-setting is not an annual event that happens in February. It is a three-month process that starts in December.

The board that gathers the data, plans the community input, and communicates the superintendent's work plan in December will adopt goals that are measurable, publicly supported, and operationally feasible by March.

The board that waits until February to start asking what data it needs will adopt goals in May — or not at all.

The difference is decided now.
