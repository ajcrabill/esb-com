**The Community Progress Report — Publishing It Is Not the Final Step, Responding to What Comes Back Is**

August 9, 2027

---

The board started the Q2 community progress report in July. The data was collected. The goal-by-goal progress was drafted. The governance highlights were written. The challenges and adjustments section was prepared.

Now it is August. The report needs to be published. And there is a decision the board needs to make before pressing publish.

**The decision that determines whether the report is governance or communication**

Most boards think of the community progress report as a communication exercise. The board did work. The board wants the community to know about it. So the board publishes a report that says, essentially, "We are making progress. Things are going well. Thank you for your support."

That is not governance. That is public relations.

A governance community progress report does something different. It tells the community what the board committed to — the goals adopted in January — and then it shows the data. The actual data. The progress that was made, the progress that was not made, and what the board is doing about the gap.

A governance community progress report is uncomfortable. Because it requires transparency when the data is not what the board hoped it would be. And that is precisely why the community needs to see it.

**The publication checklist**

Before the board publishes the Q2 report, four items should be confirmed:

**One: The data is current.**

The report should include the most recent data available for each goal metric. If the board adopted a goal in January that targeted a specific literacy rate by December, the report should show the January baseline, the most recent assessment data, and the gap between current performance and target performance.

If the most recent data is from April and the board is publishing in August, the board needs to ask the superintendent for a mid-summer data update. A Q2 report that uses Q1 data is not a Q2 report.

**Two: The board's governance actions are documented alongside the data.**

The community does not just need to see the numbers. The community needs to understand what the board did that affected those numbers. Did the board adopt a policy that changed instructional practice? Did the board approve a budget adjustment that directed resources to the goal area? Did the board hold the superintendent accountable for progress in the evaluation conversation?

If the board's governance actions are not included in the report, the community sees data without context. And data without context can be confusing or — worse — misleading.

**Three: The challenges section is honest.**

Every board has goal areas where progress is slower than expected. The community progress report should name those areas directly. Not as failures, but as areas requiring attention, adjustment, or additional resources.

A community progress report that only reports progress is not a progress report. It is a highlight reel. And the community knows the difference.

**Four: The distribution plan is confirmed.**

The board should decide before publication: who receives the report, in what format, and with what follow-up opportunity. The report should go to every community stakeholder group that has a stake in the board's goals — parents, teachers, community organizations, local media. The format should be accessible — a one-page summary with a link to the full report. And the follow-up should be scheduled — a community forum, a board meeting public comment period, or a dedicated Q&A session.

**What happens after publication**

This is the part most boards miss.

The community progress report goes out. The community reads it. And then the community responds.

The response may take any of these forms: questions about the data, concerns about the gaps, appreciation for the transparency, or frustration with the pace of progress.

The board needs to be ready for all of them. Not defensively ready — ready to listen, ready to learn, and ready to adjust.

Here is the protocol for responding to community feedback on the progress report:

**Phase one — Acknowledge.** Within 48 hours of receiving feedback, the board president or a designated board member should acknowledge the feedback directly. Not with a detailed response. With a simple acknowledgment: "Thank you for your question. We are reviewing the data and will provide a response at the next board meeting."

**Phase two — Review.** The board should review the feedback at the next board work session or meeting. The questions should be categorized: data clarification questions (what do these numbers mean?), process questions (how was this data collected?), concern questions (why is progress slower than expected?), and suggestion questions (have you considered X approach?).

**Phase three — Respond.** The board should provide a public response at the next regular board meeting. The response should address each category of feedback. Data clarification questions should be answered with additional context. Process questions should be answered with the data collection methodology. Concern questions should be answered with the board's plan for addressing the gap. Suggestion questions should be answered with the board's consideration process.

**Phase four — Document.** The feedback and the board's responses should be documented and included in the Q3 report. The community needs to see that their input informed the board's work. Not "we heard you" — that is a communication phrase. "Based on the questions raised at the August board meeting, the board directed the superintendent to provide additional literacy intervention data at the October data review" — that is governance language.

**Why this matters for the September launch**

The community progress report is the board's most visible governance action of the summer. It tells the community that the board is governing with purpose, tracking progress against goals, and being transparent about both successes and challenges.

When the board enters September with a published community progress report and a documented community feedback response, the board enters with credibility. The community knows the board is not waiting for September to start governing. The board has been governing all summer.

The board that publishes the Q2 report in August and responds to the feedback at the September board meeting enters the fall governance cycle with the community's trust. The board that publishes the report and moves on enters September with the community wondering whether the board actually cares about the response.
