**The Community Progress Report — Publishing It Is Not the Final Step, Responding to What Comes Back Is**

August 9, 2027

---

The board started the Q2 community progress report in July. The data was collected. The goal-by-goal progress was drafted. The governance highlights were written. The challenges section was prepared.

Now it is August. The report needs to be published. And there is a decision the board needs to make before pressing publish.

**The decision that determines whether the report is governance or communication**

Most boards treat the community progress report as communication — the board did work, so the board tells the community about it. That is not governance. That is public relations.

A governance report does something different. It tells the community what the board committed to — the goals adopted in January — and then it shows the actual data. The progress made, the progress not made, and what the board is doing about the gap.

A governance community progress report is uncomfortable. Because it requires transparency when the data is not what the board hoped it would be. That is precisely why the community needs to see it.

**The publication checklist**

Before publishing the Q2 report, four items should be confirmed:

**One: The data is current.** The report should show the most recent data available for each goal metric. If the board adopted a goal targeting a specific literacy rate by December, the report should show the January baseline, the most recent assessment data, and the gap to target. If the most recent data is from April and the board is publishing in August, ask the superintendent for a mid-summer update. A Q2 report that uses Q1 data is not a Q2 report.

**Two: The board's governance actions are documented alongside the data.** The community does not just need to see the numbers. They need to understand what the board did that affected those numbers — policy changes, budget adjustments, superintendent accountability in the evaluation. Data without context can be confusing or misleading.

**Three: The challenges section is honest.** Every board has goal areas where progress is slower than expected. Name those areas directly. Not as failures, but as areas requiring attention and additional resources. A report that only reports progress is a highlight reel, and the community knows the difference.

**Four: The distribution plan is confirmed.** Decide before publication who receives the report, in what format, and with what follow-up opportunity. The report should reach every stakeholder group. The format should be accessible — a one-page summary with a link to the full report. And the follow-up should be scheduled — a community forum, board meeting comment period, or dedicated Q&A session.

**What happens after publication**

This is the part most boards miss.

The report goes out. The community reads it. And then the community responds — with questions about the data, concerns about the gaps, appreciation for the transparency, or frustration with the pace of progress.

The board needs to be ready for all of it. Not defensively — ready to listen, learn, and adjust.

Here is the protocol for responding to community feedback:

**Phase one — Acknowledge.** Within 48 hours, the board president or a designated member should acknowledge the feedback directly. Not a detailed response — a simple acknowledgment: "Thank you for your question. We are reviewing the data and will respond at the next board meeting."

**Phase two — Review.** At the next board work session, categorize the feedback: data clarification questions, process questions, concern questions, and suggestion questions. Each category requires a different type of response.

**Phase three — Respond.** At the next regular board meeting, address each category. Data questions get additional context. Process questions get the methodology. Concern questions get the board's plan. Suggestion questions get the board's consideration process.

**Phase four — Document.** Include the feedback and responses in the Q3 report. The community needs to see their input informed the board's work — not "we heard you," but "based on questions raised at the August meeting, the board directed the superintendent to provide additional literacy data at the October review." That is governance language.

**Why this matters for the September launch**

The community progress report is the board's most visible governance action of the summer. It tells the community that the board is governing with purpose, tracking progress against goals, and being transparent about both successes and challenges.

When the board enters September with a published community progress report and a documented feedback response, it enters with credibility. The community knows the board has been governing all summer.

A board that publishes the report in August and responds to feedback at the September meeting enters the fall with trust. A board that publishes and moves on enters September with the community wondering whether the board actually cares about the response.
