**Budget Season Is Coming: Start Planning Your 2027-28 Priorities Now**

December 16, 2026

**Series:** Year-End Governance Reset & 2027 Planning

---

Most school boards don't set budget priorities. They react to them.

The budget document lands on your desk in March or April. Maybe earlier if you're lucky. It's a hundred pages of line items — salaries, benefits, supplies, transportation, facilities, technology. Each number has a story behind it that you don't have time to learn.

So you ask a few questions. You push back on a couple of increases. You approve it. And then you cross your fingers that the priorities you talked about at your goal-setting retreat somehow made it into the actual spending.

Here's the truth: they didn't. Not because the superintendent is hiding something. Because priority-setting happened too late.

## The December Window

School districts start building their next fiscal year budget in late winter or early spring. The finance team starts running numbers in January. The superintendent's initial guidance goes out to department heads in February. By March, the big decisions — staffing allocations, program funding, capital investments — are already baked in.

If you wait until the budget lands on your desk to set priorities, you're not setting priorities. You're reacting to someone else's.

December is the window. The budget cycle hasn't started yet. The department heads haven't submitted their wish lists yet. The finance team hasn't run the projections yet. You have a brief opening — right now, in December — to tell the superintendent: "Here's what we care about. Build the budget around that."

## The Three Budget Alignment Questions

Every board I work with gets the same three questions about their budget. And most of them can't answer any of them.

### Question 1: Does This Spend Move Us Toward Our Goals?

This sounds simple. It is not. Because most districts' spending doesn't align with their stated goals.

I worked with a district that had a public commitment to improving early literacy. They had the goal in their strategic plan. They talked about it at board meetings. They put it on the district website.

But when we looked at their budget, they were spending less on early literacy interventions than they were on administrative overtime. Not because anyone was being dishonest. Because nobody had connected the budget process to the goal-setting process.

Here's the question: if you took each major budget line item and asked "does this spending move us toward our 2027 student outcome goals?" — how many of them would you answer yes to?

If the answer isn't most of them, you have a budget alignment problem.

### Question 2: Does This Spend Maintain What's Working?

Not everything needs to change. Some programs are producing results. Some investments are paying off. The question is whether you know which ones.

Most boards can't tell you which programs in their district are actually effective. They can tell you which programs have been around the longest. They can tell you which programs have the most vocal supporters. They can't tell you which ones improve student outcomes.

Your December priority-setting should include a simple assessment: what's working that we need to protect? If you don't name it, it might not survive the budget process — not because anyone opposes it, but because nobody defended it.

### Question 3: Does This Spend Reflect Our Community's Values?

This is the question most boards never ask, because the answer can be uncomfortable.

Your community told you what they care about. Maybe it was in a community survey. Maybe it was in public comments at board meetings. Maybe it was in the election results. They told you: we want safe schools, rigorous academics, equitable opportunities, and transparent decision-making.

Does your budget reflect those values?

I've seen districts where the community's number one priority was school safety, and the board's budget allocated less to safety improvements than to landscaping. I've seen districts where the community wanted expanded career and technical education, and the board's budget cut CTE funding because it was the easiest line item to reduce.

The budget is a values document. Every line item is a statement about what the board believes is important. The question is whether those statements match what you told the community you believe.

## The Board's Budget Guidance Letter

Here's a practical tool you can use this month.

Write a one-page budget guidance letter to your superintendent. It doesn't need to be formal or legalistic. It just needs to answer three things:

1. **Our 2027 goals.** Here are the student outcome goals we've set. The budget should prioritize resources that move us toward these goals.

2. **Our guardrails.** Here are the things we will not cut: programs that are producing measurable results, investments that support our stated priorities, resources that serve our most vulnerable students.

3. **Our questions.** Here's what we want to see in the budget presentation: goal-by-goal spending breakdown, program effectiveness data, multi-year trend comparisons.

That's it. One page. Sent in December. The superintendent gets clear direction before the budget process starts, and you get a budget that actually reflects your priorities.

## What Happens When You Do This

I've watched districts transform their budget process by taking December seriously.

One district I worked with sent their budget guidance letter in mid-December. The superintendent and finance team had it before they started running numbers. When the preliminary budget came to the board in March, every major line item was annotated with how it connected to the board's stated goals. The board could look at the budget and see their priorities in it — not because they'd demanded it, but because they'd set the direction early enough for the team to build around it.

That's the difference between priority-setting and reacting. Priority-setting means you tell the system what you want before it starts moving. Reacting means the system tells you what it's already done and you nod along.

## The Work Starts Now

Your goals are set. Your scorecard is defined. Now it's time to make sure your dollars follow your priorities.

December is the window. Not January, when the budget process has already started. Not March, when the document is already in your hands. December.

The budget guidance letter you write this month will shape the next fiscal year more than any conversation you have between January and June. Because it sets the direction before the direction is set for you.

So grab a sheet of paper. Answer the three questions. Write the letter.

The community didn't elect you to react to budgets. They elected you to set the priorities that budgets follow.
