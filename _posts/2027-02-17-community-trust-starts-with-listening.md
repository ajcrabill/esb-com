**Community Trust Starts with Listening: How to Hold Your Board's First Community Session of 2027**

February 17, 2027

---

February has been an execution month. Budget review. Data review. Evaluation check-in. Policy audit. Board-superintendent relationship reset. Every piece of the February arc has been about getting the board's internal governance right.

But there's a piece of the governance equation that doesn't happen inside the boardroom. It happens in the community.

Here's what I've seen over and over. A board runs a strong January — goal adoption with a public hearing, budget direction with community input, a transparent evaluation cycle launch. The community sees the board working. Trust builds.

Then February arrives. The board goes internal. Budget details. Data analysis. Policy reviews. The community stops hearing from the board. The next time the community sees the board is March or April — and by then, the board is explaining decisions instead of inviting input.

The boards that sustain community trust are the boards that stay in conversation with the community — not just during the high-profile January events, but during the execution months as well.

**Why February is the right moment for a community listening session**

The relationship reset piece on February 15 focused on the board-superintendent relationship. This piece focuses on the board-community relationship. And the timing is not a coincidence.

February is the moment because you have something to report. You've completed your first data review. You've reviewed the proposed budget. You can show the community what you've been doing with the goals they heard about in January. You're not showing up empty-handed. You're showing up with evidence of governance work.

But more importantly, February is the moment because the budget isn't final yet. If you hold a community listening session in February, you can hear what the community prioritizes before the budget is adopted in April or May. The input you gather can still shape the final allocation.

Contrast that with a listening session in April. The budget is practically done. The community's input arrives after the allocation decisions have been made. The session becomes a theater of listening — the board hears concerns, but can't act on them.

February is the moment for input that matters.

**How to structure a governance listening session — not a town hall**

I want to make a distinction. Most boards hold town halls. A town hall is an open forum where community members stand at a microphone and address the board. The format is familiar. It's also the least effective format for building trust.

Here's why. In a town hall, the most passionate voices dominate. The board spends its energy responding to individual concerns. The format rewards advocacy over dialogue. And the community walks away feeling like the board heard the loudest voices — not the broadest concerns.

A governance listening session is different. It's structured. It's focused. And it produces actionable input.

Here's the structure I recommend.

**Before the session: set the topic and gather input in advance**

A listening session needs a topic. Not a free-for-all. The topic for February is natural: "Our board's goals for 2027 and the proposed budget to support them."

Send a one-page summary of the goals and budget highlights to every community member who attends — and post it online for those who can't. Include the data from your February progress monitoring review. Show the community what you've done since January.

Then, gather input in advance. I recommend a brief survey — three questions — sent to the school community two weeks before the session. The questions:

1. "What's your top priority for student success this year?"
2. "What do you think the board should know about how our schools are serving your student?"
3. "What question do you want the board to address at the listening session?"

The advance survey does two things. It tells the board what the community is thinking about before you walk into the room. And it gives community members who can't attend a way to contribute their voice.

**During the session: listen first, respond second**

Here's the agenda for a ninety-minute governance listening session.

| Time | Activity | Purpose |
|------|----------|---------|
| 10 min | Board president opening | Frame the session: "We're here to listen. We've adopted goals, reviewed data, and received a proposed budget. Now we want to hear from you before we make final decisions." |
| 5 min | Data and goals overview | Superintendent or board president shares the one-page summary: Here are our goals, here's what the data shows so far, here's the budget timeline |
| 45 min | Structured small-group discussion | Community members discuss in groups of 6-8 at tables, with a trained facilitator at each table. Each group discusses: "What do these goals look like in your student's experience? What would tell you the board is making progress?" |
| 15 min | Table reports | Each facilitator reports three themes from their table — not names, just themes |
| 15 min | Board response | Board president responds to the themes: "Here's what we heard. Here's how it connects to what we're working on. Here's what we'll take back to the board table." |

Notice what's not on this agenda. There's no open microphone. There's no individual advocacy. There's no board member defending decisions. The structure is designed for listening — and the small-group format ensures that quiet voices are heard alongside passionate ones.

I've watched this format work in a district where the board had been losing community trust for two years. The first structured listening session was tense. Community members arrived skeptical. But by the end, the board president could name three concrete concerns the community had raised that the board hadn't considered. The board adjusted its budget direction. The community saw the adjustment. Trust started to rebuild.

**After the session: close the loop**

The most important part of a listening session happens after the session ends. Here's what your board needs to do within thirty days.

First, publish a summary of what you heard. Not a transcript — a synthesis. "At our February 17 listening session, community members raised four themes: concerns about reading intervention access, support for the math goal, questions about transportation funding, and interest in more frequent communication from the board."

Second, name what the board will do with each theme. "Reading intervention access: we asked the superintendent to include an access report with the March progress monitoring data. Transportation funding: this will be a topic for the March budget alignment discussion. More frequent communication: we'll establish a monthly board update starting in March."

Third, schedule the next session. The community needs to know this is a rhythm, not a one-time event. I recommend quarterly listening sessions — February, May, August, and November. Each session has a natural topic that aligns with the governance calendar.

**What this looks like in a district that gets it right**

I coached a district that built its entire community engagement model around structured listening sessions. The board was in the second year of a turnaround and had adopted aggressive goals. The community was skeptical — previous boards had adopted goals that disappeared after the first year.

The board held its first February listening session using the structured format. They heard two things they hadn't expected: parents wanted more transparency about which schools were implementing the adopted reading intervention with fidelity, and the business community wanted to partner on career readiness but didn't know who to contact.

The board responded. They added an implementation fidelity report to the March data review. They directed the superintendent to create a single point of contact for community partnerships. And they published a "You Spoke, We Listened" summary within ten days.

The next listening session had twice the attendance. The board had earned trust by closing the loop.

**The cost of not listening**

I want to be direct about the alternative. Boards that don't hold listening sessions don't lose community trust all at once. They lose it gradually — one closed meeting, one unexplained decision, one missed opportunity at a time. And by the time the board needs community support — for a bond referendum, a tax increase, or a difficult consolidation decision — the trust isn't there.

The February listening session is the board's chance to build the trust it will need before it needs it. The budget isn't final. The goals are fresh. The community is paying attention.

Show up. Listen. Close the loop. That's how trust gets built — one session at a time.
