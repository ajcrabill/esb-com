**The Christmas Present Your Community Actually Wants**

December 23, 2026

**Series:** Year-End Governance Reset & 2027 Planning

---

I've been thinking about what communities really want from their school boards this time of year.

It's not more meetings. You've already had enough of those.

It's not more policies. Your policy manual is thick enough.

It's not more process, more reports, more committee assignments.

I think what your community actually wants for Christmas is simpler than any of that.

They want to know you have a plan.

They want to know that the board — the seven (or five, or nine) people elected to oversee their children's education — knows where it's going. That there's a direction. That the meetings aren't just meetings, that they add up to something.

That's it. That's the Christmas present they actually want.

## The Gift of Clarity

Think about what it feels like to be a parent in a district where the board doesn't have clear goals. You show up to board meetings and hear reports about things that happened but never a clear statement about what the board is trying to achieve. You read the strategic plan and find generic language — "educational excellence," "student success," "community partnerships" — that could mean anything or nothing. You vote in board elections based on campaign promises, and then you never hear another word about those promises until the next election cycle.

That's not trust. That's drift.

Now think about what it feels like to be a parent in a district where the board has done the December governance reset. Where the board can tell you — in one page — what they're trying to accomplish in 2027, how they'll measure it, and when they'll report back.

That's not a press release. That's a promise.

The difference between those two experiences is the difference between a board that meets and a board that governs. And the community can feel it.

## What You Can Give Them

I'm not suggesting you wrap a strategic plan and put it under the tree.

I'm suggesting something simpler. I want you to write — today, this week, before the holiday break — a one-page Board Commitment Statement for 2027.

Here's what it says:

"To our community: In 2027, this board commits to improving [specific outcome] from [baseline] to [target]. We will track our progress by reviewing [specific data] at every board meeting. We will report back to you in [month] with an update. Here are the three things we will prioritize to make this happen."

That's it. One page. No jargon. No strategic plan boilerplate. A promise that the community can hold you to.

## The Work Is Already Done

Here's the good news: if you've been following this December arc, you've already done the work.

The Year-End Governance Reset (Dec 7) showed you how to review the data and decide what needs to change.

The Goal-Setting framework (Dec 9) gave you a three-step process for setting 2027 targets.

The Scorecard (Dec 14) helped you define how you'll measure progress.

The Budget Priorities piece (Dec 16) showed you how to align resources with goals.

The Year-in-Review (Dec 21) helped you understand what worked and what didn't in 2026.

All that's left is to write it down. One page. A commitment to your community.

That's the gift.

## What the Community Does With It

When your community opens their inbox and sees a clear, specific, measurable commitment from their school board — not a holiday greeting, not a year-in-review newsletter, a real promise — something shifts.

They stop wondering whether the board knows what it's doing. They start watching to see if the board keeps its word.

And that watching — that accountability — is exactly what makes boards better.

So here's my ask. Before you close your laptop for the holiday break, write that one-page commitment. Send it to your community. Not as a press release. As a letter. From the board. Signed.

You might think it's too simple to matter. But I've seen communities transform when they finally get a straight answer from their school board about what it's trying to achieve. It's not the gift they expected. It's the gift they needed.

Merry Christmas. Now get back to work. There's a community waiting for you to tell them where you're going.
