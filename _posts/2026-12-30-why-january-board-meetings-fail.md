**Why January Board Meetings Fail (And How Yours Won't)**

December 30, 2026

---

I've sat through more January board meetings than I care to count. And I can tell you with confidence: most of them fail.

Not in the legal sense. No open meetings act violations. No improper executive sessions. They fail in the strategic sense. They fail because they don't accomplish anything that moves the district forward.

Here's what a failed January board meeting looks like. A two-hour agenda with thirty minutes of strategic content and ninety minutes of process. A superintendent report that covers every department and resolves nothing. A board discussion that touches on seventeen topics and commits to zero. A room full of people who leave feeling like they just spent an evening being busy without being productive.

And here's the hard truth I tell every board I work with: the problem isn't January. The problem is December.

Your January board meeting is already determined. It was determined by whether you used the past four weeks to plan — to set goals, build your scorecard, align your budget priorities, and design your first ninety days. If you did that work, your January meeting will be the most productive meeting of the year. If you didn't, your January meeting will be directionless.

The choice is already made. The question is whether you know what you chose.

## The Four Reasons January Board Meetings Fail

I've identified four consistent failure modes in January board meetings. They're predictable. They're preventable. And they're all rooted in the same cause: a board that didn't use December to prepare.

### Failure 1: No Clear Purpose

The most common January board meeting failure is simple: no one — not the board president, not the superintendent, not the board members — has articulated what the meeting is supposed to accomplish.

The agenda is a default agenda. Call to order. Pledge. Public comment. Minutes. Committee reports. Superintendent report. Action items. Board comments. Adjourn. It's the same agenda as October, November, and every other month. There's nothing about it that says "this is the start of a new year."

When a meeting has no clear purpose, every agenda item becomes equally important — which means no item is truly important. The strategic work gets buried under process. The board spends two hours being busy and zero hours being strategic.

### Failure 2: No Goals to Set or Affirm

I've watched boards walk into January meetings — the first meeting of a brand new calendar year — with no goals to set, no goals to affirm, and no goals to discuss. The strategic plan gets a passing mention. The superintendent says "we're still working on our goals for this year." The board nods. And the meeting moves on.

Here's what that communicates to everyone in the room, including the community watching the livestream: accountability can wait. January doesn't matter. We'll get to the important stuff eventually.

The board that walks into January with draft goals ready for discussion and approval is an entirely different organization. The community sees a board that arrived prepared. The superintendent sees a board that knows what it wants. The board members see a path forward.

### Failure 3: No Data to Review

Name one other organization — a business, a nonprofit, a sports team — that starts a new season without looking at the data from the previous one. It would be unthinkable. You'd be making decisions in the dark.

And yet, January board meetings routinely proceed without any meaningful data review. No year-end student outcome data. No trend lines. No baseline to measure against. The board is making decisions about the future without understanding the present.

If your December work included a year-end review (Dec 21), your January meeting can start with data: here's where we ended 2026. Here's where we need to be in 2027. Here's the gap we're closing. Without that data, your January meeting is a discussion in a vacuum.

### Failure 4: No Strategic Decisions

The most damning failure mode: a January board meeting that makes no decisions that shape the year ahead.

Committees are appointed. Minutes are approved. Routine purchases are authorized. But no decision is made about the direction of the district. No goal is formally adopted. No budget priority is set. No data review cadence is established. The board goes through the motions of governance without actually governing.

Strategic decisions don't happen by accident. They happen because they're on the agenda. And they're on the agenda because someone — the board president, the superintendent, a board member — made the intentional choice to put them there. If no one made that choice in December, the decisions won't happen in January.

## What a Successful January Board Meeting Looks Like

Now let me describe what a successful January board meeting looks like. Because I've seen plenty of them. And they follow a consistent pattern.

**The agenda has one strategic item that goes first.** Not third. Not sixth. First. The board's most important work — goal-setting, goal affirmation, strategic direction — is agenda item one. Everything else is secondary.

**The board has data to discuss.** The year-end review data from December is summarized on one page. Every board member has seen it before the meeting. The conversation starts from a shared factual foundation, not from impressions and opinions.

**The board makes decisions, not just discussion.** By the end of the meeting, the board has formally approved — or at least affirmed — its 2027 goals. It has given budget direction to the superintendent. It has established a data review cadence. It has made decisions that shape the year ahead.

**The community understands what happened.** Someone summarizes the board's decisions in plain language, either in the meeting or in a follow-up communication. The community doesn't need to parse the board packet to understand what the board decided. The board tells them.

## The January Board Meeting Template

Here's a specific agenda template for your January board meeting. Use it. Modify it for your context. But don't let the default agenda steal your most important meeting of the year.

**Agenda Structure (90-120 minutes)**

- Call to Order & Pledge (5 min)
- **Strategic Item 1: 2027 Board Goals** (40 min)
  - Board president presents draft goals
  - Superintendent presents baseline data for each goal
  - Board discussion: are these the right goals? Are the targets achievable?
  - Board vote to adopt goals
- **Strategic Item 2: Data Cadence Confirmation** (15 min)
  - Board confirms which data will be reviewed at each meeting
  - Board confirms format for data presentation
  - Board confirms who is responsible for preparing data
- **Strategic Item 3: Budget Priority Direction** (20 min)
  - Board reviews alignment between goals and draft budget priorities
  - Board passes motion directing superintendent on budget priorities
- Consent Agenda (10 min)
- Public Comment (15 min)
- Board Member Comments (10 min)
- Adjournment

Notice what's different from a default agenda:
- The strategic items come first, not after an hour of process
- Every strategic item produces a decision, not just discussion
- The community sees the board acting with purpose from the first minute
