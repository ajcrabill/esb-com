**Closing the Governance Year — The Community Report That Answers 'What Did Your Board Accomplish?'**

July 5, 2027

---

The board's spring progress reports told the community about goal progress. The March report introduced the framework. The May report covered the January-through-May data. The community saw student outcome numbers, goal trajectories, and budget alignment.

Those reports answered the question: how are students doing?

A different question is coming. It may come at the August board meeting, on social media, during the candidate filing period, or at the back-to-school event. The question is: what did the board accomplish?

Not the district. Not the superintendent. Not the students. The board. What did the seven or nine or five people sitting around the dais actually do during the governance year that just closed?

The year-end community governance report answers that question.

**Why the governance report is different from the progress report**

The progress reports the board published in March and May were about district outcomes — student achievement data, goal progress, budget execution. Those reports are essential. They tell the community whether the district is moving in the right direction.

The governance report is about board outcomes. It tells the community whether the board governed with discipline and effectiveness. It answers different questions:

- How many meetings did the board hold?
- How many decisions did the board make?
- What percentage of those decisions were followed through?
- Did the board complete its own governance calendar, or did events get skipped?
- Did the board evaluate the superintendent on time and with a clear framework?
- Did the board evaluate itself and identify specific improvement commitments?
- Did the board engage the community through testimony sessions, progress reports, and listening opportunities?
- Did the board complete its summer work plan — reading, training, policy development?
- What changed in board governance practices as a result of the year's work?

These are not questions most boards answer publicly. Most boards publish student outcome data and assume the community will connect the outcomes to the board's governance work — but the connection is invisible to anyone who isn't in the boardroom.

The governance report makes the connection visible.

**What the governance report includes**

The governance report should be a public-facing document — posted on the district website, shared in the newsletter, submitted to the local news outlet, and presented at a regular board meeting. It should be no more than two pages. The community should be able to read it in ten minutes.

Here's what it includes.

**Governance activity summary.** One paragraph and one table. The paragraph states the governance year timeframe (January through June or whatever cycle the board operates on) and the board's adopted governance framework. The table shows the numbers: regular meetings held, work sessions held, governance calendar events scheduled versus completed, recorded decisions made, decision follow-through rate, board member attendance rate, community engagement events held, and community participation numbers.

The numbers should be honest. A board that scheduled twelve governance events and completed nine publishes eighty percent fidelity. A board that decided forty-two items and tracked thirty-seven publishes eighty-eight percent follow-through. The numbers are not about making the board look good. They are about demonstrating that the board measures its own performance.

**Governance improvement commitments.** A short section summarizing the board's self-assessment findings and the specific governance practices the board committed to improve in the next cycle. The community sees that the board conducted a self-assessment, identified specific gaps, and made specific commitments. Not "we will communicate better" — but "we will publish agenda packets five business days before each meeting" and "we will review decision follow-through at the start of each meeting."

**Summer work plan.** A section showing what the board committed to during the summer break — the reading plan, training assignments, policy development work, and fall calendar preparation. The community sees that governance does not stop when school is out.

**Fall governance preview.** A forward-looking section showing the community what the board has planned for the fall governance year. The fall calendar highlights — first data review date, budget guidance work session date, goal-setting timeline, community engagement events. The community sees the board's agenda for the next six months.

**The board's governance scorecard.** The one-page scorecard from the closeout work session, appended as a summary graphic or reference. The scorecard captures the full governance year at a glance — the board's goals, its governance fidelity, its community engagement reach, its decision-making discipline, and its improvement commitments.

**Who prepares the report and when**

The board president or the board governance committee prepares the draft using the data from the closeout work session, the superintendent evaluation, and the board self-assessment. The draft should be ready within two weeks of the closeout work session — typically mid-July. The board reviews the draft at the July or early August study session and votes to publish it as an official board document.

The vote matters. The board is not publishing a staff report or a consultant's summary. The board is publishing its own governance record, approved by the full board, available to the community, and referenced in the board's own documentation system. When a community member asks what the board accomplished, the board points to the governance report.

**What happens when the board doesn't publish a governance report**

The community that doesn't receive a governance report from the board fills the information gap with whatever is available — meeting clips on social media, superintendent updates, rumors, candidate statements during election season. The board's governance work becomes whatever people say it was, because the board hasn't defined it.

The board that publishes a governance report owns its narrative. The board says: here is what we did, here is how we measured ourselves, here is where we fell short, and here is what we committed to improve. The community gets the full picture, and the board positions itself as a body that takes its own governance seriously enough to report on it publicly.

That is the standard the community deserves. And it is the standard the board should hold itself to.
