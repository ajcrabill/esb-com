In December I told you the difference between a board that adopts goals by March and a board that is still drafting in May is decided in the six weeks between mid-December and the end of January.

Welcome to the end of January.

Your organizational meeting is complete. Your officers are elected. Your committee charges are set. Now the 2028 goal adoption process begins — and it moves fast.

Here is how the next sixty days should unfold — from the data packet on the January agenda to the final vote in March.

---

**The data packet has landed. Your job is to read it critically.**

If your board followed the December playbook, your superintendent delivered a 2028 Goal Data Packet on the January agenda. It includes the 2027 baseline data, current trajectory, student group disaggregation, and resource implications.

The packet for a January goal-adoption work session should contain, at minimum:

- **A one-page Goal Data Dashboard for each goal area.** Baseline, current, trend line, and student group breakdown. Every board member reads and understands this before the meeting. If the dashboard reveals a flat trajectory in algebra readiness or a fifteen-point gap between student groups, that is the conversation your board should be having — not whether the font on page thirty-seven is readable.

- **The superintendent's proposed goal language for each area.** The superintendent should present draft goals with specific, measurable targets based on the data. "Increase the percentage of third-grade students reading at or above grade level from 62 percent to 70 percent by May 2028" — not "improve student achievement."

- **The resource memo.** The superintendent should identify what it would take to achieve each proposed target. New staffing? Extended learning time? Curriculum adoption? The resource memo is not a budget proposal. It is the superintendent's best professional judgment about what the goal requires, so the board can have an honest conversation about feasibility before — not after — it adopts the goal.

Your board's job during the data packet review is to ask the right questions. Not to accept the packet as final. Not to rewrite it in the meeting. To ask questions that surface what the board needs to know to make a decision.

Questions like these:

- "This trajectory shows we improved by 4 points last year and the superintendent is proposing a 6-point target. What is the basis for accelerating the rate of improvement?"

- "The disaggregated data shows our Hispanic students are at 54 percent proficient and our white students are at 71 percent. Does the proposed goal address this gap directly, or is it an aggregate target that could be achieved without closing the gap?"

- "The resource memo says we would need to add three reading interventionists to reach this target. Is that a one-time investment or a recurring cost, and when would the board need to decide?"

The board that reads the data packet in the packet — not from the dais — and prepares these questions in advance will have a productive work session. The board that opens the packet for the first time at the meeting will spend the session catching up instead of deciding.

---

**The public hearing: your community's opportunity — and your board's credibility test.**

Most states require a public hearing as part of the goal adoption process. But I want to talk about the public hearing that matters, not the public hearing that checks a box.

A box-checking public hearing looks like this: the board president opens the floor, three people speak for three minutes each, the board thanks them and moves on. The community feels heard in theory and ignored in practice.

A public hearing that builds trust looks different. Here is the format I recommend.

The board opens with a fifteen-minute presentation summarizing the data. Not the full packet — the highlights. "Here is where we were in 2027. Here is where we are now. Here is what the data tells us about where we need to go." The community should understand the context before it is asked to contribute.

Then the board opens the floor for questions and input — not three-minute speeches limited to a predetermined topic. The board president facilitates. The superintendent answers data questions. The board listens.

Then the board takes fifteen minutes of public deliberation — on the record — showing the community how it is processing what it heard. "I heard concern about the algebra readiness target. Let's discuss whether we need to revisit that trajectory before the February work session."

Here is what this format produces that the box-checking format does not: the community sees the board listening, discussing, and adjusting. That is what builds trust — not the hearing itself, but the visible evidence that the hearing changed something.

Your board should schedule the public hearing for the January regular meeting or a dedicated work session. Announce it in the January board packet. Publish the data summary on the district website at least one week before. The community cannot respond to an invitation that has not been extended — and the community cannot prepare for a hearing without the data.

---

**Board deliberation: the work session where goals become real.**

The public hearing is input. The board work session is where the board decides.

Here is the deliberation protocol I use with boards.

One goal at a time. The superintendent presents the proposed goal — the language, the target, the timeline, the resource implications. The board reviews the goal against four questions:

1. **Is the goal specific?** Can we measure it at the end of the year and know, with certainty, whether we hit it?

2. **Is the target ambitious enough?** Does it require improvement beyond the current trajectory, or is it a continuation of the status quo?

3. **Is the target realistic enough?** Does the superintendent's resource analysis support achieving it, or are we adopting a goal we cannot fund?

4. **Does the goal advance equity?** Does the target require closing gaps between student groups, or does the aggregate target allow the gaps to persist?

The board discusses the goal. The superintendent provides the data behind the answer to each question. The board revises the goal language as needed. The board votes to advance the goal to the public comment draft.

Then the board moves to the next goal.

The entire work session — four to six goals, including the deliberation protocol and revisions — takes two to three hours. It is the most important board conversation of the first quarter.

Here is what I watch for as a facilitator.

The board that debates targets without looking at the data will negotiate ambition down to a number everyone is comfortable with — and the goal will not require improvement. The board leader's job is to say, "Let's look at the trajectory before we adjust. What does the data say is possible?"

The board that adds goals without removing goals produces a wish list, not a set of priorities. My recommendation: three to five goals, and rank them. The board that knows its top priority will resource it first. The board that treats all six equally will resource none well enough.

---

**The timeline: from data packet to final vote.**

Here is the sixty-day calendar that works.

**January:** Board receives and reviews the data packet. Board holds public hearing. Board conducts the goal-adoption work session. Board directs the superintendent to publish draft goals based on the work session outcomes.

**February:** Superintendent publishes draft goals for public review. The twenty-eight-day public comment window opens. Board holds a second work session — usually February's regular meeting or a dedicated session — to review the public comment and finalize goal language.

**March:** Board adopts final 2028 goals at a regular board meeting with the community present. The vote is the culmination of two months of data review, public input, and board deliberation. The community that participated in January sees its input reflected in the language the board adopts in March. That is how trust is built over a single goal-setting cycle.

If your board is on this timeline, the January organizational meeting gave you the foundation. The goal adoption work session this week — or next week, if you have not scheduled it yet — is the critical decision point.

Schedule the work session now. Announce the public hearing now. Tell the superintendent the board expects the final draft goals by February 1.

The board that moves through this process with purpose will adopt goals that are measurable, publicly supported, and operationally feasible by March.

---

**The connection to what comes next.**

Once the board adopts goals — or even as the board is finalizing the language — the progress monitoring cycle begins. The January data packet is not just the starting point for goal-setting. It is the baseline for the first 2028 progress monitoring check-in.

Next week I will walk you through that first check-in: what to measure, what data sources to use, what discussion protocol produces the most useful board conversation, and what follow-up actions the board should expect after a progress monitoring session.

The board that adopts goals in January and checks progress in February is the board that stays ahead of the issues — not behind them.

---

**The bottom line.**

Goal adoption is not a single board meeting in March. It is a sixty-day process that starts the moment your board receives the data packet.

The board that reads the packet critically, holds a real public hearing, deliberates goals one at a time against data, and follows a disciplined timeline will adopt goals that drive improvement.

The board that skips the public hearing, debates all six goals at once without the data, and waits until March to start will adopt goals in May — or will not adopt at all.

The difference is decided in January.
