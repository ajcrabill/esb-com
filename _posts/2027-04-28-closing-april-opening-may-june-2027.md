**April's Work Sets Up the Rest of the Year — Here's What Comes Next in May and June**

April 28, 2027

---

The April arc is closing. Let me step back and name what this month produced.

Your board navigated the budget public hearing — not as a procedural requirement, but as a trust-building event. You prepared the community with a published progress report. You briefed the board on the hearing protocol. You designated spokespersons and ran the simulation. The community walked out understanding how the budget was built and why.

Your board conducted the mid-year governance check-in — not as a formality, but as a performance review. You evaluated five indicators with three months of data. You identified which February adjustments worked and which needed structural change. You committed to specific adjustments for the second half of the governance year.

Your board set up post-adoption budget monitoring — not as an afterthought, but as an infrastructure decision. You agreed on the reporting cadence, the goal-alignment report format, the variance thresholds, and the oversight assignment. Your board enters the new fiscal year with the monitoring system in place.

And your board closed the loop on community testimony — not by ignoring it, but by acknowledging it, investigating it, and acting on it. The community learned that participating in the hearing produced a visible response.

That's a full month of governance. The boards that complete this April arc are the boards that enter the remainder of the governance year with a foundation that most boards don't have.

**The April-to-May connection**

The spring governance arc is designed so that each month's work creates the conditions for the next. April's work sets up May in four specific ways.

The public hearing and community testimony protocol created a community engagement record that the board can reference all year. When the board needs community input on the strategic plan, the facilities discussion, or the superintendent evaluation, the community has recent evidence that their input matters.

The mid-year check-in produced specific adjustments that the board will test through May and June. The board knows what changed and what persisted. The board knows which members carry which governance indicators through the end of the year. The board has documented commitments that will be evaluated at the year-end check-in.

The post-adoption budget monitoring setup ensures that when the board reviews May's data, the budget-to-actual information is available in the same format. The board reviews student outcome data and resource allocation data together — and can answer the question of whether the resources are producing the trajectory the board committed to.

The community testimony response created a documentation standard that the board can extend to all community engagement. Every interaction — not just hearings — can be documented, acknowledged, and acted on.

**What May requires**

May's governance calendar has two main events.

The second full data review. February's first data review established the cadence. May's data review checks whether the board's direction produced results. The board reviews student outcome data for each adopted goal, compares it to the trajectory, and makes recorded decisions about adjustments. The budget-to-actual report — set up in April — is now part of the data review, so the board asks not just "are students on track?" but "are the resources allocated to this goal producing the expected results?"

The continuation of the superintendent evaluation process. The evaluation began with the February 90-day check-in. The board identified areas for growth and support. The superintendent has been working on those areas. May is the midpoint between that check-in and the June final evaluation. The board should schedule a second check-in — less formal than the February one, structured around the specific areas the board identified.

**What June requires**

June's governance calendar has two main events and one preparation task.

The superintendent evaluation. The board has five months of data, five months of recorded decisions, and the mid-year check-in findings to incorporate. The evaluation is a governance document — not a personnel document — that connects the superintendent's performance to the board's adopted goals and the district's progress toward them.

The year-end progress update. The March progress report was the first. June's report covers the full first semester and sets up the summer work. The community sees the full spring governance arc in one document — what the board set out to accomplish and whether it made progress.

The summer planning preparation. The June board meeting should include a discussion of the summer governance work — what the board will read, what training it will complete, what policy development it will pursue, and what preparatory work will set up the next governance year. The board that plans its summer in June is the board that enters the fall already running.

**The full year so far**

Let me close by naming what the January-through-April Governance to Impact arc has produced.

January: Goals adopted. Governance calendar locked. Superintendent evaluation process established.

February: First data review with recorded decisions. Budget review for goal alignment. Superintendent 90-day check-in. Policy audit. Board-superintendent relationship reset. Listening session protocol. Governance team health check-in. Budget adoption preparation.

March: Budget alignment review using the three-question framework. Goal-to-budget crosswalk. Community progress report. Combined data review and budget alignment meeting. Trust communication strengthened.

April: Budget public hearing as a trust-building event. Mid-year governance check-in as a performance review. Post-adoption budget monitoring setup. Community testimony response closure.

That's a governance year in four months. Most boards don't accomplish this much in twelve. If your board has been following this arc, you're operating at a level that puts you in the top tier of school boards in the country.

**What the rest of the year requires**

The governance year doesn't end with June. The summer months are the board's opportunity to prepare for the next cycle — to read, to train, to plan, to build the conditions for the January-to-June arc to be even stronger next year.

A board that finishes spring strong and uses summer wisely is a board that builds a compounding governance practice. Each year builds on the previous year. Each board member gets better at their role. Each governance cycle produces stronger student outcomes.

The May-through-June arc is the sprint to the finish of the first governance year. The summer arc is the foundation for year two. The work isn't over. But the board that completes what April required has set itself up to succeed in whatever comes next.
