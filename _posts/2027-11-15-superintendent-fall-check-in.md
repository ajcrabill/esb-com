**Superintendent Fall Check-In — The November Conversation That Separates Governance from Management**

November 15, 2027

---

Here is what every board president discovers after the second round of fall data reviews.

You have the data. You have made your written decisions. The superintendent has responded to each board decision with a plan. Everything looks orderly on paper.

But something is missing.

You have not had the real conversation — the one where you ask your superintendent how they are doing. The one where you talk about what the data is not telling you. The one where you align on what the board needs to stop doing so the superintendent can focus on what matters.

That conversation is the superintendent fall check-in. And November is the right month to have it.

**Why November is the right month for the superintendent check-in**

Here is the governance calendar logic. The board conducted its first data review in October. That review was diagnostic — what do the fall benchmark assessments tell us about progress toward each goal?

The board conducted its second data review in early November. That review was comparative — how much growth between October and November? Which interventions are working? Which are not?

By mid-November, the board has enough data to make informed observations. But the board does not yet have enough data for a formal evaluation. That gap — between informed observation and formal evaluation — is exactly where the fall check-in lives.

Here is what that means. October was about the data. November is about the relationship.

The superintendent fall check-in is not a data review. It is a governance conversation. It asks: How is the partnership working? Where is the board getting in the way? What does the superintendent need from the board between now and January?

If you wait until the formal evaluation in the spring, you are having this conversation six months too late. November gives you time to course-correct. November gives you time to rebuild trust if the relationship has frayed. November gives the superintendent a clear signal about what the board values.

Your board needs to schedule this conversation before Thanksgiving. After Thanksgiving, the governance calendar shifts to year-end activities, and the window closes.

**The five-question superintendent check-in protocol**

Your board does not need a lengthy instrument for this check-in. You need five questions asked in a structured conversation. Here is the protocol.

Question one: "On a scale of one to ten, how confident are you that we will meet each of our student outcome goals by the end of the year?"

This is not a gotcha question. This is a calibration question. The superintendent's answer tells you whether your data reviews have been accurate. If the superintendent says nine but your data shows flat growth, you have a candor problem. If the superintendent says four but your data shows strong growth, you have a confidence problem.

Question two: "Where are you spending time that the board should be handling?"

This is the most important question in the check-in. Your superintendent should not be drafting board policies. Your superintendent should not be managing board communications. Your superintendent should not be preparing board meeting materials that the board president should be preparing. If your superintendent is doing any of these things, the board is governing poorly.

Question three: "What is one thing the board could stop doing right now that would free up capacity for student outcomes?"

Here is the hard truth. Every board meeting that runs past ten PM is a governance failure. Every board request for additional data that you could have asked for in the October review is a governance failure. Every special-called meeting that could have been an email is a governance failure.

Question four: "What support do you need from the board between now and January?"

The answer might be budgetary clarity. It might be board president advocacy at a community meeting. It might be a public vote of confidence at the next board meeting. Ask the question. Write down the answer. Follow through.

Question five: "Is there anything you need to tell me that you cannot say in a public meeting?"

Board presidents, this question exists for a reason. Ask it. Listen to the answer. Do not react. Do not interrupt. Just listen.

**How the fall check-in differs from the superintendent evaluation**

Here is the distinction every board needs to understand.

The superintendent evaluation is a formal, public process that happens on a defined timeline, usually in the spring. The evaluation produces a written document. The evaluation determines compensation decisions. The evaluation is the board speaking as a corporate body.

The fall check-in is a confidential, informal conversation that happens between the board president and the superintendent. It produces no written evaluation. It determines nothing about compensation. It is the board president speaking as a governance partner.

Your board needs both. They serve different purposes.

Here is what happens when boards confuse the two. Some boards skip the fall check-in entirely and rely on the spring evaluation. By the time the evaluation arrives, problems that could have been addressed in November have festered for six months. The evaluation becomes a confrontation instead of a summary.

Other boards turn the fall check-in into a mini-evaluation. They bring in the full board. They use a rubric. They assign scores. The superintendent walks out defensive instead of collaborative. The relationship erodes.

The fall check-in is not a mini-evaluation. It is a partnership check. The board president asks the five questions. The superintendent answers honestly. The board president leaves with clarity about what to stop, start, and continue.

Here is the rule. If you would not put it in a public board packet, it belongs in the fall check-in. If you would put it in a public board packet, save it for the spring evaluation.

**Documenting the check-in for board president continuity**

Board presidents turn over. The average board president serves two years. That means the institutional knowledge of the board-superintendent relationship lives in one person's notes.

Here is what your board needs to do. After the fall check-in, the board president writes a one-page confidential summary. No names. No direct quotes. Just themes, patterns, and action items.

That document belongs in a board president transition binder. When the next board president takes the gavel, they read the binder. They see that in November 2027, the superintendent said the board was spending too much time on facilities and not enough time on instruction. They see that the board agreed to stop discussing facilities at regular meetings and move those conversations to committee.

Without that documentation, the next board president starts from zero. They do not know what the board promised. They do not know what the superintendent needs. They inherit the relationship without the context.

Here is the ESB protocol. One-page document. Four sections: what went well, what needs to change, action items, and date of next check-in. Stored in a board president transition folder. Handed to the next president before they take the gavel.

Your board can do better than spring evaluations that arrive too late and fall check-ins that leave no trace. Document the conversation. Preserve the continuity. Protect the partnership.
