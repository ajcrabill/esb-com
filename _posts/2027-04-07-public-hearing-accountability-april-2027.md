**The Public Hearing Is an Accountability Event — Here's How to Treat It Like One**

April 7, 2027

---

Monday's piece opened the Spring Accountability arc by naming four spring accountability events. The first event is the budget public hearing. And the public hearing may be the most misunderstood governance event of the spring.

Most boards treat the public hearing as a procedural requirement. They schedule the minimum notice period. They give the superintendent fifteen minutes to present the budget. They open the floor for comments. They close the hearing. They vote to adopt. The entire event is structured around getting through it.

I've watched boards operate that way, and I understand the instinct. The public hearing can feel like a risk. The community might ask hard questions. A board member might say something that gets quoted in the local paper. The conversation might go sideways. The safest approach seems to be to minimize exposure — keep it short, keep it controlled, keep it procedural.

Here's what I've learned from watching the boards that do it differently. The public hearing is not a risk to minimize. It's the first spring accountability event — the moment when the board demonstrates that it owns the budget it's about to adopt. And the boards that treat it that way are the boards the community trusts with their tax dollars.

**What the public hearing actually tests**

The public hearing tests three things, and they're all different from what most boards think the hearing tests.

It tests whether the board can articulate the connection between the budget and the goals — not in the abstract, but in response to specific community questions. A board member who says "the reading intervention funding increased by 12% because our third-grade reading goal requires a 14-point gain and the trajectory analysis showed flat funding wouldn't produce it" has passed the test. A board member who says "we increased reading funding because it's a priority" has not. The community can hear the difference between a specific answer and a talking point.

It tests whether the board can speak with one voice — even when individual members have different priorities. The public hearing is where communication discipline gets its hardest test. The community is watching. The press is taking notes. The board president can't control what every member says. But the board can agree on a protocol before the hearing — who responds to what types of questions, what the board's consistent message is, and what individual members do when they're asked a question outside their designated area.

It tests whether the board can follow through — whether the community's participation produces a visible response. The community member who stood up and asked about transportation funding wants to know whether the board heard the question and what the board will do with it. The board that documents every question, acknowledges it during the hearing, and follows up with a written response within twenty-four hours demonstrates that community input matters. The board that lets questions hang in the air with no follow-through demonstrates the opposite.

**How to prepare for the hearing as an accountability event**

Preparation for the public hearing changes when you treat it as an accountability event rather than a procedural requirement.

The board president briefs the full board on the hearing protocol at least one week in advance. Every board member knows: what the opening statement will say, who responds to which types of questions, what the consistent message is, and how the hearing will close.

The board runs a hearing simulation. This is the step most boards skip, and it's the step that produces the most value. Board members take turns asking the hardest questions they can think of. The designated respondent answers. The rest of the board observes and offers feedback. The simulation reveals gaps in preparation that the actual hearing would expose — and gives the board a chance to close those gaps before the community arrives.

The board publishes a progress report or budget summary at least one week before the hearing. The community should not be learning about the board's budget decisions for the first time at the hearing. The hearing is for questions and clarifications, not for information delivery. If the board has done the work — adopted goals, reviewed data, aligned the budget, published updates — the hearing is the place to affirm those decisions. If the board hasn't done that work, the hearing becomes a crash course in budget transparency, and the community will learn in one night what the board should have communicated over three months.

**How to run the hearing as an accountability event**

The hearing itself follows a structure that matches its accountability purpose.

The board president opens with a statement that frames the hearing as an accountability event — not a presentation. Something like: "This board adopted three goals in January. Our budget alignment review in March confirmed that the budget before you funds those goals at the level the trajectory requires. Tonight, we're here to hear your questions, record your concerns, and demonstrate that this board owns the decisions it's about to make."

The superintendent's budget presentation is brief — no more than fifteen minutes — and structured around the board's adopted goals. The presentation answers: what did the board ask for, what does the budget provide, and what trade-offs did we make?

Board members respond to questions with specific references to the board's recorded decisions — not personal opinions. A board member who says "the board voted on this in February, and here's what we decided" reinforces collective accountability. A board member who says "I personally think" undermines it.

The board president closes the hearing by naming what will happen next: "Every question asked tonight will be documented. We will respond to each one in writing within twenty-four hours. The adoption vote will occur at our next regular meeting on [date]. Thank you for holding this board accountable for the decisions it is about to make."

**What happens after the hearing matters most**

The hearing itself is one event. The follow-through is what determines whether the community trusts the board.

The board publishes the documented questions and responses within twenty-four hours. This can be a simple document posted on the district website and linked in a board communication. The community sees that their participation produced a visible response.

The adoption meeting one to two weeks later includes a brief segment — ten minutes — where the board president summarizes the hearing input and names any adjustments or clarifications that resulted. The community sees that their input influenced the board's understanding of the budget, even if the budget itself didn't change.

The board that treats the public hearing as an accountability event runs a longer hearing — ninety minutes instead of sixty — but produces a community that understands the budget and trusts the board. The board that treats the hearing as a procedural requirement runs a shorter hearing but produces a community that suspects the board is hiding something.

The first spring accountability event is coming. Your board decides what kind of event it will be.
