**The Superintendent Evaluation Countdown — April Is When the Evidence Comes Together**

April 14, 2027

---

The superintendent evaluation process has been running since February. That's when the 90-day check-in established the framework — the board identified expectations, the superintendent named progress and challenges, and both sides agreed on areas for attention through the spring.

June is when the evaluation goes to a vote. The board will review evidence, deliberate, and produce a final evaluation that connects the superintendent's performance to the board's adopted goals and the district's progress toward them.

April is the midpoint between those two events. And April is where most boards either set themselves up for a clean June evaluation or create conditions for a June crisis.

**What the April check-in requires**

The April superintendent check-in is different from February's. February's check-in was the first structured conversation of the year. The board and superintendent were establishing a baseline — identifying expectations, naming priorities, and agreeing on how the evaluation process would work.

April's check-in is a progress review. The board has three months of evidence since February. The superintendent has been working on the areas the board identified. The question for April is not "what are we evaluating" — that was settled in February. The question is "are we seeing the progress the board expected?"

The April check-in should be structured around three questions.

**Question 1: Is the evidence aligned with the February expectations?**

The February check-in should have produced a written document — brief, one page — that names the board's expectations and the superintendent's commitments. Not a formal evaluation instrument. A shared reference document that both sides can point to and say "this is what we agreed to focus on."

April asks: does the evidence collected since February align with those expectations? The board should bring specific observations — not impressions — tied to board meetings, data reviews, and recorded decisions.

Here's the difference. An impression: "I feel like the superintendent has been more responsive to board requests." A specific observation: "At the March combined data and budget review meeting, the superintendent presented the resource-to-outcome analysis the board requested in February, and the analysis showed a direct connection between the reading intervention funding and the third-grade reading goal trajectory."

The April check-in is where the board tests whether its observations are grounded in evidence. If the board can point to specific instances — meetings, decisions, data presentations — the June evaluation will be straightforward. If the board can only offer impressions, the June evaluation will require a crash course in evidence collection that the board should have been doing since February.

**Question 2: What has changed since February?**

The four months between February and June always produce changes. New challenges emerge. Board priorities shift. Community concerns surface that weren't visible in February. The assessment landscape changes. State funding formulas adjust.

The April check-in is the moment to name those changes explicitly. Not to reopen the February framework but to acknowledge that the context has shifted and to determine whether the board's expectations need adjusting.

The board asks the superintendent: "Since February, what has changed that affects how you're working on the areas we identified?" The superintendent names the changes. The board discusses whether the changes require an adjustment to expectations. If they do, the board documents the adjustment so the June evaluation incorporates the full context — not just February's framework.

I've watched a board conduct an April check-in where the superintendent named a mid-year enrollment shift that required reallocating resources from the intervention program the board was evaluating. The board hadn't known about the shift. The April check-in gave the board the chance to adjust its evaluation lens — evaluating the superintendent's response to the enrollment shift rather than the original intervention plan. Without the April check-in, the June evaluation would have penalized the superintendent for a resource allocation decision that the board didn't know was necessary.

**Question 3: What does the board need to see between now and June?**

The April check-in is also the moment to name what the board needs between now and June to complete the evaluation.

Specific data presentations. A follow-up on a particular initiative. A progress update on a board direction from the March combined meeting. The board names what it needs, and the superintendent commits to delivering it.

This question is also the board's opportunity to identify gaps in its own evidence collection. If the board realizes it hasn't been documenting observations systematically since February, April is the moment to start. The board designates one member — typically the board president or the evaluation committee chair — to collect and organize evidence from April through June. Two months of systematic documentation, combined with the February framework and the April check-in findings, produces a credible June evaluation.

**What the April check-in should produce**

The April check-in should produce three things.

First, a written summary that documents the board's assessment of progress since February. Not a formal evaluation — a check-in summary that names what's on track, what needs attention, and what has changed.

Second, a list of evidence the board needs between April and June. Specific items with expected delivery dates. The superintendent knows what the board expects. The board knows what to watch for.

Third, confirmation of the June evaluation timeline. The board confirms the evaluation document deadline, the board deliberation session, and the final vote meeting date. Every board member leaves the April check-in with the same calendar.

**The cost of skipping the April check-in**

I've watched boards skip the April superintendent check-in. The February check-in was productive. The board felt good about the framework. The superintendent was responsive. The board decided the April check-in wasn't necessary — they'd collect evidence informally and complete the evaluation in June.

In June, the board discovered that three members had different assessments of the superintendent's performance. One member had been tracking specific evidence. Two members were relying on impressions — and those impressions were shaped by recent events rather than the full five months. The board spent the entire June deliberation session reconciling different accounts instead of evaluating performance. The evaluation document was generic. The board-superintendent relationship was strained.

The April check-in exists to prevent that outcome. It's a forty-five-minute conversation that aligns the board's evidence collection, checks the board's expectations against the superintendent's progress, and sets up a June evaluation that is based on data and recorded decisions rather than impressions and last-minute recollections.

The February check-in started the process. The June vote completes it. April is the midpoint that ensures the two connect.
