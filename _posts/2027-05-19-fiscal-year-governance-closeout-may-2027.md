**Fiscal Year Governance Closeout — Wrapping the Books on Year One of the Governance Cycle**

May 19, 2027

---

The fiscal year ends June 30 in most districts. The governance year ends earlier. By the time the closing entries are written and the accounts are final, the board's attention should already be on the next cycle.

The fiscal year governance closeout is not an accounting exercise. The superintendent's finance team closes the books. The board's closeout is about its own governance work — verifying that every governance event on the January-through-June calendar was completed, documenting the board's decisions and their outcomes, evaluating what the governance model produced, and preparing the records that make the next governance year more effective than the first.

**What the governance closeout covers**

The closeout has four components. Each one produces something the board needs for the next cycle.

**Component 1: The governance calendar audit.** The board's January-through-June governance calendar contained specific events with specific dates. The audit asks: did each event happen on schedule? If not, did it happen within a reasonable window? What caused any delays and what does the board need to adjust in the next calendar?

The audit is straightforward. The board president or the designated calendar tracker creates a simple table:

| Event | Scheduled | Completed | Variance | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Goal adoption | Jan 11 | Jan 11 | On schedule | |
| First data review | Feb 3 | Feb 3 | On schedule | |
| Superintendent 90-day check-in | Feb 17 | Feb 17 | On schedule | |
| Budget review | Feb 1-14 | Feb 12 | 2 days early | |
| Policy audit | Feb 22 | Mar 1 | 1 week late | Policy audit took extra meeting |
| Community progress report 1 | Mar 8 | Mar 10 | 2 days late | Board member responsible started late |
| Public hearing | Apr 5 | Apr 5 | On schedule | |
| Budget adoption | Apr 7 | Apr 12 | 5 days late | Two-step separation adopted |
| Post-adoption monitoring setup | Apr 21 | Apr 21 | On schedule | |
| Second data review | May 3 | May 3 | On schedule | |
| Superintendent midpoint check-in | May 17 | May 17 | On schedule | |
| Community progress report 2 | May 24 | | Scheduled | |

The audit tells the board two things. First, where the calendar was realistic. Events that happened on schedule indicate calendar assumptions that were correct. Second, where the calendar needs adjustment for the next year. Events that slipped — and why they slipped — inform how the board schedules the next governance year.

**Component 2: The decision record review.** Over six months of governance, the board made recorded decisions. Goal adoption decisions. Data review decisions. Budget alignment decisions. Hearing protocol decisions. Monitoring setup decisions. The decision record review asks: which decisions produced the expected results? Which decisions need revisiting? Which decisions were not executed and need to be tracked into the next governance year?

The board reviews each recorded decision from January through May and attaches a status: implemented and effective, implemented and needs adjustment, not yet implemented, or superseded by a later decision. This is the board's version of a closeout audit — not of dollars, but of direction.

**Component 3: The governance model self-assessment.** The board has been governing under a specific model for six months. The closeout asks: did the model work?

I recommend the board answer five questions in this assessment:

1. Did the board's goal-setting process produce goals that the board actually used in data reviews and budget decisions? Or did the goals sit on a shelf after January?

2. Did the board's data review cadence provide the information the board needed to make timely decisions? Or did the board discover that quarterly reviews were too infrequent or that the data format was not actionable?

3. Did the budget alignment process produce a budget that reflected the board's priorities? Or did the board discover during the year that the alignment was more aspirational than operational?

4. Did the board's community engagement strategy produce trust? Or did the board discover through the public hearing and progress reports that the community still didn't understand the board's governance work?

5. Did the board's communication discipline hold through the most demanding testing — the budget cycle and the public hearing? Or did individual members undermine the board's collective position?

The board documents the answers. The honest answers — not the comfortable ones — are the foundation for the next governance year.

**Component 4: The records handoff.** Every governance year produces documentation that the next governance year needs. The closeout organizes and packages that documentation so the board — and any new members elected in the spring — has access to it.

The records package should include: the governance calendar audit, the decision record review with statuses, the governance model self-assessment, the May data review findings and deeper review schedule, the superintendent evaluation framework from the midpoint check-in, and the community progress reports.

The board president ensures the package is stored in the board's document management system and distributed to all board members, including any newly elected members who joined after the spring cycle.

**How to run the closeout**

The governance closeout should be a single sixty-minute agenda item at the last regular board meeting in May or the first meeting in June. It is not a separate meeting.

| Time | Section | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 10 min | Calendar audit review | Board president or designated tracker presents the calendar audit table. Board confirms or corrects. |
| 15 min | Decision record review | Board president presents the decision record review. Board identifies any decisions that need tracking into the next governance year. |
| 20 min | Governance model self-assessment | Board president leads the five-question assessment. Each board member answers each question in writing before the meeting. The meeting discussion focuses on areas where there is disagreement or concern. |
| 10 min | Records handoff confirmation | Board president confirms that the records package is complete and accessible. Board identifies any documentation gaps. |
| 5 min | Connection to summer planning | Board president notes that the closeout findings inform the summer planning discussion scheduled for the next meeting. |

**What boards miss**

I've watched boards skip the governance closeout for one of three reasons.

First, they think it's the superintendent's job. It's not. The superintendent closes the operational and financial books. The board closes its own governance books. One doesn't substitute for the other.

Second, they think the end-of-year board meeting is the closeout. It's not. The end-of-year board meeting covers operational and financial closeouts — budget amendments, carryover approvals, year-end reports. The governance closeout is a separate agenda item within that meeting.

Third, they think the closeout is backward-looking. It's not. The closeout produces the documentation that makes the next governance year more effective. A board that knows which calendar assumptions were wrong, which decisions produced results, and which governance model elements need adjustment enters the next year having learned from the first year.
