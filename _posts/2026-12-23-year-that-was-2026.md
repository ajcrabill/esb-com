Tomorrow is Christmas Eve. The holiday lights are up, the year is winding down, and if you're a school board member, you're probably either grading the superintendent evaluation you just delivered or trying not to think about the January organizational meeting until January.

Take a moment. Before we get to the urgency of year-end planning — and we will, on the 30th — let's look back at the year in school board governance. Five stories. Five lessons. Because the year that was tells us what the year ahead demands.

**Story 1: The post-election transition wave**

The November midterms reshaped school boards across the country. Thousands of new members took seats. In Chicago, voters elected a 21-member board for the first time — the largest shift from mayoral control to elected governance in a generation. In Los Angeles, voters returned incumbents in Districts 4 and 6 while filling District 2 with a newcomer, all against the backdrop of an FBI investigation and an $877M deficit.

The lesson: elections change who sits in the chairs, but they don't change the fundamental demands on those chairs. Every new board member needs to learn how to govern — not just how to show up and vote. And every returning board member needs to be ready to teach them.

**Story 2: LAUSD's accountability failure**

This was the year Los Angeles Unified became a national case study in board accountability failure. An $877M deficit. 657 jobs cut. An FBI raid. The superintendent placed on paid leave. A Title IX investigation. And through it all, the board — until the November election — remained largely the same.

The lesson is painful but necessary: when a board doesn't monitor financial data, doesn't ask hard questions, and doesn't hold its superintendent accountable, the consequences cascade. The deficit doesn't appear overnight. It appears over months of board meetings where nobody asked: *where's the data on our financial projections?* Every board in the country should look at LAUSD and ask: could this happen here? And the answer should not be "we trust our superintendent."

**Story 3: Fiscal crisis governance across America**

LAUSD was the highest-profile story, but it wasn't the only one. WCCUSD entered the year with a $127.2M solvency need and a negative certification. Hartford faced a $74.5M deficit. EPISD declared financial exigency. Dozens of other districts, large and small, spent the year making budget cuts that affected classrooms.

The lesson: fiscal crisis governance is governance — it's just governance under pressure. The boards that handled these crises best were the ones that had governance structures in place before the crisis hit: clear goals, regular financial reporting, a process for aligning budget decisions with priorities. The boards that handled them worst were the ones trying to build those structures in the middle of the emergency.

**Story 4: The state takeover dynamic evolves**

Houston ISD remains under TEA Board of Managers control with no return-to-local-control date in sight. But elsewhere, the trend shifted. Little Rock School District began moving toward a return to local control after years of state intervention. The narrative is becoming more nuanced: state takeovers aren't permanent, but the path back requires boards to demonstrate they can govern effectively.

The lesson: a board's right to govern isn't guaranteed. It's earned. The 26 states that have passed legislation limiting school board authority since 2021 are a warning sign. Boards that demonstrate effective governance — aligned goals, transparent decision-making, accountability for outcomes — protect their institutional autonomy. Boards that don't invite intervention.

**Story 5: Governance resilience — the boards that got it right**

Here's the story that doesn't make headlines but matters most. Across the country, there were boards that governed well this year. Boards that set clear goals and monitored progress. Boards that conducted honest self-assessments. Boards that maintained goal continuity through election cycles. Boards that earned — and kept — their community's trust.

Napa Valley USD used a post-election transition to reset board norms after a polarized period. Montgomery County published an annual governance report with self-assessment results and goal progress data. San Jose USD adopted ESB-aligned governance structures with documentation that survived member turnover.

The lesson: good governance is possible. It's not easy. It requires discipline, honesty, and a willingness to have uncomfortable conversations. But the boards that do it produce better outcomes for students, more stable relationships with superintendents, and stronger trust with their communities.

**What 2027 demands from boards**

These five stories point to the same conclusion. 2027 will not be easier than 2026. The fiscal pressures aren't going away. The political dynamics aren't stabilizing. The federal funding landscape is uncertain as ESSER winds down and the policy direction under a second Trump administration takes shape.

What will make the difference, for every board, is governance quality. Not the superintendent's operational skill — though that matters. Not the district's resources — though that matters too. The board's capacity to set direction, monitor progress, hold itself and the superintendent accountable, and earn the community's trust.

That's the work of the Governance Reset. Assess, evaluate, plan, commit. It's what this arc has been about. And it's what 2027 will demand.
