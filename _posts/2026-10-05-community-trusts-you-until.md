**Your Community Trusts You Until It Doesn't. Here's How to Keep It.**

I've worked with hundreds of school boards across this country. And I can tell you this with absolute certainty: every board that loses the public's trust believes they saw it coming. But they didn't — not really. They saw the symptoms. They didn't see the cause.

Here's what I've learned about trust: it's not built by press releases. It's not built by ribbon cuttings. It's not even built by good test scores, as important as those are.

Community trust is built by governance. Specifically, it's built by three things that are entirely within your board's control.

A board in the Pacific Northwest learned this the hard way. They had a facilities bond coming up. The administration recommended a $180 million package. The board spent three meetings debating it internally. They brought in a consultant to design the campaign. They planned the community rollout for the week before the election.

And then the community showed up. Not to support the bond — to oppose it. The bond failed by 22 points. The board president told me afterward, "We spent six months talking to ourselves. We assumed the community would trust us because we're the board. We were wrong."

A board in Florida did it differently. They started with a question: "What does a well-equipped school look like to you?" Twelve community conversations before the board ever discussed a dollar amount. By the time they started debating bond size, the community had already told them what they valued — air conditioning in every classroom, science lab upgrades, secure entrances. The bond passed with 68 percent.

The difference between those two outcomes isn't about bond strategy. It's about trust architecture.

Here's what I've found works. Three pillars. Every board that maintains community trust through difficult decisions builds on all three.

**Pillar One: Transparent Decision-Making.**

Not transparent in the legal sense. Not "we posted the agenda 72 hours in advance." Transparent in the governance sense. Your community can tell the difference between a board that's done the work and a board that's following a script.

Transparent decision-making means your community understands not just what you decided, but how you decided it. It means the board packet includes the options you rejected and why. It means your agenda items have a clear question the board is answering, not "discussion item" followed by twenty minutes of rambling.

**Pillar Two: Structured Community Input.**

Most boards ask for public comment. Few boards use it. The difference between input theater and genuine input is structure.

Structure means your community knows exactly when and how their voice will be heard. It means you have multiple channels — not just three minutes at the podium. It means you report back on what you heard and what changed as a result. It means the community can see their fingerprints on your decisions.

**Pillar Three: Visible Follow-Through.**

This is the pillar most boards miss. You made a decision. Now show the community what happened. Public dashboards. Quarterly governance reports. Community meetings where you report on progress, not just ask for input.

One board I coached started publishing a quarterly governance report — three pages, plain English, covering: What we decided, Why we decided it, What's happened since, What's coming next. Subscription rate among registered voters in the district: 14 percent in the first year. That's 14 percent of voters who now know more about their school board's work than any board in the country can claim.

Now let me connect this to the moment we're in.

The Los Angeles Unified School District is in the midst of an FBI investigation into an AI contract that went sideways. The superintendent is on paid leave. The board is navigating an $877 million deficit. The budget vote passed 5-2 — a split that reflects deep divisions on the board itself.

I'm not singling out LAUSD. Every major urban district in this country is facing the same trust vacuum. When the Brookings Institution studied school board elections across twenty major cities, they found that voter trust in local school boards has declined 23 percent since 2020. Boards with visible internal conflict saw the steepest drops.

Here's the hard truth I've learned after years of working with boards in crisis: the community doesn't stop trusting you because of any single scandal. They stop trusting you because they watched you not do the work of governance day after day, month after month. And then one day, something breaks — and they already had no reason to believe you could fix it.

Rebuilding trust takes governance, not a PR firm. It starts with the three pillars. It starts with this week's action items.

**What You Can Do This Week.**

One. Audit your last three board meeting agendas. How many items clearly stated what the board was being asked to decide? How many were labeled "discussion" or "information"? That's your transparency baseline.

Two. Review your public comment structure. Is it designed to generate useful input, or to check a legal box? If it's the latter, redesign it.

Three. Ask your superintendent: what does the public know about the work we've done this quarter? If the answer is "not much," you have a follow-through gap.
