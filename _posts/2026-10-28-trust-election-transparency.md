**The Trust Election: Why November's School Board Races Are a Referendum on Transparency**

One week from today, school board elections will be held across the country. And if I'm being direct with you — and I always am — these elections are not really about the candidates. They're about something deeper.

They're a referendum on trust.

The Brookings Institution released a report in June 2026 that quantified what many of us have felt. They found that local school board conflict increased sharply during the pandemic and has only partially subsided. And they put a price tag on it: between $2.5 million and $3.5 million in measurable costs from each major policy reversal driven by board turnover.

That's the cost of broken trust.

Here's what the data showed more specifically: districts with high board turnover experienced an average of 2.3 major policy reversals in the two years following an election. Each reversal — a new curriculum adoption reversed, a strategic plan abandoned, a superintendent contract bought out — carried real costs. New materials purchased and then scrapped. Staff hours spent on initiatives that were never implemented. Community relationships damaged. The Brookings authors called it "policy whiplash," and it hits hardest when new board members come in without governance training and immediately try to undo everything the previous board did.

That's the environment voters are looking at right now.

**What the Voters Are Actually Deciding**

Voters have watched the LAUSD FBI investigation unfold. They've read about the WCCUSD solvency crisis. They've seen the TEA misconduct dashboard. They're asking: *can I trust this board to govern well?*

Let me connect the dots for you. The LAUSD probe — where the FBI executed search warrants at district headquarters in February 2026 — is a transparency failure of the highest order. When federal agents show up, voters reasonably ask: what was the board not paying attention to? The WCCUSD crisis — a $127.2 million solvency gap that built up over years of deficit spending without public disclosure — is a transparency failure of a different kind. The board approved budgets that were structurally unbalanced, and the community didn't know until it was too late. And the TEA misconduct dashboard — 13,390 educator misconduct cases tracked by the Texas Education Agency — represents a systemic failure of oversight that board governance is supposed to prevent.

These aren't isolated incidents. They're symptoms of boards that governed without transparency, without structured input, without community engagement. And voters have noticed.

Here's what I've learned watching these stories unfold. In each case, the warning signs were public. Budget documents showed the deficit growing. Whistleblower complaints existed. But there was no mechanism for the community to connect those dots because the boards weren't practicing governance-level transparency. The problem wasn't that the information didn't exist. It's that no one in the community had the tools to find it, understand it, or act on it before it was too late. That's what a trust deficit looks like in practice.

That question isn't answered by campaign promises. It's answered by governance practices. The boards that have built trust — through transparent decision-making, structured community input, strategic communications, and governance-level engagement — are the boards that survive this election cycle intact.

**What Incumbents Should Do**

Hold a listening session. Not a town hall where you talk. Ninety minutes of questions and note-taking. Say: "If I earn another term, what do you want me focused on?" That's not a campaign event. That's a governance act.

And do something the night of the election that no one talks about: prepare a transition memo. Whether you win or lose, write down everything the board has in motion — pending policy changes, active contracts, upcoming votes, partnership commitments, superintendent evaluation status. Hand it to the incoming members, whether they're you or someone else. This is the single best defense against the policy whiplash the Brookings report documented. A board that shares institutional knowledge loses nothing. A board that withholds it loses the community's time and money.

**What the Newly Elected Should Do**

Governance training covering the ESB framework. A transition protocol to prevent policy whiplash (Brookings showed damage is worst in the first six months after turnover). A public commitment to the practices this series has covered.

Specifically: commit to a 90-day learning period before you vote on any major policy change. Read the board packet from the past six months. Meet with the superintendent individually (not as a faction). Learn the difference between the district's operational work and the board's governance role. The newly elected board members who do the most damage are the ones who show up on day one ready to fix everything and don't realize they're breaking the machinery they don't understand yet.

**The Bottom Line**

This election isn't about party labels. It's about whether voters trust their local school board to make good decisions for their kids. And that trust is earned — not through campaign ads, but through governance.

The practices this series has covered — transparent decision-making, strategic communications, deliberative engagement, community partnerships — are not nice-to-haves. They are the work. And next week, voters will decide which boards have been doing it.
