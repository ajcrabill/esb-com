**The Governance That Happens Between Meetings — and Why It Determines Your Board's 2027**

December 23, 2026

**Series:** Year-End Governance Reset & 2027 Foundation (Dec 2026)

---

I want to talk about something that doesn't happen at a board meeting.

Today is December 23. For most districts, the last board meeting of the year happened last night or the night before. The agenda packets are filed. The votes are recorded. The board room is quiet.

And now, in the quiet, the real governance work begins.

I've spent years watching boards operate, and here's what I've learned: the most important governance work doesn't happen in the boardroom. It happens in the spaces between meetings. The phone call with the superintendent on a Tuesday afternoon. The text exchange with a fellow board member about a concern that never made it to the agenda. The hour of reading and reflection before a committee meeting. The five-minute conversation in the parking lot after a public forum.

Those moments — unscripted, unrecorded, unglamorous — determine more about your board's effectiveness than any resolution you'll pass next year.

## What Between-Meeting Governance Looks Like

Let me give you some examples of what I'm talking about.

**The check-in.** A board chair calls the superintendent on a Thursday morning. Not to discuss a crisis. Just to ask: how's the week going? Is there anything coming up that the board needs to be thinking about? The call takes twelve minutes. It builds the trust that makes difficult conversations possible later.

**The clarification.** A board member reads through the agenda packet and finds a recommendation that doesn't make sense. Instead of raising it during the meeting — where it will derail the agenda and create public confusion — they email the superintendent the day before. "Can you help me understand the thinking behind this?" The superintendent responds with context the board member didn't have. The concern is resolved before the meeting starts.

**The alignment.** Two board members have been quietly frustrated by the same issue. They haven't talked about it because they assumed they were on opposite sides. Over coffee between meetings, one of them mentions it. The other says "I've been thinking the same thing." They discover they agree. A shared position emerges that neither would have advanced alone.

**The preparation.** A board member spends thirty minutes on a Saturday morning reviewing the monitoring data for the upcoming meeting. They notice a trend that raises questions. They write them down. At the meeting, those questions lead to a deeper discussion about whether the district's current approach is working.

None of this happens in a board meeting. All of it makes board meetings better.

## The Three Questions

Here's what I want you to do during the quiet week ahead. Not a homework assignment. A reflection. Three questions to ask yourself as you think about the year ahead.

**Question 1: Am I clear on my role?**

The most common source of board dysfunction is role confusion. Board members who think they're supposed to manage the district. Board members who think their job is to represent a single constituency. Board members who don't understand the difference between governance and administration.

Your role as a board member is simple, but it's not easy: set the direction, provide the resources, and hold the system accountable. Everything else — the operational details, the personnel decisions, the day-to-day management — is someone else's job.

If you're not clear on that distinction, the conversations you have between meetings will create confusion instead of clarity. You'll give the superintendent mixed signals. You'll step into areas where you don't belong. You'll undermine the very partnership you're trying to build.

**Question 2: Am I strengthening my partnership with the superintendent?**

The board-president relationship is the most important relationship in the district. Not the board's relationship with the superintendent — your individual relationship. Each board member's partnership with the superintendent determines whether the board functions as a unified governing body or as seven separate bosses.

The between-meeting conversations you have with the superintendent either build this partnership or erode it. The check-in call that builds trust. The late-night email that creates confusion. The parking lot conversation that respects boundaries. The phone call that crosses them.

Ask yourself: do the conversations I have with the superintendent between meetings make us stronger or weaker as a team?

**Question 3: Am I preparing for January?**

Everything this December arc has walked you through — the data review, the goal-setting process, the superintendent evaluation framework, the board self-assessment — requires preparation. Not just meeting attendance. Preparation.

The board member who walks into January having reviewed the data, thought through the goals, and reflected on their own governance habits is ready to govern. The board member who walks in cold, expecting the packet to tell them everything they need to know, is along for the ride.

Which one will you be?

## The Quiet Week

I know the week between Christmas and New Year's is a strange time. The board calendar is empty. The district office is quiet. It doesn't feel like governance season.

But this is when the board members who take governance seriously separate themselves from the ones who show up for meetings. This is when you reflect. When you prepare. When you have the conversations that don't fit on an agenda.

I'm not asking you to work through the holidays. I'm asking you to use five minutes of the quiet time to ask yourself those three questions. And then to set an intention for how you'll show up in January.

The governance that happens between meetings is invisible. But it's not optional. It's where boards are built or broken. It's where trust is earned or lost. It's where the work of January is prepared or postponed.
