**The Q2 Community Progress Report — Closing the Governance Loop with the Community You Serve**

July 12, 2027

---

Your board adopted goals in January. You set specific, measurable targets for student outcomes. You reviewed data in February, March, and May. You adjusted course when the data told you to. You completed the superintendent evaluation in June — the accountability event that closed the spring governance cycle.

That is six months of serious governance work. And if you haven't told the community what that work produced, you have done only half the job.

The Q2 community progress report — published in July, covering the first half of the governance year — is the board's most important communication of the year. It is the board's accountability demonstration: here are the goals we adopted, here is the progress we've made, here are the challenges we're addressing, and here is what we're doing about them.

Most boards don't publish a community progress report. Most boards publish a newsletter, a budget summary, or a superintendent's report. Those are useful documents. But they don't answer the question the community is asking: is the school system getting better?

A community progress report answers that question.

**What a Q2 community progress report includes**

The report should be one to two pages — longer if there's a detailed appendix — and it should cover four sections:

**Goal-by-goal progress.** For each goal the board adopted in January, the report shows: the target the board set, the current data, the trend (improving, holding steady, declining), and the board's assessment. The board's assessment is the key element. The data shows what happened. The board's assessment tells the community what the board thinks about what happened. Is the board confident in the trajectory? Is the board concerned? Is the board planning adjustments?

The assessment should be honest. The community can read data. If the trend is flat, the board should say the trend is flat and explain what the board is doing about it. A progress report that only reports success is not a progress report — it's a public relations document.

**Board governance highlights.** The community needs to know what the board did to produce the results being reported. This section covers the board's governance actions: the data reviews the board conducted, the budget alignment decisions the board made, the superintendent evaluation the board completed, the training the board has invested in. The highlights don't need to be exhaustive — three to five bullet points that demonstrate the board is governing with purpose.

**Challenges and adjustments.** Every board faces challenges. The Q2 report should name them — not to make excuses, but to demonstrate that the board is aware of the challenges and is addressing them. If a goal is behind trajectory, the board should say so and explain the adjustments the board has directed. If the policy framework needs revision, the board should say so and describe the work underway.

**What's coming in Q3.** The report should look forward: what the board will focus on in the fall, when the next data review happens, when the community can expect the next report, and how the community can engage with the board's work.

**The format**

The format should be designed for the community, not for the board. A dense data packet that looks like a board meeting agenda packet will be ignored. A one-page summary with clear visuals, plain language, and a prominent invitation for community input will be read.

Here are the format elements that work:

**One-page summary.** The one-page version is the report. Everything else is an appendix. If the community reads only one page, they should understand: what the board's goals are, whether the district is on track, and what the board is doing about it.

**Data visualization.** A simple bar chart showing goal-by-goal progress — target line versus current data — is more effective than a table of numbers. The community should be able to see the trajectory in seconds, not minutes.

**Plain language.** "Third grade reading proficiency" not "ELA achievement at grade 3 benchmark level." "On track" not "trajectory-aligned progress indicator." The language should be language the community uses.

**Actionable invitation.** The report should end with a clear invitation: "The board will discuss this report at the August board meeting. We invite community input. Here's how to share your perspective."

**When to publish and how to distribute**

The Q2 report should be published in July — after the superintendent evaluation, before the summer preparation work shifts into fall readiness. July publication gives the community a mid-year update and gives the board the summer to address any concerns the report raises.

Distribution should include: email to the district mailing list, publication on the district website with a dedicated page, social media posts with the one-page summary as a graphic, and inclusion in the August board meeting agenda packet.

A community progress report is not an optional communication. It is the board's accountability demonstration — the public evidence that the board is governing with purpose and producing results that matter to the community. The board that publishes a Q2 report in July enters August with the community's trust intact. The board that doesn't enters August with a question the board has not answered.
