**Fall Community Progress Report — Year Two Communication**

October 24, 2028

---

Your board published its first community progress report in Fall 2027. It was new territory — deciding what to include, how to frame the board's work for a public audience, what kind of transparency would build trust instead of confusion. You figured it out. You got a report out the door. And your community saw something it had never seen before: a school board voluntarily reporting on its own governance performance.

Now it is Fall 2028. And the question has shifted from *can we produce a progress report?* to *what story does two years of governance tell?*

That is a fundamentally different question. And it changes how you build the report.

**The difference between a first report and a second report**

The Fall 2027 community progress report answered a simple question: *What has your board accomplished in its first year of governance?* The answer was a list — a board orientation, a budget adoption, data reviews, a community engagement process, a superintendent evaluation. The report was valuable because it was the first time the board had told the full story of its work in one place.

The Fall 2028 report answers a harder question: *What changed between Year One and Year Two of governance?*

That question cannot be answered with a list. It requires comparison. It requires the board to look at its own performance across two cycles and say: *Here is what was better this year. Here is what stayed the same. Here is what we need to improve.*

A community that sees a board comparing its own year-over-year performance is a community that trusts the board is serious about governance. Not because the report is perfect, but because it shows the board is measuring itself — not just reporting.

**What the year-over-year report should contain**

The Fall 2028 community progress report has four sections that the Fall 2027 report did not. These are the sections that turn a first-year report into a two-year governance story.

**Section one: Goal trajectory comparison — same goals, different year**

This section shows your board's student outcome goals with two data points: where the metrics stood in Fall 2027 and where they stand in Fall 2028. Same metrics. Same sources. Same comparison period.

The format is simple. For each goal:
- Fall 2027 baseline or first measure
- Fall 2028 current measure
- Direction of change (improved, held, declined)
- One sentence on what the board thinks the change means

This is not a detailed data review. That is the board's internal work. The community report is a summary — a one-page view that any parent or community member can read in three minutes and understand whether student outcomes are improving.

If your board does not have comparable year-over-year data for every goal, name that gap. A community progress report that honestly says *we are building our baseline and do not have comparable data yet for this goal* is more credible than one that pretends everything is measurable. Your community knows when you are papering over gaps. Name them. Then commit to filling them.

**Section two: Governance activity comparison — what the board did in two cycles**

This section answers the question: *What did the board do in two years of governance that it did not do before?*

The comparison format works here too. A two-column table:
- **Fall 2027:** First data review cycle completed. First budget adopted under new framework. First superintendent evaluation. First community progress report.
- **Fall 2028:** Second data review cycle with year-over-year comparison. Budget monitoring through three quarters. Summer governance renewal completed. Board self-evaluation administered.

The power of this comparison is not the individual items. It is the pattern — a board that did something once, then did it again and did it better. Your community sees a governance practice building momentum, not a board starting from scratch every year.

**Section three: What the board committed to in its summer renewal and what it delivered**

This is the accountability section. In Summer 2028, your board completed a protect-apply-add renewal exercise. You named what you would protect, apply more consistently, and add. The Fall 2028 progress report is the place to report back: *Here is what we said we would do, and here is what we did.*

For each renewal commitment, the report shows:
- The commitment (e.g., "protect the five-business-day data packet rule")
- The status (protected, in progress, not yet achieved)
- The evidence (e.g., "data packets delivered on time for all four fall data reviews")

This section is what separates a governance report from a public relations document. A PR document tells the community what the board wants them to hear. A governance report tells the community what the board committed to and whether it followed through. The difference is trust.

**Section four: What the board is working on for Year Three**

Close the report with the board's governance priorities for Spring 2029 — the third full cycle. Not a list of promises. A list of focus areas: *We are working on improving our goal trajectory data for the one goal where we cannot yet show a two-year trend. We are deepening our budget monitoring practice to connect every expenditure category to a student outcome goal. We are refining our board self-evaluation to produce more specific improvement targets.*

The community reads this section and sees a board that is not finished — and knows it is not finished. That is the hallmark of a governance practice that is maturing.

**How to produce the report efficiently**

Your board has already produced two of the four sections. The October 3 data review produced the goal trajectory comparison data. The October 31 governance check produced the protect-apply-add follow-through assessment. The November 7 goal trajectory check will refine the year-over-year picture.

The Fall 2028 community progress report is not a new production. It is an assembly — pulling together the outputs your board has already generated over the fall cycle and presenting them in a community-facing format.

The board president or governance committee chair should draft the report in a single board working session. The session produces:
- The year-over-year goal comparison summary (from October data review outputs)
- The governance activity comparison (from the board calendar)
- The renewal commitment follow-through (from the October governance check)
- The Year Three priority list (from the November goal trajectory check and November governance reset)

One working session. Four sections. One report.

**The message your community needs to hear**

Here is what your community needs to hear from your board this October, whether they know to ask for it or not: *We have been doing this for two years. We are better at it than we were a year ago. We know where we need to improve. And we are going to keep reporting to you on all of it.*

That message — improvement, honesty, continuity — is what the Fall 2028 community progress report is designed to deliver. It is not a repeat of last year's report. It is the next chapter in a governance story that your board is writing in public.
