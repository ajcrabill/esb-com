Today is December 16. In California, the First Interim Budget Report was due yesterday to every County Office of Education. In Florida, the governor's budget preview is being finalized. In Texas, districts are in the middle of their fall budget planning cycle. And across the country, 46 out of 50 states operate on a July 1 fiscal year — which means right now is the window when preliminary budget guidance from the board to the superintendent determines whether next year's budget aligns with your goals or reactive to your gaps.

Most boards miss this window. They wait until the superintendent presents a proposed budget in the spring. Then they negotiate line items, argue about priorities, and end up with a budget that reflects the loudest voices in the room rather than the goals the board adopted.

Here's the ESB finding that should change how you approach this: boards that set goals in January — before the budget process begins — are 2x more likely to report budget alignment with their strategic priorities compared to boards that wait until March or later.

The reason is simple. When goals come first, the budget becomes a tool for achieving those goals. When goals come later — or not at all — the budget becomes a document that just covers last year's expenses with a few adjustments.

**Why December is the critical month**

The December window is your board's opportunity to provide preliminary budget guidance to the superintendent. Not a line-item budget. Not a demands document. Guidance.

Here's what that looks like:

The board formally communicates to the superintendent — through a board resolution, a work session directive, or a board chair letter — what the board expects the fiscal year 2027-28 budget to reflect.

This guidance typically covers three areas:

**1. Revenue assumptions.** What is the board's expectation for revenue growth, flat funding, or decline? Most boards don't have a formal conversation about this. The superintendent makes assumptions — often conservative ones — and builds a budget around them. Then, in the spring, the board discovers those assumptions and either accepts or rejects them. The December guidance conversation eliminates that disconnect. The board and superintendent agree on a set of revenue assumptions upfront.

**2. Priority areas.** Based on the board's goals, where should new or redirected resources go? If your board adopted a goal to improve early literacy, this is where you say: "We expect the budget to reflect increased investment in K-3 reading intervention." If your board is focused on closing achievement gaps, the guidance should name that. This is not micromanagement. It's governance. The board sets the priorities; the superintendent builds the operational plan to deliver them.

**3. Staffing expectations.** Does the board expect staffing levels to remain flat? Is there a class size target? Is the board willing to consider position reductions in non-instructional areas to protect instructional investments? Most boards avoid this conversation because it's hard. But avoiding it means the superintendent builds a budget based on guesswork about what the board will accept.

**The difference between guidance and micromanagement**

I need to be clear about what preliminary budget guidance is not. It is not a list of demands about specific schools, specific programs, or specific positions. It is not a line-item directive. It is not the board telling the superintendent how to do their job.

The ESB framework draws a clear line: the board governs through goals and priorities. The superintendent manages through operations and implementation. Preliminary budget guidance stays on the governance side of that line. It says: *here are the outcomes we expect the budget to serve.* It does not say: *here is the exact staffing formula you must use.*

Here's a test: if you can't explain *why* a budget priority connects to a board-adopted student outcome goal, it's probably not a governance priority — it's an operational preference. Send it back.

**The California deadline and what it means**

If your district is in California, December 15's First Interim deadline is the most important fiscal governance milestone of the year. This is the report that shows whether your district is on track to meet its financial obligations. A positive certification means the district expects to meet its obligations. A negative certification means — and this is the language — "the district may not be able to meet its financial obligations."

WCCUSD just received a negative certification. A $127.2M solvency need. Hartford Public Schools is facing a $74.5M deficit. Los Angeles Unified is cutting 657 positions against an $877M deficit. These are the consequences of budget governance failures — some inherited, some self-inflicted.

The districts that navigate these challenges best are the ones whose boards understood that budget guidance isn't a spring activity. It starts in December, when you have the time to think strategically instead of reactively.
