**The Year in Review: What Your Board Actually Accomplished in 2026**

December 21, 2026

**Series:** Year-End Governance Reset & 2027 Planning

---

Here's a question I want you to answer before you read another word.

What did your board actually accomplish in 2026?

Not what did you try to do. Not what did you hope to do. What did you accomplish?

I've asked this question to dozens of boards in December. And the most common answer I get is a long pause, followed by a list of activities. We adopted a budget. We passed a policy. We held community forums. We hired a new principal.

Those are things that happened. They're not accomplishments, at least not in the sense I mean.

An accomplishment is a measurable improvement in student outcomes or board performance that you can point to and say: "Because of what we did, students are better off."

Most boards can't do that. Not because they didn't work hard. Because they never defined what success would look like before they started.

## The Four-Question Review

Here's a simple framework for your board's year-end review. Four questions. One honest conversation. No PowerPoints.

### Question 1: Did Student Outcomes Improve?

This is the only question that ultimately matters. Everything else is a means to this end.

Pull the data you set out to improve at the beginning of the year. Not the state test scores that won't arrive until spring — the data you committed to tracking. Reading benchmarks. Attendance rates. Math assessments. Graduation progress.

Compare where you started to where you ended.

If student outcomes improved, name it. Celebrate it publicly. Tell your community. Too many boards are afraid to acknowledge success, as if admitting something worked would somehow jinx it.

If student outcomes didn't improve, name that too. Not as a failure — as information. You know what didn't work. Now you can make different choices.

### Question 2: Did the Board Meet Its Own Governance Commitments?

This is the question nobody asks because the answer is usually uncomfortable.

At the beginning of 2026, your board made commitments. Maybe you committed to reviewing data at every board meeting. Maybe you committed to regular community engagement. Maybe you committed to a board self-evaluation.

Did you do it?

I worked with a board that committed in January to reviewing student outcome data at every meeting. By March, they had stopped. By June, they'd forgotten the commitment entirely. By December, nobody on the board even remembered making it.

That's not a failure of intention. That's a failure of accountability. The board made a promise to itself and broke it — not because anyone was malicious, but because nobody was tracking.

Your governance commitments matter because they're the mechanism that produces student outcome improvement. If you can't keep your governance commitments, you can't expect your district to keep its academic commitments.

### Question 3: What Went Well That We Should Repeat?

Most boards spend their year-end review focused on what went wrong. The problems. The gaps. The failures.

That's important. But it's not the whole picture.

Every board has something that worked this year. A new meeting structure that improved engagement. A data dashboard that clarified decision-making. A community forum that rebuilt trust. A superintendent partnership that deepened.

Name it. Document it. Make a plan to do it again next year.

The boards that improve are the ones that don't just fix what's broken — they also protect what's working.

### Question 4: What Didn't Work That We Should Stop Doing?

Here's the hardest question. Because stopping something means admitting you were wrong to start it — or at least that you didn't execute it well.

But this is where growth happens.

A committee that met for a year without producing anything useful. A policy that created more paperwork than protection. A program that consumed resources without improving outcomes. A meeting format that nobody liked but everyone tolerated.

Name it. And commit to stopping it.

The most effective boards I've worked with are the ones that have the discipline to stop doing what isn't working — even if it's comfortable, even if it's traditional, even if nobody wants to be the one to say it.

## The Gift of Honest Reflection

I know December is a busy month. The holiday calendar is crowded. Board members have family obligations. The temptation is to skip the year-end review and jump straight to January planning.

Don't do that.

The year-end review is the foundation that makes January planning meaningful. You can't plan a better 2027 if you don't understand 2026. You can't set better goals if you don't know whether your previous goals worked. You can't build on your strengths if you haven't taken the time to name them.

A board that does a real year-end review — the kind that answers all four questions honestly — walks into January with clarity. They know what they accomplished. They know what they didn't. They know what to repeat and what to stop.

A board that skips the review walks into January with hope. And hope is not a strategy.

## Your Year-End Assignment

Before your next board meeting — whether it's this week or in January — schedule a work session for just the board. No public. No staff (except the superintendent, if you choose). Two hours. The four questions. No agenda items except the review.

Bring the data. Bring your governance commitments from January. Bring honest answers.

And when you're done, write down three things:

1. One accomplishment from 2026 that you're proud of.
2. One governance commitment you made but didn't keep.
3. One thing you're going to stop doing in 2027.

That's your year in review. Not a report. Not a presentation. An honest assessment of whether students are better off than they were a year ago.

The answer might surprise you. The process of getting to it will make you a better board.
