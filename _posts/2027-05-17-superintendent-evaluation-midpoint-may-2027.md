**The Superintendent Evaluation Midpoint — What May's Check-In Should Cover Between February and June**

May 17, 2027

---

February's 90-day superintendent check-in established the evaluation framework. The board identified the specific areas where the superintendent needed support or development. The superintendent has been working on those areas for three months.

May is the midpoint between February's check-in and June's formal evaluation. The board should not wait until June to evaluate progress. A midpoint check-in — less formal than the February session, more structured than a general conversation — gives the board the information it needs to enter June's formal evaluation with documented evidence and avoids the surprise of discovering in June that an area the board thought was improving has not improved at all.

**What the midpoint check-in is not**

Let me start with what the midpoint check-in is not. It is not a formal evaluation. The June evaluation is the formal evaluation. The midpoint check-in is a structured progress review.

It is not a surprise. The board and the superintendent should have agreed in February that a midpoint check-in would happen in May. The superintendent should know what areas will be reviewed and what evidence will be considered.

It is not a re-evaluation of the February framework. The February check-in produced a documented list of growth areas and support commitments. The midpoint check-in evaluates progress against that list. If the board wants to add a new area of concern, that goes into June's evaluation — not into the midpoint check-in, which evaluates the existing framework.

**What the midpoint check-in includes**

The midpoint check-in has four components.

**Component 1: Review of the February growth areas.** For each area the board identified in February, the board reviews: what the superintendent committed to, what evidence of progress is available, whether the board sees improvement, and what remains to be addressed in June. The data review findings from the preceding two weeks should be the primary evidence source. If the board identified "strengthen data communication to the board" as a growth area, the May data review is the evidence — was the data packet complete, was it delivered on time, was the superintendent prepared to answer questions?

**Component 2: Review of board support commitments.** The February check-in included commitments from the board. The board committed to providing specific support — clarifying the data format, adjusting the meeting calendar, ensuring the timeline for budget information was realistic, providing timely policy direction. The midpoint check-in asks the board: did we deliver what we committed to? A board that asks the superintendent to improve data communication while providing late policy direction or unclear data format expectations is a board that has not fulfilled its side of the evaluation agreement. The midpoint check-in is the time to name that and correct it.

**Component 3: Incorporation of the May data review findings.** The data review the board completed in the preceding two weeks is the most current evidence of district performance. The midpoint check-in incorporates those findings in a governance context — not to evaluate the superintendent's operational performance, but to inform the board's understanding of how the district is responding to board direction. If the data review revealed a goal with a red combined assessment, the midpoint check-in asks: where does the superintendent need additional board support or clearer direction on this goal? Not: why is this goal off track? That's the deeper review conversation. The midpoint check-in asks: does the board's direction need to be clarified to support the superintendent's execution?

**Component 4: Preview of the June evaluation structure.** The midpoint check-in should end with a clear preview of what the June evaluation will include. The board reviews: the evaluation format (written assessment, board discussion, joint meeting, or combination), the timeline (when the superintendent receives the written evaluation, when the board discusses it, when the board and superintendent meet to review it together), and what the board expects from the superintendent in terms of a self-assessment or progress report.

**How to structure the meeting**

The midpoint check-in should be a sixty-minute meeting with a specific agenda and a written record.

| Time | Section | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 5 min | Framing | Board president restates the purpose: progress review against the February framework, not formal evaluation. Confirms the June evaluation timeline. |
| 15 min | Growth area review | For each area identified in February, the board reviews evidence and discusses progress. The board president records a status for each area: on track, needs continued focus, or concern. |
| 10 min | Board support review | The board reviews whether it delivered what it committed to in February. This is the board's self-assessment. |
| 15 min | Data review integration | Board president summarizes the May data review findings relevant to the superintendent evaluation — not the operational details, but what the data reveals about the effectiveness of the board-superintendent working relationship and the clarity of board direction. |
| 10 min | June evaluation preview | Board president reviews the evaluation format, timeline, and expectations. Superintendent asks clarifying questions. |
| 5 min | Closeout | Board president confirms next steps: written record of the midpoint check-in, timeline for the June evaluation, any immediate follow-up items. |

**What the board president should prepare**

The board president carries the most responsibility for a productive midpoint check-in. Here's what I recommend the president prepare.

First, the February check-in record. The written record of the February session — growth areas, board commitments, timeline — should be distributed to the board and the superintendent at least one week before the midpoint check-in. Everyone enters the meeting with the same starting point.

Second, a one-page summary of the May data review findings. Not the full data packet. A summary that answers: which goals had green, yellow, or red combined assessments? What decisions did the board make? What deeper reviews were scheduled? The summary goes into the record.

Third, a draft evaluation timeline for June. The board president should have a proposed timeline ready — not a final decision, but a starting point for discussion. The timeline includes: when the board receives the superintendent's self-assessment, when the board prepares individual written evaluations, when the board discusses the evaluation in closed session, when the board and superintendent meet to review the evaluation together, and when the evaluation is formally presented in open session.

**What the superintendent should prepare**

The superintendent should prepare a one-page progress update aligned to the February growth areas. For each area: what the superintendent has done since February, what evidence is available, where the superintendent needs additional board support or clarity. The board receives this update with the check-in agenda.

The superintendent should also bring questions about the June evaluation format. The midpoint check-in is the time to resolve format questions — not in June, when the evaluation is already in process.

**Why this matters for the June evaluation**

A board that completes a structured midpoint check-in walks into June's formal evaluation with documented progress, identified gaps, and an agreed-upon format. The evaluation becomes a conversation about evidence, not a conversation about surprises.

A board that skips the midpoint check-in and expects the June evaluation to produce a complete picture is a board that will discover gaps in the evaluation meeting — areas the board thought were progressing but weren't, format disagreements that should have been resolved earlier, evidence the board needs but doesn't have.

The midpoint check-in is the board's opportunity to enter June prepared. The board that takes it enters the formal evaluation with the confidence that it has done the governance work to evaluate the superintendent fairly and thoroughly.
