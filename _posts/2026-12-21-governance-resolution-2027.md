Every January, school boards across the country pass resolutions. Organizational resolutions: electing a president, appointing a clerk, setting meeting dates. Procedural resolutions: adopting Robert's Rules, approving committee assignments.

But there's one resolution most boards never pass — the one that matters most.

A governance resolution. A formal, board-adopted commitment to how you will govern in the year ahead.

I've seen boards transform themselves by making this commitment. Montgomery County Public Schools publishes an annual governance report that includes self-assessment results, superintendent evaluation overview, and a goal progress dashboard. They won an NSBA Magna Award for governance transparency. Other districts publish annual governance reports modeled on that framework.

But a governance resolution doesn't need to be a 40-page document. It needs to be a clear, public commitment that the board holds itself to. Here's what it should include:

**1. A commitment to goal-aligned governance.**

This is the foundation. The board commits to making every significant decision — budget adoption, policy changes, superintendent evaluation — in reference to the student outcome goals the board has adopted. Not "we'll try to think about the goals." A formal commitment: *the goals will drive the work, not the other way around.*

Write it down. Vote on it. Post it on the district website. So that when a decision comes up in March that's popular but doesn't connect to the goals, someone can point to the resolution and say: "Is this about our goals or something else?"

**2. Meeting structure commitments.**

How does your board use its meeting time? I've studied this data across hundreds of boards. Before 2020, boards spent roughly 80% of their meeting time on procedural items. After 2020, that number dropped to about 55% — but it's creeping back up. Boards are falling into the trap of filling agendas with operational trivia and calling it governance.

A governance resolution commits to a meeting structure that protects time for strategic discussion: data reviews, goal conversations, community dialogue. It schedules items instead of letting them pile up. It sets a standard for board packet timing — materials must be available at least 72 hours before the meeting — so members arrive prepared instead of reading on the fly.

**3. Board norms renewal.**

Every board has norms — written or unwritten. The unwritten ones are usually the problem.

Your governance resolution is the perfect vehicle to renew your board's norms for the coming year. How will you disagree without being disagreeable? How will you handle communication outside of meetings? How will you ensure every member has a voice, not just the loudest ones?

Don't copy norms from another district. Your board has specific dynamics, specific challenges, specific relationships. The norms conversation is the most valuable hour your board can spend in January — but only if you take it seriously.

**4. Community trust-building commitments.**

What will your board do in 2027 to build — or rebuild — community trust? Not what the district will do. Not what the superintendent will do. What the *board* will do.

Maybe it's holding a quarterly community forum in a different part of the district each time. Maybe it's simplifying board meeting agendas so the public can actually follow what's being decided. Maybe it's publishing a regular board newsletter — like this one — that communicates directly with the community about what the board is working on and why.

Whatever you choose, put it in the resolution. Make it measurable. Come back to it in December 2027 and ask: did we do it?

**5. Self-assessment follow-through.**

If you completed the board self-assessment we talked about on December 9, you identified one or two areas for improvement. The governance resolution is where you commit to acting on them.

Your self-assessment showed a gap in progress monitoring? The resolution says: "The board commits to receiving data updates on each student outcome goal at every regular meeting, and to spending at least 20 minutes per meeting discussing data."

Your assessment showed a gap in community trust? The resolution says: "The board commits to holding at least four community listening sessions in 2027, one per quarter, in geographically diverse locations across the district."

A resolution without follow-through is a wish. A resolution with a specific, measurable commitment is a governance practice.

**Why this matters for 2027**

Here's what I know about boards that adopt governance resolutions and boards that don't. The boards that make this commitment — that put in writing how they intend to govern — enter the year with clarity. There's no guessing about what the board prioritizes. There's no confusion about how meetings will run. There's no wondering whether the board norms will be enforced.

The boards that don't make this commitment enter the year the same way they entered every year: hoping the dynamics will be better this time, assuming everyone shares the same understanding, and discovering in March that they don't.
