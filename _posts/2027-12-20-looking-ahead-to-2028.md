**Looking Ahead to 2028 — The Fall Arc Closes and the New Year Begins**

December 20, 2027

---

Here is what your board has built this fall.

You started in October with data. Two rounds of benchmark data. Budget alignment. Escalation protocol. Progress monitoring cadence.

You moved through November with partnership. The mid-year accountability check. Goal writing. The superintendent fall check-in. The Thanksgiving governance reset.

You closed in December with structure. The annual organizational meeting. The year-end governance review. January goal adoption preparation.

Twelve weeks. Twelve posts. One complete governance cycle.

Now the work of 2028 begins. Here is what the fall arc built, what January requires, and what your board should be thinking about as you close 2027.

**Section 1: Fall arc retrospective**

Let me name what your board accomplished if you followed the fall arc.

First, your board now has a data review rhythm that works. You conducted two data reviews in the fall. You made written decisions after each one. You connected those decisions to the superintendent's implementation plan. That rhythm does not disappear in January. It carries forward. Your board now knows what it means to govern with data instead of governing with anecdotes.

Second, your board has a superintendent partnership protocol. The fall check-in gave you a framework for honest conversation that is neither a rubber stamp nor a confrontation. Your board president and superintendent now know how to talk about governance without slipping into operational management.

Third, your board has a governance calendar. The organizational meeting produced committee assignments aligned with priorities, a year-round meeting schedule, and an operations resolution. Your board has a plan for the year, not a stack of meeting dates.

Fourth, your board has a goal adoption process. The January goal language is drafted, previewed, and ready for community feedback. Your board will adopt goals in January that are informed by data, shaped by community input, and supported by board consensus.

Here is what that adds up to. Your board is no longer reactive. Your board has a governance operating cycle. That cycle will serve you in 2028 the same way it served you in the fall — if you commit to maintaining it.

The boards that improve fastest are not the boards with the most talented superintendents or the highest per-pupil spending. They are the boards that build a governance operating cycle and follow it. You built yours this fall. Do not abandon it in January.

**Section 2: 2028 governance calendar preview (Q1 and Q2)**

Here is what the first half of 2028 looks like on the governance calendar your board set in the organizational meeting.

January: goal adoption meeting. The board adopts 2028 goals. This is the only agenda item. No reports. No presentations. No public comment. Just goal adoption.

February: first data review. Baseline data collection for 2028. Where is the district starting relative to the adopted goals? The board reviews the data, makes written decisions, and gives the superintendent direction.

March: community engagement check-in. The board reports progress to the community. This is not a board presentation. This is a structured conversation. The board says "here are our goals, here is the data so far, here is what we are learning." The community asks questions. The board listens.

April: second data review. Growth monitoring. Are the interventions working? Are any student groups falling behind? The board reviews the data and makes course-correction decisions if needed.

May: budget alignment. The board connects the spring data to the next year's budget. If reading scores are growing, the board ensures that growth is funded. If math scores are flat, the board asks whether the math budget needs adjustment.

June: third data review. Year-end projections. The board looks at the trajectory and asks: Are we on pace to meet the annual goals? If not, what needs to change between now and the end of the year?

July: summer board retreat. Governance skill building. Goal refinement for the second half of the year. Team building. The board steps back from the weekly rhythm to reflect on governance practice.

That is the first half of 2028. It is mapped. It is scheduled. It is ready. Your board's job is to execute it.

**Section 3: Final pre-communications for board president**

Board president, here is your December checklist.

Item one: send a year-end governance summary to the full board. One page. Bullet points. What the board accomplished in the fall arc, what is scheduled for January, and what each board member needs to do before the January meeting. No surprises. No last-minute scrambling.

Item two: confirm the January goal adoption meeting date and time with every board member. Not a calendar invite you send and forget. A personal confirmation: "I have you down for January 10 at 6 PM. Is that still working for you?" If a board member cannot make it, reschedule the meeting. Do not proceed without full board participation for goal adoption.

Item three: schedule one-on-one conversations with each board member between December 20 and January 5. The purpose: confirm that each board member has read the draft goal language, understands it, and supports it. If any board member has concerns, address them before the January meeting. The January meeting is not the place for surprise objections.

Item four: send the January meeting agenda to the board and the superintendent at least one week before the meeting. The agenda should have one item: goal adoption. Nothing else. The board member who sees the agenda knows exactly what the meeting is about and exactly how to prepare.

Item five: write your board president transition documentation. If your term ends at the organizational meeting, your job is not done until the next board president has everything they need. The transition binder. The superintendent check-in notes. The governance calendar. The committee assignments. The goal adoption timeline. Hand it over. Make the transition seamless.

**Section 4: January launch sequence preview**

Here is the January launch sequence your board will execute.

Week one of January: board president one-on-one conversations complete. Final goal language revisions based on board member feedback.

Week two of January: goal adoption meeting. The board adopts 2028 goals. The superintendent receives a clear written directive: "These are the goals. Build your implementation plan. Report back in February."

Week three of January: board president and superintendent meet to align on the February data review format. What data will be presented? What format will it be in? What questions will the board be prepared to answer?

Week four of January: board governance review. The board reviews its own governance process for January. Did the goal adoption go smoothly? Did the board follow the calendar? What can the board improve for February?

Here is the key insight about the January launch sequence. It is the first test of your fall arc work. If your board follows the sequence, the fall arc was successful. If your board abandons the sequence and falls back into reactive governance, the fall arc was a temporary improvement, not a permanent change.

The fall arc built the governance operating cycle. January is the proof point. Execute the cycle. Trust the process. And when February arrives, do the data review you scheduled. Then March's community check-in. Then April's data review. Month after month, decision after decision.

That is what it means to govern on purpose. You built the system this fall. Now operate it.

See you in 2028.
