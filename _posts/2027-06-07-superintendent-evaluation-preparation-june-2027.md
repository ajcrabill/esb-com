**Preparing the Superintendent Formal Evaluation — Evidence, Structure, and Board Alignment**

June 7, 2027

---

Last week's piece laid out the three-phase evaluation structure — board preparation, board discussion, and joint evaluation meeting. Today's piece goes deep on phase one. Because the quality of the evaluation depends almost entirely on the quality of the preparation.

A superintendent evaluation based on documented evidence, aligned board expectations, and a structured evaluation instrument produces a fair assessment and clear direction for the next cycle. A superintendent evaluation based on whatever board members remember from the past six months, with no shared framework, produces a document that doesn't help anyone.

Here's what the board needs to prepare.

**The evidence base the board already has**

The board has been collecting evaluation evidence since February. Not because the board was doing the superintendent evaluation all year — but because the board was doing governance all year, and governance produces evidence that feeds directly into the evaluation.

Here's the evidence the board should have ready:

**Framework documents.** The evaluation framework the board and superintendent agreed on in February — the specific growth areas, the support commitments, the timeline. If the board completed the February 90-day check-in and the May midpoint check-in, those documents exist. If they don't, the board needs to reconstruct them before the evaluation, because the evaluation evaluates progress against what was agreed to in February.

**Data review records.** Every data review the board conducted produced recorded decisions — the board's assessment of goal progress, the adjustments the board directed, the areas where the board requested deeper review. Those decisions document the board's assessment of district performance, which is the board's primary evidence for evaluating the superintendent's execution of board direction.

**Midpoint check-in findings.** The May midpoint check-in produced a documented status for each growth area: on track, needs continued focus, or concern. Those statuses are the board's own assessment from three weeks before the formal evaluation. They are the starting point for the written evaluation.

**Board support delivery records.** The board committed to specific support actions in February — adjusting the data format, clarifying policy direction, providing timely budget guidance. The board's own performance on those commitments is a factor in the evaluation. A board that asks the superintendent to improve and then fails to deliver the support it committed to has created a situation where the board's own performance affects the superintendent's results.

**How each board member prepares a written evaluation**

The written evaluation is each board member's individual assessment. But "individual" doesn't mean "without a shared framework." Every board member should use the same evaluation instrument and the same evidence base.

The evaluation instrument should include:

**Goal-progress assessment.** For each adopted goal, the board member assesses the superintendent's performance in executing the board's direction. The assessment should be based on data — the trajectory the board set in January, the progress data from each review, the superintendent's responsiveness to board direction. Not: "The superintendent seems to be doing a good job with literacy." Instead: "The board's January goal set a trajectory of 85% third-grade reading proficiency by June. May data showed 76%, two points ahead of the trajectory. The superintendent provided timely data packets, responded to board questions at each review, and implemented the instructional coaching adjustments the board directed after the February data review."

**Growth area progress.** For each growth area identified in the February check-in, the board member assesses whether the superintendent has demonstrated improvement, what evidence supports that assessment, and whether the area requires continued focus in the next cycle.

**Board support assessment.** The board member assesses whether the board delivered on its support commitments. This is the board holding itself accountable as part of the evaluation process.

**Overall assessment.** The board member provides a summary assessment and specific recommendations for the next evaluation cycle. The assessment should be specific enough that the superintendent can act on it.

**How the board aligns before the joint meeting**

The board discussion phase is where the individual evaluations become a single board evaluation. The board president or evaluation committee chair collects all written evaluations, identifies areas of alignment and disagreement, and prepares a consolidated document.

The discussion should cover:

**Areas of consensus.** Where does every board member agree on the superintendent's performance? Those areas are straightforward — they go into the board evaluation as the board's unified assessment.

**Areas of disagreement.** Where board members' assessments differ, the board needs to discuss why. Is the disagreement about the evidence? Is it about the standard being applied? Is it about one board member having information others don't? The discussion should resolve the disagreement, not paper it over. If the board cannot reach consensus on an area, the board evaluation should note the range of assessment and the basis for each position.

**The overall evaluation direction.** The board should agree on the overall assessment before the joint meeting. The joint meeting is for presenting the evaluation and discussing next steps — not for the board to discover what it thinks while the superintendent watches.

**What the board should have ready before the joint meeting**

Before the board and superintendent meet, the board should have:

The consolidated board evaluation document — one document that represents the board's collective assessment.
A list of growth areas for the next evaluation cycle — based on what the evaluation reveals.
A list of support commitments the board is prepared to make — based on what the board learned from its own support assessment.
A proposed timeline for the first check-in of the next governance year — typically September or October, after the summer preparation period.

The board that prepares this way enters the joint evaluation meeting with clarity and confidence. The board that doesn't prepare enters the joint meeting hoping the conversation goes well. One approach produces accountability. The other produces procedural compliance.
