**Summer Governance: What the Board Does Between June and September**

July 2, 2027

---

Last week's piece closed the June arc with the fall governance calendar draft. The superintendent evaluation is complete. The board self-assessment is finished. The summer work plan is approved. You have a draft calendar that tells you what September through December should look like.

Now comes the hard part. Not the planning — that's done. The execution. The work between the plan and the first board meeting of the fall governance cycle.

I've worked with boards that do summer well and boards that don't. The difference is not resources or time. The difference is a single belief: the board that treats summer as downtime enters September scrambling. The board that treats summer as preparation time enters September running.

July is the first full month of that preparation time. Here's what the board needs to do between now and September.

**The seven governance priorities for July**

There are seven pieces of governance work the board should complete in July. Not all seven will apply to every board — your board might have already addressed some of them, or your self-assessment might have identified priorities that change the emphasis. But every board should check each one and decide whether it needs attention.

**Priority one: The superintendent summer check-in.** The June evaluation produced growth areas, support commitments, and a plan for the fall. The July check-in is different. It is not an evaluation. It is a conversation — held outside the evaluation framework — that confirms whether the growth areas are still the right ones, whether any new information has emerged, and whether the board is delivering on its support commitments. A 45-minute conversation in July can prevent months of misalignment in the fall.

**Priority two: The board's summer learning agenda.** Your board self-assessment identified specific gaps in your governance practice. The summer learning agenda is how you close those gaps. Not by vaguely committing to "do some training" but by identifying the specific training, reading, or work session that addresses each gap, assigning it to specific board members or committees, and setting a completion date before September.

**Priority three: The community progress report.** Your board adopted goals in January. You reviewed data in February, March, and May. You completed the superintendent evaluation in June. But if you haven't told the community what your governance work produced, you've done only half the job. The Q2 community progress report — published in July — is the board's most important communication of the year. It demonstrates accountability for the goals you adopted.

**Priority four: Fall goal-setting foundation work.** The goals your board adopts in January 2028 will determine the quality of the entire next governance year. But the work that produces good goals starts now, not in January. July is when the board collects baseline data, reviews the previous year's goal outcomes, and begins framing the goal-setting conversation for the fall.

**Priority five: Summer policy work.** Your board's policies are the institutional framework for your governance priorities. If the policies don't support the goals, the goals won't be achieved. July is when the board audits its policy manual, identifies the policies that need revision to align with the next year's direction, and drafts the revisions for fall adoption.

**Priority six: Data review system redesign.** The spring data review cycle is complete. You know what worked and what didn't. July is when you redesign the data review system — the metric set, the format, the schedule, and the decision-recording protocol — before the fall data reviews begin.

**Priority seven: Fall budget cycle preparation.** The budget for the next fiscal year starts taking shape in the fall. The board's ability to give effective budget guidance in October depends on the preparation the board does in July — reviewing the current budget's execution, collecting expenditure trend data, and drafting the budget guidance document.
