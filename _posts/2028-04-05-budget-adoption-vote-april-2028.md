**The Budget Adoption Vote — The Board's Final Decision on Resource Allocation for 2028-2029**

2028-04-05

---

Three months ago, your board gave the superintendent written budget direction.

Two months ago, your board built the goal-to-budget crosswalk, identified alignment gaps, and directed corrections.

One month ago, your board confirmed the corrections, validated the progress monitoring data, asked whether the governance framework worked, and published the community engagement impact statement.

Two weeks ago, your board previewed the three findings that the adoption meeting requires. Last week, your board walked through the transition from budget oversight to data review.

Tonight — April 5 — your board takes the final vote.

Here is what that meeting looks like when it is done right.

---

**The adoption meeting is not a procedural checkbox**

Most school boards treat the budget adoption vote as the end of a process. The superintendent presents the budget. The board asks a few questions. The board votes yes. The meeting moves on. The public hearing runs long because the community is hearing the information for the first time. The board adopts the budget without any record of whether the dollars align with the goals the board adopted in January.

A board that has been executing the governance cycle since January does not have that meeting.

Your board has the three findings drafted — the Budget Alignment Finding, the Governance Health Check Finding, and the Community Engagement Finding. Your board has the crosswalk updated with any adjustments directed in March. Your board has the community engagement impact statement published. Your board has the adoption resolution ready.

The adoption meeting is not the end of the board's work. It is the moment the board demonstrates that the work has been happening since January.

Here is the exact sequence.

**Action one: The public hearing.** The board president opens with the three-document presentation — the crosswalk projected on the screen showing the allocation for each adopted goal, the progress monitoring data showing the current trajectory for each goal, and the community engagement summary showing what the community said and what the board did with it. The president's opening statement takes five minutes. The community asks questions from the evidence. The hearing runs forty-five minutes instead of two hours because the community has already seen the documents online and the board is prepared to answer from the record.

I have watched boards in **Lawrence (KS)** open the public hearing this way. The board president says: "In January, we adopted three goals and gave the superintendent written budget direction. In February, we built the goal-to-budget crosswalk, identified three alignment gaps, and directed the superintendent to make corrections. In March, the superintendent returned with the revised budget, we confirmed the corrections, we reviewed progress monitoring data, and we asked whether our governance framework produced better decisions than we would have made without it. Here is what we found. Here is the budget. Here is the data. Here is what you told us three months ago. We are ready for your questions." The room knows in the first five minutes that this board did the work.

**Action two: The superintendent's budget presentation.** The superintendent presents the final proposed budget — the document that incorporates every adjustment the board directed during the March alignment review. The presentation covers the total budget, the major changes from the preliminary budget, the closing fund balance, and any remaining uncertainties. The superintendent answers questions from the board and the community.

**Action three: The board findings.** The board president — or the finance committee chair — reads each of the three findings into the record. The board votes to adopt the findings as part of the meeting record.

**Action four: The adoption resolution.** The board president reads the adoption resolution, which states that the board has reviewed the proposed budget, found it aligned with the adopted goals, and adopts it as the official budget for the fiscal year. The board votes.

**Action five: The post-adoption transition.** The board president acknowledges the transition from the budget adoption season to the spring governance execution phase and previews the Q2 governance calendar — the April governance check, the May progress monitoring check-in, the June committee reports, and the superintendent evaluation kickoff.

A board that follows this sequence enters the adoption meeting knowing exactly what will happen, in what order, and what each action produces. A board that arrives without the three findings drafted has one outcome: the board votes on a budget without knowing whether the work that should have preceded the vote was actually done.

---

**What the three findings mean at the adoption meeting**

The three findings are the board's record. They serve three different audiences.

**The Budget Alignment Finding serves the superintendent.** It says: We gave you direction. You built a budget that reflects that direction. We reviewed it, challenged it, and confirmed it. The budget is adopted with the board's written finding that the allocation is aligned with the goals. The superintendent enters the fiscal year with the board's documented confidence.

**The Governance Health Check Finding serves the board itself.** It says: Our governance framework functioned as designed in Q1. We set goals, allocated resources, monitored progress, and assessed our own performance. Here is the evidence that the framework worked, and here are the corrections we are making for Q2. The board enters Q2 with a baseline for improvement.

**The Community Engagement Finding serves the community.** It says: You showed up. You spoke. We listened. Here is what you said and here is what changed as a result. The community sees that the board's engagement was genuine — not a compliance checkbox.

I have watched boards in **Adams 14 (CO)** read all three findings at the adoption meeting. The meeting minutes record each finding as an exhibit. Six months later, when the board conducts the Q2 Governance Health Check, the board can pull the Q1 findings and compare. The board can measure whether the framework improved, held steady, or declined. Without the findings, there is no record to compare against.

---

**The transition happens the moment the vote is recorded**

The budget adoption vote is the capstone of the budget season. It is also the launch of the spring governance execution phase.

Here is what changes the moment the vote is recorded.

**The governance focus shifts from resource allocation to performance monitoring.** The dollars are allocated. The board's spring work is monitoring whether those allocations produce the outcomes the board expects. The progress monitoring cadence that begins this month is the board's tool for that work.

**The board's engagement with the superintendent shifts.** Through the budget season, the board gave direction and the superintendent built the budget to match. Through the spring execution phase, the board monitors progress and the superintendent reports results. The relationship stays productive. The role shifts.

**The board's community reporting shifts.** The spring governance report is not about gathering input. It is about reporting results. The board publishes a one-page document that shows what the board adopted in the budget, what the board is monitoring, and what the community can expect in the fall.

I have watched boards in **Albemarle County (VA)** handle this transition explicitly. The board president opens the meeting following the adoption vote with a two-minute statement that names the three governance priorities for the spring — progress monitoring, committee oversight, and community reporting — and confirms the dates for each on the governance calendar. The transition is intentional. The board does not drift into the spring. The board leads.
