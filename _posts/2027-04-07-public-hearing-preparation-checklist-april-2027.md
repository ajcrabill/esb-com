**The Seven Days Before the Public Hearing — What Your Board Should Have Ready**

April 7, 2027

---

Monday's piece described what the public hearing tests — preparation, discipline, and trust. If your board is nodding along without having a plan for the next seven days, this piece is for you. Here's the week-by-week checklist that takes preparation from abstract to specific.

I've watched boards treat the week before the public hearing the same way they treat the day before a board meeting — cramming, hoping, and trusting that the superintendent has it covered. That approach works about as well as you'd expect. The public hearing is the most scrutinized board event of the spring. The community is paying attention. The press is paying attention. The superintendent can prepare the presentation; only the board can prepare the governance.

**Seven days out: Confirm the progress report distribution**

The community progress report your board published in March should already be public. If it's not, day seven is the deadline. The report needs to be on the district website, linked in the district newsletter, and referenced in the hearing notice.

Here's the question I ask boards: can a community member who reads the progress report walk into the hearing already understanding why the board made the budget decisions it made? If the answer is no, the report needs more work. If the answer is yes, you're ready for the next step.

**Six days out: Draft the board president's opening statement**

The board president opens the public hearing. The opening statement sets the tone for the entire evening. It should take three to five minutes and cover three things:

One: a summary of the board's governance calendar since January — goals adopted, budget reviewed, alignment verified. The community should hear that the budget reflects a process, not a night's work.

Two: an explanation of the hearing format — how testimony is taken, how the board will respond, how the record is preserved. The community should know what to expect.

Three: a commitment to transparency — a statement that the board will consider all testimony and will document how that testimony affected the final adoption decision at the separate adoption meeting.

The draft should be shared with the board for input. The final version should be approved by the board — not line by line, but as a representative statement of the board's collective position.

**Five days out: Identify the board's hearing spokespersons**

Monday's piece recommended that one or two board members serve as designated spokespersons for the hearing. Day five is when those designations should be made — and agreed to by the full board.

The spokespersons need two qualities. First, they need to be fluent in the budget-goal connection. They should be able to answer any question about how a line item connects to an adopted goal without consulting notes. Second, they need discipline. They need to answer the question that was asked, stay within the scope of board decisions, and avoid personal opinions.

The remaining board members support the spokespersons by deferring to them — not by offering parallel answers. The board president reinforces the protocol at the start of the hearing: "The board has designated members Smith and Jones to respond to testimony tonight. Other board members may direct clarifying questions to the superintendent, but responses to community testimony will come through the designated spokespersons."

**Four days out: Run the hearing simulation**

Monday's piece recommended a fifteen-minute hearing simulation. Four days out is the time to run it.

Here's the format. The board president opens with the drafted statement. Two board members play the role of community members asking the three most likely questions — which the board should have identified during the day seven briefing. The designated spokespersons respond. The superintendent presents the budget summary. The board practices the transition from hearing to adjournment without adopting.

The simulation reveals everything. A spokesperson who answers with personal opinion instead of board position. A president who rushes the opening. A superintendent who presents the budget without connecting it to goals. A question the board didn't anticipate.

Run the simulation three days before the hearing — that gives the board time to address whatever the simulation reveals.

**Three days out: Brief the full board on the hearing protocol**

The board president convenes a thirty-minute briefing — can be a special meeting or the first thirty minutes of a regular meeting. The agenda: hearing format and timeline, spokesperson designations and rationale, the three most likely questions and board-prepared answers, what to do when a question can't be answered immediately, and how to handle community members who raise concerns the board hasn't previously discussed.

Every board member leaves the briefing knowing their role. Every member knows where to sit, how to address the community, what to say — and what not to say — during testimony, and how to refer questions to the designated spokespersons.

**Two days out: Confirm logistics**

The day before the day before. This is about making sure the event itself runs smoothly. Confirm the room setup, the microphone placement, the seating arrangement. Confirm the sign-in process for community members who want to speak. Confirm the timekeeping protocol — how long each speaker gets, how the board communicates time warnings. Confirm the recording or transcription arrangement.

The logistics belong to the superintendent to coordinate, but a board member — typically the board president or the committee member assigned to hearing preparation — should verify them.

**One day out: Rest**

The day before the hearing is not the day to discover a problem. Everything should be confirmed, briefed, simulated, and ready. The board president makes one final check that the opening statement is printed, the spokespersons are prepared, and the hearing protocol document is accessible. Then the board does what it can't do during the hearing: step back, trust the preparation, and get the rest needed to be present for the community.

**The week after**

A quick note on what comes after the hearing. Monday April 12's piece covers running the hearing itself. But the day after the hearing, the board should have one thing ready: a brief statement acknowledging community participation and confirming the date of the separate adoption meeting. That statement goes out the same week — ideally the next day — so the community sees that the board took the hearing seriously enough to act on it promptly.
