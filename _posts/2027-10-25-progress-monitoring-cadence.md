**Progress Monitoring Cadence — turning data reviews into a governance discipline**

October 25, 2027

---

One data review is not a monitoring system.

Your board has completed its October data review. It has aligned its budget to the data. It has defined its escalation protocol. Those are essential first steps.

But a single October data review, no matter how well executed, will not produce sustained governance improvement. Progress monitoring requires a cadence — a regular, predictable rhythm of data reviews that builds discipline over time.

Here is how to build that cadence from October through December.

---

**Designing the data review cadence**

The cadence has three elements: frequency, format, and duration.

Frequency: your board should review progress data at every regular meeting from October through December. That means monthly data reviews at minimum. Boards that can sustain biweekly data reviews get faster feedback loops and earlier course corrections. Monthly is the floor, not the ceiling.

Format: every data review follows the same structure. The board president opens with the framing question. The superintendent presents data aligned to board goals. The board applies the three-decision protocol. The board documents decisions. Predictable format reduces cognitive load and lets the board focus on the data instead of the process.

Duration: data reviews should be time-boxed. Sixty minutes per review is the standard. The board president protects the time block. If the data requires more discussion, the president determines whether to extend or to carry the item to the next meeting. Data reviews that run indefinitely lose focus.

A board that sets frequency, format, and duration in October builds a rhythm that sustains through the fall. A board that improvises the structure at every meeting never develops the discipline.

---

**The progress monitoring dashboard — one page per goal**

Data dumps destroy data reviews.

The solution is a progress monitoring dashboard. One page per board goal. The page contains four elements.

Element one: the goal statement. Written exactly as the board adopted it. No paraphrasing. The goal is the reference point for every data point.

Element two: the current data point. The most recent available data for the goal. Displayed in a simple format — a single number, a percentage, or a trend line. The board should be able to read this in five seconds.

Element three: the trajectory indicator. Green for on track. Yellow for at risk. Red for off track. The trajectory is determined by comparing the current data point to the goal's expected timeline. Subjective judgment is replaced by a simple calculation.

Element four: the decision from the last data review. What did the board decide? Accept trajectory. Direct change. Request more information. The decision is recorded here so the board can assess follow-through at the current review.

One page per goal. Printed or projected at every data review. The dashboard is the single source of truth for the board's progress monitoring work.

Boards that use a dashboard spend meeting time on decisions, not on data comprehension. Boards that do not spend their data reviews trying to understand what they are looking at.

---

**Follow-through protocol: written decisions from every data review**

The dashboard is useless if decisions are not documented.

Here is the follow-through protocol. At the end of every data review, the board secretary or a designated recorder writes the decision for each goal area addressed. The decision document includes: the goal, the data reviewed, the decision made, the action required from the superintendent, and the return-to-board date.

The decision document is reviewed by the board president before it is finalized. It is distributed to the full board within 48 hours. It is entered into the meeting minutes as an addendum.

At the next data review, the first agenda item is follow-through. The superintendent reports on the action taken since the last review. The board compares the action to the written decision. If the action does not match the decision, the board addresses the gap before reviewing new data.

This protocol creates a closed loop. Decision leads to action leads to review leads to the next decision. Open loops — decisions that are made but not tracked — are the primary reason boards feel like their work does not produce results.

---

**Connecting Oct-Dec cadence to January goal adoption**

The cadence from October through December is not just about this fall's governance. It is the preparation for January's goal adoption cycle.

Here is why. The data your board reviews in October, November, and December reveals what is working and what is not. That evidence base is the foundation for the next goal cycle. Boards that monitor progress through the fall enter January with a clear picture of where their current goals are producing results and where new goals are needed.

Three specific connections.

One: the October data review establishes the baseline for judging the December year-end review. The board can see whether trajectory improved, stayed flat, or declined over the three-month period.

Two: the November mid-year check (covered in next week's post) produces the accountability findings that inform January goal adjustments.

Three: the December year-end review produces the data set the board uses to decide which goals to renew, which to revise, and which to retire.

A board that builds the Oct-Dec cadence well enters January ready to adopt goals that are grounded in evidence, not aspiration. A board that skips the cadence enters January guessing.
