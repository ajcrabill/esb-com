November gave you the checklist. December is where the work begins.

I know how this month usually goes. School boards across the country hold their last meetings in mid-December, approve a few routine items, wish each other happy holidays, and call it a year. January arrives, and suddenly you're scrambling — first board meeting of the new year, organizational votes, a goal-setting session that should have been scheduled weeks ago, and a superintendent evaluation that nobody has thought about since November.

It doesn't have to be this way. In fact, the research consistently shows that the November-December window is the most consequential period for board governance quality. But most boards squander it on compliance tasks instead of strategic transition.

Here's the reframe: December isn't a throwaway month. It's your governance reset.

**Why December matters more than you think**

Let me show you what I mean. I've watched boards across the country navigate this window for years, and the pattern is consistent: boards that manage year-end transitions well carry their goals — and their governance momentum — into the new year. Meanwhile, districts with rapid 3-2 and 4-3 policy reversals — the whiplash boards — see superintendent turnover at 2.3 times the rate of stable boards.

Translation: the work you do — or don't do — between December 1 and December 31 determines whether your board enters January with momentum or with whiplash.

The statistic that got my attention: since 2021, 26 states have passed legislation limiting school board authority. When boards don't govern well — when they appear chaotic, reactive, unfocused — states notice. And state legislatures are becoming less patient with boards that can't demonstrate they're governing effectively.

December is when you prove otherwise.

**The Governance Reset: Three Phases**

November's arc — Board Accountability & Governance Continuity — gave you the December Governor's Checklist. Seven actions, three weeks, your path to a strong finish. If you haven't read it yet, the full checklist is available in the newsletter archive. Go read it. I'll wait.

Now that you have the checklist, here's the framework for actually doing the work. The Governance Reset has three phases, and they map to the four weeks of December:

**Phase 1: Assess (December 1–12).** Look back honestly at 2026. Where did your board succeed? Where did you fall short? Not the district's performance — the board's governance. Did you follow through on the goals you set? Did you monitor progress consistently? Did you function as a governance team, or a collection of individuals? This phase includes the board self-assessment that too many boards skip.

**Phase 2: Evaluate (December 14–19).** The hard work of assessment. Is your superintendent evaluation measuring what matters — or is it measuring attendance at board meetings? Have you delivered preliminary budget guidance for the next fiscal year? This is the week when goal-aligned governance becomes concrete.

**Phase 3: Plan and Commit (December 21–30).** Set up 2027 with intention. What does the board commit to for the coming year? A governance resolution. Renewed board norms. A year-end reflection that your community can read and trust. And a set of actions to take in the final days of December that January will thank you for.

**The ask**

December is where good boards become great boards. It's where the checklist becomes action. It's where you stop reacting to the year that was and start building the year that will be.

But here's the thing about a governance reset: you can't do it alone. A reset requires the full board to be in the same room — not just physically, but intellectually and strategically. It requires honest self-assessment. It requires the kind of conversation most boards avoid because it's easier to just fill the agenda and go home.

Don't let December be the month that slipped away. Let it be the month your board decided to govern differently.
