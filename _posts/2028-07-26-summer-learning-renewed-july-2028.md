**Summer Learning, Renewed — What an Experienced Board Reads and Does Between Cycles**

July 26, 2028

---

Your board completed the June self-assessment. You identified your gaps. The July 5 arc opener asked a question: *What changes when a board has a full cycle behind it?*

Three weeks later, here is the answer. What changes is not the framework. The framework stays the same — goal clarity, progress monitoring, aligned resources, governance team functioning, superintendent partnership. Those practices do not change between cycles.

What changes is the board's relationship to the framework.

A first-cycle board learns the practices. An experienced board calibrates them. A first-cycle board asks *what do we do?* An experienced board asks *how well are we doing it, and what would make it better?*

That distinction matters because the summer reading list for a first-cycle board is different from the reading list for a board that has already executed spring. If your board is still using the introductory summer checklist, you are undershooting what this summer can produce.

Here is what an experienced board reads and does between cycles.

**Four advanced learning areas to study this summer**

The question is not what to read. The question is what to study. Reading is passive. Study is active. Study produces decisions.

**Governance system design.** Your board completed a full governance cycle in spring 2028. You used a specific framework — the ESB governance model. Now the question is whether the framework's default form is the optimal form for your board's context. The summer is the right time to study how other districts have adapted the same framework. Look for districts that have completed multiple governance cycles. Read their board self-evaluations from year two. Look at the adjustments they made to their governance calendars between cycles. The adjustments tell you what the framework cannot do in its default form and what successful boards added to make it work in their specific context.

**Data literacy for governance decisions.** Your board reviewed student outcome data twice during the spring cycle. That is more data review cycles than most boards complete in three years. The next level is not more data — it is deeper analysis of the data you already have. Study how to read trend lines across multiple data points, how to distinguish signal from noise in interim assessment data, and how to ask questions that move the data conversation from *what happened* to *why it happened* to *what we are going to do about it*. The finance committee and the student outcomes committee should each identify one data literacy skill gap from the spring cycle and study it together before September.

**Board-superintendent partnership depth.** The evaluation cycle provided the formal structure for the board-superintendent relationship. The summer provides the informal structure. This is the practice that experienced boards undervalue most consistently. The strongest board-superintendent relationships are built outside the board room — in structured but non-procedural conversations. I recommend three summer conversations between the board president and superintendent: one on fall goal calibration (are the targets too ambitious or too conservative based on spring data?), one on board support calibration (what did the board do that helped and what did the board do that hindered?), and one on communication preferences (what information do each of you need, and in what format?). The conversations are not decisions. They are calibrations. The fall cycle will run more smoothly because the calibrations happened in July.

**Community engagement strategy beyond the meeting room.** Your board met the community engagement requirements of the governance calendar in the spring. The next level is not more meetings — it is more strategic engagement. Study how your district's community communicates about board decisions. Look at the data from the spring cycle: which community members attended meetings, which ones provided public comment, which ones engaged through surveys or forums. Identify the engagement gaps. Then design a summer communication strategy that reaches the communities that were absent in the spring — not with more information, but with specific invitations to participate in the fall goal renewal process.

**Mapping self-assessment gaps to summer learning**

Here is the protocol I recommend.

The board governance committee (or the board president) should take the June self-assessment findings and map each identified gap to one of the four advanced learning areas above. A gap in data review practices maps to data literacy. A gap in board-superintendent communication maps to partnership depth. A gap in community understanding maps to engagement strategy. A gap in committee functioning maps to governance system design.

Each board member selects one gap from the self-assessment and studies the corresponding advanced learning area. The output is a one-page reflection: what the board member learned, how it connects to the governance framework, and one specific practice the board could add or adjust in the fall cycle to address the gap.

The one-page reflections are due to the board president by August 15. The August 2 organizational meeting will give you the committee structure to carry this learning forward. The August 9 governance calendar construction will determine where the practices land in the fall schedule. But the learning itself — the reading, the studying, the conversations — happens now.

**The difference between reading and renewing**

Let me close with a distinction that matters.

Summer reading is not the same as summer renewal. A board can read every governance book on the recommended list, complete every self-assessment, and hold every superintendent conversation — and still enter the fall cycle with the same governance framework it used in the spring. Reading without decision is information collection. Reading with decision is renewal.

The question is not whether your board reads this summer. The question is whether your board makes decisions based on what it reads.
