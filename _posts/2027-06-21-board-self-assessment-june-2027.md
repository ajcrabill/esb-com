**Board Self-Assessment in June — Evaluating the Governance Year**

June 21, 2027

---

The superintendent evaluation is complete. The board has assessed the superintendent's performance, identified growth areas, made support commitments, and set the first check-in date for the fall. That was the board's most important accountability action of the spring cycle.

Now the board needs to do the same thing for itself.

The board self-assessment in June — conducted after the superintendent evaluation, before the summer work begins — is the board's opportunity to evaluate its own governance performance. The board that assesses itself honestly enters the summer with a clear understanding of what it did well, what it needs to improve, and what it should focus on in the summer preparation period.

**Why June is the right time for board self-assessment**

Most boards do a self-assessment in December or January, as part of the new governance year kickoff. That timing has a problem: the board is assessing a governance year that ended six months ago. The board's memory has faded. The specific events, decisions, and challenges are blurry. The assessment becomes general — a vague scorecard that produces generic resolutions.

June self-assessment is different. The governance year just ended. The board's January-through-June work is fresh. The superintendent evaluation is complete, which means the board has already reviewed its own role in supporting the superintendent. And the board is about to enter the summer preparation period, which means the self-assessment findings can directly inform what the board reads, trains on, and plans during the summer.

The June self-assessment is not a replacement for the year-end review. It is a tactical evaluation that feeds the summer work plan. The deep, strategic self-assessment — the one that produces the governance model adjustments and the year-ahead priorities — happens in November or December, when the board has the full perspective of the calendar year. The June assessment is focused: what did this governance year reveal about the board's strengths and gaps, and what should the board do about it this summer?

**The four domains of the June self-assessment**

The board self-assessment should cover the same four ESB domains that structure the board's governance work throughout the year.

**Goal Clarity.** Did the board adopt specific, measurable goals in January that genuinely drove the governance year? Or did the board adopt goals that were broad enough to mean anything — and therefore drove nothing? The board should assess whether its goals passed the specificity test. If the goals were specific, the board should assess whether those goals actually informed the board's data reviews, budget decisions, and community communications. If the goals were vague, the board should make goal-writing the focus of its summer reading and training.

**Progress Monitoring.** Did the board maintain a consistent data review cadence? Did every data review produce a recorded decision? Did the board's data review format improve over the course of the year, or did the board repeat the same format without improvement? The board should assess whether its data reviews produced actionable decisions or became informational briefings. If the data reviews produced decisions, the board should assess whether those decisions were executed. If not, the board should make decision tracking the focus of its summer policy development.

**Governance Team.** Did the board function as a governance team or as eight individuals who happen to share a meeting room? The board should assess meeting discipline — whether meetings started and ended on time, whether the agenda was followed, whether board members were prepared. The board should assess communication discipline — whether board members used the professional channel for communication with the superintendent, whether public statements reflected board consensus or individual positions. The board should assess whether it conducted the February check-in and May midpoint check-in as a unified governance team or whether those conversations revealed fragmentation that the board hasn't addressed.

**Superintendent Partnership.** Did the board maintain a clear, professional partnership with the superintendent throughout the governance year? The board should assess whether the evaluation framework it established in February was fair and whether the board delivered on the support commitments it made. The board should assess whether communication was clear and timely. The board should assess whether the board's actions — not just its words — demonstrated partnership.

**How to structure the self-assessment work session**

The board self-assessment should be a sixty- to ninety-minute work session — separate from the evaluation meeting, separate from the regular board meeting. The work session should be structured as follows:

| Time | Section | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 10 min | Framing | Board president restates the purpose: the self-assessment informs the summer work plan, it is not a permanent verdict. Each domain is assessed on what the board learned, not on what the board did wrong. |
| 15 min per domain | Domain assessment | For each of the four domains, the board discusses two questions: (1) What did this governance year reveal about our strength in this domain? (2) What did this governance year reveal about a gap we need to address this summer? The board records specific findings, not general impressions. |
| 10 min | Priority setting | The board identifies the two or three findings that require summer action and connects each finding to a specific domain in the summer work plan — reading, training, policy development, or next-cycle planning. |

**How the self-assessment connects to the summer work plan**

The June self-assessment should not produce a separate document. It should produce a set of findings that directly feed the summer governance plan approved in the June board meeting.

If the board discovered that its goals were too vague, the summer reading list should include a book on outcome-focused goal-setting. If the board discovered that its data review format didn't evolve, the summer training schedule should include a session on data review design. If the board discovered that board meeting discipline needs improvement, the summer policy development work should include a governance meeting protocol policy revision.

The board that connects its self-assessment to its summer preparation enters the fall not just with a summer work plan — but with a summer work plan that addresses the specific gaps the board identified in its own governance practice.
