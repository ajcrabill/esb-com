**Superintendent Evaluation Preparation — Framework, Timeline, and Board Alignment**

2028-06-07

---

Your board closed the spring data cycle in May.

You have the Progress Monitoring findings from the May 3 data check-in — three data points for every adopted goal, maintain-adjust-escalate decisions, year-end projections. You have the Budget Outcome review from May 10 — the allocation-outcome analysis for every goal, the Q3 adjustment directives. You have the evaluation focus memo from the Mid-Spring Governance Check on May 17 — the board's internal preparation document that names the goals on track, the goals off track, and what the board wants the evaluation to emphasize.

You have everything you need. The question is not whether your board is ready. The question is whether your board has a structured process for turning that evidence into the June evaluation conversation.

That is what this week's piece addresses.

---

**The June arc: Four weeks to close the governance cycle**

The June 2028 Superintendent Evaluation & Year-End Closeout arc runs four weeks. Here is what your board will complete.

**Week one (this week): Superintendent evaluation preparation.** The board takes the evaluation focus memo from the Mid-Spring Check and builds the framework for the June evaluation conversation — the rubric alignment, the evidence package, the conversation structure, and the preparation timeline.

**Week two (June 14): Superintendent evaluation day.** The board conducts the evaluation using the rubric and evidence package prepared in week one. The meeting produces a written finding, a board vote, and a superintendent response. No surprises. No drift. No performance that turns into a personal critique.

**Week three (June 21): Board self-evaluation.** The superintendent evaluation is complete. Now the board turns the lens on itself. The board reviews its own performance across the full January-through-June governance cycle — the calendar adherence, the decision quality, the governance practices that drove results and the ones that added process without impact.

**Week four (June 28): Year-end governance closeout.** The academic year closes. The board reviews what the spring 2028 cycle accomplished, what carries forward to the summer arc, and what the board should prepare for the fall 2028 governance launch.

Four weeks, four specific governance outcomes. Let me walk you through week one.

---

**What your board should accomplish this week**

The superintendent evaluation preparation has four steps. Each step has a specific output. Do not skip any of them.

**Step one: Review the evaluation focus memo against the adopted rubric.**

The evaluation focus memo your board produced in the Mid-Spring Governance Check has a list: the goals on track, the goals off track, the specific areas to emphasize, the questions the board wants answered.

Your board's first task this week is to map that memo onto the adopted superintendent evaluation rubric. Every item on the memo should correspond to a rubric domain or criterion. If an item does not map cleanly, you have two choices: expand the rubric interpretation to include it, or remove the item from the focus. You do not want any evaluation observation that lives outside the rubric — those become ungrounded feedback that neither the board nor the superintendent can anchor to the adopted framework.

I have watched a board in **Aldine (TX)** handle this well. Their board president prepared a one-page crosswalk: the evaluation focus memo items on the left, the corresponding rubric criteria on the right, and a notes column for how the spring evidence maps to each criterion. The crosswalk took forty-five minutes to prepare and saved two hours of confusion in the evaluation meeting.

**Step two: Build the evidence package.**

The evaluation focus memo and the rubric criteria tell the board what to evaluate. The evidence package tells the board how to support each evaluation finding.

Your board's evidence package should include the Progress Monitoring findings from May 3, the Budget Outcome Review findings from May 10, the Q2 Progress Report from May 24, and the superintendent's year-end trajectory narrative from the Mid-Spring Check. Each piece of evidence should be attached to the rubric criterion it supports.

The board president or governance committee chair should prepare this package before the evaluation preparation meeting, not during it. Board members should receive the package at least five days before the evaluation day so they arrive having reviewed the evidence, not reading it for the first time in the meeting.

**Step three: Align the board on the evaluation conversation structure.**

Every board member will walk into the evaluation with some version of their own assessment. The preparation conversation is where the board aligns on a single structure so those individual assessments converge rather than collide.

Here is the structure I recommend.

The evaluation conversation has three phases. Phase one: the board president frames the conversation, reviews the rubric, and confirms the evidence base. Phase two: the board discusses each rubric criterion in order, using the evidence package to support observations. A board member may flag a criterion they want to discuss, but no member introduces an observation that is not supported by the evidence. Phase three: the board synthesizes the discussion into a written finding and a rating for each rubric domain, then votes on the consolidated evaluation.

I have watched boards conduct this structure well and boards conduct it poorly. The boards that conduct it well dedicate the first fifteen minutes of the evaluation to agreeing on the process. The boards that conduct it poorly dive straight into discussion and spend the meeting negotiating structure and substance at the same time.

**Step four: Prepare the superintendent for a productive conversation.**

The superintendent evaluation is not a surprise. The board president should share the evaluation focus memo with the superintendent before the evaluation day — not the board's internal version with raw observations, but a consolidated preview that communicates what the board will emphasize and how the conversation will be structured.

This is professional courtesy, not a concession. Every superintendent I have worked with who received a preview walked into the evaluation ready to engage productively. Every superintendent who walked in cold spent the first thirty minutes of the evaluation defensive. The preview is an investment in the evaluative relationship.

---

**What the evaluation focus memo already tells you**

Your board does not start this week from scratch. The Mid-Spring Governance Check in May produced the evaluation focus memo. That memo already identifies what the board should emphasize in the evaluation.

Here is what a well-prepared evaluation focus memo contains.

**The goals where the district is on track — and what the board attributes that success to.** The board should name the factors that drove progress: the strategy the superintendent proposed, the implementation fidelity, the board's resource allocation, the community partnerships. This prevents the evaluation from becoming a deficit-only conversation.

**The goals where the district is off track — and what the board understands about the factors driving the gap.** The board should distinguish between factors within the superintendent's control and factors outside it. A goal that is off track because of an unanticipated external factor tells a different story than a goal that is off track because the proposed strategy was not evidence-based.

**The specific areas the board wants the evaluation rubric to emphasize.** The board should translate its observations into rubric terminology. If the board wants to emphasize strategic leadership, it should identify which rubric criteria under the strategic leadership domain the evidence supports.

**Any questions the board wants answered before the June evaluation.** The board should list questions the superintendent should address in the evaluation conversation. The questions become part of the evaluation preparation agenda: the board confirms the questions with the superintendent in advance, and the superintendent prepares responses for the evaluation day.

Your board already has this memo. The preparation week is about turning it into the evaluation framework.

---

**The connection to the summer arc**

The Summer Planning Preview from May 31 covered the July-August governance arc — the board retreat, the goal refinement session, the Board Self-Evaluation, the July-August governance calendar.

That arc depends on a clean June closeout.

A board that completes the superintendent evaluation with a well-prepared evidence package and a structured conversation will walk into the summer arc knowing exactly where the district stands. A board that rushes the evaluation or conducts it without preparation will carry unfinished business into the summer — and that unfinished business will surface at the August retreat as a re-litigation of the spring data.

The four-week June arc is designed to prevent that. Each week closes one door so the next week can open the next one. Preparation closes the door on ambiguity. The evaluation day closes the door on the superintendent assessment. The board self-evaluation closes the door on the governance cycle. The year-end closeout closes the door on the academic year.

When your board hits the summer arc in July, all four doors are closed. The board's summer work is forward-looking, not backward-repairing.
