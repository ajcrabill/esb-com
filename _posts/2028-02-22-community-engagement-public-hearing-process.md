**The Public Hearing Is Not a Check-the-Box: How to Run a Community Budget Engagement That Actually Informs Your Vote**

February 22, 2028

---

Every state requires a public hearing on the proposed school budget.

The legal requirements vary — how many days before the adoption vote, how the notice is published, whether the hearing happens during a regular meeting or a standalone session. But here is what I have learned from watching boards across the country: the legal minimum and genuine community engagement are two different things.

A board that runs the bare-minimum hearing gets a lawful adoption. A board that runs a genuine engagement process gets input that surfaces blind spots, trust that carries through the adoption season, and a budget the community recognizes as a reflection of shared priorities.

Your board has already done the hard part. You adopted goals in January. You reviewed the preliminary budget through a goal-alignment lens. You built the goal-to-budget crosswalk. Those documents give your board something concrete to present — a story, not a stack of paper: here are the goals the community helped set, here is how the proposed budget funds them, and here is where your input can still make a difference.

This piece covers what to present, what format to use, and what to do with the input once you have it.

---

**What the law requires vs. what builds trust**

The legal requirements for a public hearing on the budget are almost always about timing, notice, and access.

Most states require the hearing to occur between thirty and forty-five days before the adoption vote. Most require published notice a certain number of days in advance. Most require the budget to be available for public inspection before the hearing. And most require the hearing to be conducted in a public meeting format where community members can speak.

Those are the legal minimums. Meeting them means your budget adoption process is lawful. It does not mean your community feels heard.

Here is what separates boards that meet the legal minimum from boards that build trust.

**Timing matters.** A hearing scheduled for Tuesday at six reaches the people who already attend board meetings. That is a narrow slice. Boards that build trust offer multiple engagement points — a dedicated evening hearing, an online Q&A during daytime hours, a targeted session at a school in the neighborhood most affected by the budget decisions.

**Presentation matters.** Telling the community "the budget is available for review" and opening the floor asks citizens to read a hundred-page document and form opinions on the spot. Boards that build trust present the budget visually — the goals, the crosswalk, the trade-offs — and then invite questions about specific decisions.

**Response matters.** Listening and moving to the next agenda item communicates that the hearing was procedural. Boards that build trust document every question, explain which items can be adjusted and which cannot, and return at the next meeting with a summary of what they heard and how it informed their decisions.

---

**What to present at the hearing**

Your board has two documents from Pieces 1 and 2 of this arc that change what a public hearing can accomplish.

The first is the **preliminary budget review** you conducted through a goal-alignment lens. Instead of presenting a line-item budget organized by functional category — instruction, administration, operations, transportation — frame the presentation around the goals the community heard you adopt in January. The presentation format should answer three questions:

- What did the board commit to in January?
- How does the proposed budget fund those commitments?
- Where were the trade-offs, and why were they made?

The second document is the **goal-to-budget crosswalk** from last week. This is the most powerful community engagement tool your board can bring. A single page mapping each adopted goal to the budget allocations that support it. The community can see, in ninety seconds, whether the money follows the priorities.

Put the crosswalk on a screen. Put it on a handout. Put it on the district website before the hearing. It is the bridge between "here is what we said we wanted" and "here is how we are spending money to get there." Without it, the hearing becomes a conversation about whether taxes are too high. With it, the conversation shifts to whether the allocations match the priorities the community helped set.

**Frame the trade-offs honestly.** Every budget contains trade-offs. The reading intervention program got funding; the technology refresh got deferred. The ELL staffing increased; the professional development travel budget decreased. Boards that build trust name those trade-offs before the community asks about them. Boards that wait to be asked are seen as defensive. Boards that lead with their trade-offs are seen as transparent.

---

**Community engagement strategies beyond the hearing**

A single public hearing on a Tuesday night will not reach every segment of your community. Building trust requires multiple channels.

**Town halls.** Schedule at least one dedicated budget town hall outside the regular board meeting format. Hold it at a school in the neighborhood where the budget decisions will have the most visible impact. Frame the agenda around the goals — not the line items. Start with a fifteen-minute presentation showing the goals, the crosswalk, and the trade-offs. Then open the floor. The board should listen more than it talks.

**Online Q&A.** Publish a recorded presentation of the crosswalk and the budget overview on the district website, then host a live Q&A session during evening hours. Some community members who will not attend an in-person hearing will participate online. Some will submit written questions in advance. Boards that build trust respond to every written question in writing, posted publicly, before the adoption vote.

**Targeted outreach.** The community members most affected by budget decisions are often the least likely to attend a public hearing. If your budget includes changes to a Title I program, an ELL service, or a special education staffing model, those families should hear about it in a format that works for them. A dedicated session at the school that serves those students. A translated summary of the budget and crosswalk. A contact person whose job is to answer questions about that specific program. Boards that build trust go to the community instead of waiting for the community to come to them.

---

**How to gather, document, and respond to public input**

This is the step that separates boards that collect input from boards that act on it.

**Gather systematically.** Assign a board member or staff member to document every question, comment, and concern raised during the hearing and every engagement event. Not just "the community had concerns about funding." Specific, attributable input: "Three parents asked about the ELL staffing reduction at the Washington Elementary session. Two community members asked whether the reading intervention allocation is sufficient to meet the Goal 1 target. One question was submitted online about the technology refresh deferral."

**Document publicly.** Publish a summary of all community input received — organized by goal area or budget category — within one week of the hearing. The summary should include the board's initial response: which items the board can address before the adoption vote, which items require more information, and which items are outside the board's discretion. This document is the record that shows the community its input was taken seriously.

**Respond before the vote.** The April adoption vote should not happen without the board referencing the input it received. A brief agenda item before the vote: "We received forty-two comments during the public engagement process. These are the three themes that emerged most frequently. Here is how each theme informed the final budget." Even if the board cannot accommodate every request, naming what the board heard and explaining the decision builds more trust than silence.

---

**The public hearing is the middle of the process, not the end**

If your board treats the public hearing as a procedural checkpoint, the community will treat it the same way — a night to show up, speak, and wonder whether anyone was actually listening.

If your board treats the public hearing as one component of a broader community engagement process — anchored by the goals the community heard you adopt, informed by the crosswalk that shows how the money moves, and followed by a documented response — the community walks away from the adoption vote knowing its voice was part of the decision.

The difference is not about meeting the legal requirement. The difference is about whether the community trusts the budget it is about to vote on.

Your board has the goals. Your board has the crosswalk. Your board has the next six weeks before the April adoption vote. The question is whether your board will use that time to listen — and whether your community will recognize its voice in the final document.
