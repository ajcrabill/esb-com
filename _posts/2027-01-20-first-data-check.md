**Your First Data Check — What Three Districts Can Teach You About Progress Monitoring**

January 20, 2027

---

Nine days ago, your board adopted its 2027 goals. Specific targets. Measurable metrics. A public commitment.

Today is the first data check-in.

I know what you're thinking: nine days isn't enough time to see progress on anything. You're right. The first data review isn't about measuring results. It's about establishing something more important: the cadence.

If your board reviews data in January — even when there's barely any data to review — it's a board that will review data in February, March, and every month after. If you postpone the first check-in because "there's nothing to measure yet," you're a board that will postpone again in March because "the budget hasn't been approved."

The cadence is the point. And the cadence starts today.

**Three Districts, Three Dashboard Formats, One Lesson**

I've spent time with boards that do progress monitoring well — and boards that don't do it at all. The difference isn't technology or budget. It's the decision to start.

Consider Council Bluffs Community School District in Iowa. Their board uses a printed one-page scorecard. Literally a sheet of paper. Each adopted goal gets a row: baseline data, current data point, target, and a green/yellow/red status indicator. The board reviews that single page at every regular meeting. That's it. No dashboard software. No interactive portal. A piece of paper.

One of their board members told me, "We don't need a system. We need consistency." They're right.

Wichita Public Schools in Kansas takes a different approach. Their board dashboard lives on the district's strategic plan website. It's digital, publicly accessible, and organized by strategic priority. Anyone — board members, parents, community stakeholders — can click through and see where the district stands on each goal. The format changes, but the principle doesn't: every meeting includes a data review anchored to the adopted goals.

Houston ISD operates at yet another scale. Their board performance dashboard is detailed, public, and updated regularly — reflecting the realities of a large urban district under state intervention. The board reviews a broad set of metrics at every meeting, and the public can access the same data the board uses. Transparency isn't an add-on. It's baked into the process.

Three districts. Three different formats. One common thread: every one of them starts the year with a data review and keeps the rhythm going.

**What All Three Dashboard Formats Have in Common**

When you look at how these districts structure their progress monitoring, five design principles emerge:

1. **One-page format per goal set** — whether printed or digital, the entire goal portfolio fits on a single view. No clicking through slides. No searching for the right metric.
2. **Visual status indicators** — green/yellow/red or on-track/off-track. The board can assess the district's health in seconds, not minutes.
3. **Baseline-to-current-to-target comparison** — the board sees where the district started, where it is now, and where it committed to go. Without all three, the data is meaningless.
4. **Data frequency aligns with board meeting cadence** — the data updates as often as the board meets. Not quarterly. Not annually. Every meeting.
5. **Public accessibility** — the same data the board sees, the community can see. Trust flows from transparency.

Notice what isn't on this list: expensive software. Custom-built platforms. Data scientists. The most effective progress monitoring systems are the ones boards actually use, and boards use simple systems.

**My Recommendation: Start With a Printed One-Page Scorecard**

If your board is new to progress monitoring — or if your current process involves a binder of narrative reports — start with the Council Bluffs model.

A single sheet of paper. One row per goal. Four columns: baseline, current, target, status. The superintendent sends it to board members five days before the meeting. Board members spend five minutes reading it before they walk in. The meeting time is spent on questions and decisions, not on reading data aloud.

You can graduate to a digital dashboard later. You can add public transparency later. You can layer in more metrics later. What you cannot do later is establish a cadence you didn't start.

**The Anatomy of a 30-Minute Data Review**

Here's what the meeting itself looks like. I've watched boards complete a meaningful data review in thirty minutes — leaving the room with a clear understanding of where they stand and what they need to do next. And I've watched boards turn the same task into a two-hour slog that produced nothing but frustration.

The difference isn't the volume of data. It's the structure.

**Before the meeting: 5 minutes of board member prep**

Five days out, the superintendent sends the one-page dashboard. Board members read it. That's it. Five minutes of reading time means board members walk in knowing what the data says. The meeting time is spent asking questions and making decisions.

**The superintendent's briefing: 10 minutes**

The superintendent walks through the dashboard. Not a narrative report — a data briefing. Here's where we are on each goal. Here's what changed since the last data point. Here's what we're doing about anything off track.

This is not a presentation. The superintendent shouldn't be explaining basic data points to the board. The board already read the dashboard. The superintendent's job is to add context that isn't visible in the numbers: the intervention that started last week, the staffing change that affected data collection, the community initiative gaining momentum.

Ten minutes. No more.

**Board questions and discussion: 10 minutes**

This is where the ESB framework makes the difference. Board members ask clarifying questions. The goal is understanding, not interrogating.

"Help me understand why we're seeing this trend in the third-grade reading data" — that's a good question.

"Why haven't you fixed this yet?" — that is not a good question.

The board's job is to ensure the superintendent has a plan for anything off track. The superintendent's job is to execute that plan and report back. Green data: acknowledge progress. Yellow data: ask what's being done. Red data: request a written intervention plan with a specific timeline.

**Decision or direction: 5 minutes**

Every data review should produce an outcome. Not a discussion that trails off. A decision.

- On track: the board acknowledges progress and moves on.
- Off track: the board gives direction — provide an intervention plan, report back in 30 days, adjust the target if the baseline was wrong.
- Data unclear: the board asks the superintendent to clarify and report back at the next meeting.

Five minutes. One decision per goal. The meeting ends with everyone knowing what happens next.

**The Dashboard Template**

Here's what the one-page scorecard looks like in practice:

| Goal | Baseline | Current Data | Target | Status |
|------|----------|-------------|--------|--------|
| Increase third-grade reading proficiency to 52% | 42% (Aug) | 43% (Jan interim) | 52% (May) | 🟢 On track |
| Increase Algebra I passing to 68% | 58% (prior year) | 60% (semester 1) | 68% (June) | 🟢 On track |
| Reduce chronic absenteeism to 15% | 17% (start of year) | 18% (Dec) | 15% (May) | 🟡 Slight concern |

Five minutes of reading. Ten minutes of briefing. Ten minutes of questions. Five minutes of decisions. Thirty minutes total.

**Why the First Check-In Matters More Than Any Other**

The first progress monitoring check-in of the year is the most important one — not because of what the data shows, but because of what it establishes.

It establishes that the board takes its goals seriously. The community saw you adopt those goals nine days ago. The superintendent saw you adopt them. If you don't schedule a data review until March, both the community and the superintendent learn that the goals were rhetoric, not commitment.

It establishes the rhythm of the governance year. The board that reviews data in January will review data in February. The board that skips January will find reasons to skip February. The rhythm is set in the first meeting.

And it establishes the relationship between the board and the superintendent. A data review that is collaborative, focused, and productive — with clear questions and clear decisions — tells the superintendent: this board governs with data, not with opinions. That's the foundation of a strong superintendent partnership.

Monday's MLK Day piece asked your board three equity questions. One of them was: when you review data, do you look at district averages or student group breakdowns? Today's data check-in is where you answer that question for real. The averages may look fine. The breakdowns will tell a different story. The board that looks at both — and adjusts its direction accordingly — is a board that governs equitably.

Your board adopted its goals on January 11. The first data check-in is today. Nine days between commitment and accountability.

Establish the cadence now. The rest of the year takes care of itself.
