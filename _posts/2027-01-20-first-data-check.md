**Your First Progress Monitoring Check-In of 2027: Make It Count**

January 20, 2027

---

Nine days ago, your board adopted its 2027 goals. Specific targets. Measurable metrics. A public commitment.

Today is the first check-in.

I know what you're thinking: nine days isn't enough time to see progress on anything. You're right. The first data review isn't about measuring results. It's about establishing a cadence. If your board reviews data in January — even when there's barely any data to review — it's a board that will review data in February, March, and every month after that. If you postpone the first check-in to February because "there's nothing to measure yet," you're a board that will postpone again in March because "the budget hasn't been approved."

The cadence is the point. And the cadence starts today.

**The Anatomy of a 30-Minute Data Review**

I've watched boards turn a data review into a two-hour slog that produces nothing but frustration. I've also watched boards complete a meaningful data review in thirty minutes — leaving the room with a clear understanding of where they stand and what they need to do next.

The difference isn't the volume of data. It's the structure. Here's the structure I've seen work across dozens of districts.

**Before the meeting: 5 minutes of board member prep**

This is the step most boards skip, and it's the step that makes everything else possible.

Five days before the meeting, the superintendent sends a one-page data dashboard. Not a binder. Not a fifty-slide PowerPoint. A single page that shows, for each adopted goal:

- The baseline data (where we started)
- The current data point (where we are)
- The target (where we're going)
- A trend indicator (green — on track, yellow — slight concern, red — off track)

Board members don't need to analyze the dashboard. They need to have seen it. Five minutes of reading time before the meeting means board members walk in knowing what the data says. The meeting time is spent asking questions and making decisions — not reading slides aloud.

**The superintendent's briefing: 10 minutes**

At the meeting, the superintendent walks through the dashboard. Not a narrative report — a data briefing. Here's where we are on each goal. Here's what changed since the last data point. Here's what we're doing about anything that's off track.

The key word is "briefing." This is not a presentation. The superintendent shouldn't be explaining basic reading comprehension scores to the board. The board already read the dashboard. The superintendent's job is to add context that isn't visible in the numbers: the intervention that started last week, the staffing change that affected the data collection, the community initiative that's gaining momentum.

Ten minutes. No more.

**Board questions and discussion: 10 minutes**

This is where most boards go wrong — and where the ESB framework makes the difference.

Board members ask clarifying questions. The goal is understanding, not interrogating. The distinction matters:

"Help me understand why we're seeing this trend in the third-grade reading data" is a good question.

"Why haven't you fixed this yet?" is not a good question.

The ESB framework expects the board to hold the superintendent accountable for results. But accountability is not criticism. Accountability is asking the right questions and expecting meaningful answers. The board's job is to ensure the superintendent has a plan for anything that's off track. The superintendent's job is to execute that plan and report back.

If the data is green — acknowledge the progress. If the data is yellow — ask what's being done. If the data is red — ask for a written intervention plan with a specific timeline.

Ten minutes of this kind of discussion produces more governance insight than an hour of unfocused conversation.

**Decision or direction: 5 minutes**

Every data review should produce an outcome. Not a discussion that trails off. A decision.

- If the data is on track: the board acknowledges progress and moves on.
- If the data is off track: the board gives direction — provide an intervention plan, report back in 30 days, adjust the goal target if the baseline was wrong.
- If the data is unclear: the board asks the superintendent to clarify the data and report back at the next meeting.

Five minutes. One decision per goal. The meeting ends with a clear understanding of what happens next.

**What a Good Dashboard Looks Like**

I've seen a lot of bad data dashboards. Spreadsheets with forty columns. Narrative reports that describe the data without showing it. Presentations that bury the key metrics on slide twelve.

A good board dashboard has four elements:

1. **Goal name** — the specific goal the board adopted
2. **Current data** — the most recent data point
3. **Baseline comparison** — current vs. where the district started
4. **Status indicator** — on track, off track, or data not yet available

That's it. Four elements. One page. Every goal.

Here's what it looks like in practice:

| Goal | Current Data | Baseline | Status |
|------|-------------|----------|--------|
| Increase third-grade reading prof. to 52% | 43% (Jan interim) | 42% (Dec) | 🟢 On track |
| Increase Algebra I passing to 68% | 60% (semester 1) | 58% (prior year) | 🟢 On track |
| Reduce chronic absenteeism to 15% | 18% (Dec) | 17% (start of year) | 🟡 Slight concern |

The board reads this page in five minutes. The superintendent briefs the context in ten minutes. The board asks questions for ten minutes. The board makes decisions in five minutes.

Thirty minutes. One dashboard. Clear outcomes.

**Why the First Check-In Matters More Than Any Other**

The first progress monitoring check-in of the year is the most important one — not because of what the data shows, but because of what it establishes.

It establishes that the board takes its goals seriously. The community saw the board adopt those goals nine days ago. The superintendent saw the board adopt those goals nine days ago. If the board doesn't schedule a data review until March, both the community and the superintendent learn that the goals were rhetoric, not commitment.

It establishes the rhythm of the governance year. The board that reviews data in January will review data in February. The board that skips January will find reasons to skip February. The rhythm is set in the first meeting.

And it establishes the relationship between the board and the superintendent. A data review that is collaborative, focused, and productive — with clear questions and clear decisions — tells the superintendent: this board governs with data, not with opinions. That's the foundation of a strong superintendent partnership.

Monday's MLK Day piece asked your board three equity questions. One of them was: when you review data, do you look at district averages or student group breakdowns? Today's data check-in is where you answer that question for real. The averages may look fine. The breakdowns will tell a different story. The board that looks at both — and adjusts its direction accordingly — is a board that governs equitably.

Your board adopted its goals on January 11. The first data check-in is January 20. That's nine days between commitment and accountability. Establish the cadence now, and you won't have to establish it later.

The rest of the year takes care of itself.
