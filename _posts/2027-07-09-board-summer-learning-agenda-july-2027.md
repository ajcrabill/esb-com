**Designing the Board's Summer Learning Agenda — From Self-Assessment Findings to Targeted Training**

July 9, 2027

---

Two weeks ago, your board completed its June self-assessment. You identified strengths. You identified gaps. You probably came out of that session with two or three areas where the board needs to improve.

Most boards treat those findings as information. A few boards treat them as action items. The best boards treat them as the curriculum for their summer learning.

The summer learning agenda is the most underused governance tool available to school boards. Every board talks about doing training. Very few boards design their training around what their self-assessment actually revealed. The result is training that feels valuable in the moment but doesn't close the gap — because the training was selected before the gap was identified, or the training was generic enough to apply to any board and therefore specific enough to apply to none.

Here's how to design a summer learning agenda that produces measurable governance improvement by September.

**Step one: Extract the training implications from your self-assessment**

The self-assessment produced findings for each of the four ESB domains. Not every finding requires training. Some findings require policy changes, calendar adjustments, or process redesigns. But most findings have a training implication.

Here are examples of self-assessment findings and their training implications:

| Self-assessment finding | Training implication |
|------------------------|---------------------|
| Our goals are too broad — they don't drive specific decisions | Board needs training on writing measurable, outcome-focused goals |
| Our data reviews don't produce decisions | Board needs training on progress monitoring facilitation |
| Board members communicate directly with the superintendent outside the framework | Board needs training on governance team communication protocols |
| We don't have a framework for evaluating the superintendent | Board needs training on superintendent evaluation process design |
| Our community doesn't know what we're doing | Board needs training on community progress reporting and governance transparency |

Each finding maps to a specific training need. Generic training — "governance best practices" — doesn't close the gap. Targeted training — "how to write a measurable goal that drives resource allocation" — does.

**Step two: Select the training format**

Different gaps require different training formats. A knowledge gap — the board doesn't know what it doesn't know — can be closed with reading or a workshop. A practice gap — the board knows what to do but doesn't do it — requires a facilitated work session where the board practices the skill with real board business.

| Gap type | Best format | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Knowledge gap | Reading, workshop, or online course | "Read Chapter 3 of *Measure What Matters* on outcome-driven goal-setting" |
| Skill gap | Facilitated practice session | "Practice writing measurable goals using your district's current data" |
| Process gap | Policy or protocol development session | "Draft the board communication protocol that defines the professional channel" |
| Discipline gap | Governance team work session with accountability commitment | "Each board member commits to three specific behavioral changes for the fall, checked at the October board meeting" |

**Step three: Assign and schedule**

Each training item needs three things: a specific board member or committee responsible for organizing it, a completion date, and a deliverable. The deliverable is what the board member brings back to the rest of the board — a written reflection, a proposed protocol, a policy draft, a recommended resource.

Without a deliverable, the training is an event. With a deliverable, the training is an investment that produces a return for the full board.

**Step four: Build in accountability**

The board should decide at the start of the summer how it will check that the training was completed. Options include: a dedicated agenda item at the August board meeting where each board member reports on their training, a written summary submitted to the board president by August 15, or a facilitated discussion at the August work session where the board connects the training to the fall governance calendar.

The accountability mechanism should be agreed on before the training begins — not after the training is supposed to be done, when it's easier to let it slide.

**Sample summer learning agenda**

Here's what a complete summer learning agenda looks like for a board whose self-assessment revealed vague goals and unproductive data reviews:

| Board member | Training item | Format | Completion date | Deliverable |
|-------------|---------------|--------|-----------------|-------------|
| Board president | *Measure What Matters* — chapters 1-4 | Reading | Jul 25 | One-page reflection on how goal-setting improves board decision-making |
| Vice president | ESB Progress Monitoring online module | Online course | Jul 30 | Data review protocol draft for August board discussion |
| Board member 3 | Council Bluffs goal alignment case study | Case study review | Aug 1 | Proposed goal-writing framework for August work session |
| Board member 4 | Board communication protocol template | Review and draft | Aug 10 | Draft board communication protocol for August board discussion |

The board that follows this structure enters August with completed training, concrete deliverables, and a plan for integrating the learning into the fall governance cycle.
