**Pre-Thanksgiving Governance Reset — The Pivot from Reactive to Intentional Governance**

November 22, 2027

---

Here is what happens on most school boards in late November.

The board has a brief meeting the Tuesday before Thanksgiving. A few board members are traveling. The superintendent is distracted by December deadlines. The agenda is light. Everyone is looking forward to a long weekend.

And then December arrives.

December is the most compressed governance month of the year. The annual organizational meeting. The year-end governance review. Goal adoption preparation. Budget forecasting. Holiday schedules. The board goes from a light November to a sprint December without any transition planning.

Here is what your board needs to do differently. Use Thanksgiving week as a governance reset. Not as a vacation from governance — as an intentional pause to assess where you are and where you are headed.

**Section 1: Why Thanksgiving week is the governance reset opportunity**

Thanksgiving week sits at a unique point in the governance calendar.

October was heavy. Two data reviews. Budget alignment conversations. Escalation protocol decisions. Progress monitoring cadence adjustments.

Early November was focused. The mid-year accountability check. Goal writing. The superintendent fall check-in.

By Thanksgiving week, the board has completed the execution phase of the fall arc. The next month — December — is the transition phase. The board shifts from data review and partnership conversations to structural decisions for the year ahead.

Here is why that transition matters. October and November are about asking questions. December is about making decisions. The organizational meeting, the year-end review, and the goal adoption process all require the board to act as a unified body. If the board enters December divided, unclear, or exhausted, those decisions will suffer.

Thanksgiving week gives the board a natural break. Not from work — from the weekly meeting rhythm. Here is what your board should do with that break.

First, stop meeting. Do not schedule a board meeting the week of Thanksgiving. Give board members and the superintendent time to think instead of time to deliberate.

Second, reflect individually. Each board member should answer three questions before December: What is working about our governance? What is not working? What do I want to change?

Third, come back ready. The December planning session should be the board's first conversation after Thanksgiving — not the December regular meeting. Plan before you meet.

**Section 2: The board culture check**

Here is the uncomfortable question most boards avoid. Is your board functioning as a governance team?

By late November, every board has enough data to answer this question honestly. You have been meeting together for four months since the summer retreat. You have had tough conversations about data, budget, and superintendent performance. You know who prepares for meetings and who does not. You know who trusts the superintendent and who does not. You know who respects the governance process and who treats board meetings as a platform.

Here is the board culture check protocol. Each board member answers five questions privately and shares the results with the board president.

Question one: Do I feel safe expressing a dissenting opinion at the board table?

Question two: Does the board president ensure all voices are heard before decisions are made?

Question three: Do I trust that my board colleagues are acting in good faith?

Question four: Do our board meetings start and end on time?

Question five: Do I leave board meetings feeling that our time was well spent?

Here is what your board will discover. Most boards score high on questions one and three. Most boards score low on questions four and five.

That pattern tells you something. Your board has the raw ingredients for high-functioning governance — trust and safety. But your board has a meeting rhythm problem. Meetings run long. Agendas are padded. Discussion goes in circles.

A board culture check that reveals a meeting rhythm problem is a gift. It means the board does not need a relationship overhaul. It needs a meeting structure overhaul. And that is fixable in a single planning session.

**Section 3: December planning session**

Your board needs one planning session in December before the regular meeting schedule resumes.

Here is the agenda for that session.

First, review the governance calendar. December has the annual organizational meeting and likely one or two regular meetings. Map those meetings against the governance requirements. The organizational meeting must produce committee assignments, a board president and vice president election, and a governance calendar for 2028. The regular meetings must complete the year-end governance review. If the board has both of these on the same meeting night, the board will be in session for four hours. Split them. Use the planning session to decide the meeting calendar.

Second, align on December meeting roles. Who runs the organizational meeting? Who presents the year-end review? Who drafts the goal adoption timeline? Assign each December deliverable to a board member or the superintendent. Do not leave December to chance.

Third, set January expectations. The January goal adoption meeting is the board's most consequential governance decision of the new year. The board needs to agree, in December, on the goal adoption process. Who drafts the goal language? What data informs the goals? What community engagement happens before the January meeting? The planning session is the right place to make those decisions before December's meeting rhythm takes over.

**Section 4: Connecting the reset to January 2028 launch**

Here is the leadership move your board president needs to make.

The December planning session produces a January launch sequence. That sequence has three components.

Component one: the goal adoption meeting. By the second week of January, the board should adopt goals for the 2028 governance year. Those goals should be informed by the October and November data reviews. They should reflect the superintendent's capacity as assessed in the fall check-in. And they should be the board's only focus for the January meeting.

Component two: the board president's January communications. Between December and January, the board president should meet individually with each board member to confirm their understanding of the 2028 goals. If there is disagreement, the president addresses it in January. If there is confusion, the president clarifies it in January. The January meeting should not be the first time a board member sees the goal language.

Component three: the governance calendar for the first quarter of 2028. The planning session should produce a January-through-March meeting schedule. Data review dates. Committee meetings. Community engagement sessions. Published on the district website before January 15.

Here is what your board gains from the Thanksgiving reset. Intentionality. The board that pauses in November to plan December and January is a board that governs. The board that stumbles into December without a plan is a board that reacts.

Thanksgiving week is your reset. Take it.
