**The Goal-to-Budget Crosswalk — How Your Board Verifies Resource Allocation Matches Priority**

February 8, 2028

---

Last week I showed you the three-bucket framework for your February preliminary budget review — goal alignment, fiscal guardrails, and community alignment.

This week I am going deep on the most important bucket. The one that separates boards that govern the budget from boards that just approve it.

The goal-to-budget crosswalk.

Here is exactly what it is, why it matters more than most boards realize, and how your board builds one in time for the February meeting.

---

**What the goal-to-budget crosswalk is — exactly**

Let me be precise about this.

A goal-to-budget crosswalk is a document that maps every adopted goal to the specific budget allocations — line items, programs, staffing positions, instructional materials, contract services — that directly fund progress on that goal.

I am not talking about a general statement like "the budget supports early literacy." I am talking about a specific answer to a specific question: *For each adopted goal, what is the total dollar amount and what percentage of the general fund does it represent?*

The crosswalk is not the budget itself. The budget is organized by function and object — instruction, administration, operations, transportation. The crosswalk is organized by goal. It takes the budget and re-sorts it through the lens of the board's adopted priorities.

Here is the format I recommend.

A one-page table with three columns: the adopted goals listed in priority order on the left, the specific budget allocations for each goal in the middle, and the percentage of the general fund allocated to each goal on the right.

That is the crosswalk. One page. Three columns. Fifteen minutes for the board to review.

I referenced this format last week when I talked about the **Adams 14 (CO)** board's Goal-to-Budget Alignment Summary. Today I am going to walk through exactly how your board builds it.

---

**Why the crosswalk matters more than most boards realize**

I have watched hundreds of boards adopt measurable student outcome goals. I have watched those same boards approve budgets that had no identifiable connection to the goals they just adopted.

Here is what happens.

The board adopts five goals in January. The superintendent builds the budget using the same process she has used for the last ten years — start with last year's budget, adjust for enrollment changes, add a cost-of-living increase for staff, and balance to projected revenue. The budget is presented in February. The board reviews it. The board approves it.

The goals and the budget never touch each other. They exist in parallel universes — the goals in the board's strategic plan binder, the budget in the finance department's spreadsheet. The board goes through the motions of goal setting and budget adoption without ever asking whether the two documents are connected.

The crosswalk forces that connection.

Let me give you a specific example.

I worked with a board that had adopted four goals in January, clearly ranked in priority order. Goal one was early literacy. Goal two was math proficiency. Goal three was college and career readiness. Goal four was operational efficiency.

The board asked the superintendent to build a goal-to-budget crosswalk for the February meeting. When the crosswalk came back, the numbers told a different story than the priorities.

Early literacy — the board's top priority — received 12 percent of the general fund allocations that were mapped to goals. Math proficiency — priority two — received 31 percent. The board's fourth-priority goal — operational efficiency — received more than the top-priority goal.

The superintendent had not built the budget against the goals. She had built the budget the same way she always had — protect existing programs, fund what was easiest to fund, and hope the goals were general enough to fit somewhere. The crosswalk exposed the gap.

The board had a choice to make. Accept that the goals were aspirational and the budget was not going to fund them — which meant telling the community the goals were not really operational. Or direct the superintendent to reallocate resources so that the funding matched the priorities — which meant cutting something to fund early literacy.

That choice is governance. The crosswalk is what makes the choice visible.

A board that does not build the crosswalk does not face that choice. It approves a budget that may or may not fund its goals and discovers in December — when the data comes back — that the goals were never resourced to succeed.

---

**The four-step crosswalk methodology**

Here is exactly how your board builds the crosswalk.

**Step one: List the adopted goals in priority order.**

Your board did this as part of the January budget direction conversation. If you have five adopted goals but never ranked them, the crosswalk will not help you. The crosswalk is a comparison between what the board said matters most and what the budget actually funds. You need the board's priority ranking to make that comparison.

If your board did not rank the goals during the January budget direction conversation, do it now. It takes ten minutes at the February work session. List the goals. Vote on the priority order. Then proceed with the crosswalk review.

**Step two: Ask the superintendent to build the crosswalk.**

Give the superintendent a clear template — the one-page, three-column format I described above. The left column is the board's goals in priority order. The superintendent fills in the middle column — the specific budget allocations that fund each goal — and the right column — the percentage of the general fund each allocation represents.

This is not a request the superintendent should have to figure out on her own. The board should hand her the template at the end of January and say: *For the February meeting, we need this one-page document.*

A superintendent who has built the budget from the goals will fill out the crosswalk in an hour. A superintendent who has not will take longer, because she will have to reverse-engineer the connection between the budget she built and the goals the board adopted. Both outcomes are useful information for the board.

**Step three: Review the crosswalk as a board.**

When the crosswalk arrives in the February board packet, here is how the board reviews it.

First, check completeness. Does every goal have an identifiable allocation? If a goal has no allocation — or an allocation labeled "general fund — unallocated" — the board has a question to ask. Either the goal is not funded, or the superintendent could not connect the goal to a specific resource. Either answer needs discussion.

Second, check the percentage. Compare the priority ranking (step one) to the funding ranking (calculated from the percentage column). If goal one in priority is goal four in funding, that is a governance problem. The board needs to decide whether the gap is acceptable or whether it needs correction.

Third, check for reasonableness. Some goals legitimately require smaller allocations than others. A goal focused on operational efficiency may require less new spending than a goal focused on early literacy intervention. The crosswalk does not demand that every goal receives the same percentage. It demands that the board can see the relationship between priority and funding and make a conscious decision about whether that relationship is acceptable.

**Step four: Adopt a finding on goal-to-budget alignment.**

The board's review of the crosswalk should produce a written finding — adopted by board action at the February meeting — that does three things:

1. Names whether the budget allocation for each adopted goal matches the board's priority ranking
2. Identifies any goals whose allocation is significantly higher or lower than the priority ranking would suggest
3. Directs the superintendent to either maintain the current alignment or prepare a revision for the March budget alignment review

A board that adopts this finding in February enters March with a clear position. The March budget alignment review becomes a targeted check on the corrections the board requested — not a re-litigation of the February crosswalk conversation.

A board that reviews the crosswalk in February without adopting written findings will discover in March that no one remembers what the board decided and the superintendent has no direction to work from.

---

**What the crosswalk reveals that nothing else will**

I want to name something important about the crosswalk that most boards miss.

The crosswalk does not just reveal whether the budget funds the goals. It reveals whether the board and the superintendent share the same understanding of what the goals mean.

Here is what I mean.

A board adopts a goal that third-grade reading proficiency will increase from 42 percent to 55 percent by December 2028. The board sees that goal as an operational commitment — the district will allocate resources, adjust instruction, and measure progress until the target is met.

The superintendent sees the same goal as an aspiration — something the district hopes to achieve if funding allows and if other priorities do not interfere.

The crosswalk exposes that gap. When the superintendent submits a crosswalk that shows a minimal allocation for the third-grade reading goal, the board has evidence — not a suspicion — that the goal was not treated as operational. The board can then have a specific conversation: *We adopted this goal as an operational commitment. The crosswalk shows a minimal allocation. Do you see this goal the way the board sees it?*

That conversation cannot happen without the crosswalk. Without it, the board and superintendent can go through an entire budget cycle operating on different assumptions about what the adopted goals mean — and no one discovers the misalignment until the data comes back in December showing no progress.

---

**The connection to the second Progress Monitoring check-in**

Your board's second Progress Monitoring check-in is happening this month — the follow-up to the January baseline data check.

Here is the connection your board should be making explicitly.

The crosswalk answers the question: *Are we funding what we said matters?*

The Progress Monitoring check-in answers the question: *Is what we are funding producing results?*

These are not separate questions. They are the same governance question viewed from two angles. A board that answers both questions in the same meeting — using the crosswalk for the resource side and the Progress Monitoring data for the results side — has a complete picture of whether its governance framework is working.

If the crosswalk shows adequate funding for a goal and the Progress Monitoring data shows that goal is on trajectory, the board has confirmed alignment. The framework is working.

If the crosswalk shows adequate funding for a goal and the data shows the goal is behind trajectory, the board has identified a program effectiveness problem — the resources are there, but the strategy is not producing the expected results. The board asks the superintendent about the strategy, not the funding.

If the crosswalk shows inadequate funding for a goal and the data shows that goal is behind trajectory, the board has identified a governance problem — the board set the priority but did not ensure the priority was funded. The board needs to decide whether to reallocate resources or adjust expectations.

This is the most powerful governance conversation a board can have in February. It is also the one most boards never have — because they never built the crosswalk that makes it possible.

---

**What the February crosswalk conversation should produce**

The output of the February goal-to-budget crosswalk conversation should be three things.

First, a completed one-page crosswalk document that becomes part of the official February board packet — not a working document that sits on the finance committee chair's desk. The full board sees it, discusses it, and votes on the findings it produces.

Second, a written board action that adopts the board's finding on goal-to-budget alignment — whether the allocations match the board's priority ranking, which goals are over- or under-funded relative to priority, and what direction the superintendent has for the next budget revision.

Third, a specific date for the March budget alignment review — the meeting where the board will check whether the corrections requested in February have been made.

A board that produces these three outputs in February enters March with a governance framework that is complete. The budget is connected to the goals. The board knows where the gaps are. The superintendent has clear direction for the next revision.

A board that reviews the crosswalk in February without producing these outputs will find itself in March with unanswered questions, unresolved gaps, and a superintendent who does not know whether the board wants changes or not.
