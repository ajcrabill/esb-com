**September Community Connection — Communicating the Year Ahead Before Fall Execution**

September 27, 2028

---

The community has not heard from you since June. Three months is a long silence in a relationship that depends on trust.

I am not talking about the meeting minutes that get posted to the district website or the public hearing notices that appear in the legal section of the local newspaper. I am talking about an intentional communication — a moment when the board looks at the community it serves and says, "Here is what we did, here is what we prepared, and here is what you should expect from us in the months ahead."

September is that moment. Before the fall execution cycle begins in October, the board has an opportunity to reconnect. The community wants to know three things, and the board should answer all three clearly.

**What the board accomplished in the spring cycle**

The community does not follow board meetings. They do not read the 80-page budget document. What they know about the board's spring work comes from what the board chooses to tell them.

September is the time to tell them. Not in a 45-minute presentation at a regular meeting that three people attend. In a community-facing document — a one-page summary, a newsletter, a video update posted to the district's communication channels — that names the board's spring accomplishments in terms the community can understand.

Here is what that document should include.

First, the goals the board set in January. Name them. Third-grade reading proficiency. Chronic absenteeism. Algebra readiness. Whatever the board's 2028 goals were, the community should be able to see them in plain language. Not the goal language from the board packet. The plain-language version that a parent at the grocery store can read and understand.

Second, what the board found. The spring Progress Monitoring cycle produced data — not just numbers, but findings. "We found that third-grade reading proficiency improved from 42 percent to 46 percent after the spring intervention." "We found that chronic absenteeism in the middle schools increased in April and May, and that trend requires the board's attention this fall." The community needs to know what the board's data review actually revealed. Boards that keep their findings internal are boards that do not trust their communities with the truth.

Third, what the board decided. Every Progress Monitoring check-in produced maintain, adjust, or escalate decisions. The community should know what those decisions were. "We maintained the literacy intervention allocation because the data showed it was working." "We adjusted the attendance target because the spring trend revealed a deeper challenge than we anticipated." "We escalated the algebra readiness goal to a formal committee review because the data showed it was not on track." The decisions demonstrate that the board is acting on the data, not just reviewing it.

**What the board prepared over the summer**

Most communities assume the school board goes dormant in June and reawakens in August. That assumption is reinforced every time the board goes three months without communicating.

The summer preparation work — the Organizational Reset sub-arc, the committee restructuring, the governance calendar construction, the three sub-arcs the board completed from July through September — is invisible unless the board makes it visible.

The community does not know the board redesigned its committee structure unless the board explains what changed and why. They do not know the board built a governance calendar for fall execution unless the board publishes that calendar. They do not know the board completed its goal renewal unless the board announces the renewed goals.

Here is what the community communication should cover from the summer preparation work. The committee structure change and the rationale. The governance calendar from October through December. The goal renewal decisions — which goals carried forward, which goals were refreshed, and why. The budget monitoring system the board launched in September. Each of these represents work the board did when the community assumed the board was on break. Each is an opportunity to demonstrate that the board is governing year-round.

**What the community should expect in the fall**

This is the forward-looking piece, and it is the most important one. The community needs to know what the board will be doing from October through December so the community can engage, respond, and hold the board accountable.

The board's fall communication should name four things.

The first Progress Monitoring check-in date. The community should know when the board will review fall data for the first time and what data the board will be looking at. October 10 is the first fall data review. The community should know that date exists and what the board expects to learn from it.

The community progress report schedule. The board commits to publishing a fall community progress report — this is the October 24 piece in the governance calendar — that updates the community on the fall cycle findings. The date goes on the community's calendar.

The public engagement opportunities. If the board is holding a community input session, a town hall, or a public hearing for the mid-year budget revision, those dates should be published now. Not in October. Not in November. Now, while the community can plan to participate.

The board's decision-making framework. The community should know that the board will apply the maintain-adjust-escalate framework to every data review finding and every budget variance. This is not jargon. It is a commitment the board makes to the community: "We will not look at your children's data and do nothing. We will decide what to maintain, what to adjust, and what to escalate. And we will tell you what we decided."

**The communication vehicle**

The vehicle matters less than the content. Some boards publish a September community newsletter. Some boards produce a three-minute video update from the board president. Some boards include a community-facing summary in the September regular meeting agenda packet and distribute it to local media. Some boards schedule a community conversation — a voluntary event, not a board meeting — where community members can ask questions about the fall execution cycle.

The right vehicle is the one that reaches the community where they are. If your community reads the local newspaper, publish an op-ed. If your community follows the district's social media, produce a short video. If your community shows up to board meetings, include the summary in the board packet and read it into the record.

What does not work is silence. Three months of silence followed by a board meeting in October where the board expects the community to understand what happened in the spring and what is about to happen in the fall — that is not how trust works.

**Springfield MO offers a useful example.** The board I coached there produced a "Year Ahead" one-pager every September. It was a single page, double-sided. One side summarized the spring cycle accomplishments and the summer preparation work. The other side laid out the fall calendar with key dates and described the board's decision-making framework. The board president distributed it at the September regular meeting, posted it to the district website, and submitted it to the local newspaper's community section. The community response was not overwhelming — most people did not read it. But the people who did read it had a framework for understanding what the board was doing and why. And when a controversy arose in November about a mid-year budget adjustment, the board could point to the September document and say, "We told you this was coming."

That is the standard. Communicate before the controversy, not during it.
