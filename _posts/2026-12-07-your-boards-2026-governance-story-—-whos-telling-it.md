**Your Board's 2026 Governance Story — Who's Telling It?**

December 07, 2026

**Series:** Year-End Governance Reset & 2027 Planning

---

Here's a question I want you to answer honestly: who gets to tell the story of your board's 2026?

If your answer is "the local news" — a reporter who covered three board meetings all year and wrote the year-end recap based on the most controversial moments — that's a problem. If your answer is "nobody" — the year came and went and the community has no idea what the board actually did — that's a bigger problem.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: if your board can't articulate what it accomplished, what it learned, and what it's accountable for, you haven't done the reflective work that accountability requires. A year-in-review communication isn't a nice-to-have. It's a governance act.

## Why the Year-in-Review Matters More Than You Think

November's accountability arc walked you through seven actions to close the year strong. Action 7 was "Send a Year-End Community Communication." The December Governor's Checklist made it sound simple — a brief thank-you, a nod to accomplishments, a forward look.

And it is that simple. But it's also more important than you might realize.

Let me tell you why. Most boards spend the year communicating reactively. A crisis hits and you issue a statement. A budget vote comes up and you send a newsletter. A facility bond fails and you hold a community forum. You're always responding to events instead of shaping the narrative.

The year-in-review is your chance to be proactive. It's the one time during the year when the board gets to define the story on its own terms — not in response to a crisis, not to defend a decision, but simply to say: here's what we committed to, here's what we achieved, here's what we didn't, and here's what we're accountable for going forward.

That's not PR. That's governance. A board that can't give an honest account of its year hasn't done the work of self-reflection that every governing body owes the people it serves.

## The Difference Between a Press Release and a Governance Account

I've seen dozens of school district year-end communications. Most of them fall into one of two categories:

**The press release.** "The board is proud to announce a year of progress and achievement in our schools." Three paragraphs of self-congratulation. Photos of board members cutting ribbons. Nobody reads them because nobody trusts them.

**The compliance document.** "As required by board policy, attached please find the annual report." A PDF that goes into a digital folder and disappears. Nobody reads these either.

Neither one tells the community what it actually needs to know: did students get better? Did the board keep its promises? What did the board learn from its mistakes?

A governance account is different. It has five parts:

### 1. What We Set Out to Do

Start with the goals your board adopted at the beginning of the year — or whenever your current goal cycle started. List them plainly. Not a summary. The actual goals: "Increase third-grade reading proficiency from 62% to 70% by June 2027." "Reduce chronic absenteeism to 5% districtwide."

The community may not know your board has specific student outcome goals. Tell them. Showing your work is the first step toward earning their trust.

### 2. What We Achieved

For each goal, report the data. Not a narrative. The numbers. Third-grade reading proficiency: currently at 65%, up three points, on track. Chronic absenteeism: 7%, down two points from last year but above the target.

Let the community see the progress. Let them see the gaps. A board that hides its data is a board that doesn't trust its community with the truth.

### 3. What We Didn't Achieve

This is where most year-end communications stop. They report the wins and avoid the losses. That's not an account — it's a highlight reel.

If a goal was abandoned or fell short, name it. Say why. "We set a goal to reduce chronic absenteeism to 5%. We reached 7%. The schools we targeted showed improvement, but we didn't have the resources to scale the intervention districtwide. We're reworking the approach for 2027."

The community doesn't expect perfection. They expect honesty. A board that publicly acknowledges where it fell short earns more trust than one that acts like everything went perfectly.

### 4. What We Learned

Every shortfall is a learning opportunity. What would the board do differently? What surprised you about the data? What did you discover about the district's challenges that you didn't understand at the beginning of the year?

This is the reflective part. It signals that the board sees itself as a learner, not a manager. It tells the community: we're paying attention, we're adjusting, and we're not satisfied with the status quo.

### 5. What We're Accountable for Going Forward

Close with the board's commitments for the coming year. What goals will you carry forward into 2027? What new goals are you setting? What are you promising to monitor and report on?

This is the accountability bridge. It turns a year-end reflection into a forward-looking commitment. The community knows what to hold you accountable for in the year ahead.
