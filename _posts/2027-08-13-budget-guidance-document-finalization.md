**The Budget Guidance Document — Why the Board's August Draft Determines October's Quality**

August 13, 2027

---

The board started the fall budget cycle preparation in July. The current year budget execution was reviewed against the goals adopted in January. Expenditure trend data was collected.

That work positioned the board to produce the budget guidance document — the written direction the board delivers to the superintendent in October, outlining the board's priorities and expectations for the next fiscal year's budget.

Here is the timeline most boards get wrong: the guidance document should be drafted in August, not October. The October conversation should be a confirmation of the draft, not a first draft conversation.

**Why August is the right time to draft**

The budget cycle starts in October, when the board delivers budget guidance to the superintendent. The superintendent then develops the budget proposal over the following months for spring adoption.

A clear, specific guidance document produces a budget proposal that reflects the board's priorities. A vague document produces a proposal based on the superintendent's assumptions.

Drafting in August — two months before the October conversation — changes the dynamic. The board has time to discuss, revise, and finalize without deadline pressure. The board has time to share the draft with the superintendent for informal feedback before the formal October conversation.

**What the budget guidance document should contain**

A governance-quality budget guidance document has five sections.

**Section one: Strategic priorities.** State the board's top three to five priorities for the next fiscal year, flowing directly from the goals the board adopted in January. A priority statement: "The board expects the FY28 budget to prioritize resources toward improving third-grade reading proficiency, as measured by the state reading assessment, consistent with the board's adopted goal of 85% proficiency by December 2028."

**Section two: Resource allocation principles.** State how new or additional resources should be directed, how existing resources should be evaluated based on effectiveness, and how reductions should be prioritized.

**Section three: Budget development parameters.** State the specific parameters — revenue projection assumptions, staffing cost assumptions, fund balance targets, debt service coverage requirements.

**Section four: Timeline and decision points.** State the budget development timeline: formal guidance in October, preliminary proposal in January or February, public hearing in March or April, final adoption in April or May.

**Section five: Linkage to goal outcomes.** State how the board will evaluate the budget proposal — not on whether it is balanced, but on whether it allocates resources likely to produce progress toward the board's adopted goals. The balance is a constraint. The priorities are the purpose.

**How to complete the draft in August**

Three steps.

**Step one:** The board president or finance chair drafts the document before the August board work session, based on the budget preparation work completed in July.

**Step two:** The full board reviews and revises the draft at the August board work session — forty-five minutes, each section read and discussed. The goal is a document every board member can defend to the community.

**Step three:** The board president shares the draft with the superintendent for informal feedback before the October formal conversation. Does the guidance create any practical problems? Is anything impossible or counterproductive to implement? The feedback ensures the board's direction is grounded in operational reality.

**What the board avoids by drafting in August**

Every board that has told me "We gave budget guidance in October and the budget proposal came back looking nothing like what we asked for" has one thing in common: their guidance was drafted in October under time pressure, without adequate preparation.

The board that drafts the guidance document in August — with time for discussion, the current year budget execution as context, and the goal-setting foundation work as the framework — produces guidance the superintendent can actually implement. And when the budget proposal arrives in January, the board has a written document to hold against it, not a vague memory of what was discussed in October.
