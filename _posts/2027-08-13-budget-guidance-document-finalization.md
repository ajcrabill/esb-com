**The Budget Guidance Document — Why the Board's August Draft Determines October's Quality**

August 13, 2027

---

The board started the fall budget cycle preparation in July. The current year budget execution was reviewed against the goals adopted in January. Expenditure trend data was collected. The budget development timeline for the fall was identified.

That work positioned the board to produce the budget guidance document — the written direction the board delivers to the superintendent in October, outlining the board's priorities and expectations for the next fiscal year's budget.

But here is the timeline that most boards get wrong: the budget guidance document should be drafted in August, not October. The October conversation with the superintendent should be a confirmation of the draft, not a first draft conversation.

**Why August is the right time to draft**

The budget cycle for the next fiscal year starts in October, when the board delivers budget guidance to the superintendent. The superintendent then develops the budget proposal over the following months, presenting it to the board for review and adoption in the spring.

The guidance document is the board's most important budget action of the year. It sets the framework for everything that follows. A clear, specific guidance document produces a budget proposal that reflects the board's priorities. A vague, general guidance document produces a budget proposal that reflects the superintendent's assumptions about the board's priorities — which may or may not be accurate.

Here is the reason most boards produce vague guidance documents: they write them in October, under time pressure, without adequate preparation.

Drafting the guidance document in August — two months before the October conversation — changes the dynamic. The board has time to discuss, revise, and finalize the document without the pressure of an approaching deadline. The board has time to align around specific priorities rather than settling for general statements. The board has time to share the draft with the superintendent for feedback before the formal October conversation.

**What the budget guidance document should contain**

A governance-quality budget guidance document contains five sections:

**Section one: Strategic priorities.**

This section states the board's top three to five priorities for the next fiscal year. These priorities should flow directly from the goals the board adopted in January and the goal-setting foundation work completed in July and August.

A priority statement looks like this: "The board expects the FY28 budget to prioritize resources toward improving third-grade reading proficiency, as measured by the state reading assessment, consistent with the board's adopted goal of 85% proficiency by December 2028."

A non-example: "The board expects the budget to support student achievement." That is a sentiment, not a priority. It does not tell the superintendent what to fund, what to protect, or what to cut.

**Section two: Resource allocation principles.**

This section states the principles the board expects the superintendent to apply when allocating resources across the district. These principles should address: how new or additional resources should be directed (toward the strategic priorities), how existing resources should be evaluated (based on effectiveness in producing outcomes), and how reductions — if necessary — should be prioritized (protect core instructional priorities, reduce administrative non-essentials first).

**Section three: Budget development parameters.**

This section states the specific parameters the superintendent should use when developing the budget proposal. Parameters include: the revenue projection assumption the board expects the superintendent to use, the staffing cost assumptions, the fund balance target, the debt service coverage requirements, and any other financial constraints the board wants to acknowledge.

**Section four: Timeline and decision points.**

This section states the budget development timeline and the board's decision points. The timeline should include: the date the board will deliver formal budget guidance to the superintendent (October), the date the superintendent will present the preliminary budget proposal (January or February), the date of the public hearing and first reading (March or April), and the date of the final adoption (April or May).

Each decision point should specify what the board expects to receive and what the board will decide.

**Section five: Linkage to goal outcomes.**

This section closes the loop. It states how the board will evaluate the budget proposal: not on whether it is balanced, but on whether it allocates resources in a way that is likely to produce progress toward the board's adopted goals.

This is the section that separates a governance budget guidance document from an administrative budget guidance document. The board is not asking the superintendent to produce a balanced budget. The board is asking the superintendent to produce a budget that advances the board's priorities. The balance is a constraint. The priorities are the purpose.

**How to complete the draft in August**

Three steps for the board this month:

**Step one: The board president or finance chair drafts the document.**

One person should draft the guidance document before the August board work session. The draft should be based on the budget preparation work completed in July — the current year budget execution review, the expenditure trend data, and the board's goal-setting foundation work.

**Step two: The full board reviews and revises the draft at the August board work session.**

The board should allocate 45 minutes on the August board work session agenda to review the draft. Each section should be read aloud, discussed, and either confirmed or revised. The goal is not to produce a perfect document. The goal is to produce a document that every board member can defend to the community.

**Step three: The board president shares the draft with the superintendent for informal feedback.**

Before the October formal conversation, the board president should share the draft with the superintendent and ask: does this guidance create any practical problems for the budget development process? Is there anything in the draft that would be impossible or counterproductive to implement? This informal feedback does not change the board's direction. It ensures the board's direction is grounded in operational reality.

**What the board avoids by drafting in August**

Every board that has told me, "We gave budget guidance in October and then the budget proposal came back looking nothing like what we asked for" has one thing in common: their guidance was drafted in October, under time pressure, without the preparation work that makes budget guidance specific enough to hold the superintendent accountable.

The board that drafts the guidance document in August — with time for discussion and revision, with the current year budget execution as context, with the goal-setting foundation work as the framework — produces guidance that the superintendent can actually implement.

And when the budget proposal arrives in January, the board has a document to hold against it. Not a vague memory of what the board discussed in October. A written document with specific priorities, principles, and parameters that the board can use to evaluate whether the budget proposal reflects the board's direction.
