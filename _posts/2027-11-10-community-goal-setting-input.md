**Community Input for Goal-Setting — How to Gather Stakeholder Feedback Before January's Adoption Vote**

November 10, 2027

---

The board has drafted goal language. The data from the October and November data reviews provides the baseline. The superintendent has reviewed the targets for feasibility.

Now comes the step that most boards skip: asking the community what it thinks before the board votes.

Here is why most boards skip it: the board is accountable for student outcomes. The board adopted the framework. The board reviewed the data. The board drafted the language. The board is the governing body. Why should the board ask the community for input on goals that the board is elected to set?

The answer is not about governance authority. It is about governance effectiveness. A goal that the community does not understand, does not support, or does not know exists is a goal that the board will defend alone when the data review shows slow progress.

A goal that the community has been invited to shape is a goal the community will support through the inevitable periods when the data is not what everyone hoped it would be.

**What community input for goal-setting looks like**

Community input for goal-setting does not mean the community votes on the goal language. The board retains adoption authority. Community input means the community has an opportunity to review the draft goals, understand the data that supports them, and provide feedback before the board's final vote.

Here are three formats that work, depending on the district size and community engagement capacity.

**Format one: A public goal-setting input session.** The board hosts a dedicated meeting — not a board meeting, not a work session — focused on the draft goals. The board presents the data that supports each goal, the proposed target, and the timeline. The community provides verbal feedback, asks questions, and submits written comments. The board takes the feedback into consideration before the January adoption vote.

This format works best for districts where community engagement is already established. The board can expect a modest turnout from parents, advocacy groups, and community organizations. The session should be recorded and published for community members who cannot attend.

**Format two: A written comment period.** The board publishes the draft goals on the district website with a two-week comment period. The community submits written feedback through a structured form. The board reviews the feedback at a public meeting and publishes a summary of what was received and how it informed the final goal language.

This format works for every district. It requires no additional meeting capacity. It gives community members time to review the goals, think about their feedback, and submit considered input. The board should publish the draft goals with a brief data summary so the community understands the rationale behind the targets.

**Format three: A stakeholder survey.** The board distributes a brief survey to parents, staff, and community members through the district's communication channels. The survey asks three questions: what priorities should the board focus on in the coming year, what concerns does the community have about student outcomes, and what does the community want the board to know as it finalizes the goals.

This format reaches the broadest audience. It is not a substitute for a public session or a comment period, but it provides the board with a wider range of input than either format alone.

**What the board should do with the input**

Community input is not binding. The board should make clear from the beginning that the input is advisory — the board retains the authority to set goals based on its governance judgment.

But the board should also take the input seriously enough to document it and respond to it. I recommend three actions after the input period closes:

One. The board or a board committee reviews every piece of input received — verbal, written, survey responses — and identifies themes. Three to five themes is typical.

Two. The board publishes a brief summary of the input received and how it informed the goal language. The summary does not need to address every individual comment. It should identify the themes and explain how the board's final goal language reflects or addresses the community's feedback.

Three. The board includes the input summary in the January goal adoption packet. The community sees that its input was received, reviewed, and responded to before the board voted.

**What the board should do this month**

If the board has draft goal language from the November goal-drafting work, the board should launch the community input process this month. The timeline:

- Week 2 of November: Publish the draft goals and announce the community input process
- Weeks 2-3 of November: Run the input process (public session, comment period, or survey)
- Week 4 of November: Review the input and refine the goal language
- December: Board reviews the refined draft and prepares for January adoption

The board that launches the input process in November enters January with goals that are data-informed, community-aware, and ready for adoption.
