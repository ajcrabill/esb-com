**Summer Work Sets Up Fall Success — What Your Board Should Be Planning Now**

April 26, 2027

---

Let me step back and name what this April arc produced.

Your board completed the spring accountability cycle. The public hearing was treated as an accountability event — not a procedural requirement. The mid-year governance check-in evaluated whether February's adjustments worked. The superintendent evaluation process reached its midpoint with structured evidence collection and a clear path to June. The year-end progress report framework was established. The spring community engagement plan was designed.

That's a full month of accountability work. The boards that complete this arc are the boards that enter the summer with a clear picture of where their governance practice stands.

But the governance year doesn't end with June. The summer months — July and August in most districts — are the board's opportunity to prepare for the next cycle. And the boards that use summer well are the boards that enter the fall already running.

**The three categories of summer governance work**

Summer governance work falls into three categories.

**Category 1: Professional development.** The board identifies one or two areas where its governance practice needs strengthening and commits to reading, training, or learning on those topics over the summer.

The mid-year governance check-in identified which of the five indicators need the most attention. If meeting structure was a persistent issue, the board reads about effective board meeting design. If governance model alignment eroded during the spring, the board reviews the Governance Team framework and discusses how to reinforce it in the fall.

The board should assign one or two pieces of reading for the summer — a case study, a framework document, a chapter from *The Governance of the Board of Trustees for Educational Excellence*. The June board meeting includes a discussion of what each board member will read and when the board will reconvene in August to discuss it.

I've watched boards assign summer reading and never discuss it. The books sit on shelves. The learning doesn't transfer to practice. The board that structures summer professional development with a specific discussion session — "we'll meet in August for two hours to discuss what we read and what it means for our board" — creates accountability for the learning.

**Category 2: Next-year goal preparation.** The board uses the year-end progress report data and the spring community engagement findings to prepare for the next goal-setting cycle.

The goal-setting work starts in summer, not fall. The board that waits until September to think about next year's goals is the board that rushes through goal adoption in October and adopts goals that haven't been tested against data or community input.

Summer goal preparation has three steps.

Step 1: The board reviews the year-end data for each current goal. Which goals met their trajectory? Which ones didn't? What does the data suggest about whether the target should be maintained, adjusted, or replaced for the next year?

Step 2: The board reviews the spring community engagement findings. What did the community identify as priorities? How do those priorities align with the year-end data? What tensions exist between community input and the data — and how should the board resolve them?

Step 3: The board develops draft goal options. Not final goals — draft options that the board will refine and finalize in the fall. The draft options give the board a head start on the fall goal-setting work and allow the board to present community-tested options rather than starting from scratch.

I've watched a board complete its summer goal preparation and present three goal options to the community in September. The community had two months to react before the November adoption vote. The board adopted goals that reflected both the data and the community input — because the board started the work in June, not September.

**Category 3: Governance calendar planning.** The board builds the next governance year's calendar before the year starts.

The calendar doesn't have to be detailed. It should establish the major milestones — goal adoption month, data review cadence, budget review timing, evaluation check-ins, and community engagement events. The board that enters the fall with a calendar has a structure to work within. The board that enters the fall without one spends September and October building a calendar that should have been ready in August.

The summer calendar planning session should produce a one-page document: July through June, with the major governance events marked. The board reviews it at the August meeting, adjusts it for any fall developments, and confirms it at the September meeting. The calendar becomes the board's governance road map for the year.

**The summer board meeting structure**

The summer months typically have one or two board meetings. The board that uses those meetings for governance preparation — not just operational business — creates momentum for the fall.

I recommend a summer schedule that looks like this:

| Month | Meeting Focus | Outcome |
|-------|---------------|---------|
| June | Year-end report publication, superintendent evaluation, summer reading assignments | Governance year closes, summer work begins |
| July | Light business meeting (if required) | Operational continuity |
| August | Summer reading discussion, draft goal options review, governance calendar review | Board enters fall with shared knowledge, goal options, and a calendar |

The August meeting is the most important. It's the board's transition from summer preparation to fall execution. The board discusses what it read, reviews the draft goal options, and confirms the governance calendar. By the end of the August meeting, the board should have a shared understanding of what the fall requires and when the key milestones are.

**What the Spring Accountability arc produced**

Let me close by naming what the full January-through-April Governance to Impact arc has produced.

January: Goals adopted. Governance calendar locked. Superintendent evaluation process established.

February: First data review with recorded decisions for each goal. Budget reviewed for goal alignment. Superintendent 90-day check-in completed. Policy audit conducted. Board-superintendent relationship reset. Governance team health check-in completed.

March: Budget alignment review using the three-question framework. Goal-to-budget crosswalk built and analyzed. Community progress report published. Combined data review and budget alignment meeting conducted. Board-to-community trust communication strengthened.

April: Spring accountability arc completed — public hearing as an accountability event, mid-year governance check-in, superintendent evaluation midpoint, year-end reporting framework, spring community engagement plan.

That's a governance year in four months. Most boards don't accomplish this much in twelve. If your board has been following this arc, you're operating at a level that puts you in the top tier of school boards in the country.

The summer work sets up the next cycle. The fall launches the next year. The board that completes spring accountability and uses summer wisely is a board that builds a compounding governance practice — each year stronger than the last, each board member more effective, each governance cycle producing better conditions for student success.
