**The Year-End Governance Reset: Why December Matters More Than You Think**

December 7, 2026

---

Most boards treat December like an airport layover.

You know exactly what I'm talking about. The board meeting is short. The agenda is thin. A couple of routine votes, maybe a holiday reception. Everyone's distracted by the calendar — wrapping up work projects, planning family gatherings, trying to figure out when winter break actually starts. And somewhere in the back of everyone's mind: we'll deal with the serious stuff in January.

Here's what I've learned watching boards for two decades: December is the most strategically important month of the year. And most boards waste it.

## The December Drift

November's accountability arc gave you a clear picture of where you stand. You reviewed your goals. You assessed your board's performance. You checked your governance continuity plan. You know what's working and what isn't.

That's valuable information. But information without action is just expensive trivia.

The problem with December is that it's sandwiched between two high-energy periods: the accountability work of November and the goal-setting push of January. It feels like a natural down month — a time to coast while the calendar winds down. The truth is exactly the opposite. December is the runway. If you don't use it to prepare for takeoff, January will be a taxi to nowhere.

I've worked with districts where the board's December meeting was the shortest of the year — thirty minutes, consent agenda, holiday closing, done. And I've worked with districts where the December board meeting was a four-hour governance reset that completely reshaped the following year. The difference wasn't the budget. It wasn't the superintendent. It was a deliberate decision by the board to treat December as work, not downtime.

## The Governance Reset: Three Moves

A real governance reset has three components. None of them require a committee. None of them require a policy change. All three of them require the board to be intentional about how it uses the next four weeks.

### Move 1: Review the Data — Honestly

This is the hard part. November gave you the framework for accountability. December is where you actually look at the numbers and ask the uncomfortable question: did we move the needle or not?

Here's what I mean. Your board set specific student outcome goals at some point — maybe last January, maybe when you adopted your strategic plan. Pull out those goals. Look at the data from this school year. Not the anecdotes. Not the vibes. The actual data.

Is third-grade reading proficiency up or down compared to this time last year?
Did chronic absenteeism improve in the schools you prioritized?
Are the students who were behind in math catching up, holding steady, or falling further behind?

If you can't answer those three questions with actual data — not impressions, not "we think so," not a report you remember seeing somewhere — then the first move of your governance reset is building a data system that gives you answers. Because you cannot reset what you cannot measure.

### Move 2: Decide What Needs to Change

Here's where most boards get stuck. They review the data. They see the gaps. And then they do nothing, because the gaps feel overwhelming and the holiday calendar is thin and it's easier to kick the hard decisions to January.

Don't do that.

The data will tell you one of three things:

1. **We're on track.** Our goals are right, our strategies are working, and we need to stay the course. In this case, the reset is about reinforcing what's working and identifying where we can push harder.

2. **We're off track but know why.** Our goals are right but our strategies need adjustment. In this case, the reset is about naming the gap and deciding what changes to make.

3. **We're off track and don't know why.** Our goals are right but we don't have enough data to diagnose the problem. In this case, the reset is about building the diagnostic capacity you should have had from the start.

I've never worked with a board that fell into category one and stayed there. The districts that are on track are the ones that are constantly reviewing and adjusting. The ones that fall off track are the ones that set goals in January and don't look at them again until budget season.

### Move 3: Build the 2027 Plan

This is where the reset becomes actionable. Once you know where you stand (Move 1) and what needs to change (Move 2), you build the plan for 2027.

I'm not talking about a new strategic plan. I'm talking about a one-page governance plan that answers three questions:

- What are our three to five student outcome goals for 2027?
- What data will we review at every board meeting to track progress?
- What decisions do we need to make in the first quarter to set ourselves up for success?

That's it. Three questions. One page. If you can answer those three questions by December 31, you'll walk into January with more clarity than ninety percent of school boards in the country.

## Why December Works

Here's what most boards don't understand about timing: December is the only month of the year when there's enough distance from the previous year and enough proximity to the next year to do a real reset.

January is too busy. Everyone's back from break, the legislative session is starting, the budget calendar is looming, and the immediate demands of running a district push strategic thinking to the margins.

March is too late. By March, the school year's rhythm is set. You're reacting to what's happening, not shaping what will happen.

December is the sweet spot. The first semester data is available. The spring initiatives haven't started. The budget cycle is still months away. You have a window — a narrow one — to step back, assess honestly, and build a plan before the machinery of the new year kicks into gear.

## The Reset Starts Now

November gave you the accountability framework. December is where you use it.

I've seen boards transform their entire year by taking December seriously — by treating it as a work month instead of a layover. The boards that do this consistently are the ones that don't just survive the new year. They thrive in it.

Your December Governor's Checklist from November gave you the what. This governance reset gives you the why and the how. Now the question is whether your board will use the next four weeks to prepare for takeoff — or taxi aimlessly into January.

The community is watching. And they're not looking for a board that drifts through December hoping for a better 2027. They're looking for a board that uses every month — including this one — to deliver the education their students deserve.

Let's get to work.
