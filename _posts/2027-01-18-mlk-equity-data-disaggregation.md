**MLK Day: The Equity Data Your Board Isn't Disaggregating (But Should Be)**

January 18, 2027

---

Martin Luther King Jr. Day is the only federal holiday that asks us to do more than remember. It asks us to act.

And there's no more powerful governance action you can take on this day — or this week — than to look at the data you're not looking at.

I've worked with boards across the country who tell me they're committed to equity. They've passed resolutions. They've formed committees. They've made public statements. And I believe they mean it.

But when I ask to see the data — disaggregated by race, by socioeconomic status, by English learner status, by special education status — most boards can't produce it. Not because the data doesn't exist. Because they've never asked for it.

## The Failure of the Aggregate

Most school boards review data in aggregate. Third-grade reading proficiency is 65%. Chronic absenteeism is 18%. Math proficiency is 58%.

Those numbers hide more than they reveal.

A district can have a 65% third-grade reading proficiency rate and still have a 35-percentage-point gap between white students and Black students. That aggregate 65% feels like progress. The disaggregated data tells a different story.

I've seen districts celebrate "improving" graduation rates while their graduation gap between economically disadvantaged students and their peers remained unchanged for five years. The aggregate was going up. But the equity gap wasn't closing.

You cannot govern for equity using aggregate data. Aggregate data tells you whether the district is improving on average. It doesn't tell you who is being left behind.

## The Equity Data Checklist

Here's what I want every board to review this week. Not next month. Not when the equity committee meets. This week.

### Check 1: Disaggregate Every Metric Your Board Reviews

For every metric on your 2027 scorecard, ask the superintendent to provide the data disaggregated by at least:

- Race and ethnicity
- Socioeconomic status (free/reduced lunch eligible vs. not)
- English learner status
- Special education status

If the data is available by subgroup, ask for subgroup data. If it's not available, ask why not and when it will be.

### Check 2: Identify the Widest Gaps

Once you have disaggregated data, identify the three widest gaps in your district.

Not the gaps that are most politically convenient to address. The gaps that are widest — where the difference between student groups is largest and most persistent.

Those gaps are your board's equity signal. They tell you where the system is failing students most consistently. And they tell you where your governance attention needs to be directed.

### Check 3: Ask Whether Your Goals Address the Gaps

You set your 2027 goals in December or early January. Now go back and look at them through an equity lens.

Do your goals explicitly address the gaps you identified? If your third-grade reading goal is to move from 65% to 72%, but the gap between white and Black students is 35 percentage points, raising the aggregate to 72% won't close that gap. You need a goal that directly targets the gap.

The most effective equity goals I've seen are specific: "Reduce the third-grade reading proficiency gap between Black and white students from 35 to 25 percentage points by June 2027." That's a goal you can govern toward. That's a goal you can monitor. That's a goal that demands action.

### Check 4: Establish Equity-Focused Data Cadence

Your January data cadence should include a standing equity data review. Not an annual equity report. A data review at every board meeting, disaggregated by the subgroups that matter in your district.

The format is the same as your regular data dashboard — metric, current data, target, trend, indicator — but with an equity overlay. For each metric, the dashboard shows both the aggregate and the subgroup data. The board can see at a glance whether all groups are improving, whether gaps are closing, or whether some groups are being left behind.

## The MLK Question

Here's the question I want every board to ask itself this week.

If Dr. King walked into your boardroom and asked to see your data, could you show him which students your system is serving well and which students it's leaving behind?

Not a mission statement. Not a resolution. Not a committee charter. The actual data, disaggregated by the dimensions that matter.

If you can answer that question with a dashboard on your meeting room screen, you're on the right track. If you can't, you have work to do — and this week is the right time to start it.

Equity isn't a value statement. It's a data question. And the data is already there, waiting for you to ask for it.
