**Summer Governance Planning — Why the Board That Plans Its Summer in May Enters Fall Running**

May 26, 2027

---

The June board meeting is the last scheduled governance event of the spring arc. After it, most boards slow down. Meetings become less frequent. Agendas become lighter. Board members check out for the summer and return in August or September to start the next governance year.

That pattern costs the board the most productive months of the year.

June, July, and August are the board's preparation months. The district's operational pace slows — schools are closed or operating at reduced capacity, the superintendent's calendar is less packed with school-year demands. That creates time for the board to do the work it can't do during the school year: reading, training, policy development, relationship building, and planning the next governance cycle.

A board that plans its summer in May is a board that enters the fall having done the preparation work. A board that waits until September to figure out its summer work is a board that enters the fall having lost three months.

**What the summer governance plan should include**

The summer plan covers four domains.

**Reading.** The board should agree on a reading list for the summer. I recommend two to three books on governance, leadership, or student outcomes, plus the district's own documents — the strategic plan, the most recent district performance audit, the superintendent's annual report, and any state or federal policy changes that affect the district. Each board member reads the same materials so the board enters the fall with a shared knowledge base.

The board should also agree on how the reading will be discussed. I recommend a ninety-minute summer work session — in July or August — dedicated to discussing the reading. Each board member comes prepared with their top three takeaways and at least one recommendation for how the board's practice should change based on what they read.

**Training.** Most states require board member training hours. The summer is the time to complete them. But I recommend going beyond the minimum requirement. The board should identify specific training topics that address the weaknesses the governance closeout revealed.

If the governance self-assessment identified gaps in governance model alignment, the board should schedule a governance model refresher training. If the board discovered that its data review protocol wasn't producing actionable decisions, the board should schedule a data literacy training. If the board realized its communication discipline needs reinforcement, the board should schedule a board communication protocol training.

The training should be specific to the board's needs, not generic. A board that completed a full spring governance cycle has specific gaps that only a custom training or a targeted coaching session can address.

**Policy development.** The summer is the board's best opportunity for policy work. The school year agenda is packed with operational items, budget amendments, and time-sensitive decisions. The summer agenda is lighter. The board can use the time to review policies that need updating, develop new policies based on the spring governance cycle's findings, and prepare policy revisions for fall adoption.

The policy work should be connected to the governance closeout findings. If the closeout revealed that the board's policy on data reporting format was unclear, the summer is the time to revise it. If the closeout revealed that the board's superintendent evaluation policy needs updating to reflect the governance-evaluation framework, the summer is the time to develop the revision.

**Next governance year planning.** The summer planning session should produce a draft governance calendar for the fall. The calendar should reflect what the board learned from the spring closeout — which events should be scheduled differently, which events need more time, which events could be combined or eliminated.

The planning should also include goal-setting preparation. If the board plans to adopt new goals in fall, the summer is the time to collect baseline data, review the current goals' effectiveness, and prepare the board for a goal-setting work session.

**How to structure the summer planning discussion**

The summer planning discussion should be part of the June board meeting or a dedicated June work session. I recommend a sixty-minute agenda item structured as follows:

| Time | Section | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 10 min | Framing: Why summer planning matters | Board president frames the summer as preparation time, not downtime. Reviews the governance closeout findings that inform the summer plan. |
| 15 min | Reading plan | Board agrees on the reading list and the discussion work session date. |
| 10 min | Training plan | Board reviews the state training requirements, identifies governance-specific training needs from the closeout, and schedules training sessions. |
| 15 min | Policy development priorities | Board reviews the closeout findings and identifies two to three policy priorities for summer work. Board assigns board members to draft or lead each priority. |
| 10 min | Next governance year calendar | Board reviews the draft fall calendar and identifies any dates that conflict with known commitments. Board schedules the goal-setting preparation work. |

**What the board president should prepare**

The board president carries the responsibility for preparing the summer planning discussion. Here's what I recommend the president prepare.

First, a draft reading list with two to three governance books and a note on why each one is relevant to the board's specific situation. The list should be circulated before the June meeting so board members can add their own recommendations.

Second, a draft training schedule with the state requirement, the training the board needs based on the closeout, and proposed dates. The president should identify whether the training will be delivered by an external provider or a staff member.

Third, a draft fall governance calendar with the key events and their proposed dates. The calendar should explicitly reflect adjustments from the spring closeout — if the spring calendar had events that were too tightly scheduled, the fall calendar should address that.

Fourth, a draft list of policy priorities with three to five policies the board should review or develop over the summer. Each priority should be connected to a governance closeout finding.

**What the board produces**

The summer planning discussion should produce a written summer governance plan covering: the reading list and discussion date, the training schedule and provider, the policy development priorities and assignments, and the draft fall governance calendar.

The plan is distributed to all board members. The board president tracks progress through the summer. The board reviews the plan at the first fall meeting and adjusts as needed.

A board that enters fall with a completed reading discussion, a completed training, progress on policy development, and a finalized governance calendar is a board that starts the next governance cycle at full speed. A board that enters fall with none of that preparation done is a board that spends September figuring out what it should have done in July.

The spring governance arc demonstrates what a board can accomplish when it plans, executes, and evaluates. The summer is where the board demonstrates that it can sustain that discipline through the full year.
