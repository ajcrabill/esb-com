Your board just changed. Your governance doesn't have to.

I've worked with enough post-election boards to know the pattern. Election night arrives, the votes are counted, and the next morning three things happen at once:

1. The returning members breathe a sigh of relief — they kept their seats.
2. The newly elected members start figuring out what they actually signed up for.
3. The board collectively wonders: *how do we go from here to functioning as a team?*

Here's what the research shows. Kogan, Lavertu, and Peskowitz studied school board turnover across hundreds of districts. Their finding: turnover alone doesn't predict outcomes. What matters is *how* boards manage the transition. And the Institute for Education Sciences confirms that new board members need 6 to 12 months to reach full effectiveness.

Six to twelve months. That's half your board's annual operating cycle spent getting up to speed. And in that window, governance continuity is fragile.

**The continuity question nobody asks**

Let me tell you about a board I worked with that did this right. In late 2025, after a contentious election, this board — I'll call it District A — faced 40% turnover. Three new members joining two incumbents. The natural instinct would have been to reset everything. New goals. New priorities. New way of doing business.

But the board chair did something different. Before the new members were even sworn in, she organized a preboarding session. Not onboarding — preboarding. The distinction matters.

Onboarding is what happens after someone takes their seat. Preboarding is what happens *before* — when the returning members document the board's current commitments, current goals, current governance practices, and hand them to the incoming members with a simple statement: *here's where we are. You're joining this work, not replacing it.*

The result? That board maintained goal continuity through the transition. They didn't lose January and February to organizational chaos. They started the new year with all five members — new and returning — aligned on the same set of goals, the same governance calendar, and the same commitment to student outcomes.

**The framework for a governance-preserving transition**

Here's what I've learned from watching boards that manage transitions well and boards that don't. The difference comes down to three things:

**1. Separate preboarding from onboarding.**

Preboarding is about institutional knowledge transfer, not procedural orientation. Before new members take their seats:

- Document the board's current goals, governance calendar, and key commitments.
- Schedule a one-on-one with each incoming member — not to tell them how to vote, but to explain how the board governs.
- Give them the full year's worth of board packets and meeting minutes. That sounds like a lot, and it is. But new members who read the last twelve months of board materials arrive at their first meeting with context instead of confusion.

Preboarding isn't about controlling new members. It's about respecting them enough to give them the full picture.

**2. Assign a governance mentor.**

One of the most effective things I've seen is pairing each new member with a returning member for the first six months. Not a formal mentorship program with binders and checklists. A simple agreement: you have a question about how this board works, you call this person first.

This accomplishes two things. It gives the new member a trusted source of context — someone who can explain why the board does something without making them feel stupid for asking. And it gives the returning member a direct connection to the new member's perspective, which is often where fresh ideas come from.

**3. Protect the goals.**

Here's where most boards lose the plot. New members arrive with campaign priorities. Those priorities may be good ones. But the board's existing goals are a promise the board made to the community — not just to the people in the room last year.

Before any goal-setting conversation happens, the full board — new members included — needs to answer a simple question: *do the goals we adopted still reflect what this community needs?*

If the answer is yes — and for most boards it should be — you don't start over. You recommit. You say to the new members: "You're joining a board that has made a commitment to improve third-grade reading proficiency by X points. We need your energy, your ideas, and your voice to help us get there. But the destination is already set."

If the answer is genuinely no — because the community changed, or the data changed, or the district's situation changed — then you make a deliberate, board-wide decision to reset. You don't let it happen passively through the drift of new members finding their footing.

**What's at stake in your district**

The Chicago Board of Education just went through the largest governance transition in a generation — 21 elected members taking over from an appointed 7-member board. That's a historic shift from mayoral control to elected governance. In Los Angeles, new board members are being seated amid an $877M deficit and an FBI investigation that put the superintendent on paid leave. These are national-scale examples of the transition challenge.

But smaller districts face the same dynamic. Every post-election board — whether it's a rural district with 300 students or a suburban district with 30,000 — has to answer the same question: *will this transition strengthen our governance or weaken it?*

The research is clear: high-continuity boards have stronger goal alignment, more stable superintendent relationships, and better student outcome trajectories. The boards that let every election reset the agenda end up governing from crisis to crisis, never building the institutional momentum that real improvement requires.
