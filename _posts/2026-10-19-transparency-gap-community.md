**The Transparency Gap: What Your Community Sees vs. What It Needs to See**

I asked a board president in Michigan a simple question: "If I walked into a coffee shop in your district and asked someone to describe your board's top three priorities, what would they say?"

She paused for a long time. "I don't think they'd know," she said. "But that's not our fault. We publish our goals every year."

She was right that they published their goals. She was wrong that it wasn't her fault. The gap between what a board publishes and what the community actually sees is the board's responsibility to close.

I call this the Transparency Gap. It's the distance between the information a board makes available and the information a community can actually use. Most boards have a wider transparency gap than they realize.

Here are the five dimensions of board transparency. Rate yourself on each.

**Dimension One: Decision Transparency.**

Does the community know not just what you decided, but how you decided it?

Most boards score well on the first question and poorly on the second. The agenda says "Approval of textbook adoption." The board votes 7-0. The community knows a textbook was adopted. They don't know that the board rejected the primary staff recommendation because of cost concerns. They don't know that two board members nearly voted no because of concerns about the reading level. They don't know that the final decision was a compromise reached after a forty-minute discussion in closed session.

Decision transparency means the board packet includes the options considered and the reasoning behind the choice. It means the board minutes capture not just the vote but the substantive discussion.

**Dimension Two: Financial Transparency.**

Can a parent with a high school education understand your district budget?

Every board publishes a budget. Very few publish a budget that a non-accountant can understand. The difference between a compliant budget and a transparent budget is the difference between a 200-page PDF and a two-page summary that shows: where the money comes from, what it's spent on, and how this year compares to last year.

**Dimension Three: Policy Transparency.**

Can a parent find your policy on cell phones, homework, or attendance in under five minutes?

Most district policy manuals are thousands of pages long and searchable only by people who know the numbering system. A board I worked with in Illinois created a "parent-friendly policy index" — fifty common questions, each linked to the relevant policy, written in plain English, available on the district website's homepage. It cost nothing and took a staff member three days to build.

**Dimension Four: Performance Transparency.**

Does the community know whether the district is making progress?

Not test scores. Not graduation rates. Those are lagging indicators. Performance transparency means the community knows: Are we meeting our goals this year? Are our improvement strategies working? Where are we falling short?

One board I coached created a public dashboard showing progress on each of their five strategic priorities. Color-coded. Updated quarterly. Linked from the homepage. The board reviews it publicly at every meeting. The local newspaper started publishing the dashboard in its education section. That's performance transparency.

**Dimension Five: Governance Transparency.**

Does the community understand how the board itself operates?

How agendas are set. How decisions are made. How the public can influence outcomes. How the board evaluates itself. Most communities don't understand the difference between what the board does and what the administration does. And that confusion is the board's fault, not the community's.

**The Gap Assessment.**

Here's a simple test. Ask five people who don't work for the district — a parent, a local business owner, a senior citizen without kids in school, a high school student, and a local journalist — each of these five questions. Score 1-5 on how well they can answer.

- What did the board decide at the last meeting?
- How much money does the district have and what's it being spent on?
- Where can I find the policy on school safety?
- Is the district making progress on its goals?
- How does the board make decisions?

If your average score is below 3, you have a transparency gap. Most boards score between 1.5 and 2.5.

**Closing the Gap.**

Close one dimension at a time. Don't try to fix all five at once. Pick the dimension where the gap is widest — usually decision transparency or governance transparency — and commit to one improvement per month.
