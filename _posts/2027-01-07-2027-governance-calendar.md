**Your 2027 Governance Calendar: The Most Important Document You'll Approve This Month**

January 7, 2027

---

On Monday, I asked your board to make three strategic decisions in your organizational meeting: aligned officers, goal-connected committees, and a locked-in governance calendar.

That third one deserves its own conversation. Because the governance calendar is the single most underutilized tool in school board governance — and it's also the easiest one to fix.

Here's what most boards approve in January. A list of dates. "Board Meeting — Third Tuesday." "Work Session — First Thursday." "Committee Meetings — As Scheduled." That's it. The calendar tells you when the board will meet but not why. The assumption is that every meeting is just "the regular board meeting" and the agenda will fill itself.

Here's a different approach: a governance calendar that names, for every meeting, the governance outcome that meeting is designed to produce.

I've watched boards transform their effectiveness not by meeting more often, but by knowing what each meeting is for. The governance calendar is the tool that makes that explicit.

**The Five Fixed Milestones of a Governance Year**

A well-designed 2027 governance calendar has five fixed milestones. These are non-negotiable. Everything else fits around them.

**1. January — Goal adoption**

The board formally adopts its 2027 goals with measurable targets and a defined monitoring cadence. Not a draft. Not a work-in-progress. Adopted goals that bind the board and the superintendent.

The date matters. If goal adoption happens in January, the board has eleven months to monitor progress. If it happens in March — as it does in many districts — the board has nine. The difference doesn't sound like much until you realize that by March, budget decisions have already been made and the school year's trajectory is set.

This January-first rhythm isn't just ESB's framework. It's the universal starting point across the four major school board governance frameworks — NSBA's Key Work, CSBA's governance calendar template, and TASB's board planning cycle all point to January as the mandated first checkpoint of the governance year. Your framework says it. Your data says it. Your calendar should too.

**2. February — First data review**

The first progress monitoring check-in against the adopted goals. The superintendent presents baseline data. The board asks questions. The board gives direction if the data shows early warning signs.

This date is critical because it establishes the cadence. The board that reviews data in February is a board that reviews data every month. The board that postpones the first review to March is a board that will postpone again in April.

Council Bluffs Community School District in Iowa starts every year this way — January goal adoption, February data review, March board self-evaluation. The calendar is printed for every meeting. Board members bring their scorecards. They know what month they're in and what the month is supposed to produce. It's not complicated. It's just consistent.

**3. March — Budget alignment review**

The board reviews the preliminary budget for alignment with adopted goals. Every major expenditure mapped to a board-adopted goal. Any expenditure that doesn't connect to a goal is flagged for review.

March is when most boards see the budget for the first time. A governance calendar that includes a March budget alignment review ensures the board walks into that conversation with clear direction: "We told you our goals in January. Show us how this budget achieves them."

**4. April/May — Mid-year governance check-in**

The board reviews its own performance against the commitments it made in December and January. Remember the self-assessment from December? This is where the board checks whether it followed through.

This is also the point where the board course-corrects. If the data cadence isn't working, fix it. If the committee structure isn't producing results, adjust it. If the board-superintendent relationship needs attention, address it.

**5. June — Superintendent evaluation**

The board completes its annual evaluation of the superintendent, based on progress toward the goals adopted in January.

The brilliance of a January-to-June evaluation cycle is that the evaluation is never a surprise. The data that drives the evaluation is the same data the board has reviewed at every meeting since February. The evaluation is a summary of what the board already knows, not a single-meeting judgment based on impressions.

**What Every Regular Meeting Produces**

Between these five milestones, every regular board meeting needs a designated governance function.

A meeting calendar that just says "Regular Board Meeting — Third Tuesday" produces a reactive agenda. The superintendent fills it with reports. The board fills it with questions. Nobody knows what the meeting is supposed to produce, so it produces nothing but discussion.

A meeting calendar that says "Regular Board Meeting — Data Review and Goal Progress" produces a different meeting. The superintendent prepares data. The board reviews it. The meeting produces a decision: are we on track, and if not, what are we doing about it?

Assign every meeting a primary governance function:

| Meeting | Primary Function |
|---------|-----------------|
| January (Org) | Officers, committees, calendar lock-in |
| January (Regular) | Goal adoption |
| February | First data review, preliminary budget |
| March | Budget alignment, community progress report |
| April | Mid-year governance check-in |
| May | Superintendent evidence gathering, policy review |
| June | Superintendent evaluation, year-end review |
| August/September | Back-to-school data reset, 2027-28 goal preview |
| October-December | Progress monitoring, annual governance cycle |

Not every meeting has to produce a major decision. But every meeting should produce *a* decision — a data review outcome, a policy vote, a budget direction, a community communication.

**The Calendar as a Governance Accountability Tool**

Here's what I've learned from watching boards that use a governance calendar versus boards that don't.

The board with a governance calendar walks into January knowing what it will produce every month. The board president can look at the calendar and say "our priority this month is the mid-year check-in" instead of "what's on the agenda for this month?"

The board with a governance calendar doesn't let urgent items crowd out important ones. When a crisis arises — and it will — the board knows what strategic work it's deferring and can consciously reschedule it instead of silently abandoning it.

And the board with a governance calendar has an accountability conversation with itself at the end of the year. Did we hit our milestones? Did every meeting produce a governance outcome? If not, what needs to change in next year's calendar?

**Where Boards Are Doing This Right**

Wichita USD 259 in Kansas publishes its Annual Board Calendar with a Strategic Plan Dashboard. Every meeting has a named governance function and a data review component. The dashboard is public. The community can see what the board is tracking and when.

Hazelwood School District in Missouri — an ESB framework adopter — publishes board goals with quarterly data check-ins. Their calendar doesn't just say when they meet. It says which goal they're reviewing and what the current target is. It's one page, it's public, and it forces the board to prepare for every meeting with a clear purpose.

These districts aren't doing anything complicated. They've committed to a calendar that names the outcome of every meeting, and they've made that calendar public so the community can hold them accountable to it. That's it. That's the difference.

**Your January Assignment**

Your board's organizational meeting is this week or next. When you approve the 2027 meeting calendar, don't just approve a list of dates. Approve a governance document that names what each meeting is designed to produce.

Five milestones. Twelve meetings. One governance outcome per meeting.

Do that, and your board enters the year knowing not just when it will meet, but why.

The rest of the year takes care of itself.
