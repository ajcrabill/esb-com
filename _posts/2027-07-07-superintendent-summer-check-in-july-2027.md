**The Superintendent Summer Check-In — Why the First Post-Evaluation Conversation Matters More Than the Evaluation Itself**

July 7, 2027

---

The formal evaluation is complete. The board assessed the superintendent's performance. The superintendent responded. Growth areas were identified. Support commitments were made. The first check-in date was set for the fall.

That was three weeks ago. And already, the evaluation is starting to fade.

The board is entering summer mode. The superintendent is entering summer mode. The evaluation document is filed. The commitments are remembered, but they're not yet actioned. And the natural tendency — for both the board and the superintendent — is to assume the evaluation is done and the next check-in is in October.

That assumption is the most dangerous thing that can happen to the evaluation process.

Here's why: the formal evaluation is a framework-driven accountability event. It is structured, documented, and deliberate. But the period between the evaluation and the first check-in is unstructured. No meetings. No data reviews. No governance calendar deadlines. The evaluation commitments — the growth areas, the support commitments, the timeline — face their first real test not in October, but in the first week of July, when the board and superintendent each decide whether to keep the commitments at the front of their minds or let them slip to the back.

**Why the July check-in is different from the evaluation**

The July check-in is not an evaluation. It is not an assessment. It is not a performance conversation. It is a single-purpose conversation: alignment.

The purpose of the July check-in is to confirm that both the board and the superintendent are working from the same understanding of what the evaluation produced and what comes next. The conversation covers three questions:

**Are the growth areas still the right ones?** Three weeks after the evaluation, the superintendent has had time to reflect. New information may have emerged. The board may have had conversations that changed its thinking. The July check-in is the time to adjust — not to change the evaluation, but to confirm that the growth areas are still accurate and still the right focus for the fall.

**Has any new information emerged that changes the priorities?** The superintendent may have received new data. A state policy change may affect district operations. A community concern may have surfaced. The July check-in is the opportunity to identify anything that has changed since the evaluation and decide whether it affects the fall plan.

**Is the board delivering on its support commitments?** The board committed to specific support actions during the evaluation. Those commitments are as important as the superintendent's growth areas. The board needs to assess its own performance on those commitments before it asks the superintendent to assess his or her performance on the growth areas.

**The check-in structure**

The superintendent check-in should be a 45-minute conversation. It should be held by the board president and the superintendent — not the full board. The full board can review the outcomes at the next board work session or August board meeting.

The agenda:

**First 10 minutes — Growth area confirmation.** The board president reviews the growth areas from the June evaluation. For each area, the superintendent confirms whether the area is still the right focus, whether progress has begun, and whether any adjustment is needed.

**Next 15 minutes — Emerging priorities.** The board president asks the superintendent: has anything changed since the evaluation that affects the growth areas, the support commitments, or the fall plan? The superintendent responds. The board president listens and takes notes.

**Next 15 minutes — Board support delivery check.** The board president reviews the board's support commitments from the evaluation. For each commitment, the board president reports on the board's progress. If the board is behind, the board president says so and commits to a specific timeline for catching up.

**Last 5 minutes — Documentation and next steps.** The board president and superintendent agree on any adjustments to the growth areas or timeline. The board president confirms the fall check-in date. Both receive a written summary of the conversation within 48 hours.

**What the check-in produces**

The July check-in produces three things: confirmation that the board and superintendent are aligned on the evaluation outcomes, any adjustments that new information requires, and a clear line of sight from the July check-in to the fall check-in.

A board and superintendent that have a productive July check-in enter the fall with the trust that the evaluation was real — that the evaluation document was not the endpoint but the starting point. A board and superintendent that skip the July check-in enter the fall with the risk that the evaluation has already started to fade, that the growth areas are already half-forgotten, and that the October check-in will require the board and superintendent to reconstruct a conversation that should have been a continuation, not a restart.

The formal evaluation is the event that produces the framework. The July check-in is the conversation that makes the framework real.
