**The Budget Crosswalk — Year Three Precision**

February 1, 2029

---

Your board adopted goals two weeks ago.

The goal adoption meeting produced a written decision for each goal the board committed to. Three years of trend data informed the targets. Every board member publicly affirmed them. The superintendent has the adopted goals in hand.

Now February arrives with a different question.

Does every dollar in the proposed budget connect to those goals?

That is the budget crosswalk. And in Year Three, it is a precision instrument.

---

### What Changes in Year Three

In Year One, the budget crosswalk was a learning experience. The board connected dollars to goals for the first time. The crosswalk document was a new format. The board president facilitated a discussion that the board had never had before. The board produced a written budget direction, and the superintendent took that direction into budget development. It was a first draft. And first drafts are valuable because they create the baseline.

In Year Two, the budget crosswalk was a refinement experience. The board had one year of expenditure data connected to goals. The board could ask whether last year's allocation produced the expected outcome — but the answer was based on a single cycle of data. The board produced a more precise budget direction. The crosswalk document was cleaner. The discussion was faster.

In Year Three, the budget crosswalk is an accountability experience. The board has two full years of expenditure data connected to goal outcomes. The board can ask not just whether the allocation was spent but whether the spending produced the intended result. The board can compare Year One's allocation effectiveness against Year Two's. The board can see patterns — which goal areas consistently produce improvement when invested in, and which goal areas need a different resource strategy.

---

### The Year-Over-Year Expenditure Effectiveness Analysis

Here is the new work that Year Three makes possible.

The superintendent prepares a document that shows, for every major goal-aligned allocation from the previous two budget cycles:

- The goal the allocation was intended to advance
- The amount allocated in Year One (2027-28)
- The amount allocated in Year Two (2028-29)
- The amount proposed for Year Three (2029-30)
- The student outcome data at the time of allocation
- The student outcome data at the end of the cycle
- The change — in percentage points or raw scores
- The board's assessment: Did the allocation produce the expected outcome?

The board reviews every allocation across this framework and makes a judgment. Not a budget analyst's judgment. A governance judgment. The board is not managing the budget. The board is deciding whether the district's resource allocation strategy is producing results for students.

Here is what that judgment looks like in practice.

**Allocations that produced improvement.** A goal-aligned program received a specific allocation in Year One and Year Two. The student outcome data improved over those two years. The Year Three question is: Should the board allocate at the same level, increase the allocation to accelerate improvement, or maintain the allocation and expect continued improvement? The answer depends on whether the improvement rate is sufficient to meet the adopted target.

**Allocations that did not produce improvement.** A goal-aligned program received a specific allocation in Year One and Year Two. The student outcome data stayed flat or declined. The Year Three question is not: Should we allocate more? The Year Three question is: Did the allocation go to the right strategy? If a program is funded at the same level for two years and student outcomes do not improve, the board should not continue the allocation without a strategy change.

**Allocations with incomplete data.** Some allocations may not have two full years of outcome data attached to them — new programs, pilot initiatives, or allocations that were made mid-cycle. The board flags these for continued monitoring and asks: Does the superintendent expect the investment to produce outcome data by what date? The board schedules a follow-up data check.

This is not a compliance exercise. It is the board's highest-leverage governance action of the spring cycle. A board that connects every dollar to a goal, and then evaluates whether those dollars produced the intended result, is a board that treats the budget as a governance instrument — not a financial document.

---

### The Three-Question Crosswalk Review

The budget crosswalk study session is a dedicated meeting. Not a regular meeting agenda item. Not a committee report buried in the consent agenda. A two-hour board study session with one agenda item: the budget crosswalk.

The board president facilitates the session using three questions. Every allocation is reviewed against these three questions.

**Question one: Does this allocation advance an adopted goal?**

This is the binary threshold question. The board reviews each major allocation and confirms whether it is connected to a goal. Allocations that are not connected to a goal receive additional scrutiny. Some allocations — facilities maintenance, debt service, administrative operations — are necessary for district operations but may not directly advance a student outcome goal. The board acknowledges those allocations as infrastructure costs. The board ensures infrastructure costs do not consume the majority of new funding.

**Question two: Has this allocation historically produced the expected outcome?**

This is the Year Three question. The superintendent presents the expenditure effectiveness data for every allocation that has been in place for at least two years. The board reviews the trend. Allocations that consistently produce improvement are flagged for continued or increased investment. Allocations that have not produced improvement are flagged for strategy review or reallocation.

**Question three: Does the proposed allocation for the coming year match the priority the board set in the adopted goals?**

This is the alignment question. The board adopted goals with specific targets and trajectory decisions. The proposed budget should reflect those priorities. If the board adopted an accelerate decision for early literacy and the proposed literacy allocation is flat, the board has a question. If the board adopted a maintain decision for math proficiency and the proposed math allocation is increasing significantly, the board has a different question. The alignment is not about equal allocation across goals. It is about proportional allocation consistent with the board's adopted priority.

The board president facilitates the discussion so that every allocation receives all three questions. The board produces a written answer for each allocation: connected to goal, historically effective, and aligned with priority — or not. Allocations that fail any of the three questions are flagged for the superintendent to address in the revised budget.

### The Written Budget Direction

The crosswalk study session produces one output: a written budget direction to the superintendent.

The direction is not a vague statement of support. It is a specific, measurable governance directive. Here is the format.

**The adoption statement:** *The board has reviewed the preliminary budget crosswalk and confirms that the proposed budget allocates X percent to Goal 1, Y percent to Goal 2, Z percent to Goal 3, and maintains the fiscal guardrail at W percent.*

**The adjustments:** *The board directs the superintendent to: (1) increase the allocation to Goal 1 early literacy by A percent to support the board's accelerate trajectory decision, (2) maintain the Goal 2 math proficiency allocation at the current level pending the May data review, (3) redirect B dollars from the Goal 3 program that has not produced improvement in two years to a redesigned strategy for the superintendent to present at the March public hearing preparation session.*

**The fiscal guardrail confirmation:** *The board confirms that the fiscal guardrail (maximum fund balance draw / minimum reserve / debt service cap) is intact at C percent.*

**The timeline:** *The superintendent will present the revised budget incorporating this direction at the March public hearing preparation session.*

The board adopts the direction by vote at the conclusion of the study session or at the next regular board meeting. The direction becomes the board's official governance position on the budget. The superintendent takes the direction into the budget development process with a clear mandate.

---

### Three Things That Change in Year Three Crosswalk

Here is what I have watched change in boards that reach Year Three with intact crosswalk practice.

**The board asks different questions.** In Year One, the board asked: *What is a crosswalk? Why are we doing this?* In Year Two, the board asked: *Is the format right? Are we looking at the right categories?* In Year Three, the board asks: *Is this allocation producing the outcome it was designed to produce? If not, what evidence supports continuing it?* The questions shift from process to effectiveness. That is precisely where they belong.

**The superintendent's crosswalk document is tighter.** In Year One, the superintendent produced a crosswalk document that may have been the first the district had ever produced. The board received it, reviewed it, and learned together. In Year Three, the superintendent's crosswalk document includes the year-over-year expenditure effectiveness analysis by default. It is not an additional request. It is the standard format. The superintendent knows the board expects it because the board has expected it for three cycles.

**The study session is faster and produces a sharper direction.** In Year One, the crosswalk study session ran two hours and the board produced a direction that contained general priorities. In Year Three, the same session runs ninety minutes and the board produces a direction that names specific adjustments, identifies the allocations that need redesign, and sets a timeline. The board has done this before. The efficiency gain is real — and it produces a better output.

---

### What the Board Does After the Crosswalk

The budget crosswalk study session produces the board's budget direction. Here is what happens next.

**The superintendent revises the budget.** The direction the board issued names specific adjustments. The superintendent takes those adjustments into the budget development process. The revised budget reflects the board's direction.

**The public hearing preparation begins.** March's public hearing is where the board presents the budget to the community. The board needs the superintendent's revised budget at least two weeks before the public hearing to prepare the presentation materials. The board president ensures the timeline is clear.

**The Q1 Governance Health Check is on the calendar.** March includes two governance actions: the Q1 Health Check and public hearing preparation. The board president confirms both are scheduled before the February study session ends.

The budget crosswalk is not a once-and-done exercise. It is the board's first budget decision of the spring cycle. The April budget adoption is the conclusion. Everything between February and April — the public hearing, the community engagement, the revised budget — is execution of the direction the board set in the crosswalk study session.

A board that produces a precise budget direction in February enters April with confidence. A board that produces a vague budget direction in February enters April hoping the details work out.

Year Three boards do not hope. They direct.
