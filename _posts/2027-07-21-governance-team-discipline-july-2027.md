**Strengthening Governance Team Discipline — What the Board Works On When No One Is Watching**

July 21, 2027

---

The board operates differently in a public meeting than it does in the months between meetings. The public meeting has an audience. The public meeting has an agenda. The public meeting has parliamentary procedure. The public meeting creates public accountability for board member behavior.

The months between meetings — the conversations on email, the phone calls with the superintendent, the hallway conversations with other board members, the social media posts, the community appearances — have none of those structures. And it is in those unstructured months that the board's true governance discipline is revealed.

Summer is the longest stretch of unstructured time in the governance calendar. From the last June board meeting to the first September board meeting, the board has minimal formal interaction. There are no weekly board meetings. No data reviews. No public hearings. No regular staff reports. The discipline the board practices during this period — the discipline the board practices when no one is watching — determines whether the board enters September stronger or weaker.

**The four disciplines of summer governance team behavior**

**Communication discipline.** The most common governance team breakdown I see is communication breakdown — a board member contacts the superintendent directly with an operational question, a board member makes a public statement that contradicts board consensus, a board member sends an email to other board members that creates confusion about the board's position.

The summer test: does each board member use the professional channel for communication with the superintendent? Does each board member check with the board president before making a public statement about board business? Does each board member communicate with other board members in ways that build alignment rather than create confusion?

The standard should be clear: all board-superintendent communication goes through the board president or the designated board-superintendent liaison. All public statements about board business are cleared with the board president. All board-to-board communication about governance matters is shared with the full board, not conducted in subgroups.

**Meeting preparation discipline.** The board's meeting preparation habits are formed during the summer — not during the governance year, when meetings are frequent enough to force discipline. The board member who lets meeting preparation slide during the summer will enter September with a habit of showing up unprepared for the first data review, the first budget conversation, the first community engagement event.

The summer test: does each board member complete the summer reading and training they committed to? Does each board member arrive at summer work sessions on time and prepared? Does each board member meet the deadlines the board president sets for feedback and input?

**Engagement discipline.** Summer is the period when board members have the most discretionary time. It is also the period when board member engagement drops most dramatically — board members check out, check out of communication loops, and check back in when school starts.

The summer test: does each board member respond to board president communications within a reasonable timeframe (24-48 hours for non-urgent matters)? Does each board member attend scheduled summer work sessions or communicate conflicts in advance? Does each board member complete their assigned summer work items by the agreed deadlines?

**Governance team cohesion discipline.** Summer is when governance team relationships are built or eroded. The board that checks out from each other during the summer enters September as eight individuals who happen to share a board room. The board that maintains connection — through a structured check-in, a shared project, or a professional development experience together — enters September as a governance team.

The summer test: does the board president check in with each board member at least once during the summer? Does the board have a shared summer project — the community progress report, the policy audit, the fall goal-setting preparation — that requires collaboration? Does the board have a forum — a work session, a conference call, a shared document — for discussing the summer reading and training?

**The governance team discipline self-check**

Every board member should answer these seven questions honestly — not as a self-assessment to be shared, but as a personal check on their own discipline:

1. Have I contacted the superintendent directly this summer about a governance matter, or has all communication gone through the proper channel?
2. Have I completed the reading and training I committed to in the summer work plan?
3. Have I responded to board president communications within 24 hours?
4. Have I made any public statements about board business without clearing them with the board president?
5. Have I maintained professional relationships with every member of the board, including those I disagree with?
6. Have I reviewed the fall governance calendar and the summer work plan in the past seven days?
7. Am I entering the fall governance year more prepared than I entered the spring governance year?

A board member who answers yes to all seven questions is governing with discipline. A board member who answers no to even one has a gap to close before September.

**What the board president does with the discipline check**

The board president should not ask board members to submit their discipline self-check responses. The self-check is a personal instrument. But the board president should hold a brief conversation with each board member in late July or early August — a ten-minute check-in that covers: how is your summer going, have you been able to complete your reading and training, are there any concerns about the board's communication or functioning, and is there anything I can do to support you as we prepare for the fall.

The board president who holds those conversations enters September knowing the state of the board's discipline. The board president who doesn't hold those conversations enters September hoping the board members are prepared — and hope is not a governance strategy.
