**Q1 Governance Health Check — Year Three Self-Assessment**

March 1, 2029

---

The Q1 Governance Health Check is the board's earliest opportunity to catch a drift before it becomes a gap.

A drift is small. A drift is a meeting that runs five minutes over because one agenda item took longer than planned. A drift is a board member who did not receive the data packet on time. A drift is a written budget direction that was transmitted three days late instead of two.

A gap is the consequence of allowing drifts to accumulate. A gap is a community progress report that was supposed to publish in May but did not go out until June. A gap is a superintendent evaluation that felt rushed because the board ran out of time.

The health check exists to catch drifts. In Year One, it was a learning tool. Your board was figuring out what to assess and how. In Year Two, it was a discipline tool. Your board knew what to assess and made sure the assessment happened on time.

In Year Three, it is a precision tool. Your board assesses not just whether you did the work, but whether you did it well — and whether you did it better than last year.

**The Year Three Self-Assessment Framework**

The health check assesses five areas, and Year Three adds a critical dimension: year-over-year comparison.

**Area One: Goal adoption.** Did the January process go smoothly? Did every board member publicly affirm the adopted goals? Was the three-year trend data used effectively? And here is the Year Three question: compared to last year's goal adoption, was this year's process more precise?

**Area Two: Budget crosswalk.** Did the board complete the crosswalk review by end of February? Did the board issue a written budget direction within two business days? Did the direction include effectiveness review triggers? And the Year Three question: did the crosswalk discussion go deeper than last year's?

**Area Three: Progress monitoring.** Is the spring data review scheduled for May? Is the data packet protocol established? The Year Three question: has the board confirmed the data review date and data packet format earlier than in prior years?

**Area Four: Board-superintendent partnership.** Is the communication protocol functioning for the spring cycle? Are there unresolved issues from the crosswalk that need attention? The Year Three question: has the partnership improved since this time last year?

**Area Five: Community connection.** When is the spring community progress report scheduled? Is the year-over-year comparison data prepared? The Year Three question: does the community engagement plan show evidence of getting better?

**The One-Page Self-Assessment**

The assessment itself takes thirty minutes. The board president leads it using a one-page document.

For each of the five areas, the board produces a rating:

— On Track: the area is functioning as designed. The protocol was followed. The outcome met expectations.

— Needs Attention: the area is functioning but has a specific gap that needs to be addressed before Q2.

— At Risk: the area is not functioning as designed. A significant gap exists that requires immediate board action.

The board produces three written outputs from the health check:

1. Strengths to protect — what went well that the board should keep doing
2. Gaps to address — what needs attention and who is responsible for addressing it
3. Timing adjustments for Q2 — what changes to the calendar or protocol are needed

**What Year Three Adds to the Health Check**

The most significant addition in Year Three is the year-over-year comparison.

Your board should review its prior health check outputs. Look at the Q1 health check from last year. How did the board rate in each area? Did the ratings improve? Did you address the gaps you identified? Did you protect the strengths you named?

If the board rated itself lower on goal adoption this year than last year, that is not a failure — it is data. It tells you that the higher bar of Year Three precision revealed a gap that the Year Two assessment did not catch. The response is not to lower the bar. The response is to identify the gap and address it.

If the board rated itself higher on budget crosswalk this year, name why. Did the enhanced crosswalk document produce a better discussion? Did the board president facilitate more effectively? What can you replicate in next year's process?

**Setting Improvement Targets**

Year Three also adds specific improvement targets for Q2.

Instead of a general aspiration like "improve community communication," the board sets a specific target: "By the end of March, the board president will confirm the spring data review date with the superintendent and ensure the data packet template is distributed to all board members. The Q2 health check will assess whether this target was met."

Specific targets produce specific accountability. General aspirations produce good intentions.

**Common Pitfall in Year Three**

The most common mistake is going through the motions — checking the boxes without checking the quality.

A board that has done the health check three times can fall into the trap of completing the assessment without asking the harder questions. The protocol becomes routine. The ratings become predictable. The outputs become boilerplate.

The health check should produce a specific action item for the board president. Not "improve board-superintendent communication" — but "by the March board meeting, the president will confirm the spring data review date with the superintendent and ensure the data packet template is distributed to all board members."

If your board finishes the health check without a written list of adjustments for Q2, the check was a conversation, not a governance action.

**The Value of the Check**

A governance health check is only as valuable as the actions it produces.

The purpose is not to fill out a form. The purpose is to identify the one or two adjustments that will make the second quarter more precise than the first.

Your board has done this work for three cycles. You know what good looks like. The health check is your opportunity to name where you are falling short of that standard — and to decide what to do about it.
