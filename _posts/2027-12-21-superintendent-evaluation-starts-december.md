**The Superintendent Evaluation Starts in December — Not April — Here Is the 45-Day Rule**

December 21, 2027

---

I have watched boards spend more time arguing about the superintendent evaluation format than actually evaluating the superintendent.

The board says the evaluation is due in June. In April, the board president realizes no one has started gathering evidence. In May, the board scrambles to collect artifacts, asks the superintendent for a "self-reflection," and produces a document that tells the superintendent what the board already said in hallway conversations but never put on paper.

The superintendent receives the evaluation. The superintendent reviews it. The superintendent finds factual inaccuracies — because the board rushed the evidence-gathering and got the dates wrong, or attributed contract issues to performance, or included an event that was actually a school board decision, not a superintendent decision.

The board argues about whether the inaccuracies matter. The superintendent loses confidence in the process. The board loses credibility with the superintendent.

Everyone loses.

Here is the fix. Start the evaluation process in December, not April. Give yourself four months of evidence-gathering. Use the 45-day rule to eliminate factual accuracy disputes. Structure the evaluation committee so the board is represented, the superintendent is informed, and the process is transparent.

Here is exactly how that works.

---

**The evaluation committee: Who and what.**

The ESB framework recommends a board evaluation committee of three to five board members, appointed by the board president. The committee is responsible for the evaluation process, not the evaluation outcome. The full board votes on the final evaluation. The committee is the working group that makes the process happen.

Here is what the committee does.

December through January: The committee determines the evidence-gathering process. What data sources will the board use? What artifacts will the board review? What stakeholder input, if any, will the board gather?

The evidence sources should be named explicitly — and they should be grounded in the board's adopted goals and the superintendent's contract.

A meaningful superintendent evaluation is not a popularity contest. It is not a collection of anonymous staff surveys that measure whether the superintendent is liked. It is an assessment of whether the superintendent is making progress toward the goals the board adopted, and whether the superintendent is meeting the performance expectations established in the contract.

The evidence sources I recommend:

- Goal progress data. Did the district make measurable progress toward each board-adopted goal during the evaluation period?
- Board-adopted superintendent performance indicators. If the board and superintendent agreed on specific performance indicators at the start of the evaluation period, those indicators are the primary evidence source.
- Board observation notes. Individual board members observe the superintendent in action — board meetings, community events, executive sessions — and document what they observe using a structured format, not free-form impressions.
- Superintendent self-reflection. The superintendent provides a written self-assessment of performance against the agreed goals and indicators. This is a starting point for the board's discussion, not the evaluation itself.
- Contract compliance check. Did the superintendent meet the contractual obligations — reporting deadlines, board communications, professional development requirements?

I have watched districts like **Grand Prairie (TX)** use this evidence structure effectively. Their evaluation committee publishes the evidence framework in December — naming every data source and timeline before a single piece of evidence is collected. The superintendent knows in December what the board will evaluate. No surprises in May.

---

**The evidence-gathering window: December through March.**

Here is the mistake most boards make. They treat the evidence-gathering process as something that happens in the two weeks before the evaluation is due.

That produces a thin evaluation. The board finds what is easy to find, not what is important to find.

A four-month evidence-gathering window — December through March — produces a complete evaluation. The board has time to observe the superintendent across multiple board meetings, community events, and operational cycles. The board has time to gather goal progress data from the fall and winter assessment windows. The board has time to review the superintendent's performance across the full scope of the contract, not just the most recent events.

Here is how the four-month window breaks down.

December and January: The evaluation committee determines the evidence sources and communicates the framework to the superintendent. The committee also begins collecting board member observation notes — using a standard format that asks for specific, observable evidence tied to the adopted goals and performance indicators.

February: The committee gathers the goal progress data from the winter assessment window and the superintendent's self-reflection. The committee also gathers any stakeholder input the board has decided to include.

March: The committee drafts the evaluation based on the evidence collected. The draft is presented to the full board for a preliminary review. The board discusses the draft and provides input. The committee revises.

At the end of March, the committee has a draft evaluation ready for the next step — the 45-day factual accuracy check.

---

**The 45-day rule: Why it matters.**

Here is the rule that separates evaluations that work from evaluations that fail.

The board must provide the superintendent with the draft evaluation at least forty-five days before the final evaluation is due.

Forty-five days is not arbitrary. It is the minimum time required for the superintendent to review the draft, identify factual inaccuracies, and provide corrections — without rushing, without stress, and without feeling that the evaluation is being imposed without a chance for the superintendent to respond.

Here is how the 45-day process works.

Day 45: The evaluation committee delivers the draft evaluation to the superintendent. The superintendent has a defined window — typically fourteen to twenty-one days — to review the draft and submit factual corrections.

The superintendent is not asking for a different rating or a better outcome. The superintendent is correcting factual errors. "The board meeting referenced on page 3 was January 12, not February 9." "The student achievement data cited for the third-grade reading goal reflects the spring 2027 assessment, not the fall 2027 assessment." "The superintendent did not make that staffing decision — the board made it during the April executive session."

Factual corrections. Not opinion corrections.

Day 30: The evaluation committee reviews the superintendent's factual corrections and incorporates them into the draft. The committee does not debate whether the corrections matter. If the superintendent identifies a factual error, the committee fixes it. Accuracy is not negotiable.

Day 15: The revised draft is presented to the full board. The board has two weeks to review the evaluation before the final vote.

Day 0: The board conducts the formal evaluation conversation with the superintendent — a public meeting or executive session, depending on state law — and votes on the final evaluation.

The entire process, from draft delivery to final vote, takes forty-five days.

I have seen the alternative. A board that delivers the draft evaluation to the superintendent five days before the final vote. The superintendent finds ten factual inaccuracies. The board says "we do not have time to fix them." The superintendent objects. The board defends the evaluation. The relationship is damaged for the next cycle.

The 45-day rule prevents that. It is the difference between an evaluation process the superintendent trusts and one the superintendent resists.

---

**What December looks like in practice.**

Here is what the board does this month to start the cycle.

First, the board president appoints the evaluation committee at the December board meeting. The committee should include the board president and two to four board members who commit to the four-month process.

Second, the board president communicates the evaluation timeline to the superintendent — the evidence-gathering process, the committee structure, the 45-day schedule, and the final evaluation date in June. The superintendent should receive this in writing before the end of December.

Third, the committee holds its first meeting in December to determine the evidence sources and the observation note format. The committee publishes the evidence framework to the full board and the superintendent.

That is it. Three actions this month. Every action increases the probability of a meaningful evaluation in June.

The board that appoints the committee in December will produce an evaluation in June that the superintendent respects. The board that appoints the committee in May will produce an evaluation that the superintendent resists.

The choice is clear.

---

**The forward connection: January through June 2028.**

Starting the evaluation process in December sets up the January-through-June 2028 evaluation arc that your board will execute next year.

The January strategic organizational meeting will include the evaluation committee report — what the committee has done since December, what evidence has been collected, and what the remaining timeline looks like.

The February and March board meetings will include the evidence-gathering milestones. The community will see that the board takes the superintendent evaluation seriously — and that the process is transparent, structured, and grounded in evidence, not private conversations.

The April mid-year check-in will include a progress report from the evaluation committee.

The May and June board meetings will complete the cycle — the 45-day check, the board review, the formal evaluation conversation, and the final vote.

The board that starts in December governs the evaluation process. The board that starts in April is governed by it.
