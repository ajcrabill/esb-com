**The Board's End-of-Year Governance Check-Up: What to Review Before December**

I sat in a boardroom last December watching a school board try to set goals for the coming year without once looking back at the year behind them.

They spent three hours debating aspirational language — "world-class," "rigorous," "excellence for all" — without stopping to ask the hard question: *Did the goals we set last year actually move the needle for students?*

Nobody wanted to go there. It felt like grading themselves. And honestly, it kind of is.

But here's what I've learned about boards that actually improve outcomes for students: they build in a structured check-up every year before the December rush begins. They don't wait for the superintendent evaluation cycle or the annual report deadline. They do it *before* — when there's still time to course-correct, recommit, and set up January for success.

If your board hasn't done its end-of-year governance check-up yet, November's the time. Here are the four questions I'd start with.

## 1. Did We Make Real Progress on Student Outcome Goals?

This is the only question that actually matters. Everything else is supporting cast.

Pull out the goals you set at the beginning of this year — or the middle of last year if your calendar follows the academic cycle. Not the vision statement in the district strategic plan. I mean the specific, measurable student outcome goals your board adopted. The ones with a baseline and a target.

Now ask yourselves honestly:

- Which goals are on track?
- Which ones are stalled?
- Which ones did we stop monitoring altogether?

In my experience, most boards discover at least one goal that quietly slipped off the agenda somewhere around March. It's not malicious. It's just that the urgent crowded out the important — a mid-year budget revision, a personnel issue, a parent complaint that turned into a four-hour meeting. And suddenly the dashboard you promised to review quarterly hasn't been opened since spring.

The point of this question isn't shame. It's *information*. If you don't know which goals are slipping, you can't fix them. And if you wait until January to figure it out, you've already lost the window for meaningful conversation about the budget and priorities for next year.

## 2. Is the Budget Actually Aligned With Our Priorities?

Here's where things get uncomfortable.

Your board probably adopted a budget last spring that claimed to support your student outcome goals. Great. But nine months later, the question isn't what you *said* you'd fund. It's what you actually funded.

I've seen districts where the board's top goal was third-grade reading proficiency, but when you actually trace the dollars, the biggest year-over-year increase went to facilities maintenance. I've seen boards proclaim equity as a core value while cutting the program that served their highest-need students.

This doesn't mean your board is dishonest. It means most boards never actually *track* budget alignment after adoption. The budget comes back as a quarterly report nobody reads, and by the time December rolls around, nobody can remember what they agreed to fund.

So pull out the budget. Compare actual spending to planned spending. Ask your CFO to show you where the money went — not in aggregate, but by program and category. If your third-grade literacy goal got a 3% increase but your custodial overtime line item grew by 12%, you've got some real conversation to have before you start next year's budget cycle.

## 3. How Is the Governance Team Actually Functioning?

This one takes more courage to ask.

Because it's not about data. It's about relationships, norms, and the patterns that are hard to see when you're inside them.

Give each board member thirty seconds (yes, thirty seconds — don't let this turn into a therapy session) to answer one question: *On a scale of 1 to 10, how effectively did this board function as a governance team this year?*

Then have someone tally it. Don't discuss it yet. Just get the range.

If you see a spread of more than three points — say, three board members say 8 or 9 while two say 4 or 5 — you've got a perception gap worth unpacking. If the scores are uniformly mediocre, you already know what your work is.

The ESB framework calls this *governance team function*, and it's the single most under-assessed dimension of board work. Boards measure student outcomes (good). They measure budget performance (smart). But they almost never turn the lens on themselves — how they communicate, how they resolve disagreement, how they use their meeting time, whether the superintendent partnership is working.

December is the time to schedule that conversation. Not in a public meeting. In a work session where the only agenda item is your own effectiveness.

## 4. Did We Earn the Trust We Were Given?

This is the November question — the one that ties back to everything October's Community Engagement & Trust arc was about.

Your community gave this board a mandate. Voters went to the polls, or parents chose your district, or taxpayers said "we trust you with this budget." The question is: did you earn that trust back?

Not through press releases. Through *evidence*.

- When you promised transparency, did you publish the data?
- When you said you'd listen, did you build a mechanism for community voice?
- When a crisis happened — and it always does — did the community walk away feeling heard, or feeling managed?

I don't know your district. But I know that the boards I've seen lose community trust didn't lose it in one dramatic moment. They lost it gradually, meeting by meeting, by skipping this question year after year.

You can start earning it back by asking it now.

## Your Check-Up Kit

Here's what I'd recommend: take these four questions to your next board work session. Give each one fifteen minutes — an hour total. No debate, no decisions. Just honest answers on paper.

Then look at the pattern. Are you strong in budget but weak in governance team function? Strong on trust but not tracking whether goals are being met? The patterns tell you what to prioritize in December's governance reset.
