**The Board's End-of-Year Governance Check-Up: What to Review Before December**

November 23, 2026

Here's a question I ask every board I work with in November:

If your board's calendar year ended today, what would you say you accomplished?

Most boards can't answer that question without checking their minutes. And that's a problem — not because the minutes aren't accurate, but because if the answer requires research, the board hasn't internalized its own progress.

November is the month for the governance equivalent of an annual physical. Not a crisis response. Not a compliance exercise. A deliberate, structured review of how the board performed across the domains that actually matter for student outcomes.

Let me walk you through the check-up.

**The Four Questions Every Board Should Answer Before December**

I've organized this around four governance checkpoints spanning the ESB framework — Goal Clarity & Progress, Budget Alignment, Governance Team, and Community Trust. Each checkpoint has one question and one evidence test.

*Question One: Did We Make Progress on Our Student Outcome Goals?*

This is the one that matters most, so let's start here.

Pull up the goals your board adopted — whether that was in January, during strategic planning, or at the start of the school year. For each goal, answer three things:

- What was the baseline?
- What is the current data point?
- Are we on track, off track, or too early to tell?

If you can't answer all three for each goal, that's not a data problem. That's a governance problem. The board adopted goals without building a system to monitor them.

I work with a board in the Midwest that discovered during their year-end review that they had adopted five ambitious goals in January but never once asked the superintendent for a progress report on any of them. Not once. By November, they had no idea whether students were better off than they were eleven months earlier.

That board wasn't bad. They were busy. But busy without monitoring is just activity. And activity doesn't move student outcomes.

**The evidence test for Question One:** Can you produce, in under five minutes, a one-page summary showing current progress against each goal? If not, this is your priority for December.

*Question Two: Did Our Budget Align With Our Priorities?*

This is where good intentions meet reality.

Most boards adopt a budget that they believe reflects their priorities. But the year-end check-up asks a harder question: did the actual spending match the stated priorities?

I've seen boards that adopted "early literacy" as their number one priority but spent 80% of their discretionary budget on facilities. I've seen boards that said "teacher retention" was critical but allocated zero additional funding for compensation or support.

The budget doesn't lie. Look at where the money actually went — not where you said it would go. If there's a gap between your priorities and your spending, that's not necessarily a mistake. But it's a decision you should make consciously, not discover accidentally.

**The evidence test for Question Two:** Can you map your top three board priorities to specific line items in the current year's budget? If not, your budget and your priorities are living separate lives.

*Question Three: Did We Function as a Governance Team?*

This is the question boards are most likely to skip — because it requires honesty about how you work together, and that can be uncomfortable.

But here's what I've learned from working with hundreds of boards: a board that can't function as a team can't govern effectively, no matter how good their goals are.

Ask yourselves:

- Did we have at least one board retreat or governance work session this year?
- Did we complete a board self-assessment — and actually discuss the results?
- Did we address conflicts directly, or did we avoid them?
- Did new members get onboarding that prepared them to govern, not just to comply with open meetings requirements?

The boards that function best as teams share one characteristic: they invest in their own development. They treat governance skills as learnable, not innate. They don't assume that being elected means being ready.

**The evidence test for Question Three:** Can every board member name the board's three operating norms? If not, your norms exist on paper but not in practice.

*Question Four: Did We Earn and Keep Community Trust?*

This is the question that connects November's arc back to October's Community Engagement & Trust series.

Trust isn't built in a board meeting. It's built in the consistent, day-after-day demonstration that the board is acting in the community's interest, not its own.

Ask yourselves:

- Did the public show up to board meetings this year? If not, why?
- Did the board make decisions that were explainable — not just defensible?
- Did the board communicate its rationale publicly and proactively?
- When trust was broken, did the board acknowledge it and take steps to rebuild?

One board I coached realized during their year-end review that they had not held a single community forum all year. They had approved a new school boundaries map — one of the most contentious decisions a board can make — without ever presenting it to the public before the vote. The community was furious, and rightfully so.

The board hadn't intended to be secretive. They had just been busy — and busy had become a substitute for engagement.

**The evidence test for Question Four:** Can you point to three specific ways the community had a voice in your decisions this year? If not, you've been governing in a vacuum.

**Putting It Together: The Year-End Governance Scorecard**

Here's a simple tool you can use with your board this month. Score each domain:

| Domain | Score (1-5) | Evidence | Priority for December |
|--------|-------------|----------|----------------------|
| Goal Clarity & Progress | | | |
| Budget Alignment | | | |
| Governance Team | | | |
| Community Trust | | | |

A score of 4 or 5 means you're in good shape. A score of 3 means there's work to do. A score of 1 or 2 means this needs to be your board's top priority before January.

Don't try to fix everything at once. Pick the one domain where the gap between your current state and where you want to be is largest — and build your December board agenda around closing that gap.

**What Your Board Should Do This Week**

1. Schedule a 90-minute year-end governance review session (board only, no superintendent — this is the board's self-assessment)
2. Each board member completes the four-question check-up individually before the session
3. In the session, share scores, discuss evidence, and agree on priorities for December
4. Document the results — this becomes the baseline for next year's review

The boards that finish the year strong aren't the ones that had the best year. They're the ones that stopped to ask what they'd actually accomplished — and had the courage to answer honestly.
