**Your Board's Communication Strategy Isn't a Newsletter. It's a Governance Act.**

January 25, 2027

---

Most boards misunderstand what community communication is for.

They think it's about keeping the public informed. Announcements. Meeting summaries. Enrollment numbers. Bond updates. A newsletter every month or two.

That's not wrong. It's just incomplete.

Community communication is not an information delivery system. It's a governance act. It's how you demonstrate — in public, on the record — that your board is doing what it committed to do.

And January is the month to get it right.

## The Communication-Governance Connection

Here's what I've observed in districts where the board communicates well. The community trusts the board. The board has permission to govern. When hard decisions need to be made — a budget cut, a school closure, a program change — the community doesn't assume the worst. They assume the board is operating in good faith.

And here's what I've observed in districts where the board communicates poorly. Every decision is questioned. Every meeting is contentious. The community fills the information vacuum with suspicion.

The difference isn't the district's budget or its test scores. It's the board's willingness to communicate proactively, transparently, and consistently.

## The January Communication Imperative

January is the most important communication month of the governance year.

Here's why. In January, your board has just set its goals for the year. You know what you're trying to accomplish. The community does not — unless you tell them.

If the community doesn't hear from you in January about your goals, they'll spend the rest of the year guessing. They'll form their own narrative about what the board is doing. And that narrative will be based on whatever fills the vacuum — a controversial decision, a policy debate, a rumor.

The board that communicates its goals in January controls the narrative. The board that doesn't, doesn't.

## What Effective January Communication Looks Like

I've worked with boards on communication strategy for years. Here's what works in January.

### The One-Page Governance Summary

Create a one-page document that answers three questions:

1. What are our board's goals for 2027?
2. How will we measure progress?
3. When will we report back to the community?

That's it. One page. Clear language. No jargon. Published on the district website, shared in the newsletter, mentioned at the board meeting.

The purpose is not to impress anyone with the depth of your strategic planning. The purpose is to make your governance visible. To let the community see that the board has a plan and intends to follow through.

### The Board Chair's January Message

The board chair should record a two-minute video or write a brief message to the community explaining the board's January priorities.

The format doesn't need to be polished. It needs to be genuine. The chair speaks directly to the community: here's what we accomplished in December, here's what we're working on in January, here's what you can expect from us this year.

The boards that do this build trust faster than any newsletter or press release. Because the community hears directly from their elected representative, not from a communications office.

### The Public Commitment

At the January board meeting, the chair should make a public commitment: "At every board meeting this year, we will review our progress against our 2027 goals. We will report to the community at each meeting. And we will be transparent about where we're succeeding and where we're struggling."

Making that commitment publicly matters. It creates accountability. The community now knows what to expect. They can hold the board to it.

## The Three Communication Channels That Matter

Most boards overcomplicate their communication strategy. You don't need a social media team, a PR consultant, or a monthly magazine. You need three channels, used consistently.

### Channel 1: The Board Meeting

Your most important communication channel is the board meeting itself. It's the one time each month when the community can see you governing. Use it.

Your board meeting should include: a clear presentation of your goals, a data dashboard that the community can understand, a public commitment to report back at the next meeting, and time for community questions.

If your board meetings are incomprehensible to the average parent, the community will check out. Design your meetings for the person watching at home, not for the insiders in the room.

### Channel 2: The Board Newsletter

A monthly or bi-weekly newsletter from the board — not from the district communications office, from the board — is the most effective ongoing communication tool.

The newsletter should be brief. Three sections: what the board is working on, what progress has been made since the last newsletter, and what's coming up. Links to meeting materials and data dashboards. A way for community members to submit questions.

### Channel 3: Direct Board Member Communication

Every board member should have a way for community members to reach them directly. Email. A contact form on the district website. An open office hour once a month.

The boards that lose community trust are the boards that seem inaccessible. The boards that maintain trust are the boards that are reachable.

## The Cost of Not Communicating

Let me tell you what happens when a board doesn't communicate its January plan.

February arrives. A parent asks at the board meeting: "What are the board's goals for this year?" The chair says: "We adopted them in January. They're in the board packet from the January meeting."

The parent doesn't know where to find the board packet. Or they find it and it's sixty pages of dense text. They give up. They assume the board is hiding something.

By March, the local Facebook group is full of speculation. "The board doesn't have a plan." "They haven't told us what they're doing." "They're making decisions behind closed doors."

None of it is true. The board has a plan. They just never told anyone.

Communication isn't an add-on to governance. It is governance. The community can't hold you accountable for a plan they don't know exists.

January is the month to make sure they know.
