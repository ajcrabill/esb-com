**Board Self-Evaluation — How Did the Board Perform in Spring 2028?**

June 21, 2028

---

The superintendent evaluation is complete. The board answered the question: *Did the superintendent produce the outcomes the board identified as most important for students?*

Now the board answers a question about itself: *Did our governance produce better decisions than we would have made without it?*

Here is what I have learned about board self-evaluation after watching boards conduct them for more than a decade.

Most boards skip the self-evaluation. They complete the superintendent evaluation, approve the summer governance calendar, and adjourn for June. The board never formally assesses whether its own governance framework functioned as designed.

The boards that do conduct a self-evaluation often reduce it to a satisfaction survey. "Are you satisfied with the board's communication?" "Are you satisfied with the board's meeting efficiency?" The questions are about feelings, not effectiveness. A board that scores high on satisfaction but produced no measurable improvement in student outcomes is an ineffective board that feels good about being ineffective.

A substantive board self-evaluation is not a satisfaction survey. It is an effectiveness assessment. It asks four specific questions, each supported by evidence the board generated during the spring governance cycle.

---

**Question one: Did the board execute the governance calendar as planned?**

The board adopted a governance calendar in January that named what every meeting through June was designed to produce. The calendar was the board's commitment to a year of connected governance actions — not a list of dates with agendas attached, but a sequence of decisions, each one designed to inform the next.

Here is the evidence the board reviews.

Pull the governance calendar from January. Next to each meeting, write what the calendar said the meeting would produce. Then write what actually happened.

The crosswalk meeting in February. The calendar said the board would produce a written finding identifying budget allocation misalignments. Did the board produce that finding? Yes or no.

The progress monitoring check-in in May. The calendar said the board would receive the second Progress Monitoring report of the spring, discuss intervention effectiveness, and name any adjustments needed. Did the board receive the report? Did the board discuss it substantively? Did the board name adjustments? Yes or no for each.

I have watched boards in **Prairie Village (KS)** discover during this review that they had only executed 60 percent of their governance calendar through May. The board did not notice the gap during the spring because each individual meeting had a full agenda and felt productive. But when the board mapped the calendar against what was actually produced, the pattern was clear: the board was running meetings, but the meetings were not producing the governance outcomes the calendar had designed them to produce.

The score on this question is the percentage of governance actions the board completed as planned. I consider 80 percent a functional governance year. Below 70 percent, the board needs to understand why the gap exists — and whether the gap was caused by an overambitious calendar, a board that deprioritized governance actions in favor of operational items, or a superintendent who did not provide the information the board needed by the scheduled date.

---

**Question two: Did the board's key decisions hold up through the spring execution cycle?**

The board made at least five consequential decisions between January and June: the goal adoption, the budget direction, the crosswalk findings, the budget adoption, and the superintendent evaluation.

Here is the question the board asks about each one.

Six months later, would the board make the same decision? If yes, the decision was sound. If no, the board needs to understand what information it lacked at the original decision point and whether the governance framework is designed to surface that information earlier in the cycle.

The budget direction is a good test case. The board gave the superintendent written direction in January naming the goals to fund and the guardrails to respect. By June, did that direction produce the allocation the board intended? If the board reviews the final budget and sees that a priority goal was underfunded despite being named in the direction, the board needs to ask whether the direction was specific enough — or whether the board needs a mid-year budget revision process that would catch the misalignment before June.

The crosswalk findings are another test case. The board identified budget allocation misalignments in February. By June, did those misalignments get corrected? If yes, the crosswalk process worked. If no, the board needs to understand why the correction did not occur — and whether the board's written finding was specific enough to produce action.

---

**Question three: Did the board's community engagement produce measurable board action?**

The board committed to at least one community engagement event during the spring — the budget public hearing in April. Some boards also conducted a spring town hall, a community survey, or a series of school visits.

Here is the question the board asks.

Did anything change as a result of community input?

The evidence is the community input summary from each engagement event and the board's subsequent decisions. If the community named a priority at the public hearing — a specific category of spending, a specific support service, a specific program — and the board or superintendent adjusted the budget in response, the engagement produced measurable action. If the community raised a concern about a goal area — reading proficiency, attendance, postsecondary readiness — and the board named that concern in its progress monitoring conversation, the engagement influenced governance.

If the board cannot identify a single change produced by community input between January and June, the community engagement functioned as a compliance activity. The board conducted the hearing because state law required it. The community gave input because the board asked for it. But the input did not move anything.

I tell boards: a community engagement that produces no change is not neutral. It is damaging. The community learns that its input does not matter, and the next engagement event will draw fewer participants with less specific input. The board's job is not to conduct engagement events. The board's job is to demonstrate that community input produces action.

---

**Question four: Did the board operate as a governance team?**

This question is about the board's internal functioning. It is not about whether board members liked each other this spring. It is about whether the board's decision-making process produced better decisions than individual board members acting alone.

Here is the evidence.

Review the board meeting minutes from January through June. Count the board votes that were unanimous. Count the votes that were split. For the split votes, ask: did the board discuss the minority perspective before voting, or did the majority present its position and move to a vote without engaging the minority view?

Review the executive session minutes (if your state allows board members to reference them for self-evaluation purposes). Count the deliberations where board members changed their position based on new information or a stronger argument. Those are the moments where the governance team functioned as designed — individual members brought different perspectives, the board discussed them, and the best argument prevailed.

I have watched boards in **Lawrence (KS)** use this question to identify a pattern they had not seen before: one board member consistently voted against budget items without providing an alternative during discussion. The board did not name the pattern during the year because each individual vote seemed like a one-time disagreement. But when the board reviewed six months of votes together, the pattern was visible. The board discussed it during the self-evaluation, and the member committed to providing alternatives during future budget discussions.

---

**What the board produces from the self-evaluation**

The board self-evaluation produces two outputs.

**Output one: The board's governance effectiveness finding.** This is a written statement naming the board's assessment of its own governance framework. It includes the score on calendar execution, the assessment of key decisions, the community engagement finding, and the governance team functioning assessment.

**Output two: The board's summer and fall improvement priorities.** These are specific actions the board commits to before the fall governance cycle begins. A board that scored below 70 percent on calendar execution might commit to a simplified calendar with fewer, more consequential governance actions. A board that discovered its community engagement produced no measurable action might commit to a different engagement format in the fall — one designed to surface input before the budget direction is written, not after.

I recommend the board president present the self-evaluation finding at the June board meeting in open session. The community should see that the board applies the same standard to itself that it applied to the superintendent: *Did our governance produce the outcomes we identified as most important for students?*
