**Veterans Day — Three Governance Lessons We Should Never Forget**

November 11, 2026

---

I've worked with dozens of veterans who serve — or have served — on school boards. And I've noticed something about them.

They don't waste time on things that don't matter.

They show up prepared. They understand chain of command. They know the difference between a mission and a distraction. And they bring a kind of steady, unflappable presence to the boardroom that every team needs, especially during transition seasons.

On Veterans Day, I want to share three governance lessons we can learn from the men and women who've served. Because the same discipline that keeps a unit effective through turnover is the discipline that keeps a school board effective through an election cycle.

---

**1. Service before self — board members serve students, not their own agendas.**

The first lesson comes from something I hear in every military transition document I've read: the mission comes first.

A soldier doesn't get to choose which objectives matter based on personal preference. The mission is the mission. Personal preferences are subordinate to the team's purpose.

School boards are no different. The mission — ensuring every student is prepared for college, career, and citizenship — exists independent of any individual board member's opinion about curriculum, facilities, or staffing. The board member who shows up primarily to advance a personal agenda has misunderstood the assignment.

This is hardest to remember right after an election. New members arrive with campaign promises. Incumbents who lost carry disappointment. Even incumbents who won can feel emboldened to push harder. The temptation to treat the board as a platform for individual priorities is strongest when membership changes.

But the mission doesn't change. And the board's job is to serve the mission — not to use the mission as a vehicle for personal goals. That's what service before self means in governance.

---

**2. Institutional memory is strategic — the knowledge departing members carry is worth capturing.**

Here's what the military gets right that most school boards get wrong: when a service member transfers out, there's a deliberate, structured handoff of knowledge. Not a binder. Not a folder left on a desk. A conversation, a checklist, a documented transfer of what that person knows that the next person won't.

School boards lose institutional knowledge every time a member leaves. The board member who understood the district's bond history. The one who knew why the previous superintendent evaluation process didn't work. The one who had the relationships with the teachers' union president and the city council liaison and the state representative's education staffer. All of it walks out the door.

And unlike the military, most school boards have no structured process for capturing that knowledge before it leaves.

If you have departing members — from any election, not just this one — schedule an exit conversation this month. Not a farewell party. A structured knowledge transfer. Ask them: what do you know about this district that a new member won't? What's the unspoken context behind every major decision on the board's calendar? What relationships are critical to your governance work that need to be handed off?

The answers will save your new members months of learning time. And that time translates directly into better outcomes for students.

---

**3. Continuity of mission — the board's purpose outlasts any election.**

The military has a phrase for this: the mission continues.

A unit doesn't stop being effective because its commanding officer rotates out. The structure, the training, the standard operating procedures — they exist independent of the individual leader. That's by design. Because the mission is bigger than any one person.

Your school board needs the same design. Your student outcome goals, your governance protocols, your progress monitoring cycle, your board self-evaluation process — those should exist independent of any individual member. They should survive any election, any resignation, any appointment.

If they don't — if your board's direction shifts every time a member leaves or arrives — then you don't have a governance system. You have a group of well-intentioned individuals who happen to occupy seats at the same table.

Continuity of mission isn't about resisting change. It's about making sure the change that happens serves a purpose bigger than any individual term.

---

Today, we honor the men and women who served our country. Their discipline, their commitment to mission, and their ability to transfer knowledge through transition are not just military values — they're governance values.

If your board has departing members this season, take an hour to sit with them and ask the questions that matter. Capture what they know before it walks out the door. Reaffirm the mission that outlasts every election.

That's what the best boards do. And it's the best way to honor the service of those who left the boardroom — and those who served our country.
