**January Goal Adoption Prep — What Every Board Should Be Doing in December**

December 13, 2027

---

Here is what happens on most boards in January.

The board president calls the January meeting to order. The superintendent presents goal language for the coming year. Board members see the language for the first time. They have questions. They have concerns. They have competing priorities. The meeting runs long. The board adopts goals that are vague enough to get everyone to vote yes and specific enough to drive nothing.

By February, the board is reviewing data against goals that no one remembers adopting. By March, the board has abandoned the goals entirely and is back to managing operations.

Here is how to break that cycle. Start preparing for goal adoption in December. Not in January. December.

**The December preparation cycle**

Here is the December preparation cycle your board needs to follow.

Week one of December (the week of the organizational meeting): The board president and superintendent meet to establish the goal adoption timeline. By December 10, the board should have a written timeline that includes the draft language date, the community feedback window, the revision deadline, and the adoption meeting date.

Week two of December (this week): The superintendent drafts goal language based on the October and November data reviews. The draft language goes to the board president for initial review. The board president checks for alignment with the data. Does the goal language reflect what the data actually showed? If the October data showed stagnant third-grade reading scores, the 2028 goal should name third-grade reading specifically. If the November data showed growth in math, the 2028 goal should accelerate that growth target.

Week three of December: The board president circulates the draft goal language to the full board. This is not a vote. This is a preview. Board members receive the language with a simple instruction: "Read this before the December break. Come to the planning session in January with your questions and concerns."

Week four of December: Community feedback. The board publishes the draft goal language with a comment period. This can be a simple form on the district website. It can be a dedicated public comment segment at the December regular meeting. The point is that the community sees the goals before the board votes on them. Surprise goal adoption is bad governance.

**How October and November data reviews inform January goals**

Here is the direct line from fall data to winter goals.

The October data review showed the board where students were starting. Baseline reading scores. Baseline math scores. Baseline attendance rates. That is the starting line for 2028.

The November data review showed growth. How much progress between October and November? Which interventions are working? Which student groups are accelerating and which are stagnating?

The superintendent fall check-in gave the board a candid assessment of capacity. Can the system sustain more aggressive targets? Does the superintendent have the team and resources to pursue ambitious goals? The fall check-in answer determines whether 2028 goals push growth or consolidate gains.

Here is how those three inputs combine into a goal-setting framework.

First, set a baseline. The October data is the starting point. Every 2028 goal should reference an October 2027 baseline. "Increase third-grade reading proficiency from forty-two percent (October 2027 baseline) to fifty-five percent by May 2028."

Second, set a trajectory. The November data shows whether the current trajectory is sufficient. If the October-to-November growth rate is on pace to hit the 2028 target, maintain the trajectory. If the growth rate is too slow, either adjust the interventions or adjust the target.

Third, set a capacity check. The superintendent fall check-in tells you whether the system can handle the goals you are setting. If the superintendent said capacity is strained, do not layer ambitious new goals on top of an already stressed system. Prioritize. The board should adopt fewer goals with more focus.

**The community engagement strategy for goal adoption**

Here is what most boards miss about community engagement for goal adoption. They do it after the goals are written, not before.

Here is the ESB protocol. Community engagement for goal adoption is a three-phase process.

Phase one: listening. In November and December, the board holds listening sessions — not presentations, not updates, not question-and-answer sessions. Listening sessions. The board asks three questions: What outcomes matter most to you? What progress do you see? What concerns do you have? The board documents the answers and uses them to inform goal language.

Phase two: previewing. In the third week of December, the board publishes draft goal language with a comment period. The community sees what the board is considering. They have a structured way to give feedback. The board reviews the feedback and revises the language.

Phase three: adopting. At the January meeting, the board votes on goal language that the community has already seen and commented on. There are no surprises. The vote is a formality because the real work happened in December.

Here is why this matters. Goals that are adopted without community input are goals that the community does not own. When the board reports progress in March and the community sees flat data, the community asks "whose goals were these?" When the community participated in setting the goals, the community asks "how can we help?"

Community ownership of goals is the difference between goals that sit on a shelf and goals that drive collective action. Build that ownership in December.

**The board president's December job in goal adoption**

Here is the board president's December job description.

First, the board president manages the timeline. The superintendent needs a deadline for draft language. The board needs a deadline for feedback. The community needs a deadline for comments. The board president sets those deadlines and enforces them. If the superintendent misses the deadline, the board president follows up. If board members miss the feedback deadline, the board president follows up.

Second, the board president pre-books the January goal adoption meeting. Not the January regular meeting with goal adoption tacked on. A separate meeting dedicated to goal adoption. Two hours. No other agenda items. The board president ensures that the January goal adoption meeting is on the calendar and on everyone's calendar before the December break.

Third, the board president conducts one-on-one conversations with every board member between December 15 and January 5. Not group emails. Not a single conversation at the January meeting. One-on-one conversations. The board president asks each board member: "Do you understand the goal language? Do you support it? If not, what would need to change for your support?"

Here is why those one-on-one conversations matter. Board members who are surprised at the dais are board members who vote no. Board members who are heard in advance are board members who vote yes. The board president's job is to ensure that every board member has been heard before the January meeting.

If the board president discovers disagreement in the one-on-one conversations, that is not a problem. That is useful information. The board president can address the concerns before the meeting, revise the goal language, or schedule additional discussion. The one-on-one conversations are early warning systems. Use them.

January goal adoption should not be a drama. It should be a celebration of aligned governance. December preparation makes that possible.
