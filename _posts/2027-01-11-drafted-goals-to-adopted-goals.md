**From Drafted Goals to Adopted Goals: The Missing Step That Determines Whether Your Goals Stick**

January 11, 2027

---

If your board followed December's goal-setting arc, you walked into January with something most boards don't have: draft goals. Specific, measurable, data-informed targets for the year ahead.

That's good work. But having draft goals and having adopted goals are two different things — and the gap between them is where goals go to die.

I've seen boards spend December in productive work sessions, drafting goals that are specific, measurable, and connected to student outcome data. Three weeks later, those same goals are sitting in a folder labeled "2027 Goals — Draft." They were never formally presented. Never publicly discussed. Never adopted by board vote. They exist as an artifact of a productive December, not as a binding commitment for the year ahead.

The difference between a draft goal and an adopted goal is the adoption process itself. And that process matters more than most boards realize — because the adoption process is where accountability becomes real.

Here's what needs to happen between a draft and an adoption.

**Step 1: Present the draft goals with baseline data — before the vote**

Most boards bring their draft goals to a board meeting and call for a vote. The motion is made. The vote is unanimous. The goals are "adopted" — and immediately forgotten until the superintendent evaluation in June.

The problem isn't the vote. The problem is that the vote happened without context. The community didn't see the data that informed the goals. The board didn't articulate why these particular targets matter. The adoption was a procedural step, not a governance act.

Before the board votes, the draft goals should be presented with the data that supports them. Not a binder of data — a one-page summary for each goal that answers three questions:

1. Where are we now? (baseline data)
2. Where are we going? (target)
3. Why this target? (the data-driven rationale)

This presentation should be public. It should be on the agenda as a substantive item, not buried under consent. The community should see that the board isn't making up goals — it's responding to data.

**Step 2: Hold a public hearing**

I know what you're thinking: a public hearing on goals sounds like an invitation to chaos. What if the community doesn't like the targets? What if someone questions the data? What if the conversation goes sideways?

Here's what I've learned from watching boards that do this: the public hearing is rarely about changing the goals. It's about creating a record that the board considered community input before committing to specific targets. And the questions the community asks will almost always improve the goals.

A parent asks: "Why are you setting a 52% target for reading? My child's school is already at 70%." That's a good question — and it gives the board an opportunity to explain that the target is district-wide, and that the low-performing schools are where the focus needs to be.

A teacher asks: "What resources are attached to this goal? We can't get to 52% with the current intervention program." That's an even better question — and it gives the board an opportunity to say "that's exactly the budget conversation we're having in February. This goal is our way of telling you and the superintendent where we want resources directed."

The public hearing also accomplishes something less tangible and more important: it makes the community a partner in the work. The parent who heard the board explain its reading goal and the data behind it is a parent who knows what the board is trying to accomplish. That parent is more likely to support the board through the inevitably difficult moments when the data doesn't show immediate improvement.

**Step 3: Take the adoption vote with a statement of commitment**

This is the step most boards get right — the vote itself — and then immediately get wrong by moving on to the next agenda item without capping the moment.

When the board votes to adopt its 2027 goals, that vote should be followed by a statement from the board president. A public articulation of what this vote means:

"In adopting these goals, this board commits to reviewing progress data at every board meeting throughout 2027, reporting results to the community quarterly, and holding ourselves accountable for the targets we've set. These goals will drive the superintendent evaluation, the budget priorities, and the mid-year governance check-in. We invite the community to hold us accountable for the commitments we've made today."

The statement matters because it changes the character of the vote. Without it, the vote is a board voting on a document. With it, the vote is a board making a public commitment. The community hears the difference. The superintendent hears the difference. The board members themselves — who are now on the record — feel the difference.

**A Template for the Goal Adoption Meeting**

Here's what a 90-minute goal adoption agenda looks like:

| Time | Item | Purpose |
|------|------|---------|
| 5 min | Welcome and context | Board president explains what's happening and why |
| 15 min | Goal presentation | Superintendent presents each goal with baseline data and target rationale |
| 30 min | Public hearing | Community members ask questions and provide input |
| 10 min | Public hearing closes | Board acknowledges input received |
| 20 min | Board deliberation | Board discusses whether to adopt as presented or with modifications |
| 10 min | Adoption vote and statement | Board votes, board president reads commitment statement |

The entire meeting — including the public hearing — is designed to produce one outcome: adopted goals that the board, the superintendent, and the community all understand and are committed to.

---

I've seen boards skip the public hearing because it felt risky. I've seen boards skip the baseline data presentation because they assumed everyone already knew it. I've seen boards skip the commitment statement because it felt theatrical.

Every time, the goals were weaker for the omission. The community was less informed. The board was less bound. The accountability that should have been established in January had to be reconstructed in June — when it was too late to course-correct.

A draft goal is a work in progress. An adopted goal is a public commitment. The adoption process is what turns one into the other.

Make sure your board goes through it. The rest of the year depends on it.
