**The April Budget Adoption Timeline — How to Go from Public Hearing to a Clean Adoption Vote**

February 29, 2028

---

Leap Day.

You have one extra day this year. One day your board did not have the last time you ran a budget adoption cycle. And if you use it the way most boards use February — waiting for the next deadline to come to them — that extra day will not matter at all.

But your board is not most boards.

Your board spent January building the governance framework. You adopted measurable goals from real data. You gave the superintendent written budget direction. You held the first Progress Monitoring check-in.

Your board spent February running the Crosswalk arc. You reviewed the preliminary budget through a goal-alignment lens — does every goal have a visible budget line? Are resources proportional to priority? You built the goal-to-budget crosswalk — the matrix that maps every program allocation to the student outcome goal it serves. You designed and executed a community engagement process for the public hearing that treated the community as a governance partner, not a compliance checkbox.

Four pieces. Four weeks. Four connected governance actions that most boards do not complete in a full year.

Now comes the final stretch. The six to eight weeks between the February public hearing and the April adoption vote. And the Leap Day you have this year is not a gimmick. It is a signal. You have time you did not have last cycle. The question is whether you will use it.

Here is exactly what the final stretch looks like, week by week, including the March alignment review, what to do if your state requires a second public hearing, and how to bring your board to a clean adoption vote in April.

---

**The March alignment review: The board's last chance to check the crosswalk**

Before I talk about the adoption timeline, let me talk about March.

The March budget alignment review is the bridge between the February preliminary budget review and the April adoption vote. It is where the board answers one question: *Did the superintendent make the corrections the board requested in February?*

Here is why that question matters more than you think.

The February preliminary budget review produced a board action — written findings on goal alignment, fiscal guardrails, and community alignment. Those findings included specific directions to the superintendent: *Increase the early literacy allocation. Reduce the administrative spending to stay within the guardrails. Reallocate the non-goal program to fund the community's expressed priorities.*

The superintendent had approximately four weeks to implement those directions and produce a revised budget draft.

The March alignment review is where the board checks whether the revision reflects the directions.

**What the March alignment review looks like**

Schedule a dedicated work session — not a full board meeting with a dozen other agenda items. You need ninety minutes for this conversation, and you need every board member present.

Put three documents on the table:

1. The board's February written findings — the specific directions the board gave the superintendent
2. The revised budget draft — the superintendent's response to those directions
3. The updated goal-to-budget crosswalk — since the budget has changed, the crosswalk needs to change with it

The board's task is straightforward. Walk through each February finding one by one. For each finding, ask: *Is this correction reflected in the revised budget?*

If yes, confirm it. If no, ask why. Some corrections cannot be made within the available revenue. Some require contract renegotiations that cannot be completed before April. Some were based on assumptions about community input that the public hearing changed. The board needs to hear the explanation, decide whether the constraint is legitimate, and either adjust the direction or give the superintendent a hard deadline for compliance.

**What the March alignment review should produce**

The output of the March work session should be three things.

First, a completed updated crosswalk. If the February crosswalk showed a misalignment and the superintendent corrected it, the March crosswalk should show alignment. If the superintendent could not correct it, the crosswalk should note the constraint and the board's decision on how to proceed.

Second, a written confirmation that the board has reviewed the revised budget and finds it sufficiently aligned to the January budget direction and the February findings — or a written list of remaining items the board expects to see resolved before the final budget draft is produced.

Third, a specific date for the final budget draft delivery. The superintendent should know, by the end of the March work session, when the final draft is due. Four weeks before the adoption vote is the minimum. Three weeks is tight but workable. Two weeks is dangerous — it leaves the board insufficient time to review and the community insufficient time to see the final document before the vote.

A board that conducts the March alignment review with discipline enters April knowing exactly where the budget stands. A board that skips the March alignment review will discover in April — during the adoption vote itself — that the February corrections were never made, and the budget the board is being asked to approve does not align with the goals the board adopted in January.

**Handling a second public hearing if your state requires one**

Here is something most boards do not plan for.

Some states require a second public hearing after the budget is revised but before the final adoption vote. The rationale is straightforward: if the budget changes materially between the initial public hearing and the adoption vote, the community deserves another opportunity to respond to the changes.

If your state has this requirement, you need to know it now — not discover it in March when your legal counsel tells you the hearing notice has to go out in seven days.

**How to check whether a second hearing applies**

Ask your board attorney or business office this question: *Does our state require a second public hearing if the budget is materially revised between the initial hearing and the adoption vote?*

The answer varies by state. Some states require a second hearing only if the total budget amount changes by a specific percentage — typically 5 to 10 percent. Some require a second hearing only if revenue estimates change materially — which they often do between February and April as state funding formulas are finalized. Some do not require a second hearing at all if the board held a hearing on the preliminary budget and no material changes were made.

Your board attorney should be able to answer this question in ten minutes. Ask it at the March work session — or better, ask it now.

**How to prepare for a second hearing**

If a second hearing is required, here is how your board should handle it.

First, schedule it early enough that it does not compress the adoption timeline. A second hearing that happens the week before the adoption vote is not a governance conversation — it is a compliance sprint. The community sees the notice, shows up, and is told the budget is essentially final. That is not meaningful engagement. Schedule the second hearing at least two weeks before the adoption vote so the board has time to consider any new input.

Second, use the second hearing to show what changed. The community attended the first hearing in February. They heard the goals, saw the crosswalk, and provided input. The second hearing is the board's opportunity to demonstrate that the input was heard and acted on. The board president opens the hearing by naming the major themes from the first hearing, then presenting the changes the board made in response. *The community asked about early literacy funding. Here is what we did. The community raised concerns about transportation. Here is why we did not make a change — and here is what we are doing instead.*

Third, connect the second hearing explicitly to the March alignment review. The board's March findings — the updated crosswalk, the confirmed corrections, the remaining items — become the content of the second hearing presentation. The community sees not just the budget, but the board's governance process behind it.

A board that handles the second hearing this way turns a compliance requirement into a community trust-building opportunity. A board that treats the second hearing as a procedural nuisance — notice published, five minutes of presentation, three minutes of questions, hearing closed — wastes the one opportunity it has to demonstrate that the community's first round of input actually shaped the final document.

**Bringing the board to a clean adoption vote in April**

Now the adoption vote itself.

Let me be direct about what a clean adoption vote looks like. It is not a unanimous vote — though unanimous is fine. It is not a vote with no questions — a board that asks zero questions before approving a multimillion-dollar budget has not done its job.

A clean adoption vote is a vote that happens because every board member has had adequate time to review the final budget, every board member's questions have been answered before the meeting, and the conversation on adoption night is about confirming understanding — not about discovering disagreements.

Here is how you get there.

**Give board members the final budget at least two weeks before the vote.**

Two weeks is the minimum. Three is better. A board member who receives the final budget the Friday before a Tuesday adoption vote has not had time to read it through once, let alone compare it to the crosswalk and the February findings. If you want clean adoption votes, you give your board members time to do their homework.

The superintendent should deliver the final budget draft with a cover memo that names: the significant changes since the February preliminary budget, how each change connects to a board direction or a community input item, and any items the board flagged in the March alignment review that remain unresolved.

**Hold a pre-vote Q&A session.**

Schedule a thirty-minute agenda item at the meeting before the adoption vote — or a separate fifteen-minute board caucus — where board members can ask questions about the final draft without the pressure of a public vote. The superintendent attends. The finance director attends. Board members ask whatever questions they have. No votes are taken. No public testimony is heard.

The purpose is to surface questions that would otherwise emerge during the adoption vote itself — and answer them before the vote, not during it.

I have watched boards adopt budgets in under five minutes because the pre-vote Q&A session resolved every question. I have watched boards spend forty minutes on a budget adoption because the pre-vote Q&A was never scheduled and board members were reading the final budget for the first time.

**Structure the adoption night agenda item to demonstrate alignment.**

The adoption night agenda item should have four components, in this order:

1. **The board president names the arc.** A two-minute summary of the governance process that produced this budget. *In January we adopted goals and gave budget direction. In February we reviewed the preliminary budget, built the crosswalk, and held the public hearing. In March we conducted the alignment review and confirmed that the corrections were made. Tonight we adopt the final budget.*

2. **The superintendent presents the final crosswalk.** One page. Goals on the left. Allocations in the middle. Percentage of the general fund on the right. The same crosswalk format the board has seen since February. No surprises.

3. **The board president presents the community input response.** A one-minute summary of how community input from the February public hearing — and the second hearing, if one was held — shaped the final budget. *The community asked for increased early literacy funding. The final budget reflects a 2.1 percent increase in the early literacy allocation. The community raised concerns about transportation fees. The budget maintains current fee levels and directs a program review for the 2029 cycle.*

4. **The board votes.** The roll call is taken. The budget is adopted.

A board that structures its adoption night this way demonstrates to the community and the superintendent that the budget was not approved on a whim. It was adopted because every step of the governance framework — goals, crosswalk, hearings, alignment review — produced a document that reflects deliberate choices.

---

**The connection back to Progress Monitoring**

I want to name one more connection before I close.

The April adoption vote is not the end of the board's budget responsibility. It is the beginning of the board's monitoring responsibility.

The crosswalk your board built in February and updated in March is not a one-time document. It is the baseline against which every future Progress Monitoring check-in will be measured. When the board reviews second-quarter data in May, the question is not just *Are the goals on track?* It is *Is the budget we adopted producing the results we intended?*

If a goal is behind trajectory in May and the crosswalk shows that goal received adequate funding, the board asks about strategy — not funding. If a goal is behind trajectory and the crosswalk shows that goal was underfunded relative to its priority, the board asks about itself — did the board set a priority it was not willing to fund?

That is the governance discipline the crosswalk arc has built. Your board does not have to guess whether the budget and the goals are connected. You have the document that proves the connection — or exposes the gap.

---

**Closing the crosswalk arc**

Let me step back and name what your board has done since February 1.

Piece 1 — you reviewed the preliminary budget through the three-bucket framework: goal alignment, fiscal guardrails, community alignment. You answered the hardest question a board can ask in February: *Does this budget fund the goals we adopted?*

Piece 2 — you built the goal-to-budget crosswalk. One page. Three columns. Goals mapped to dollars. You discovered where the money actually goes.

Piece 3 — you designed and executed a community engagement process for the public hearing that treated the community as a governance partner. You presented the crosswalk. You asked specific questions. You gathered input that shaped the March revision.

Piece 4 — this piece. The April budget adoption timeline. The March alignment review. The second public hearing, if required. The clean adoption vote. The connection forward to Progress Monitoring.

Four pieces. Four weeks. One complete governance arc from preliminary budget review to final adoption.

Your board is not the board that waits until April to read the budget for the first time. Your board is not the board that holds a silent public hearing and calls it engagement. Your board is not the board that adopts a budget without knowing whether it funds the goals.

Your board ran the arc. Now close it.

The April adoption vote is the formal conclusion. The March Progress Monitoring check-in, the Q1 governance health check, the May data review, the June year-end conversation — those are the next steps in a governance cycle that does not end with the adoption vote.

But today — on this Leap Day that only comes once every four years — recognize what your board has accomplished. You built a budget from the goals, validated it with the community, and prepared for a clean adoption vote.

The crosswalk arc is closed. Spring governance is waiting.
