**Q2 Governance Launch — Spring Execution, Progress Monitoring, and the Superintendent Evaluation Countdown**

2028-04-12

---

The budget is adopted. The vote is recorded. The allocations are set.

Now the board's work shifts from resource allocation to performance monitoring. That shift is the most consequential transition in the governance year — and the one most boards handle the worst.

Here is why.

Most boards spend January through March on the budget and April through June on whatever lands on the agenda. There is no intentional transition. The budget adoption meeting happens, the board moves to the next agenda item, and by May the board is reacting to end-of-year operational issues instead of leading the spring governance calendar.

Your board has a governance calendar. Your board has adopted goals with measurable targets. Your board has built a crosswalk that connects the budget to those goals. Your board has confirmed alignment, assessed its framework, and closed the community engagement loop.

The spring governance execution phase is where that work produces results. Here is the Q2 launch plan.

---

**The three pillars of Q2 governance execution**

The spring execution phase has three pillars. Every board meeting, committee meeting, and board action between now and June should serve one of these three.

**Pillar one: Progress monitoring.** The board adopted goals in January and allocated resources in April. The spring progress monitoring cadence answers the question the board has been building toward since January: Are the resources producing the outcomes?

The first spring progress monitoring check-in should appear on the May board meeting agenda. The superintendent presents the April data for each adopted goal — early literacy proficiency, math growth, ninth-grade on-track rate, or whatever your board adopted — alongside the baseline from January and the trajectory projection. The board reviews the data, asks questions, and determines whether the current trajectory is on pace to meet the annual target.

I recommend a specific format for the May check-in. The board packet includes a one-page progress monitoring dashboard — one row per adopted goal, with the baseline, the current data point, the target, and a green-yellow-red indicator for trajectory. The superintendent presents the data in fifteen minutes. The board discusses for thirty minutes. The board records a written finding: On track, needs attention, or off track.

I have watched boards in **Lawrence (KS)** use this format for their May progress monitoring check-in. The board president opens the discussion with the question: "For each goal, is the current trajectory on pace to meet the annual target?" The board answers from the data, records the finding, and moves to the next goal. The meeting runs ninety minutes instead of three hours because the format is known and the data is prepared.

**Pillar two: Committee oversight.** If your board uses committees — finance, policy, curriculum, facilities — the spring work shifts from planning to implementation.

The finance committee monitors budget execution against the adopted allocations. The committee reviews the first quarter expenditure report, compares actual spending to the budget adopted in April, and reports any variances to the full board.

The curriculum committee monitors progress monitoring data. The committee reviews the same data the full board will see in May, adds context from the instructional team, and prepares a recommendation for the board discussion.

The policy committee reviews any policy changes needed to support the adopted goals. If the board's early literacy goal requires changes to the reading intervention policy, the spring is the time to draft those changes, review them, and prepare them for adoption in the fall.

The committee chairs should submit written reports to the full board by the May board meeting. The report format is two pages maximum — what the committee reviewed, what the committee found, and what the committee recommends.

**Pillar three: Superintendent evaluation preparation.** The superintendent evaluation cycle begins in the spring. The board adopted evaluation criteria — or confirmed the existing criteria — as part of the January governance launch. The spring evaluation timeline has three phases.

**Phase one: Evidence collection (April-May).** The board president or evaluation committee chair gathers evidence related to each evaluation criterion. The evidence includes progress monitoring data from the spring check-ins, budget execution reports from the finance committee, and any board meeting observations that relate to the superintendent's performance against the criteria.

**Phase two: Draft evaluation (May-June).** The board president or evaluation committee chair drafts the evaluation document using the evidence gathered in phase one. The draft is shared with the full board for review and discussion in executive session.

**Phase three: Evaluation meeting (June).** The board meets with the superintendent to present and discuss the evaluation. The board president reads the evaluation findings, the superintendent responds, and the board adopts the evaluation as part of the June meeting record.

I have watched boards in **Albemarle County (VA)** begin the evaluation preparation in April by naming the evidence collection timeline at the board meeting following the budget adoption. The board president says: "We have ten weeks before the June evaluation meeting. Here is exactly what evidence we need, who is responsible for gathering it, and when it will be ready for board review." The evaluation process is transparent and predictable from the beginning, not rushed and reactive at the end.

---

**The Q2 governance calendar**

Here is the Q2 calendar that connects these three pillars into a single governance execution plan.

**April 19 — April Governance Check.** The Q2 launch is one week old. The April governance check confirms the board is following the calendar, the committees are meeting on schedule, and the evaluation preparation has begun.

**May 7 or 14 — Spring Progress Monitoring Check-In.** The board reviews April data for each adopted goal, records a written finding for each goal, and determines whether the trajectory is on pace for the annual target.

**May 21 or 28 — Committee Report Meeting.** The finance, curriculum, and policy committees submit written reports to the full board. The board discusses the reports and adopts any recommendations.

**June 4 — Superintendent Evaluation Draft Review.** The board reviews the draft evaluation in executive session, discusses the findings, and prepares questions for the superintendent.

**June 11 — Superintendent Evaluation Meeting.** The board presents and adopts the evaluation. The superintendent responds.

**June 18 — Board Self-Assessment and Q2 Governance Health Check.** The board completes the Q2 Health Check, compares the findings to the Q1 baseline, and records the result.

**June 25 — Spring Governance Report and Year-End Closeout.** The board publishes the spring governance report to the community and closes the governance year.

A board that has these dates on the calendar by April 12 enters the spring execution phase with clarity. A board that does not schedule these dates drifts into May without knowing whether the progress monitoring check-in, the committee reports, or the evaluation timeline are on the calendar.
