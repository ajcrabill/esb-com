**January Is Reorganization Month: Don't Waste It on Committee Assignments**

January 27, 2027

---

January is reorganization month for school boards across the country.

Officers are elected. Committee chairs are appointed. Committee assignments are made. The governance team reconstitutes itself for the new year.

And in most boardrooms, this process is treated as a procedural formality. "Welcome to the finance committee, Bob. Here's your binder. Next item on the agenda."

It shouldn't be. January's reorganization is the single best opportunity you have all year to build — or rebuild — your governance team.

But only if you use it intentionally.

## The Reorganization Trap

Here's the trap most boards fall into.

They spend their January reorganization meeting on logistics. Who's on which committee. When committees will meet. What the committee chairs' responsibilities are. By the end of the meeting, every committee assignment has been made, every role has been filled, and the board feels like they've accomplished something.

But what they've actually accomplished is filling seats. They haven't built a governance team.

A governance team is different from a group of people with committee assignments. A governance team has a shared understanding of the board's goals, a shared commitment to working together, and a shared agreement about how decisions will be made.

Most boards have assignments. Few boards have teams.

## The Three Building Blocks of a Governance Team

January reorganization should produce three things. Committee assignments are the easiest one.

### Building Block 1: Shared Purpose

Before you assign anyone to a committee, the full board should reaffirm its shared purpose.

This isn't a vision exercise. It's a simple confirmation: "We adopted these goals in January. These are our goals for the year. Everything we do — every committee meeting, every board meeting, every decision — should move us toward these goals."

Every committee chair should be able to explain how their committee's work connects to the board's goals. If the finance committee can't connect its work to student outcome goals, the committee is meeting for its own sake, not for the district's.

This takes ten minutes at the January reorganization meeting. It changes everything.

### Building Block 2: Clear Roles and Responsibilities

The most common source of governance dysfunction is unclear roles. Board members don't know what's expected of them. Committee chairs don't know the scope of their authority. The superintendent doesn't know who to work with on what.

January reorganization is the moment to clarify roles.

For each committee, establish:
- What is this committee's purpose?
- What decisions does it make?
- What decisions does it recommend to the full board?
- How does it communicate its work to the full board?
- How does it work with the superintendent and staff?

For each officer — chair, vice chair, secretary, treasurer — establish:
- What are their responsibilities?
- What authority do they have between board meetings?
- How are they supported by the superintendent and staff?

A role that isn't defined is a role that will be interpreted differently by every person in it. January is the month to define them.

### Building Block 3: Team Norms and Operating Agreements

This is the block most boards skip — and the one that makes the biggest difference.

Your board should establish a set of operating agreements that govern how you work together. Not policies. Agreements. The norms that shape your interactions.

Examples I've seen from boards that do this well:
- "We prepare for every meeting by reading the packet and writing down our questions in advance."
- "We disagree publicly, but we support the board's decision publicly."
- "We keep our discussions focused on student outcomes."
- "We direct all staff communication through the superintendent."
- "We start every meeting with our strategic priorities, not our procedural business."
- "We end every meeting with clear decisions and assigned follow-ups."

These agreements don't need to be formal policies. They're the norms the board commits to. Write them down. Review them at every reorganization. Hold each other accountable to them.

## The January Reorganization Meeting Agenda

Here's what a January reorganization meeting should look like when you use it to build a governance team.

**Opening (5 minutes):** Call to order. Election of officers. Approval of minutes.

**Building Block 1 — Shared Purpose (15 minutes):** Board chair presents the 2027 goals. Each board member affirms their commitment. Discussion: How does each goal connect to our students and community?

**Building Block 2 — Committee Structure (20 minutes):** Committee chairs present their committee's purpose and scope. Full board discusses whether committee structures are aligned with goals. Committee assignments announced.

**Building Block 3 — Team Norms (20 minutes):** Board discusses and agrees on operating agreements. Agreements are documented and distributed. Board commits to reviewing them at the next reorganization.

**Strategic Planning (20 minutes):** Board reviews the governance calendar for Q1. Key dates. Key decisions. Key data review points. Board schedules the first data review for the February meeting.

**Closing (5 minutes):** Chair summarizes commitments. Adjournment.

Total time: approximately 85 minutes. One board meeting that builds a governance team instead of just filling seats.

## The New Member Onboarding Problem

If your board has new members — elected in November, seated in January — the reorganization meeting is their first real governance experience. And most boards handle new member onboarding poorly.

Here's the typical approach: "Welcome, Susan. You're on the curriculum committee. Your binder has the policies and the strategic plan. Let us know if you have questions."

That's not onboarding. That's orientation. And orientation is not enough.

New board members need more than a binder. They need a structured introduction to:
- The board's 2027 goals and the rationale behind them
- The data dashboard and how to read it
- The board-superintendent partnership agreement
- The board's operating agreements and norms
- The committee structure and how committees work
- The budget timeline and key decision points

The most effective onboarding I've seen pairs each new member with an experienced mentor — a veteran board member who can answer questions, explain context, and help the new member navigate their first board meeting, their first committee meeting, and their first tough vote.

January is the month to build that system. Not June. Not "when Susan has time." January.

## The Cost of Not Building

Let me be direct about what happens when you waste January reorganization.

Your board enters the year without clarity about its purpose. Committees meet without connection to goals. Roles are ambiguous. New members spend their first three months figuring out what they're supposed to do. By April, the board has consumed dozens of meeting hours on confusion that could have been resolved in one intentional January meeting.

And the students? They don't benefit from a board that spends four months figuring out how to work together.

January reorganization is not a procedural requirement. It's a governance opportunity.

Build your team. Define your roles. Establish your norms. Onboard your new members.

Then spend the rest of the year governing.
