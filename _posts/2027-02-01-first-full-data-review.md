**Your First Full Data Review — Real Data, Real Decisions, Real Cadence**

February 1, 2027

---

On January 20, I asked you to conduct your first data check-in — even if there was barely any data to review. The assignment was about establishing the cadence. Show up. Look at the dashboard. Ask questions. Make a decision. Walk out knowing you'll do it again next month.

Now it's February. And for the first time in this governance year, you have real data to review.

The baseline has been set. The goals are adopted. The superintendent has had twelve days since your January 20 check-in to update the dashboard with whatever new data points are available — mid-year assessment results, attendance trends, intervention program enrollment numbers. This isn't the symbolic check-in anymore. This is the first one that counts.

**What's different about this data review**

The January 20 check-in was about sending a signal: the board takes its goals seriously. The February 1 review is about producing an outcome. The signal has been sent. Now you have to govern with the data you receive.

Here's what changes between the first check-in and the first full review:

**January:** "We don't have much data yet, but we're establishing the practice."

**February:** "We have data. What does it tell us? What do we do about it?"

The difference is the difference between warm-up and game time. The board that treats February like January — looking at the dashboard, acknowledging the data, and moving on — has established a cadence but not a governance practice. The board that treats February as the first real review — asking hard questions, giving direction, and following up — has established a governance system.

**The five questions for your February data review**

Your board approved its goals on January 11. The dashboard has been updated since then. Here are the questions — specific, actionable, February-ready — that I recommend every board member ask.

**Question 1: Is this the right baseline?**

The baseline data adopted in January may have been the best available at the time. Now, with additional data collection, the board should verify: was the baseline accurate? If a mid-year assessment shows the baseline was higher or lower than originally stated, adjust it now. A goal measured against the wrong baseline produces the wrong conversation all year.

**Question 2: Are we measuring what matters?**

The dashboard your superintendent presents in February may include metrics that are easy to measure rather than metrics that matter. Enrollment numbers. Staffing ratios. Budget execution percentages. Those are data points, but they may not be progress indicators.

Ask: "Of the metrics on this dashboard, which ones directly measure progress toward our adopted goals? Which ones are contextual but not diagnostic?" Remove the noise so the signal is visible.

**Question 3: What changed since January?**

Twelve days between your January 20 check-in and today isn't enough time to move a major metric. But it is enough time for new information to surface. A program started. A staffing change happened. A mid-cycle assessment was administered. New state data was released.

The superintendent should brief the board on what's new. If there's nothing new to report — if the dashboard looks exactly the same as it did in January — that's itself a data point. It tells the board that either (a) data collection needs to accelerate, or (b) the dashboard isn't capturing enough intermediate metrics.

**Question 4: What's the story behind the numbers?**

Green means acknowledge progress. Yellow means ask what's being done. Red means request a written intervention plan. That framework holds for every meeting.

But February is the first meeting where the board can ask a follow-up question that matters: "What's the story behind this data point?"

A math score that's on track is one thing. A math score that's on track because the district implemented a new intervention program in November — and you can see the improvement trajectory starting in November — is a different conversation. The board doesn't need causation at the first data review. But it should start looking for it.

**Question 5: What direction are we giving?**

Every data review should produce a decision. Not a discussion that trails off. A recorded decision.

For each goal, the board should leave this meeting with one of three outcomes:
- **On track:** Acknowledge progress. No further action needed.
- **Off track, plan exists:** Accept the superintendent's intervention plan. Schedule a follow-up in 30 days.
- **Off track, no plan:** Direct the superintendent to return with a written intervention plan at the next meeting. Specify the timeline and the expected outcome.

Record the decisions in the meeting minutes. The next data review starts with a check: did the board follow through on its February direction?

**Where this is already working: Council Bluffs Community School District**

Council Bluffs, Iowa — one of the earliest adopters of the ESB framework — models the transition from symbolic check-in to full data review better than any district I've worked with. Here's what their February board meeting looks like.

In January, the Council Bluffs board conducted its first data check-in of the year. The dashboard showed baseline data for three adopted goals. The board spent fifteen minutes reviewing it. The superintendent briefed. The board asked questions. The board recorded its first data-review outcome.

In February, that same board runs a structured twenty-minute review of the same metrics — but now the dashboard shows actual data progression, not just baselines. Mid-year reading assessment results are in. Attendance trends for the first semester are calculated. Math intervention enrollment numbers are updated.

The board doesn't change the format. It still uses the printed one-page scorecard — baseline, current, target, RAG status. What changes is the quality of the conversation. Instead of "Here's our starting point," the discussion becomes "Here's our trajectory." The board can see whether each goal metric is moving toward the target, staying flat, or falling behind.

What makes Council Bluffs worth studying isn't their dashboard technology — it's a printed sheet of paper. It's the fact that they conduct this structured review at every single board meeting, not every quarter. ESB coaching data shows roughly 40% higher goal attainment in districts that review progress at every meeting compared to those that review quarterly, despite the same total annual meeting time spent on data.

Your board started with the same approach in January. The paper scorecard. The thirty-minute meeting segment. The recorded outcome per goal. By February, you're running the same practice that Council Bluffs established years ago. The only difference is time. Keep the rhythm, and the results follow.

**The February meeting structure**

Here's what your February board meeting data review looks like on the agenda:

| Time | Activity | Outcome |
|------|----------|---------|
| 5 min | Board members review dashboard (pre-read, done before meeting) | Board arrives informed |
| 10 min | Superintendent briefing — what's changed since January | Board hears context behind the numbers |
| 10 min | Board questions — five questions above | Board understands the data |
| 5 min | Decisions recorded — one outcome per goal | Board leaves with clear direction |

Thirty minutes. Five minutes of prep. One recorded outcome per goal. That's the February data review.

**What happens if you skip this meeting**

I've watched boards that postponed the first real data review from February to March. The reason was always the same: "We don't have enough data yet to make it worthwhile."

Here's what actually happened. By March, the budget conversation consumed every meeting. The data review got compressed into the superintendent's report. The superintendent said "everything is on track" — which meant nobody had looked hard enough to find what was off track. By April, the board realized it had lost two months of monitoring time. By June, the goals the board adopted in January were the goals the board was still hoping to achieve — with no data to show whether they were making progress.

The February data review isn't a meeting. It's a commitment. The board that holds it is a board that governs with data. The board that postpones it is a board that governs with hope.

Don't postpone. Review the data. Ask the questions. Give the direction. Record the decisions.

The cadence you established in January becomes the practice you sustain in February. And that practice is what produces the results you'll measure in May and June.

Not by hoping. By reviewing.
