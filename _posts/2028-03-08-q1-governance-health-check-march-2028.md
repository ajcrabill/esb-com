**The Q1 Governance Health Check — Did Your Governance Framework Produce Better Budget Decisions?**

March 8, 2028

---

Last week I showed you the March budget alignment review — the two-question check that connects the February crosswalk to the March Progress Monitoring data and confirms the budget is ready for the April adoption vote.

That review answers a question about the budget.

This week I am asking a different question — one about the board itself.

The Q1 Governance Health Check is the board's first self-assessment of the 2028 governance cycle. It answers a single question: *Did our governance framework produce better budget decisions than we would have made without it?*

This is not a rhetorical question. It is an empirical one. The evidence exists in every document your board produced between January and March. The question is whether your board will look at that evidence or move on to the next item on the agenda.

Let me show you what the Health Check looks like and why boards that skip it are guessing whether their governance framework is working.

---

**Why Q1 matters more than the other quarters**

The first quarter of the governance year is the most consequential because it is the only quarter where the board's framework decisions are tested against real operational outcomes.

In January, the board adopted goals from baseline data. In February, the board evaluated the preliminary budget against those goals. In March, the board checked alignment and reviewed Progress Monitoring data. Every one of those actions was a prediction: *If we adopt these goals, set these guardrails, and allocate resources accordingly, student outcomes will improve.*

Q1 is where that prediction meets evidence. The Health Check is where the board asks whether the prediction held.

Here is why most boards never ask this question.

Most boards do not have a governance framework to evaluate. They have a meeting calendar with dates and times but no governance outcomes named. They adopt a budget in spring, listen to a data presentation in fall, and set goals in January — but they never connect those actions into a cycle where each one is designed to inform the next.

Your board is not most boards. Your board has been executing a connected governance cycle since January. That means your board has something to evaluate. And that evaluation is exactly what the Health Check provides.

---

**The three-question Health Check protocol**

The Q1 Governance Health Check is a structured conversation that takes thirty minutes on the agenda of the March board meeting. The board president facilitates. Every board member participates. The superintendent observes and provides input when asked.

Here are the three questions and the specific evidence each one requires.

**Question one: Did our January budget direction document change the February preliminary budget?**

This question tests whether the board's budget direction was meaningful.

The evidence is the January budget direction document itself — the written direction the board gave the superintendent naming the goals to fund, the guardrails to respect, and the community priorities to reflect. Compare that document to the preliminary budget the superintendent presented in February.

If the preliminary budget allocations for the priority goals are higher than the baseline year, the direction worked. If the guardrails the board named were respected in the preliminary budget, the direction worked. If the community priorities the board communicated appeared in the budget allocations, the direction worked.

If the preliminary budget looks the same as last year's budget with a different date on the cover page, the direction did not work — and the board needs to understand why.

**Question two: Did the crosswalk reveal a misalignment the board would not have otherwise seen?**

This question tests whether the crosswalk tool is valuable.

The evidence is the crosswalk from February and the board's written findings. Before the crosswalk, the board had the budget organized by function and object — instruction, administration, operations, transportation. Every board has that. After the crosswalk, the board had the budget organized by goal — a one-page matrix showing exactly what each adopted goal received.

If the crosswalk revealed that a priority goal was underfunded relative to the board's stated priority level, the crosswalk was valuable. If the crosswalk showed that a guardrail was being exceeded in a specific allocation the board would not have caught in the standard budget format, the crosswalk was valuable.

If the crosswalk confirmed what the board already knew and produced no new insight, the board should ask whether the goals are specific enough to drive resource allocation decisions — or whether the crosswalk needs to be built at a more granular level next cycle.

**Question three: Did the community engagement produce input that changed any budget allocation?**

This question tests whether the board's community engagement was genuine.

The evidence is the community input summary from the February engagement and the budget revisions the superintendent made between February and March. If the community prioritized something the board had not emphasized in its budget direction — and the superintendent adjusted the allocation in response — the engagement was genuine. If the community raised a concern the board had not considered — and the board communicated that concern to the superintendent, and the superintendent addressed it in the revised budget — the engagement was productive.

If the public hearing produced no input that affected the budget, the board should ask whether the engagement format was designed to gather meaningful input or to check a compliance box.

---

**What the board does with the answers**

The Health Check produces one of three outcomes.

**Outcome one: The governance framework is functioning.** The January direction changed the budget. The crosswalk revealed new insight. The community engagement produced actionable input. The board records this finding, confirms the framework is operating as designed, and continues executing the Q2 governance calendar.

**Outcome two: The governance framework has gaps that need correction.** One or two of the three questions produced a negative answer. The board names the gap, assigns responsibility for addressing it, and sets a timeline for correction before Q2's Health Check. The gap could be a budget direction that was too vague, a crosswalk built at too high a level, or a community engagement format that did not invite genuine input.

**Outcome three: The governance framework is not functioning.** All three questions produced negative answers. The board needs a more fundamental conversation — potentially with a facilitated work session — about whether the framework is designed correctly, whether the board is executing it faithfully, or both.

I have watched boards in **Lawrence (KS)** reach Outcome One in their first Q1 Health Check and use the finding as the foundation for the rest of the governance year. The board president reads the finding at the start of every subsequent Health Check — Q2, Q3, Q4 — and the board can see whether the framework continues to function or whether it has drifted.

I have also watched boards reach Outcome Two and treat it as a failure. It is not a failure. A gap identified in March is a gap the board has nine months to fix before the next budget cycle. A gap identified in April — or never identified at all — is a gap that will repeat.

---

**The Q2 governance calendar check-in**

The Health Check is also the right moment for the board to review the Q2 governance calendar.

The board adopted a governance calendar in January that named what every meeting through June is designed to produce. By March, some of that calendar has been executed. Some of it may need adjustment based on what the board learned in Q1.

Here are the items the board should confirm are on the Q2 calendar.

**The April budget adoption.** The public hearing date, the adoption vote date, and the three findings the board will present.

**The May Progress Monitoring check-in.** The second Progress Monitoring check-in of the spring. The board should confirm the date, the data format, and the decision protocol.

**The June committee reports and superintendent evaluation kickoff.** The board self-evaluation and the superintendent evaluation cycle begin in late spring. The Q2 calendar should include the evaluation timeline, the committee report schedule, and the board's year-end governance report to the community.

**The spring community forum or town hall.** If the board committed to a spring community engagement event beyond the budget hearing, the date and format should be confirmed in March.

A board that reviews the Q2 calendar in March enters the spring execution phase with clarity. A board that does not review the calendar drifts into April without knowing whether the month's governance actions are scheduled, resourced, and assigned.
