**From Summer Plan to Superintendent Evaluation — What the Board Does in June**

June 2, 2027

---

Last week's piece laid out the summer governance plan — the reading list, the training schedule, the policy development priorities, and the fall calendar draft. If you haven't read it, go back and start there. That piece is the framework. This piece is the first execution step.

The summer governance plan covers June, July, and August. But June is different from July and August. June is the transition month. The school year is still running. The superintendent formal evaluation happens in June. The board's work from January through May — the goal adoption, the data reviews, the budget alignment, the community engagement — all of it converges into the superintendent evaluation as the closing governance event of the spring cycle.

After the evaluation, the board turns to the summer work. But the board can't have an effective summer if the evaluation isn't done well, and the board can't have an effective evaluation if the summer isn't planned.

Here's how June works.

**The superintendent evaluation is the June governance centerpiece**

The superintendent evaluation that happens in June is not a separate event from the governance year. It is the board's final accountability action of the spring cycle. The goals the board adopted in January — the evaluation measures the superintendent's progress toward them. The data reviews the board conducted in February, March, and May — the evaluation uses those findings. The budget alignment decisions the board made in February through April — the evaluation assesses whether the superintendent executed the board's direction.

When the evaluation is disconnected from the governance cycle, it becomes what too many evaluations already are: a personality assessment based on loose impressions and vague recollections. When the evaluation is connected to the governance cycle, it becomes a framework-driven assessment based on documented evidence, recorded decisions, and objective outcomes.

The board spent five months building that framework. June is the month to use it.

**What the June evaluation requires**

The superintendent evaluation has three phases, and each phase needs to happen on a specific timeline.

**Phase 1 — Board preparation.** Each board member completes a written evaluation. The evaluation should be based on the evidence the board has been collecting since the February check-in — the growth areas identified, the progress observed, the data review findings. The written evaluation should reference specific outcomes, specific data points, and specific board expectations from the February check-in and May midpoint. Vague impressions are not evidence.

This phase needs to happen in the two weeks before the evaluation meeting. If the board waits until the night before, the evaluation will be based on whatever the board member remembers — and memory is not a reliable instrument for superintendent evaluation.

**Phase 2 — Board discussion.** The board discusses the written evaluations together without the superintendent present. The purpose is to identify areas of consensus, areas of disagreement, and the overall assessment direction. The board president or evaluation committee chair consolidates the individual evaluations into a single board evaluation document.

This phase is where the governance framework matters most. A board that has been reviewing data together, making recorded decisions together, and conducting check-ins together will have far more alignment in this discussion than a board that has been governing in silos. The evaluation discussion reveals the quality of the board's governance discipline.

**Phase 3 — Joint evaluation meeting.** The board and superintendent meet to review the completed evaluation. The board president presents the evaluation, the superintendent responds, and the board and superintendent agree on next steps — growth areas for the next evaluation cycle, support commitments from the board, and the timeline for the first check-in of the next governance year.

**How the summer work fits in**

The superintendent evaluation is the closing event of the spring governance cycle. After it, the board enters the summer preparation period.

The summer governance plan from last week's piece identifies four domains: reading, training, policy development, and next-cycle planning. Each domain has specific work the board can do between June and August. But the board needs to agree on the summer work before the board disperses for the summer break. That means the June board meeting or June work session needs to include not just the evaluation, but also the summer work plan approval.

Here's a sample June board meeting agenda that integrates both:

| Time | Section | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 15 min | Summer work plan review | Board reviews the summer governance plan drafted after the May 26 planning discussion. Approves reading list, training commitments, policy development priorities, and fall calendar preparation timeline. |
| 15 min | Evaluation preparation review | Board confirms each member has received the evaluation instrument and understands the timeline for submitting written evaluations. Confirms the evaluation discussion meeting and joint meeting dates. |
| 30 min | Board discussion (executive session if required) | Board discusses superintendent evaluation — areas of consensus, areas needing further discussion. Evaluation committee consolidates individual evaluations. |
| 60 min or separate meeting | Joint evaluation meeting | Board and superintendent review the completed evaluation, agree on growth areas and support commitments, set first check-in date for the fall. |

**What happens after the June meeting**

After the June evaluation, the board's governance work enters a different rhythm. The school year ends. The operational pace slows. The board has two and a half months — July, most of August — to complete the summer work plan before the fall governance cycle begins.

The board that treats June as the end of governance work is a board that has already lost the summer. The board that treats June as the transition — closing the spring cycle with a thorough evaluation and opening the summer preparation period with an approved work plan — is a board that enters the fall running.
