**The January Meeting That Sets the Tone for All 12 — Make Your Organizational Meeting Strategic, Not Procedural**

January 4, 2027

---

I've sat through over a hundred January organizational meetings. And I can tell you with confidence: most of them are a waste of a meeting.

Not legally. No one violates the Open Meetings Act during an organizational meeting. The problem is strategic. The organizational meeting is treated as procedural filler — elect officers, appoint committees, approve the meeting calendar, and get to the "real" agenda. 

The problem with that approach is that the organizational meeting *is* the real agenda. The choices made in that meeting — who leads the board, which committees get formed, what the meeting calendar looks like — determine the governance trajectory for the entire year.

The board that treats its organizational meeting as a thirty-minute procedural warm-up is a board that will spend the rest of the year being busy without being effective. The board that treats it as a strategic session — with intentional decisions about leadership, structure, and calendar — is a board that enters January governing instead of reacting.

Here are the three strategic decisions your organizational meeting should produce.

**1. Align officers with governance priorities**

Most boards elect officers the same way every year: someone nominates the current vice president, someone second, and the vote is unanimous. The board president becomes the person who waited their turn.

I'm not opposed to succession planning. But the question shouldn't be "who's next in line." The question should be "who is best positioned to lead this board toward its 2027 goals?"

The board president is not a master of ceremonies. They're the person responsible for keeping the board focused on its priorities — at every meeting, during every agenda item, in every conversation with the superintendent. The board president sets the tone for how the board works together. If the board's goals require difficult conversations about resource reallocation or performance accountability, the board president needs to be someone who can lead those conversations.

This means the officer election should include a conversation about what the board is trying to accomplish in 2027 and what leadership behaviors that requires. Not a formal job interview. A fifteen-minute discussion: "Here's what we're trying to achieve this year. Who on this board is best equipped to lead us there?"

**2. Build a committee structure that serves the goals, not the other way around**

Most boards have committees because they've always had committees. A curriculum committee because every board has a curriculum committee. A finance committee because every board has a finance committee. A facilities committee because someone needs to talk about the roof.

Here's the question I ask every board during January: what committees do you actually need to achieve your 2027 goals?

If your board's primary goal is increasing third-grade reading proficiency, you need a committee that owns that goal — not a curriculum committee that talks about curriculum broadly, but a committee tasked with monitoring reading progress, reviewing intervention programs, and reporting back to the full board.

If your board's goal is closing the math achievement gap, you need a committee that owns that specific work.

If a committee's scope of work doesn't connect directly to a board-adopted goal, that committee is consuming governance energy without producing governance results. Either give it a goal-connected purpose, or disband it and redistribute the work.

I've seen boards eliminate half their committees in one January meeting. The result: shorter agendas, better-focused discussions, and committee chairs who actually know what they're supposed to be accomplishing.

**3. Lock in a governance calendar that names what every meeting is designed to produce**

The third decision is the simplest and the most consequential: when will your board's five most important governance actions happen in 2027?

Not "the third Tuesday of every month." Specific dates for:

- Goal adoption
- First data review
- Budget alignment review
- Mid-year governance check-in
- Superintendent evaluation

Lock them in during the organizational meeting. Put them on the calendar. Treat them as non-negotiable — the same way you treat the budget submission deadline or the state reporting deadline.

If you don't decide in January what your board will produce in each month of 2027, you'll spend the year reacting instead of governing. The calendar will fill itself with routine items, and the strategic work will get pushed to "next month" until next month becomes June and you're scrambling to complete the superintendent evaluation.

---

Your board has already done the hard part. December's work gave you a January agenda, a set of draft goals, a first-ninety-days plan, and an understanding of why January meetings fail. The December arc ended with the question: will you execute or will you react?

The January organizational meeting is your first answer. Three decisions — aligned officers, goal-connected committees, a locked-in governance calendar — and you walk out of January knowing that the board is structured for the year ahead.

The board that does this work in one January meeting has accomplished more than most boards accomplish in a quarter.

Your choice: thirty minutes of procedure, or ninety minutes of strategic direction. The rest of your year depends on it.
