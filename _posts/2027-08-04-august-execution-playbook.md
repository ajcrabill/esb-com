**The Four Weeks That Separate Prepared Boards from Reactive Boards**

August 4, 2027

---

The July 28 piece gave you the destination. Five deliverables. September readiness. A board that enters the fall with a completed reading discussion, documented training outcomes, policy drafts ready for first reading, a confirmed fall calendar, and a goal-setting data packet.

If you have all five done, you do not need this piece. But if you are like most boards I work with at the beginning of August, you have some of the five. Maybe three of the five. Maybe two. Maybe you have the intention and not much else.

That is what this piece is for.

The four weeks between August 1 and September 1 are the board's last opportunity to turn summer planning into fall readiness. Every week matters. Every board member matters. And the difference between a board that enters September prepared and a board that enters September reactive is not in how much summer work the board planned. It is in how the board used the four weeks it had left.

Here is the week-by-week playbook.

**Week 1 — The diagnostic week**

The first week of August is not about doing the work. It is about knowing where the work stands.

The board president starts the week by reviewing the five deliverables from the July 28 piece against what the board has actually completed. I recommend a simple spreadsheet or a shared document with five rows — one for each deliverable — and three columns: status (complete, partial, not started), owner (the board member responsible), and estimated completion date.

The board president sends the document to every board member with one request: update your row with your honest assessment by Thursday.

Here is what the assessment should look like for each deliverable.

Deliverable 1 — reading discussion and Reading-to-Action Tracker. The reading discussion is either held or not. If it was held, the Tracker should already be distributed. If it was not held, the week it can be scheduled is the question. The first week of August is the best window. Every week after that gets tighter because board members leave for end-of-summer travel.

Deliverable 2 — training with documented practice changes. The board members list which training hours are complete and which are still needed. If the governance-specific training identified from the closeout has not been scheduled, the board president should identify available virtual sessions and circulate three options by Friday.

Deliverable 3 — policy drafts ready for September first reading. The policy committee or assigned board member gives a word count and a draft date estimate. Is the draft written? Circulated? Reviewed? Ready for the policy discussion session? The board president needs to know whether the policy work is on track for the September first reading or whether it needs an August special meeting.

Deliverable 4 — fall calendar confirmed. The board president and superintendent should have already had this conversation, but if they have not, it becomes the top priority for the first week. A calendar that is not finalized by mid-August will produce September scrambling.

Deliverable 5 — goal-setting data packet requested. Has the superintendent received the request? Does the superintendent know the data sources the board expects? Has the board president confirmed the delivery date? If not, the request needs to go out this week.

The board president compiles the responses by Friday. The president now has a map of what is complete, what is on track, and what is at risk. The rest of August is about closing the gaps.

**Week 2 — The gap-closing week**

The second week of August is where the board moves from assessment to action.

The board president looks at the diagnostic from Week 1 and identifies the gaps that need the most attention. Not all gaps are equal. If the reading discussion is not scheduled, that gap closes in a single ninety-minute meeting — schedule it for Week 2 or Week 3. If the policy drafts are not started, that gap requires multiple weeks of writing, review, and discussion. The board president needs to create a mini-project plan for each at-risk deliverable.

Here is what each board member should do in Week 2.

If your reading is not complete, finish it. If your training hours are not met, register for a session. If a policy draft is assigned to you, send the board president a rough outline by Friday — not the final draft, just the structure of what you plan to write.

If you are the board president, you have two specific tasks in Week 2.

First, schedule the mid-August board president-superintendent meeting. The five agenda items from the July 28 piece — reading outcomes, training outcomes, policy draft review, calendar confirmation, goal-setting data packet — need a date. I recommend a ninety-minute virtual meeting in the second half of Week 2 or the first half of Week 3.

Second, confirm the policy discussion session date. If the policy drafts are ready for review, schedule a board work session in Week 3 for the full board to discuss the substance. If the drafts are not ready, the board president needs to know before the end of Week 2 so the September first-reading timeline can be adjusted.

**Week 3 — The alignment week**

The third week of August is when the leadership structure of the board and superintendent comes together.

The board president and superintendent hold their mid-August meeting. By the end of that meeting, five things should be confirmed in writing.

The reading discussion outcomes and Reading-to-Action Tracker assignments are confirmed between the president and superintendent. The superintendent knows what the board committed to change based on its reading.

The training-to-practice changes are confirmed. The superintendent knows what new expectations the training has created for the way the board and superintendent interact.

The policy draft review schedule is confirmed. The superintendent knows which policies are coming for first reading in September and what the changes mean for district operations.

The fall calendar is confirmed. The president and superintendent have reviewed every date from September through December and confirmed there are no conflicts with major district events.

The goal-setting data packet delivery date is confirmed. The superintendent knows what data the board needs and when the data will be delivered. The board president knows what to expect and what format it will be in.

The board president documents the meeting outcomes and shares them with the full board by the end of Week 3.

If the board is holding a policy discussion session this week, the session follows the structure from the July 21 piece. Each draft is reviewed for substance — does it address the gap the closeout identified? If yes, the draft moves to first reading. If not, the assigned board member revises and brings it back.

**Week 4 — The readiness confirmation week**

The last week of August is not for starting new work. It is for confirming that the work is complete.

The board president sends a final check-in to each board member. The question is simple: on a scale of one to ten, how prepared do you feel for the September board meeting? The answer tells the president more than any checklist.

The board president reviews the diagnostic document from Week 1 and updates each deliverable to its final August status. The board is either ready for each deliverable or has a documented plan for what happens in September if it is not.

If the reading discussion is complete and the Tracker is distributed — deliverable 1 is green. If the training-to-practice changes are documented and the training certificates are logged — deliverable 2 is green. If the policy drafts are ready for first reading in September — deliverable 3 is green. If the fall calendar dates are confirmed and shared — deliverable 4 is green. If the goal-setting data packet is requested and the delivery date is confirmed — deliverable 5 is green.

The board president shares the final readiness status with the full board and the superintendent.

**What to do when a deliverable is at risk**

August is four weeks. Sometimes four weeks is not enough.

If a deliverable is at risk by Week 3, the board president has two options.

Option one is to complete the deliverable incompletely. The reading discussion happens but the Reading-to-Action Tracker is not fully populated. The training is completed but the training-to-practice changes are not documented. The policy draft is written but the full board has not reviewed it. The fall calendar is confirmed for the first three months but December is still open. The goal-setting data is requested but the superintendent needs two more weeks to produce it.

Incomplete is not failure. It is a managed gap. The board enters September knowing exactly what is unfinished and when it will be finished. The September board meeting agenda includes the unfinished items as first action items.

Option two is to adjust the fall timeline. If the policy drafts are truly not ready for September first reading, the board moves first reading to October and adjusts the adoption schedule. If the data packet cannot be produced by September, the board pushes the goal-setting work session from October to November and uses the October data review as an interim check-in.

The board that adjusts its timeline deliberately is prepared. The board that discovers in September that it is not ready and has no plan for what to do about it is reactive. The difference is whether the board looked at the August gaps and made a decision.

**What August actually determines**

The May through July planning cycle was about setting direction. The August execution month is about closing the distance between what the board planned and what the board has actually done.

The board that uses August deliberately does not enter September guessing. It enters September knowing which deliverables are complete, which gaps remain, and exactly when each gap will be closed. The board that uses August reactively enters September hoping that the summer work somehow ended up done — and hoping is not a governance strategy.

The question from June 23 — what will the board have ready by September — is answered not in what the board planned to do in May or what the board started in July. It is answered in what the board completed by September 1. And whether the board completes it depends entirely on how the board uses the four weeks it has left.
