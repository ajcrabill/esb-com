**October to December: Your Board's Fall Governance Execution Plan**

October 5, 2027

---

If you worked through the late-September arc, take stock of what your board has accomplished in fifteen days.

You set the October data review format. You confirmed the data packet deadline with your superintendent. You established the board president's facilitation role for the first real governance event of the new year.

You recognized that the board-superintendent relationship shifts between October and December — and you put the "no surprises" rule in place before the relationship needed it, not after it frayed.

You evaluated your board's own governance effectiveness before the October-December governance season began — not in June as a compliance exercise, but in September as a diagnostic tool that gives you three months to improve.

And if your board had not adopted annual goals by October 1, you started the baseline-gathering process that gives you real data for January adoption.

That is more governance preparation in two weeks than most boards do in the entire fall semester.

But September is the preparation. October through December is the execution. And execution is where governance is decided — not by what a board planned in September, but by what the board did when the data arrived, when the budget pressure built, and when the calendar demanded decisions.

Here is your board's fall governance execution plan, month by month.

---

**October: First Full Data Review + Community Progress Report**

October is where September's preparation meets October's reality.

Your board's first full Progress Monitoring review happens this month. The format is set. The data packet deadline is confirmed. The board president knows how to facilitate the three-decision protocol — accept trajectory, direct change, or request information.

The question for October is not whether the board holds the data review. The question is what the board decides when the data reveals something uncomfortable.

Every board's first data review of the governance year produces at least one goal that is off track. That is not a governance failure. It is the purpose of Progress Monitoring in the first place — to surface problems early, when the board has time to direct a change, rather than discovering them in April when the options are limited.

I have watched boards in districts like **Wichita (KS)** handle this well. Their October data review produces a public "goal trajectory report" that shows each goal's current status — on track, watch, or off track — alongside the board's decision on each one. The report is published alongside the meeting minutes. The community sees what the board sees.

Your board's October data review should produce three things:

1. A written decision for each goal — accept trajectory, direct change, or request more information
2. A community-facing summary that translates the data into what the community needs to know
3. A timeline for any directed changes, with a check-in date on the November calendar

The board that walks out of October with written decisions, a community report, and a November follow-up plan has governed. The board that walks out of October having "received the report" has held a meeting.

October is also the month for your fall community progress report. The September self-evaluation told you how your board is functioning. The October data review tells you how the district is performing against the goals your board adopted. Both should be shared with the community — not as a press release, but as a formal report at a board meeting with time for community questions.

The message: *"Here are the goals we adopted. Here is the data we reviewed this month. Here is what we decided. Here is what we are watching."*

A board that communicates its Progress Monitoring results publicly is a board that governs transparently. A board that communicates nothing publicly is a board that treats governance as a closed-door process.

---

**November: Goal-Setting Groundwork + Fall Progress Check**

November is the month where two parallel tracks converge.

**Track one: For boards with adopted goals.**

Your November meeting is the first full check-in after October's data review. The board reviews whether the directed changes from October are producing results. The superintendent reports on implementation. The board confirms the trajectory or adjusts.

But November also serves a second purpose. It is the last full board meeting before the December year-end governance reset. That means November is the month to surface any governance issues that need attention before the board evaluates its annual performance.

The question for November: *What does this board need to resolve before the December reset?*

If the board-superintendent partnership has shown strain since October's budget pressure built, November is the time to address it — not December, when the board is trying to close the year and preview the next one.

If the board's self-evaluation in September identified improvement priorities that have not been addressed, November is the time to ask why.

I have seen boards in **Hazelwood (MO)** use November effectively. They schedule a forty-five-minute "governance health check" at every November meeting — a structured review of board functioning, meeting effectiveness, and partnership quality that takes the temperature before the governance year closes. The health check is not a formal evaluation. It is a fifteen-minute board discussion that prevents small issues from becoming December problems.

**Track two: For boards without adopted goals.**

Your baseline-gathering quarter is nearly complete. October produced your first full data review. November is the month to draft your goals for December review and January adoption.

The draft goals should come from the data you gathered in October. Not from what the board assumed in August. Not from what the board wished were true. From the actual baseline data that October's Progress Monitoring produced.

The superintendent should present a draft goal set to the board by the November meeting — with baseline data, proposed targets, and the timeline for community input before final adoption in January.

If your board is on track to adopt goals by the end of January, you are ahead of nearly 40 percent of boards. Do not rush the process. Do not skip the baseline-gathering quarter. A January goal with real data behind it is worth more than an August goal with good intentions.

---

**December: Year-End Governance Reset + 2028 Goal Preview**

December closes the governance cycle and opens the next one.

Here is the most important thing I can tell you about December. Do not let the board go dark.

A board that finishes December with a year-end governance reset enters January with momentum. A board that holds its last meeting, publishes the December minutes, and does not meet again until the third week of January will spend the first month of the new year catching up to where it should have been in December.

The December board meeting — or a dedicated December work session — should accomplish five things.

**One: Close the 2027 governance cycle.**

The board reviews its annual performance against the commitments it made in January. Did the board achieve what it set out to achieve? Did the board govern at the level it committed to during the January strategic organizational meeting? The September self-evaluation was a diagnostic. The December year-end review is the closing statement.

Use the self-evaluation results from the full governance year — five domains across twelve months — to produce a one-page year-end governance summary. What improved. What did not. What carries into 2028.

**Two: Preview the 2028 goal-setting process.**

What data will the board need this winter to prepare for spring goal-setting? What timeline makes sense for community input? What should the superintendent start preparing now?

The board that answers these questions in December does not enter February wondering what to do next. The board that waits until January to start asking them has already lost January.

**Three: Set the January strategic organizational meeting agenda.**

The January organizational meeting is not a ceremony. It is the governance launch of the new year. December is the month to decide what that meeting will produce — which officers, which committees, which calendar commitments, which governance priorities for the first quarter of 2028.

A board that plans its January organizational meeting in December walks into January ready to govern. A board that plans its organizational meeting in the hallway before the January meeting walks into January having already wasted its first governance opportunity of the year.

**Four: Begin the superintendent evaluation cycle.**

The evaluation cycle that closes in June starts in December. The board's evaluation committee should determine the evidence-gathering process, establish the timeline, and communicate the evaluation framework to the superintendent before the new year.

The board that starts the evaluation process in December is prepared. The board that starts the evaluation process in April is rushed. The board that starts in May is reactive.

Fifteen years of coaching boards has shown me the same pattern every time. The boards that produce meaningful superintendent evaluations are the boards that start the evaluation conversation before the calendar demands it.

**Five: Publish a year-end governance report to the community.**

What did the board accomplish in 2027? What did the district accomplish? What are the board's priorities for 2028?

A year-end report is not a press release. It is a governance document that tells the community what the board did with its authority and responsibility for the year. Districts like **Council Bluffs (IA)** publish a one-page year-end governance summary alongside the district's annual report. The document names the goals, the progress data, the board decisions, and the 2028 priorities.

Your board can do the same thing.

---

**The October-December cycle in perspective**

Here is what three months of focused fall governance execution produces:

| Month | Governance Action | Connection to January Launch |
|-------|------------------|------------------------------|
| **October** | First full data review + community progress report | September's preparation meets execution reality; every goal gets a written decision |
| **November** | Fall progress check + goal-setting groundwork | Boards with goals resolve issues before year-end; boards without goals draft from real baseline data |
| **December** | Year-end governance reset + 2028 goal preview | The cycle closes and the next one opens — organizational meeting plan, evaluation process, community report |

The difference between a board that governs through the fall and a board that drifts through the fall is the difference between executing this plan and letting the calendar decide what happens next.

September gave you the foundation. October through December is where you build on it.
