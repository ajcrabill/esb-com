**Don't Wait for February to Look at Data**

January 13, 2027

---

I hear the same thing from boards every January: "We're going to start looking at data at our February meeting."

And every year, I ask the same question: why wait?

The answer is always some version of the same thing. January is busy. January is reorganization. January is the first meeting of the year and we have a lot to cover. We'll establish the data cadence in February.

I understand the logic. I just know from experience that it doesn't work.

The board that says "we'll start in February" almost never starts in February. Something else comes up. The agenda is full. The data isn't ready. February becomes March. March becomes "we'll pick it up in the fall."

The pattern is so consistent I can predict it with near certainty.

But the board that starts its data cadence in January — the first meeting, the first week, the first opportunity — is the board that sustains it for the entire year.

## Why January Is the Right Time to Start

There are three reasons January is the optimal time to establish your data cadence, and none of them are about data.

### Reason 1: January Is When Goals Are Fresh

You just set your 2027 goals. Your board knows what it's trying to achieve. The metrics are defined. The targets are set.

If you don't review data in January, you're missing the natural alignment between goal-setting and baseline measurement. The whole point of setting goals is to know whether you're making progress. You can't know that if you don't establish where you're starting.

January is the month to establish the baseline. Not February. Not March. January. The month your goals were set is the month your data cadence should begin.

### Reason 2: January Sets the Expectation

When your board reviews data at the first meeting of the year, you send a signal to everyone — the superintendent, the staff, the community, yourself — that data is a priority. It's not something you'll get to when things calm down. It's part of how you govern.

The superintendent sees it. The superintendent begins preparing data for every meeting. The community sees it. The community begins to expect progress updates. The board sees it. The board begins to understand that data is part of every governance conversation, not a special event.

That expectation is set in January. If you skip January, the expectation is that data can be skipped.

### Reason 3: January Gives You Feedback Before February

Here's the practical argument. Your board sets goals in January. You review baseline data. You ask questions. The superintendent may discover that the data you want isn't readily available. Or the dashboard format doesn't work. Or you realize you're measuring the wrong thing.

If you discover those issues in January, you have time to fix them before the February meeting. If you wait until February to set up your data cadence, the earliest you'll have a functional system is March — and you've already missed two months of progress monitoring.

## What a January Data Cadence Looks Like

A January data cadence doesn't need to be elaborate. Here's what it looks like in practice.

**Step 1: Confirm your three to five metrics.** These should be the metrics that map directly to your 2027 goals. Third-grade reading proficiency. Chronic absenteeism. Math proficiency. Graduation rate. Whatever your board identified as the measures that tell you whether students are better off.

**Step 2: Request baseline data from the superintendent.** For each metric, you need the most recent available data point. If the 2026-27 school year has mid-year data, use it. If not, use end-of-year 2025-26 data as your baseline. The point is to establish a starting point, not to achieve perfect data.

**Step 3: Design a one-page dashboard.** The dashboard should show: the metric name, the current data point, the target for 2027, the trend over time (at least three data points), and a simple visual indicator — green (on track), yellow (needs attention), red (off track).

**Step 4: Add a standing data review item to every meeting agenda.** This is the critical step. The data review has a permanent position on the agenda. It doesn't move. It doesn't get pushed for time. It's on the agenda, in the same position, at every meeting.

**Step 5: Review data at the January meeting.** The superintendent presents the dashboard. The board asks questions. The board identifies any early warning signs. The board determines whether any course correction is needed. The whole process takes fifteen minutes.

## The Cadence You Build in January

The board that reviews data at the January meeting is a board on a trajectory. That trajectory is not complicated.

**January:** Baseline review. Confirm metrics. Confirm dashboard format.

**February:** First progress check. Is there movement from baseline? Any early signals?

**March:** Second check. Enough data points to see a direction. Any course corrections needed?

**April, May, June:** Ongoing checks. The board knows exactly where it stands heading into the end-of-year review.

That's the cadence. Established in January. Sustained through the year.

And it starts with not waiting for February.

## What If Your Data Isn't Ready?

I anticipate the objection: "AJ, our mid-year data won't be available until February. We can't review data in January if we don't have data to review."

Fair point. Here's how to handle it.

Use whatever data you have. End-of-year 2025-26 data. The most recent interim assessment results. Attendance data from the first semester. Discipline referral counts. Graduation cohort tracking.

The purpose of the January data review is not to have perfect data. It's to establish the habit of reviewing data at every board meeting. The habit is more important than the data point. You can refine the data as better information becomes available.

If you wait for perfect data, you'll never start. Start with what you have. Refine as you go.

## The Boards That Do This

I watch two kinds of boards every year.

The first kind sets goals in January, decides to "look at data starting in February," and by March has a stack of unread dashboard templates in a shared drive. They mean well. They just never established the cadence.

The second kind sets goals in January, reviews baseline data at the same meeting, adds a standing data item to every subsequent agenda, and by June can show the community exactly how each metric moved from January to June. They don't need to "focus more on data next year." They already do.

The difference isn't resources. It's not data quality. It's not district size.

The difference is whether the board decided to start in January or wait until February.

Don't wait.
