**Your Board's Summer Reading & Training Plan — Books, Workshops, and Conferences That Matter**

May 10, 2027

---

Summers are short. Between the last June board meeting and the first September meeting, board members have roughly ten to twelve weeks — and most of that time is consumed by work, family, and the personal life that gets set aside during the governance year. The board that treats summer professional development as optional arrives in September with the same knowledge base, the same blind spots, and the same governance practices it had in January.

The board that treats summer professional development as a strategic priority arrives in September with specific new understanding, specific training completed, and specific conversations ready to have with the superintendent and the community.

This piece is the framework for building that strategic summer learning plan.

**What the board should read — together**

Individual summer reading is valuable but doesn't change board dynamics. The board that reads the same book — or the same three books — and discusses them as a team creates a shared knowledge base that changes how the board talks about governance in the fall.

I recommend three categories of summer board reading.

**Category one: Governance foundations.** One book that strengthens the board's understanding of the governance model itself. This could be a book about policy governance, board-superintendent relationships, or governance as a leadership practice rather than a compliance function. The goal is not to adopt a specific governance model — it's for every board member to understand the principles behind effective governance so the board can have a common language when it discusses how it operates.

I recommend boards choose one governance foundations book that the board president has read and can facilitate a discussion about. The book should be no more than two hundred pages. The board should commit to a discussion date in August — a ninety-minute virtual work session where every board member comes having read the book and prepared to discuss three questions: what did you learn, what does this mean for our board, and what should we do differently in the fall?

**Category two: A district-specific challenge area.** One book that addresses a specific challenge the district faces. If the board's data reviews revealed a persistent reading proficiency gap, the summer reading should include a book about literacy instruction or early intervention. If the board's budget discussions revealed structural deficit challenges, the summer reading should include a book about school finance or resource allocation in education. If the board's community engagement reflections revealed gaps in reaching specific populations, the summer reading should address community engagement in diverse communities.

The board does not need to read this book collectively — individual board members can divide and conquer. Each board member selects one challenge-area book and prepares a five-minute briefing for the board at the August work session. The board walks into September with shared expertise on three or four challenge areas instead of zero.

**Category three: Leadership and team effectiveness.** One book about teams, decision-making, communication, or organizational effectiveness. Governance is a team sport. Boards that function well do so because board members understand how to operate as a high-functioning team — not because they agree on every issue, but because they have a shared framework for working through disagreement.

This category is the one board members are most likely to skip because it doesn't seem directly relevant to governance. It's the most important category for boards that want to improve how they work together. A board that reads a book about difficult conversations and then practices having difficult conversations is a board that will survive the inevitable conflict that every governance year produces.

**What the board should attend — strategically**

State school boards association conferences are the default summer professional development for most boards. They're valuable for networking, legislative updates, and broad governance topics. But they're not enough.

I recommend boards identify one targeted training opportunity per board member before the summer break. Not the same training for everyone — targeted training based on each board member's role and growth areas. The board president might attend a board president leadership institute. The finance committee chair might attend a school budget training. A newer board member might attend a governance foundations workshop.

The key is intentionality. Each board member returns from their training with a specific takeaway that they share with the full board at the August or September study session. The board's training investment produces a return in the form of shared knowledge, not just individual learning.

**The summer learning calendar**

Here's the calendar I recommend.

**June — Selection and commitment.** The board selects the shared reading, assigns individual readings and training, and commits to the August discussion date. The board president orders the books and sends them to every board member before the end of June.

**July — Reading and training.** Board members read. Board members attend training. The board president sends a mid-July check-in message — not to check that everyone is reading, but to offer a two-sentence preview question that prepares board members for the August discussion.

**August — Discussion and integration.** The board holds the ninety-minute shared reading discussion. Each board member delivers their five-minute challenge-area briefing. Each board member shares their training takeaway. The board president captures the key insights and identifies three specific changes the board will make to its fall governance practices based on summer learning.

**September — Application.** The board reviews the summer learning insights at the first study session and the governance practices document at the first regular meeting. The fall governance year starts with the knowledge and improvement commitments the board built over the summer.
