**Your First Community Progress Report — Why It Matters and How to Build It**

March 8, 2027

---

The first two weeks of March covered the budget alignment review — the verification work that ensures your board's adjusted budget actually funds the goals you adopted. Now it's March 8, and the budget alignment review is complete. The board has documented where the budget stands.

What comes next is work most boards never do, and it's the work that determines whether your community trusts the budget conversation that's coming in April.

I'm talking about the community progress report.

**Why most boards don't publish progress reports**

I've asked boards why they don't publish community progress reports. The answers follow a pattern.

We're still gathering the data.
We don't want to release incomplete information.
We'll do it at the end of the year when we have final results.
The superintendent handles communications.

Every one of these answers reveals a misunderstanding of what a progress report is supposed to do. A progress report isn't a final accountability document. It's an interim transparency document. Its purpose is not to present perfect data. Its purpose is to show the community that the board is doing what it said it would do.

**The trust gap that progress reports close**

Here's the dynamic I see in districts across the country. A board adopts goals in January. The board holds data reviews. The board aligns the budget. The board publishes meeting minutes. The board shares a newsletter. And the community still asks: what is the board actually doing?

The gap isn't a communication problem. It's a visibility problem. The board is doing governance work — goal monitoring, budget alignment, policy oversight — and that work happens in board meetings, work sessions, and committee rooms. The community doesn't see it. All the community sees is the school board meeting agenda and the superintendent's report on the district's operations.

A community progress report closes that gap by translating the board's governance work into something the community can see. Not a list of meetings attended. A summary of decisions made and what they mean for students.

**What a community progress report should contain**

I recommend four sections in your board's first community progress report. The full report should fit on two pages.

**Section 1: Goal status at a glance**

A table with your board-adopted goals, the current data point, the trajectory toward the target, and a status indicator.

| Goal | Current Data | Trajectory | Status |
|------|-------------|--------|--------|
| Grade 3 Reading Proficiency | 44% (Feb) | 52% target by June | On pace |
| Algebra I Readiness (Grade 8) | 67% (Feb) | 72% target by June | Needs attention |
| College & Career Readiness | 81% (Feb) | 85% target by June | On pace |

The status column uses the three categories from the progress monitoring framework: on pace, needs attention, or off track. The community doesn't need the full data analysis. It needs to know whether the board's goals are moving in the right direction.

**Section 2: Budget alignment summary**

A one-paragraph summary of the budget alignment work, followed by the crosswalk percentages if the board chose to share them.

"The board completed its February budget review and March budget alignment review. After direction from the board, the superintendent adjusted the budget to increase reading intervention funding by 12% and redirected professional development spending toward math intervention training. The board verified that the adjusted budget allocates 82% of goal-directed spending to the three adopted goals. 18% of spending remains in operational and compliance categories that are not directly tied to board goals."

**Section 3: Key board decisions this quarter**

Three to five bullet points naming specific board decisions and their purpose. Not every board vote. The decisions that directly support the goals.

- Adopted three student outcome goals for 2027 (January)
- Approved the first 2027 data review with decisions recorded for each goal (February)
- Directed budget adjustments to increase reading intervention funding and redirect professional development toward goal-aligned training (February)
- Completed the March budget alignment review and confirmed the timeline to April adoption (March)

**Section 4: What's next**

A forward-looking section that tells the community what the board will do in the next quarter.

"April — budget public hearing and adoption. Mid-year governance check-in. May — second full data review. June — superintendent evaluation and year-end goal progress update. The board invites the community to attend the budget public hearing on [date] and will publish the proposed budget for public review two weeks before the hearing."

**The one-page version**

If two pages feels like too much, start with one page. The one-page version contains three elements: the goal status table, three bullet points of board decisions, and the "what's next" section. That's it. It takes the district communications director two hours to produce.

I've watched a board publish a one-page progress report for the first time and receive more positive community feedback than it had received in the prior twelve months combined. The reason: the community finally saw what the board was doing.

**How to share the progress report**

The progress report should be published in at least three channels.

1. **Published on the district website.** A dedicated page that the board controls, linked from the board's section of the district website.
2. **Shared at a board meeting.** The board president or a designated board member presents the progress report at the March board meeting, not as a read-aloud but as a two-minute overview with time for community questions.
3. **Sent to the board's email list.** If your board maintains an email list for interested community members, the progress report goes there. If your board doesn't maintain a list, this is the moment to start one.

I recommend against using the superintendent's newsletter as the primary distribution channel. The progress report is the board's document. The board should own the distribution. The superintendent can link to it, but the community needs to see the board communicating directly about the board's work.

**The cost of not publishing**

I've watched a board enter April's budget public hearing without having published any community progress report. The community's questions at the hearing weren't about the budget. They were about the board's priorities. Where did these goals come from? What data supports this spending direction? How do we know the board is making progress?

Every question was reasonable. Every question was answered in the board's January-to-March work. But that work was invisible because the board hadn't published anything showing it.

The board spent the forty-five minutes of the public hearing explaining work it had already done — instead of discussing the budget that was on the table. The hearing that should have built confidence in the budget instead built frustration that the board was asking for approval without having demonstrated progress.

A progress report — two pages, published two weeks before the hearing — would have prevented every minute of that frustration.

Your board has done the work. January's goals, February's data review and budget preparation, March's alignment review. The community doesn't know because nobody has shown them. March is the month to change that.
