**The Public Hearing Is the Board's Event — Here's Your Role, Preparation, and Follow-Through**

April 12, 2027

---

There's a misunderstanding I see in school boards across the country. Boards treat the public hearing as the superintendent's event. The superintendent builds the agenda. The superintendent presents the budget. The superintendent answers the community's questions. The board sits on the dais, listens, and votes.

That's backward. The public hearing is the board's event. It's the single most visible community accountability moment of the spring governance cycle. The superintendent presents. The community speaks. But the board owns the structure, the tone, and the follow-through.

The difference between a board that understands this and a board that doesn't is visible within the first five minutes of the hearing. The board that owns the event communicates preparation, discipline, and respect for the community's time. The board that treats the hearing as a procedural requirement communicates disengagement — and the community notices.

**The three-outcome decision framework for the public hearing**

Every board event should produce one of three outcomes. The public hearing produces a distinct outcome that is not the adoption vote itself.

**Outcome one: The hearing validates the budget's alignment story.** The community's questions confirm what the board's March alignment review already showed — the budget funds the board's adopted goals. Where the community offers input, the input can be acknowledged and directed to the post-adoption monitoring process rather than requiring a budget revision. The board closes the hearing with clear documentation of what was heard and how it will be addressed.

**Outcome two: The hearing surfaces specific concerns that require board action.** The community identifies issues the board had not fully considered — a program reduction with unanticipated community impact, a revenue assumption that the community questions, a staffing change that affects service delivery. The board acknowledges each concern, commits to reviewing it in the post-adoption monitoring cycle, and ensures the concern is documented in the hearing record. The board's adoption motion may include conditions that address the concern.

**Outcome three: The hearing reveals material misalignment that delays adoption.** Community testimony, combined with the board's own analysis, reveals that the budget as presented does not adequately fund one or more adopted goals. The board records the specific misalignment, directs the superintendent to prepare a revised budget, schedules a special meeting, and communicates the delay to the community. This outcome is rare — but a board that is prepared for it governs more effectively than a board that assumes it never happens.

Most boards only prepare for outcome one. The governance-prepared board has a protocol for all three. The preparation determines which outcome the board reaches, not the community's testimony.

**The preparation protocol: what the board does before the hearing**

The seven days before the public hearing should follow a structured preparation cycle. Here's the framework from the Focused Monitoring Cycle applied to hearing preparation.

**Day seven: Document distribution.** The superintendent's budget presentation, the board's budget alignment summary from March, and the community progress report (if published) are posted to the district website. The board confirms all documents are accessible and in a format the community can understand — not the full budget document, but a clear summary with the key numbers, the goal alignment story, and the trade-offs the board examined.

**Day five: Board president preparation.** The board president prepares the hearing's opening statement. The statement names what the board has done since January — the goal adoption, the budget alignment review, the mid-year check-in — and explains how the public hearing fits into the governance cycle. The president also prepares the closing statement that the board will deliver after the testimony is complete. The closing acknowledges the community's participation, summarizes what was heard, and commits to specific follow-through actions.

**Day three: Spokesperson designation.** If the board has designated a spokesperson for budget questions — typically the board president or finance committee chair — the spokesperson prepares answers to the questions the board expects to hear. The full board reviews the anticipated question list and confirms the spokesperson's responses align with the board's shared understanding. The board also confirms what questions are delegated to the superintendent (operational questions) and what questions the board retains (governance questions).

**Day two: Hearing simulation.** The board runs a sixty-minute hearing simulation. A board member or staff member plays the role of a community member asking the most challenging questions the board expects. The board practices: the opening statement, the transition from presentation to testimony, the response protocol, the closing statement, and the follow-through commitment. The simulation surfaces gaps in preparation that the board can address before the public hearing.

**Day one: Board briefing and rest.** The board receives a final one-page briefing: the anticipated question list, the spokesperson's talking points, the hearing timeline, and the follow-through commitment language. Board members arrive at the hearing having read the materials, confirmed their roles, and rested.

**The execution protocol: what the board does during the hearing**

The hearing itself follows a structure that communicates preparation and respect.

**The opening (5 minutes):** The board president opens with a two-minute statement establishing the hearing's purpose and context. The president explains how the hearing fits into the governance cycle, what the board has already done to review the budget, and what the board will do after the hearing. The tone is welcoming and the frame is accountability, not defense.

**The presentation (15-20 minutes):** The superintendent presents the budget. The board does not interrupt, redirect, or question during the presentation. The board's role during the presentation is to listen — both to the superintendent and to the community's body language.

**Community testimony (scheduled time):** The board listens. This is the hardest part of the hearing for board members who are used to being the ones asking questions. The board's role during testimony is to listen fully, take notes, and not respond defensively. Board members do not argue with community members. Board members do not explain why the community member's concern is unfounded. Board members listen, document, and commit to follow-through.

**The closing (5 minutes):** The board president closes with a statement that names two or three specific themes from the testimony, acknowledges the community's contribution, and commits to specific follow-through actions before the adoption vote. The closing communicates that the board heard the community — not that the board agrees with every point, but that the board listened and will act.

**What the board does not do during the hearing**

Three things the board does not do during a public hearing.

**One: The board does not debate the superintendent.** If a board member disagrees with the superintendent's presentation, the disagreement is addressed in closed session or board workshop — not in front of the community. The hearing is not the venue for board-superintendent conflict.

**Two: The board does not make substantive changes during the hearing.** If the community raises a concern that the board agrees with, the board's response is "we will review this and address it" — not "let's change the budget right now." Substantive changes belong in a properly noticed board meeting with time for deliberation.

**Three: The board does not close without a follow-through commitment.** The board that closes the hearing with "thank you for your input" and moves to the adoption vote has not closed the accountability loop. The board that closes with "we heard three themes tonight — budget transparency, reading intervention funding, and transportation routing — and we will address each in the adoption meeting documentation" has closed the loop.

**The follow-through protocol: what the board does after the hearing**

The twenty-four hours after the public hearing are the most important period for community trust.

**Within 24 hours:** The board president sends a brief communication to the community — an email, a website post, or both — summarizing what was heard at the hearing and what the board will do before the adoption vote. The communication names the specific themes and the specific follow-through actions.

**Before the adoption vote:** The board prepares a testimony summary document that lists every speaker, the substance of their testimony, and the board's response. The document is shared with the full board before the adoption vote so every board member knows what the community said and how it was addressed.

**At the adoption vote:** The board president references the hearing and the testimony summary during the adoption discussion. The community sees that the hearing was not a separate event — it was an integrated part of the budget adoption process.

Boards that execute this three-phase protocol — preparation, execution, follow-through — build community trust through the public hearing. Boards that skip any phase treat the hearing as a procedural requirement. The community can tell the difference, and trust is built or lost in that gap.
