**Your District Has AI. Does Your Board Have a Governance Plan?**

July 10, 2026

---

Let me start with a number that should stop every school board member cold.

**82% of teachers** have received no formal guidance on AI use. That's not a statistic from some think tank projection — it's from the most recent Gallup/Walton Family Foundation survey, published May 28 of this year.

Four out of five teachers in your district are making up their own rules about AI in the classroom. Some are embracing it. Some are banning it. Most are somewhere in between, trying to figure it out on their own. And nobody — not the state, not the district, and certainly not the board — has told them what the guardrails are.

Here's the part that matters for you: if your board hasn't had a conversation about AI governance, you're not behind. You're right where every board is right now. But the window is closing fast.

**The state mandate train is moving**

Seven states already require districts to develop formal AI use policies: Idaho, Maryland, Ohio, Oklahoma, Tennessee, Utah, and Virginia. Florida's SB 1194 takes effect July 1, making it number eight — requiring statewide AI standards and district-level policy adoption.

Thirty-eight states have taken some form of state-level action on cell phones in schools — legislation, state board policy, executive guidance. If you think AI governance is going to be a different conversation, think again. The same forces driving cell phone policy — parent concern, media pressure, legislative attention — are converging on AI at a much faster pace.

Education Week projects that the number of mandated states will double within 12 months. That means at least three to five more states will pass AI policy mandates in the 2027 legislative sessions.

Your district doesn't need to be in a mandated state for this to matter. By the time the mandate hits, the governance work should already be done.

**What the landscape looks like — and what's missing**

Here's the market reality. Right now, if you're a school board member looking for AI governance help, here are your options:

- **AI for Education** offers free toolkits, webinars, and a state AI guidance page — teacher-facing awareness content, not board-facing governance.
- **NSBA and NAIA** launched an AI Smart Certification in December 2025 — one module out of six. It's content consumption, not practice change.
- **ISTE, ASCD, CoSN, AASA** all have resources — but they're aimed at teachers, IT leaders, and superintendents. Not boards.
- **Google, Microsoft, Khan Academy** are building AI tools that reach millions of students — accelerating adoption without governance frameworks.

Every organization in this space serves educators and administrators with information, tools, and standards. None of them serve school boards with coaching and governance.

That's not an oversight. That's an open lane.

**What board-level AI governance actually looks like**

The ESB framework already solves the problem these organizations are trying to address. AI governance is not a separate initiative — it's a specific application of the same governance practices that make boards effective in every other domain. But there's a difference between knowing the framework and applying it to a new, fast-moving challenge. AI governance exposes gaps that other governance topics don't.

It starts with **Goal Clarity**. Does your board have a clear vision for how AI serves student outcomes in your district? Not a policy statement — a specific, measurable picture of what success looks like. The board that can answer "What does responsible AI use look like in our classrooms?" before the crisis hits is the board that governs proactively instead of reactively. I've seen boards that spend months debating curriculum adoption but have never had a 30-minute conversation about how AI tools are being evaluated for classroom use.

It requires **Progress Monitoring**. If your board gets quarterly reports on academic outcomes, budget status, and facility conditions — where's the AI adoption metric? How do you know whether the policies you set are being followed? The dashboard needs a governance dimension. And here's the thing: most boards don't even know what questions to ask because they haven't defined what responsible AI use looks like yet.

It demands a **Governance Team** approach. AI governance can't be a subcommittee assignment or a one-board-member pet project. The entire board needs to understand the landscape and agree on the approach. A divided board facing an AI controversy is the fastest way to lose community trust — faster than almost any other governance issue I've seen.

And it depends on **Superintendent Partnership** — not delegation. Your superintendent needs your board to set policy guardrails so they can manage implementation. A board that says "figure it out" and a board that says "here's what we expect" produce fundamentally different outcomes. The superintendent who walks into a board meeting with an AI policy recommendation and gets five different reactions from five different board members is a superintendent who's going to stop bringing policy recommendations at all.

**The window isn't measured in years**

Here's the thing about first-mover advantage. Organizations like NSBA, AI for Education, and state school board associations are all looking at this space right now, deciding whether it's worth their time. The one who moves first gets to define the category.

For school boards, the window is even tighter. The 2027 legislative sessions will bring new mandates. Community expectations are shifting faster than policy can keep up. And every day your board operates without an AI governance framework, you're governing by accident instead of by design.

Seven states have already made AI governance mandatory. More are coming. The platforms — Google, Microsoft, Khan Academy — are accelerating adoption faster than boards can build governance frameworks.

If your district is using AI — and it is — your board needs a plan. Not next year. Not after the mandate hits.

Now.
