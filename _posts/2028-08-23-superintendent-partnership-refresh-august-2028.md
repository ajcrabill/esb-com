**Superintendent Partnership Refresh — What Changes After a Full Evaluation Cycle**

August 23, 2028

---

The superintendent evaluation is done. The board voted. The growth areas are identified. The implementation plan is in motion.

Now comes the conversation most boards skip.

When a board completes its first full evaluation cycle, both the board and the superintendent learn things about each other that were invisible before the process existed. The superintendent learns what the board truly values — not what the board said it valued in the goal-setting workshop last January, but what the board emphasized in the evaluation deliberation when the votes were counted. The words of a goal-setting session are intentions. The emphasis of an evaluation deliberation is evidence.

The board learns how the superintendent receives feedback. Does the superintendent respond to growth areas with defensiveness or with curiosity? Does the superintendent share data proactively or only when the board requests it? Does the superintendent see the board as a partner in improvement or as a compliance body to be managed?

A partnership refresh conversation is where those learnings become a better working relationship. Here is what that conversation should cover.

**The Evaluation-Revealed Patterns**

The evaluation cycle reveals patterns in how the board and superintendent work together. Three patterns I see most often, and each needs an explicit conversation — not to assign blame, but to reset expectations before the fall cycle begins.

*Over-functioning.* The board took on work that belonged to the superintendent or the superintendent's team. During the spring cycle, perhaps individual board members met directly with principals, requested department-level data unrelated to goal monitoring, or gave direct operational guidance to staff. The board over-functioned because it was unsure of its governance role — or because it did not trust the superintendent's team to deliver. Either way, the pattern erodes the superintendent's authority and confuses the staff about who is in charge. The refresh conversation establishes boundaries: the board governs through the superintendent, not around him.

*Under-functioning.* The board deferred its governance responsibilities. Progress monitoring meetings got shortened. Data discussions got postponed. The board under-functioned because it lacked confidence in its governance expertise — or because it found operational work more satisfying than governance work. Either way, the superintendent was left governing without governance. The refresh conversation reestablishes the board's core obligations and the superintendent's right to expect those obligations to be met. The superintendent needs a governing board as much as the board needs a functioning superintendent.

*Communication gaps.* The board and superintendent discovered different expectations about what information should flow, at what frequency, and through what channel. The superintendent sent weekly written updates that the board president skimmed. The board expected a phone call that never came. The refresh conversation defines the channel, the frequency, and the content of communication that both sides commit to — in writing.

**The Board President's Renewed Role**

In the partnership refresh, the board president's role shifts. During the first governance cycle, the president was learning the role alongside the superintendent. Now both have a year of working data.

The board president becomes the partnership's relationship manager — the person responsible for monitoring the health of the board-superintendent dynamic and raising issues before they become problems. That means the president should walk into the refresh conversation with a specific frame: "Based on our evaluation cycle, here is what I think worked in our partnership and here is what I think needs adjustment. What do you see differently?"

If the board president cannot have that conversation honestly — if the president avoids the difficult topics or sugarcoats the gaps — the partnership is not healthy enough for the fall cycle. Better to surface the discomfort now, when there is time to address it, than in November when the board is in the middle of its governance calendar.

**The Fall Communication Cadence**

One concrete deliverable from the refresh conversation: a written communication cadence agreement for the fall. The standard framework I recommend:

- **Weekly.** A brief written update from the superintendent to the full board. One or two notable developments, one operational highlight, one early indicator worth flagging. Five minutes to write, five minutes to read. The board's job is to read it, not to respond to it — unless the content requires governance action. If every board member replies with questions every week, the weekly update becomes a burden rather than a service.

- **Monthly.** A structured conversation between the board president and the superintendent. Goal progress pulse, emerging issues, upcoming board agenda preview. Not a social lunch — a working session with a written agenda circulated in advance. The monthly check-in catches small issues before they become big ones.

- **Quarterly.** A full board conversation with the superintendent — not a formal evaluation, but a governance discussion about strategic direction, district performance trends, and the board's upcoming governance actions. This is the board's opportunity to ask the broader questions that the weekly updates and monthly check-ins have surfaced.

The specific cadence matters less than the agreement. What matters is that both parties know what to expect and neither has to guess whether to call, email, or wait for the next board meeting.

**Partnership Health Indicators**

The refresh conversation should also establish the indicators the board will use to monitor partnership health during the fall cycle. Without indicators, the board only knows the partnership is strained when something breaks.

Some indicators I use with boards I coach: the board president and superintendent can disagree in a meeting and continue the conversation productively afterward. The board receives bad news before it receives good news. The board's questions to the superintendent are about goal progress, not operational details. The superintendent brings data to the board before the board asks for it. Board members do not communicate directly with staff about operational matters without the superintendent's awareness.

Track these indicators monthly. The first month a partnership indicator turns negative is the month to have the refresh conversation again — not the month to wait until the formal evaluation cycle arrives in June.

The evaluation is done. The relationship is not.
