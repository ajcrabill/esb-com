**Summer Policy Work — How to Align District Policies with Next Year's Governance Priorities**

July 19, 2027

---

The board adopted goals in January. Those goals should have driven policy review throughout the spring. If they did — if the board audited its policy manual for alignment with the adopted goals — the board is ahead of most boards. If they didn't — and the February policy audit piece may have been the first time the board thought about it — summer is the catch-up period.

Here's the reality most boards face: the policy manual is too long, too dense, and too infrequently reviewed. Most policies are written to comply with state law or to respond to a past crisis. Few policies are written to advance the board's current governance priorities. And when a board adopts new goals — as your board did in January — the policy manual doesn't automatically update to reflect them.

The result is a gap between what the board says it wants to achieve and what the district's policy framework enables the district to achieve. That gap doesn't close itself. It has to be addressed deliberately, and summer — when the governance calendar is lightest — is the best time to address it.

**What a summer policy audit covers**

A summer policy audit is not a full policy manual rewrite. Most boards have hundreds of policies. Rewriting all of them in a single summer is not realistic. A targeted audit — focused on the policies that directly affect the board's priority goals — is realistic, and it produces results.

The audit covers three categories:

**Mission-critical policies.** These are the policies that directly affect student outcomes — instructional policies, assessment policies, student achievement policies, curriculum adoption policies. Every goal the board adopted in January has a policy implication. If the board's goal is to improve third-grade reading proficiency, the board needs to know: does the district's reading instruction policy align with evidence-based practices? Does the assessment policy support the data collection the board needs for progress monitoring? Does the intervention policy ensure struggling readers receive timely support?

The board should identify the top three to five mission-critical policies that directly affect its priority goals and review each one for alignment.

**Governance policies.** These are the policies that define how the board operates — board meeting policies, board communication policies, board-superintendent relationship policies, board self-evaluation policies. If the board self-assessment revealed governance team gaps — communication discipline, meeting preparation, role clarity — the board's governance policies need review. A board that identifies communication discipline as a gap but has no policy defining the professional channel for board-superintendent communication has a policy problem, not just a behavior problem.

**Policies for the upcoming year.** As the board prepares for the goal-setting process for the 2027-28 governance year, the board should identify any policies that need to be in place before the new goals take effect. If the board anticipates adopting goals that require new data collection, does the assessment policy authorize it? If the board anticipates adopting goals that require community engagement, does the public participation policy support it? The board should identify policy gaps that will need to be filled before the new governance year starts.

**How to conduct the audit**

The policy audit should be conducted by the board's policy committee — or by two designated board members if the board doesn't have a policy committee. The process has four steps:

**Step one: Identify the priority policies.** Using the framework above, the board identifies the policies that need review — no more than ten to fifteen policies for a single summer audit.

**Step two: Review each policy for alignment.** For each policy, the reviewer asks: does this policy support or undermine the board's January goals? Does this policy need to be updated for the 2027-28 governance year? Does this policy create barriers to the board's governance priorities? The reviewer documents findings: policy fully aligned, policy needs minor revision, policy needs substantial revision, or policy is obsolete and should be replaced.

**Step three: Draft revisions.** For each policy identified as needing revision, the reviewer drafts the revision with the specific changes needed. The revisions should be concrete — not "strengthen the language" but "change the assessment cycle from annual to quarterly" or "add a paragraph defining the board's role in curriculum adoption."

**Step four: Prepare for fall adoption.** The policy committee presents the recommended revisions at the August board meeting with a proposed adoption timeline. Some revisions can be adopted in August. Others may need a first reading in August and a second reading in September. The committee's job is to prepare the board to act, not to do the board's work for it.

**What the policy audit produces**

A completed policy audit produces: a documented list of policies reviewed with alignment findings for each, a set of draft policy revisions ready for board consideration, a proposed adoption timeline, and a policy review calendar for the upcoming governance year.

The board that completes a summer policy audit enters the fall knowing that its policy framework supports — rather than undermines — the board's governance priorities. The board that skips the summer policy audit enters the fall with a policy manual that was written for a different governance year with different goals.
