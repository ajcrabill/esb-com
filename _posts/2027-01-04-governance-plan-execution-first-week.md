**The Execution Arc: Your Governance Plan Is Only as Good as Your First Week**

January 4, 2027

---

I've watched boards spend December planning. I've watched them set goals, build scorecards, align budget priorities, plan their first ninety days, and design a January board meeting that's supposed to set the tone for the entire year.

And I've watched those same boards lose every bit of that momentum in the first week of January.

Here's the pattern. December is a month of intention. You have the distance of the holiday break to think strategically. You're not in the grind of weekly meetings and daily crises. You can see the forest instead of the trees.

Then January 4 arrives. The superintendent's inbox is full. The board chair has been fielding constituent emails since New Year's. There's a personnel issue that "needs immediate attention." The committee chairs want to schedule their first meetings. The agenda for the January board meeting needs to be finalized — and somehow it's already Thursday.

The plan you built in December? It evaporates.

Not because the plan was wrong. Because you didn't have a system for executing it in the first week — when the pressure is highest, the inertia is strongest, and the gravitational pull of "business as usual" is hardest to resist.

## The First-Week Execution Problem

The problem isn't that boards don't want to execute their plan. The problem is that execution requires a different muscle than planning. Planning is an intellectual exercise. Execution is a behavioral one. And behaviors are harder to sustain than ideas.

I've identified three specific execution failures that happen in the first week of January. Every one of them is preventable.

### Failure 1: The Plan Stays in a Binder

The most common execution failure is also the simplest: the December plan gets filed away instead of operationalized.

Your board spent December creating a governance plan. Goals were set. A scorecard was designed. A first-ninety-days timeline was built. A January meeting agenda was sketched out.

Where is that plan right now?

If it's in a binder on the board chair's shelf, it's not a plan — it's a souvenir.

The boards that execute successfully don't keep their governance plan in a binder. They keep it on the agenda. Every single meeting. The first item under board business is always: "Review progress against our 2027 governance plan." Every single meeting. Not just in January. In February. In March. In October.

The plan lives on the agenda. That's how it becomes real.

### Failure 2: The January Meeting Gets Hijacked

I've seen it a hundred times. A board schedules its January meeting with every intention of adopting goals, reviewing baseline data, and setting the year's direction.

Then something comes up. A parent group wants to address the board about a school closure rumor. A personnel matter requires executive session. A state mandate was released on January 2 with a compliance deadline of January 15. The board chair looks at the agenda and decides: "Let's move the strategic items to February. We'll have more time then."

You won't have more time in February. You'll have different emergencies.

The January meeting is the most important meeting of the year precisely because it's the one where the board establishes its priorities before the emergencies arrive. If you let the emergencies set your January agenda, the emergencies will set your entire year.

### Failure 3: The Chair and Superintendent Don't Align Before the First Week

The most successful January transitions I've witnessed share one thing: the board chair and the superintendent met in the first week to align on the execution plan.

Not a formal meeting. Not a two-hour strategic session. A forty-five-minute conversation with a simple agenda:

- What are the three things we need to accomplish this month?
- What's the biggest risk to our governance plan right now?
- How will we communicate progress to the board and the community?

That's it. Forty-five minutes. No staff. No committees. Just the two people most responsible for turning December's plan into January's reality, asking each other the questions that matter.

The boards that do this enter January aligned. The boards that don't enter January assuming alignment — and by February, they discover they weren't aligned at all.

## Your First-Week Execution Checklist

Let me give you a concrete checklist for this week. Not a theory. Not a framework. An actual checklist you can use today, tomorrow, and Friday.

**Monday (today) — The Plan Review**
- [ ] Board chair reads the December governance plan aloud to the board leadership team
- [ ] One-sentence confirmation: "These are still our goals for 2027"
- [ ] The plan gets added to the January meeting agenda as a standing item

**Tuesday — The Chair-Superintendent Alignment**
- [ ] Forty-five minute meeting between board chair and superintendent
- [ ] Three questions: what are our January priorities, what's the biggest risk, how do we communicate progress
- [ ] Written summary sent to full board by end of day

**Wednesday — The Board Briefing**
- [ ] Chair sends a three-paragraph update to the full board
- [ ] What the board agreed to in December
- [ ] What's happening this week
- [ ] What the January meeting will focus on

**Thursday — The Agenda Lock**
- [ ] January board meeting agenda is finalized with strategic items first
- [ ] Baseline data for each goal is requested from the superintendent
- [ ] Board packet instructions: include the one-page governance plan summary

**Friday — The Week Assessment**
- [ ] Chair asks: did we execute the plan this week, or did we let the plan sit?
- [ ] If the plan is still in the binder, what needs to change on Monday?

## What This Week Determines

There's a reason I'm writing this on January 4 instead of January 25.

The first week of January is the most consequential week of your governance year. It sets the execution pattern for every week that follows. If you start January executing your plan — contacting your superintendent, locking your agenda, putting your goals on every meeting — then execution becomes the habit.

If you start January reacting — letting emergencies set your direction, letting the agenda drift, letting the plan stay in the binder — then reaction becomes the habit.

The work you did in December matters. But it only matters if you execute it this week.

December was the plan. January is the proof.

Go prove it.
