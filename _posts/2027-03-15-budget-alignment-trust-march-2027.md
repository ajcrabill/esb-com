**Why Budget Alignment Builds Community Trust — The Mid-Arc Bridge**

March 15, 2027

---

Two weeks ago, your board completed the budget alignment review. Last week, your board published the first community progress report. Both of those are discrete tasks — distinct pieces of governance work that belong on the spring calendar.

But they're not separate activities. They're two sides of the same effort. And if your board has done both, you're in a stronger position than you probably realize heading into the April budget adoption.

Here's what I've noticed across the districts I've worked with. The boards that face the most community opposition at budget hearings are rarely the boards making unpopular spending decisions. They're the boards where the community doesn't understand how spending decisions are made.

The boards that face the least opposition are not the boards with the most popular budgets. They're the boards that have invested the preceding months in making their decision-making process visible.

**The connection between budget alignment and community trust**

I want to make an argument that sounds counterintuitive.

A budget that is perfectly aligned with the board's goals but invisible to the community will generate more opposition at the public hearing than a budget that is partially aligned but transparently communicated.

I've seen this play out more times than I can count. Board A does the full budget alignment work — adjustments list, crosswalk analysis, trajectory verification, recorded decision — but never publishes anything about it. Board B does partial alignment — the crosswalk is rougher, the trajectory question isn't fully answered — but publishes a progress report that shows the community exactly where the budget stands, what decisions the board has made, and what questions remain.

Board B faces fewer objections at the public hearing. Not because the budget is better. Because the community understands the board's decision-making process. And understanding builds trust, even when the community doesn't agree with every choice.

**Three ways transparent budgeting builds trust**

**1. It signals that the board has a process.**

The community's deepest anxiety about school boards — and I've heard this in focus groups and listening sessions across the country — is not that boards make bad decisions. It's that boards make decisions without a clear process. That the budget gets approved because the superintendent recommended it, not because the board evaluated it.

When the board publishes a progress report that explains the budget alignment process — the adjustments list, the crosswalk, the trajectory check — the community sees evidence of a process. The existence of a process is itself trust-building, regardless of the specific outcome.

**2. It makes trade-offs visible.**

Most community opposition at budget hearings comes from a specific source: someone discovers, in real time, that a program or position they value is being reduced. The discovery happens at the hearing. The person has no warning. The response is immediate and emotional.

The progress report makes trade-offs visible before the hearing. The board's budget alignment review included a discussion of trade-offs. The progress report should include a sentence about them: "To increase reading intervention funding by 12%, the board accepted reductions in non-instructional administrative spending and deferred two capital projects."

A community member who reads that sentence has time to process it before the hearing. They may disagree with the trade-off. But they're not blindsided. And a community member who is not blindsided is a community member who can have a productive conversation at the hearing rather than an emotional reaction.

**3. It demonstrates that the board is willing to share uncomfortable information.**

The progress report from last week included the data selection guide. The guide recommended including uncomfortable data — goals that are off track, targets that may not be reached — and providing context about what the board is doing in response.

This is the hardest trust-building step because it requires the board to share information that makes it vulnerable. But here's what I've learned: the community already knows when things aren't going well. Parents see the data. Teachers see the data. The community newsletter covers the test scores. The board gains nothing by pretending everything is fine.

What the board gains by sharing uncomfortable information is credibility. The community says: they told us the truth about the reading goal. They told us the trajectory needs attention. They told us what they're doing about it. I may not like the data, but I trust that the board sees what I see.

**The public hearing isn't the test — the three months before it are**

I've watched boards judge their budget communication by the quality of the public hearing. If the hearing goes smoothly, the board concludes the communication was effective. If the hearing is contentious, the board concludes the communication was insufficient.

But the hearing is not the test. The hearing is the consequence of the three months that preceded it. A board that ran a transparent January-to-March process will have a hearing that goes smoothly, because the community already knows what to expect. A board that kept its work invisible will have a hearing that is contentious, because the hearing is the community's first opportunity to react.

The March budget alignment review and the community progress report are not items on a to-do list. They are the board's investment in a productive April. The investment has already been made, if your board has been doing the work. The question now is whether your board will reap the return.

**A concrete recommendation for the week ahead**

Between now and the April budget hearing, I recommend one additional communication step.

Schedule a fifteen-minute segment at your next board meeting for the board president to present the progress report. Not a read-aloud. A summary. "Here's where our goals stand. Here's where the budget stands. Here's what we decided in our alignment review. Here's what happens next."

Then open the floor for community questions about the progress report. Not about the budget hearing — about the progress report. The community's questions about the report will surface concerns before the hearing. The board can address those concerns in the meeting, incorporate adjustments, or prepare responses for the hearing.

A community member who asks a question at the March board meeting and receives an answer walks into the April hearing with one concern already addressed. A community member who saves all their questions for the hearing arrives with a list that hasn't been shortened.

The fifteen-minute progress report segment at the March board meeting is one of the highest-return investments your board can make in a smooth April adoption.

**Boards that demonstrate trustworthiness get trust**

I want to close with a distinction that matters.

Trust is not something a board asks for. Trust is something a board demonstrates it deserves. The board that publishes a progress report and says "trust us" hasn't earned anything. The board that publishes a progress report that shows its work — the data the board reviewed, the decisions the board made, the trade-offs the board accepted — has demonstrated that it is trustworthy.

April's budget hearing will reflect whether your board has spent January through March demonstrating trustworthiness. If the work has been done, the hearing is a formality. If the work hasn't been done, the hearing becomes the work itself.

The choice belongs to your board. But the evidence of the choice belongs to the community.
