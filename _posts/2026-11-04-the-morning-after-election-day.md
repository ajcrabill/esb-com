**The Morning After Election Day — Three Things Every Board Must Do Immediately**

November 4, 2026

---

I got the call on election night — a board president I'd been coaching for two years. Two incumbents lost. One of the incoming members campaigned on firing her as superintendent. The reorganization meeting was three weeks away. What do we do?

I've had that conversation in districts where incumbents held every seat and in districts where the entire board turned over. The names change. The morning-after panic doesn't.

Here's what I've learned: the first 72 hours after election day set the tone for the next two years. Whatever the outcome in your district — incumbents held, new members elected, balance of power shifted — you have three things to do today.

---

**1. Acknowledge the outcome publicly, and do it from the board, not just individual members.**

The worst thing you can do the morning after an election is let silence fill the space.

When only individual members post statements — congratulations from winners, gracious (or not-so-gracious) farewells from losers — the public hears a fractured board. They hear individuals, not a team. And the story that fills that silence? Someone else writes it for you.

Here's what a board-level acknowledgement looks like. Draft it, approve it by whatever quick-poll mechanism your board uses for emergencies, and publish it before noon.

*"The [District Name] Board of Education acknowledges the results of yesterday's election. We thank every candidate who stepped forward to serve their community. We welcome [newly elected members] and extend our gratitude to [departing members] for their service. The board's work — ensuring every student in [District Name] is prepared for college, career, and citizenship — continues without interruption. We will meet publicly on [date] to begin transition planning and reaffirm our commitment to the district's student outcome goals."*

Three sentences. No spin. No defensiveness. No victory laps. Just accountability and a clear signal that the board as a governing body is still at work.

This is Governance Team 101: the board's legitimacy comes from the electoral process, not from any single election outcome. When you acknowledge the result as a team, you demonstrate that the institution is bigger than any one person's victory or loss.

**2. Begin transition planning immediately — don't wait for the reorganization meeting.**

Every day between election day and the first meeting with new members is lost ground if you don't use it purposefully.

If you have departing members: schedule an exit conversation this week about institutional knowledge — budget cycles, facilities challenges, union relationships, strategic plan progress. Document it. Pass it along.

If you have incoming members: reach out today. Send them a packet — board policy manual, current strategic plan, recent quarterly progress report, meeting calendar, student outcome data. New members who receive nothing in the first week arrive skeptical — and they're right to be.

If the board composition didn't change: use the moment to reaffirm governance commitments publicly. Schedule a board self-evaluation session. Complacency is what creates next cycle's turnover.

This connects directly to the ESB framework's Governance Team domain. A board that treats transitions as events — something that happens at a reorganization meeting — is a board that loses institutional memory every cycle. A board that treats transitions as processes — something that starts the morning after election day — is a board that gets stronger with every member change.

**3. Reaffirm the board's commitment to student outcome goals — and mean it.**

Here's the uncomfortable truth: the most important work your board does has nothing to do with who sits at the table.

Your district's student outcome goals — the specific, measurable targets you've set for academic achievement, graduation rates, college and career readiness, and every other measure of student success — those goals predate every member currently serving. They will outlast every member currently serving. And they are the only legitimate reason the board exists in the first place.

So today, issue a statement — or better yet, schedule a special meeting within the next 10 days — with a single agenda item: reaffirmation of the board's student outcome goals.

Read them aloud. Vote on them again. Put them on the record with the new member configuration, whatever that configuration is.

Why does this matter? Because the first thing that gets abandoned in a transition is focus. New members arrive with campaign promises. Departing members leave with unfinished priorities. The temptation is to start fresh, to revisit every goal, to let the new balance of power rewrite the agenda from scratch.

Don't do that. Do the reaffirmation first. If the new board decides to adjust goals later — and it may, legitimately — that decision should be made from a position of continuity, not from a vacuum. Reaffirm first, then deliberate.

This is the ESB framework's Progress Monitoring domain in action. Goals that survive transitions are goals that get met. Goals that get abandoned every time a member leaves or arrives are goals that weren't real in the first place.

---

**What this looks like in practice.**

Day 1 (today): Board-level acknowledgement published. Clerk sends welcome packets. Special meeting scheduled for goal reaffirmation within 10 days.

Day 2–5: Exit conversations with departing members. Board president calls each incoming member to establish working relationships. Superintendent briefs the full board on current student outcome data.

Day 7: Special meeting. Reaffirm goals. Announce transition plan and board self-evaluation dates publicly.

Day 21+: Reorganization meeting. Formal onboarding. Officer elections. Get to work.

This isn't complicated. It's disciplined. And discipline the first week is what prevents crisis the first year.

---

**The board's mission outlasts any individual member's term.**

That sentence is the entire point of this newsletter. Some of you won last night. Some of you lost. Some are still waiting on returns. I've been in all three chairs. The feeling fades. The work doesn't.

Your community doesn't care which faction won the majority. They care whether their children are learning to read and whether graduates are ready for what comes next. Those outcomes don't depend on who won last night. They depend on what you do this morning.

So do the three things. Acknowledge. Transition. Reaffirm. Then get back to work. There are students waiting.
