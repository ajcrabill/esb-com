<h2>Transcript</h2>
<div class="transcript bg-light p-4 rounded" style="line-height:1.8">
<p><strong>00:00 — 00:05</strong><br>
Every election cycle, school boards face a quiet crisis: institutional amnesia.</p>

<p><strong>00:05 — 00:10</strong><br>
The research is clear and it's damning.</p>

<p><strong>00:10 — 00:15</strong><br>
Ihrke and Ford at UW-Milwaukee tracked over 2,000 school board members across a decade.</p>

<p><strong>00:15 — 00:20</strong><br>
The average turnover rate?</p>

<p><strong>00:20 — 00:26</strong><br>
43 percent per election.</p>

<p><strong>00:26 — 00:31</strong><br>
Almost half of every board replaced each time.</p>

<p><strong>00:31 — 00:36</strong><br>
That means your long-term strategy doesn't survive your first full term.</p>

<p><strong>00:36 — 00:41</strong><br>
Here's what that looks like on the ground.</p>

<p><strong>00:41 — 00:46</strong><br>
Cy-Fair ISD in Texas.</p>

<p><strong>00:46 — 00:52</strong><br>
One of the state's largest districts.</p>

<p><strong>00:52 — 00:57</strong><br>
Three election cycles, six new board members, and suddenly nobody in the room remembers why the policy was passed in the first place.</p>

<p><strong>00:57 — 01:02</strong><br>
Institutional memory doesn't just fade — it evaporates.</p>

<p><strong>01:02 — 01:07</strong><br>
And who suffers?</p>

<p><strong>01:07 — 01:13</strong><br>
The students waiting for that multi-year improvement plan to actually land.</p>

<p><strong>01:13 — 01:18</strong><br>
Your board needs a governance continuity plan before the next election — not after.</p>

<p><strong>01:18 — 01:23</strong><br>
Not "we'll figure it out when the new members arrive." Before.</p>
