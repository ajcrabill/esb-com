<div class="transcript bg-light p-4 rounded" style="line-height:1.8">
<p><strong>00:00 — 00:04</strong><br>
Three strategies that keep your board's governance trajectory intact through election season.</p>

<p><strong>00:04 — 00:09</strong><br>
No legislation required.</p>

<p><strong>00:09 — 00:14</strong><br>
<strong>Number one: staggered terms.</strong></p>

<p><strong>00:14 — 00:19</strong><br>
If your entire board is elected on the same cycle, every election is a potential wipeout.</p>

<p><strong>00:19 — 00:24</strong><br>
Four-year staggered terms cut the turnover risk by more than half.</p>

<p><strong>00:24 — 00:28</strong><br>
It's the single highest-leverage structural change you can make.</p>

<p><strong>00:28 — 00:33</strong><br>
<strong>Number two: governance onboarding, not orientation.</strong></p>

<p><strong>00:33 — 00:38</strong><br>
A binder and a welcome packet isn't a plan.</p>

<p><strong>00:38 — 00:43</strong><br>
New board members need an intensive governance education in their first ninety days.</p>

<p><strong>00:43 — 00:48</strong><br>
Policy fundamentals.</p>

<p><strong>00:48 — 00:52</strong><br>
Fiduciary responsibility.</p>

<p><strong>00:52 — 00:57</strong><br>
The difference between voting on a curriculum and writing one.</p>

<p><strong>00:57 — 01:02</strong><br>
<strong>Number three: board-superintendent succession planning.</strong></p>

<p><strong>01:02 — 01:07</strong><br>
Codify the strategic priorities so they survive personnel changes.</p>

<p><strong>01:07 — 01:12</strong><br>
When the research says sixty-seven percent of superintendents leave after an election, you'd better have a transition plan in the drawer before the ballots are counted.</p>

<p><strong>01:12 — 01:16</strong><br>
These three moves don't require new laws.</p>

<p><strong>01:16 — 01:21</strong><br>

<p><strong>01:21 — 01:26</strong><br>
