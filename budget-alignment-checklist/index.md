<h1>Budget Alignment Checklist <small>— One-Page Audit</small></h1>

<p class="intro">Budget alignment means your spending decisions flow from your strategic goals — not from inertia, not from "that's how we've always done it," not from whoever lobbied hardest.</p>

<p class="intro">This checklist is designed for a board workshop or work session. Go through it as a full governance team — board members and superintendent together. Answer honestly. Fix what's broken. Build the alignment now, while you still have time.</p>

<hr/>

<h2>The Four Questions</h2>

<div class="question-card">
<h4>Question 1: Goal Clarity</h4>
<p>Can every board member name the district's top three student outcome goals — without looking at notes?</p>
<p>This is the baseline test. If your board members can't name the goals from memory, the goals aren't driving decisions. They're wallpaper.</p>
<p><strong>A real goal sounds like:</strong> <em>"Increase third-grade reading proficiency from 62% to 75% by June 2028."</em></p>
<p><strong>Not:</strong> "Improve student achievement." <strong>Not:</strong> "Focus on literacy." A specific, measurable, time-bound number that everyone can repeat.</p>

<h5>Checklist</h5>
<div class="checklist-item">The board has adopted no fewer than one and no more than five SMART goals</div>
<div class="checklist-item">Each goal has a specific measure, population, starting point, ending point, starting date, and ending date</div>
<div class="checklist-item">Every board member can recite the top three goals from memory</div>
<div class="checklist-item">The goals all pertain to student outcomes (not adult inputs)</div>
<div class="checklist-item">Annual targets and interim goals are in place for each goal</div>

<p class="audit-step"><strong>Your audit step:</strong> At your next meeting, ask every board member to write down the district's three top goals on an index card. Pass them in. See how many match.</p>
</div>

<div class="question-card">
<h4>Question 2: Spending Alignment</h4>
<p>Can you look at any line item and say whether it serves a goal?</p>
<p>Every significant spending decision should trace back to a student outcome goal. This is where alignment gets real.</p>

<h5>Checklist</h5>
<div class="checklist-item">The board can articulate how each major budget category connects to at least one adopted goal</div>
<div class="checklist-item">For each goal, there is at least one budget metric tracked alongside the progress metric</div>
<div class="checklist-item">The board has visibility into whether spending on goal-aligned programs is increasing or decreasing relative to non-aligned spending</div>
<div class="checklist-item">A written budget narrative exists that explains the alignment logic for major line items</div>

<p class="audit-step"><strong>Your audit step:</strong> Print your proposed budget. Pick five line items over $100,000. For each one, write the goal it serves in the margin. If you can't, that item is functionally unaligned.</p>
</div>

<div class="question-card">
<h4>Question 3: Integrated Monitoring</h4>
<p>Does your progress monitoring include a budget metric?</p>
<p>Here's the pattern I see over and over:</p>
<ol>
  <li>Board adopts goals. ✓</li>
  <li>Board gets quarterly progress reports. ✓</li>
  <li>Reports show high-level metrics with no connection to spending. ✗</li>
  <li>Budget season arrives and the goals are functionally irrelevant. ✗✗</li>
</ol>

<h5>Checklist</h5>
<div class="checklist-item">Each goal has at least one budget-linked metric in the monitoring dashboard</div>
<div class="checklist-item">Progress reports show spending data alongside outcome data</div>
<div class="checklist-item">The board can spot when progress is rising but spending is falling (or vice versa)</div>
<div class="checklist-item">Budget-linked metrics are reviewed at least quarterly alongside goal progress</div>

<p class="audit-step"><strong>Your audit step:</strong> Pull your most recent goal monitoring report. Does it show spending data alongside outcome data? If not, ask your superintendent's team to add it before the next monitoring cycle.</p>

<p><strong>Example:</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li><em>Goal:</em> Increase on-time graduation rate from 78% to 85%</li>
  <li><em>Progress metric:</em> Current rate, by subgroup</li>
  <li><em>Budget metric:</em> Percentage of discretionary spending allocated to graduation-support programs</li>
  <li><em>Health check:</em> If graduation rate is flat but spending on graduation-support programs dropped 12%, that's a governance conversation — not just a staff briefing.</li>
</ul>
</div>

<div class="question-card">
<h4>Question 4: Priority Framework</h4>
<p>Has your board agreed on a priority framework for tough choices?</p>
<p>This is the one that saved the boards that weathered Portland-sized crises. Before any specific cut is discussed, the board should agree on priority principles.</p>

<h5>Checklist</h5>
<div class="checklist-item">The board has written, agreed-upon priority principles (e.g., "We protect classroom instruction first. We protect safety second. Everything else is on the table.")</div>
<div class="checklist-item">The priority framework was adopted before any specific budget crisis arose</div>
<div class="checklist-item">The framework gives clear guidance on what gets protected and what gets cut first</div>
<div class="checklist-item">Every board member can state the priority framework from memory</div>

<p class="audit-step"><strong>Your audit step:</strong> Schedule a 45-minute board workshop. No superintendent. No staff. Just the board, a blank whiteboard, and one question: <em>"If we have to cut 5%, what do we protect and in what order?"</em> Get the answers written down and agreed before the crisis arrives.</p>
</div>

<h2>Next Steps</h2>

<ol>
  <li><strong>Share this checklist</strong> — send it to every board member before your next work session.</li>
  <li><strong>Take the audit</strong> — spend 45 minutes as a governance team scoring yourselves honestly.</li>
  <li><strong>Pick one gap to close</strong> — don't try to fix all four at once. Start with the lowest score and create a 90-day plan.</li>
  <li><strong>Schedule the workshop</strong> — if your total score is below 5, book a 45-minute board-only session to agree on priority principles before the crisis arrives.</li>
</ol>
