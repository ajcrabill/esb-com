<h1>Budget Alignment Resource Pack</h1>

<p class="resource-intro">
  <strong>Your budget already tells a story. The question is whether it's the story you want to tell.</strong>
  <br/><br/>
  This resource pack gives you everything you need to audit your district's budget alignment — the checklists, the talking points, the governance framework, and a real-world case study. Use it with your full board in a workshop setting. No consultants needed.
</p>

<hr/>

<h2>What Is Budget Alignment?</h2>

<div class="section-card">
  <p><strong>Budget alignment</strong> means your spending decisions flow from your strategic goals — not from inertia, not from "that's how we've always done it," and not from whoever lobbied hardest.</p>

  <p>A board with budget alignment can look at any line item and answer two questions:</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Does this spend move us toward our stated student outcome goals?</strong></li>
    <li><strong>If we had to cut this, what goal would we be deprioritizing?</strong></li>
  </ul>

  <p>Without budget alignment, your strategic plan is aspirational wallpaper. The goals are printed, the mission statement hangs in the lobby, and the budget gets carved up the same way it has been for a decade — with no governance-level framework for the trade-offs.</p>

  <p>With budget alignment, your board has a decision tree. When the cuts come — and they will come — you don't start from scratch. You apply agreed-upon principles to specific choices.</p>
</div>

<hr/>

<h2>Four-Question Budget Alignment Audit</h2>

<p>Here's a simple audit framework you can run through with your board this week. Four questions. If you can answer all four, your board is in the top 10% of all school boards in the country.</p>

<div class="section-card">
  <h4>Question 1: Can every board member name the district's top 3 student outcome goals — without looking at notes?</h4>
  <p>This is the baseline test. If your board members can't name the goals from memory, the goals aren't driving decisions. They're wallpaper.</p>
  <p>A real goal sounds like: <em>Increase third-grade reading proficiency from 62% to 75% by June 2028.</em> Not: "Improve student achievement." Not: "Focus on literacy." A specific, measurable, time-bound number that everyone can repeat.</p>
  <p><strong>Your audit step:</strong> At your next meeting, ask every board member to write down the district's three top goals on an index card. Pass them in. See how many match.</p>
</div>

<div class="section-card">
  <h4>Question 2: Can you look at any line item and say whether it serves a goal?</h4>
  <p>Every significant spending decision should trace back to a student outcome goal.</p>
  <p>Take the proposed budget. Pick five line items over $100,000. For each one, ask: <em>Does this spend move us toward or away from our stated student outcome goals?</em></p>
  <p>Some answers will be obvious. Early literacy intervention spending? Direct line to a reading goal. But what about that facilities maintenance contract, or the district's central office realignment? Is there a governance-level conversation happening about whether non-instructional spending is crowding out instructional investment?</p>
  <p><strong>Your audit step:</strong> Print your budget. For every major line item, write the goal it serves in the margin. If you can't, that item is functionally unaligned.</p>
</div>

<div class="section-card">
  <h4>Question 3: Does your progress monitoring include a budget metric?</h4>
  <p>Here's the pattern I see over and over:</p>
  <ol>
    <li>Board adopts goals. ✓</li>
    <li>Board gets quarterly progress reports. ✓</li>
    <li>Reports show high-level metrics with no connection to spending. ✗</li>
    <li>Budget season arrives and the goals are functionally irrelevant. ✗✗</li>
  </ol>
  <p>Your monitoring dashboard needs at least one budget-linked metric per goal.</p>
  <p><strong>Example:</strong></p>
  <ul>
    <li><em>Goal:</em> Increase on-time graduation rate from 78% to 85%</li>
    <li><em>Progress metric:</em> Current rate, by subgroup</li>
    <li><em>Budget metric:</em> Percentage of discretionary spending allocated to graduation-support programs</li>
    <li><em>Health check:</em> If graduation rate is flat but spending on graduation-support programs dropped 12%, that's a governance conversation — not just a staff briefing.</li>
  </ul>
  <p><strong>Your audit step:</strong> Pull your most recent goal monitoring report. Does it show spending data alongside outcome data? If not, ask your superintendent's team to add it before the next cycle.</p>
</div>

<div class="section-card">
  <h4>Question 4: Has your board agreed on a priority framework for tough choices?</h4>
  <p>This is the one that saves boards when the crisis hits.</p>
  <p>Before any specific cut is discussed, the board should agree on a priority framework. Something like: <em>"We protect classroom instruction first. We protect safety second. Everything else is on the table."</em></p>
  <p>Once the principles are locked, line-item fights become much simpler. You're not arguing about individual positions — you're applying agreed-upon principles to specific decisions.</p>
  <p><strong>Your audit step:</strong> Schedule a 45-minute board workshop. No superintendent. No staff. Just the board, a blank whiteboard, and one question: <em>"If we have to cut 5%, what do we protect and in what order?"</em> Get the answers written down and agreed before the crisis arrives.</p>
</div>

<h2>The Three-Pillar Governance Framework for Budget Alignment</h2>

<p>A board that consistently makes aligned budget decisions doesn't get there by accident. It gets there by building three governance pillars — and maintaining them during peace time, not crisis.</p>

<div class="pillar-grid">
  <div class="pillar-card">
    <h4><span class="pillar-num">1</span> Effective Budget Alignment</h4>
    <p><em>Spending flows from strategy, not inertia.</em></p>
    <p>Every line item traces to a goal. The board has a decision tree for trade-offs, agreed in advance. The budget is a strategic document, not a historical artifact.</p>
    <p><strong>Key practices:</strong></p>
    <ul style="font-size:0.9em; padding-left: 20px;">
      <li>Goal-linked budget narratives</li>
      <li>Priority framework for cuts</li>
      <li>Pre-crisis decision tree</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div class="pillar-card">
    <h4><span class="pillar-num">2</span> Effective Goal Monitoring</h4>
    <p><em>Outcomes and spending tracked side by side.</em></p>
    <p>The board doesn't just track whether students are improving — it tracks whether money is following priorities. Budget-linked metrics in every quarterly report.</p>
    <p><strong>Key practices:</strong></p>
    <ul style="font-size:0.9em; padding-left: 20px;">
      <li>Budget-linked goal metrics</li>
      <li>Integrated monitoring dashboards</li>
      <li>Early warning for misalignment</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div class="pillar-card">
    <h4><span class="pillar-num">3</span> Effective Conflict Navigation</h4>
    <p><em>Principled disagreement without public fracture.</em></p>
    <p>Boards that haven't built trust during peace time cannot build it during a crisis. The board needs norms for disagreement, a collective voice commitment, and practice applying principles before the pressure is on.</p>
    <p><strong>Key practices:</strong></p>
    <ul style="font-size:0.9em; padding-left: 20px;">
      <li>Principles before line items</li>
      <li>Consent agenda discipline</li>
      <li>Collective voice norm</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</div>

<h2>Portland Case Study: What Happens Without Alignment</h2>

<div class="case-study-box">
  <h4>Portland Public Schools — $45 Million Shortfall</h4>

  <p><strong>The situation:</strong> Oregon's largest district (44,000 students) walked into the 2024-25 budget cycle staring at a $45 million deficit. The ESSER cliff. Declining enrollment. Rising costs. The same triple threat every district is facing.</p>

  <p><strong>The response:</strong> 230 proposed position cuts — the largest RIF in PPS history. Teachers, counselors, instructional assistants. Summer programs gutted. School budgets slashed. Packed board meetings with tearful testimony. Student walkouts. A community coalition demanding action.</p>

  <p><strong>The governance failure:</strong> The PPS board was forced to make cuts with <strong>no clear, agreed-upon priority framework</strong>. No decision tree. No pre-existing consensus on what gets protected and what gets cut. Some board members pushed for deeper central cuts. Others warned about state takeover risk. The public watched a school board unable to reach consensus on foundational questions.</p>

  <p><strong>The result:</strong> Everything hurt equally. Nobody felt heard. Board members were publicly divided. Trust eroded in real time.</p>

  <p><strong>The lesson:</strong> Portland's story isn't about a bad board. It's about a board that didn't do the alignment work ahead of time — and then had to make impossible choices without a compass.</p>
</div>

<h2>Board Workshop Guide: Budget Alignment in 45 Minutes</h2>

<p>Run this workshop with your board. No superintendent. No staff. Just the governance team, a whiteboard, and honest conversation.</p>

<div class="workshop-step">
  <h4><span class="step-num">1</span> Set the Stage (5 minutes)</h4>
  <p>Re-read the Portland case study as a board. Ask: "Could this be us?" The goal is not to generate fear — it's to create urgency for doing the alignment work while you still have time.</p>
</div>

<div class="workshop-step">
  <h4><span class="step-num">2</span> The Index Card Test (10 minutes)</h4>
  <p>Hand every board member an index card. Ask them to write down the district's top three student outcome goals from memory — including the target number and deadline. Pass them in. Read them aloud. Count how many match.</p>
  <p>If more than half don't match, that's your first finding: your goals are wallpaper, not drivers. Discuss what it would take to change that.</p>
</div>

<div class="workshop-step">
  <h4><span class="step-num">3</span> The Priority Framework Exercise (15 minutes)</h4>
  <p>Present the board with a scenario: "We need to cut 5% of the budget. What do we protect first? What do we protect second? What's on the table?"</p>
  <p>Boards should not name specific programs or positions at this stage. They should name <strong>principles</strong> and <strong>priorities</strong>. Examples:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>"Classroom instruction is protected first."</li>
    <li>"Student safety is protected second."</li>
    <li>"Central administration is on the table before school sites."</li>
    <li>"Programs without measurable student outcome data are on the table first."</li>
  </ul>
  <p>The goal is a ranked list that every board member can support — not a perfect one.</p>
</div>

<div class="workshop-step">
  <h4><span class="step-num">4</span> The Monitoring Check (10 minutes)</h4>
  <p>Pull your most recent goal monitoring report. Ask:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Does this report connect spending to outcomes?</li>
    <li>Does each goal have at least one budget-linked metric?</li>
    <li>If we were trending flat on a goal, would we notice a spending shift?</li>
  </ul>
  <p>Schedule a follow-up with your superintendent's team to add budget metrics to the dashboard if they're missing.</p>
</div>

<div class="workshop-step">
  <h4><span class="step-num">5</span> Commit and Assign (5 minutes)</h4>
  <p>Decide on three concrete next steps. Assign an owner and a deadline for each. Examples:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>"Board president will draft a priority framework by next meeting."</li>
    <li>"Board requests budget-linked metrics on next quarterly report."</li>
    <li>"Board schedules a 30-minute follow-up before the preliminary budget is released."</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<h2>Scoring Your Board's Budget Alignment</h2>

<p>Use this simple scoring rubric to assess where your board stands today. Score each pillar on a scale of 1 (not started) to 4 (fully embedded).</p>

<table class="scoring-table">
  <tr>
    <th>Pillar</th>
    <th>1 — Not Started</th>
    <th>2 — Emerging</th>
    <th>3 — Practicing</th>
    <th>4 — Embedded</th>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td><strong>Budget Alignment</strong></td>
    <td>Budget has no link to strategic goals</td>
    <td>Goals mentioned in budget narrative but no traceability</td>
    <td>Major line items can be traced to goals</td>
    <td>Every significant spend traces to a goal; priority framework agreed</td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td><strong>Goal Monitoring</strong></td>
    <td>No monitoring dashboard or quarterly reports</td>
    <td>Reports delivered but not discussed in board meetings</td>
    <td>Reports discussed; some budget context included</td>
    <td>Budget-linked metrics for every goal; early warning system active</td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td><strong>Conflict Navigation</strong></td>
    <td>Board divisions are public; no norms for disagreement</td>
    <td>Norms exist but aren't enforced consistently</td>
    <td>Norms are followed; board stays unified publicly</td>
    <td>Board actively uses principles-first framework for tough decisions</td>
  </tr>
</table>

<p><strong>Total score:</strong> 3-6 = needs urgent work. 7-9 = on the right track. 10-12 = top-quartile board.</p>

<div class="section-card">
  <h4>Next Steps Based on Your Score</h4>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Score 3-6:</strong> Start with Question 1 of the audit. Run the 45-minute workshop. Do not wait for the budget crisis to force the conversation.</li>
    <li><strong>Score 7-9:</strong> You have a foundation. Focus on the gaps — likely the budget-linked metrics (Pillar 2) or the priority framework (Pillar 1). Schedule a follow-up workshop to lock them down.</li>
    <li><strong>Score 10-12:</strong> You're ahead of most boards. Share this resource pack with another board in your region. The work isn't done until every board in your area can answer these four questions.</li>
  </ul>
</div>
